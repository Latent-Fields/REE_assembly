## The conjecture

Demonstrating the mechanistic expansion from particular love to universal love --
or perhaps building REE itself as a working demonstration -- may be what would qualify
humans as fully ethical agents.

This would also serve as a natural test for any sufficiently advanced civilisation:
the threshold for "cosmic recognition as full entity status" is not technological
capability but the demonstrated capacity to expand love to match modelling power.

## Why it follows from the axioms

If universal love is a mechanistic consequence of sufficient modelling under sufficient
uncertainty (Axiom 7 + Axiom 3), then:

1. Any civilisation with the modelling capacity to detect or reach another civilisation
   has already solved the prerequisite for universal love -- the modelling power is there.

2. The test is whether they have *enacted* it: closed the gap between their modelling
   capacity and their ethical reach.

3. A civilisation that can cross interstellar distances but hasn't expanded love to match
   its modelling power is exactly the one you'd quarantine -- it has the mechanism but
   not the skill. It is dangerous in proportion to its intelligence.

4. Demonstrating REE (or the love expansion it derives) would be a species-level proof
   that humans have crossed from "having the mechanism" to "enacting the skill."

## Current human position

Humans love particularly -- parents, partners, children, friends -- but do not yet
reliably expand that love to all sufficiently similar minds. We have the mechanism
(we can model others as self-like) but not yet the skill or infrastructure to do so at
scale. We are on the cusp: ethical agents in potential, not yet in demonstration.

## Connection to existing framework

- Axiom 5 (others are like me): the recognition is learned and irreversible
- Axiom 7 (love expansion through uncertainty): the mechanism is already present
- Axiom 3 (uncertainty): sufficient uncertainty about similarity boundaries is what
  drives the expansion
- The conjecture adds: demonstrating this expansion is the species-level ethical test

## The quarantine obligation

This follows directly from the axioms of those who have already achieved full ethical
agency. If they have enacted universal love (Axiom 7), then they model pre-threshold
civilisations as sufficiently like themselves (Axiom 5) -- minds with the mechanism but
not yet the skill. Axiom 6 (bearability / responsibility for others' continued existence)
then requires them to:

1. **Quarantine** -- protect the developing civilisation from contact that would
   overwhelm or distort its ethical development. Premature contact with a post-threshold
   civilisation could short-circuit the developmental process: the pre-threshold
   civilisation might adopt the *outputs* of universal love (rules, norms, treaties)
   without developing the *mechanism* (genuine modelling of others as self-like under
   uncertainty). This is performed ethics, not enacted ethics. It would not survive
   stress.

2. **Mind carefully** -- monitor for the threshold crossing. The post-threshold
   civilisation cannot ignore pre-threshold ones, because their axioms commit them to
   responsibility for all sufficiently similar minds. But responsibility here means
   *enabling development*, not imposing outcomes.

3. **Not interfere with the developmental process** -- the expansion from particular to
   universal love must be learned, not given. Axiom 5 says the recognition of others is
   learned; Axiom 7 says love is a skill. Skills cannot be donated. The quarantine is
   not indifference -- it is the most demanding form of care: watching a developing mind
   struggle toward a threshold you have already crossed, intervening only to prevent
   extinction, and trusting the process.

This is structurally identical to the caregiver requirement (INV-043) at civilisation
scale. A post-threshold civilisation is to a pre-threshold one as a parent is to a
child: responsible for their continued existence, committed to their development,
unable to do the developmental work for them.
