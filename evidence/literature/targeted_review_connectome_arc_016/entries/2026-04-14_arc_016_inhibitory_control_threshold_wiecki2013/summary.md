# Wiecki & Frank 2013 -- A Computational Model of Inhibitory Control in Frontal Cortex and Basal Ganglia

**Source:** Wiecki TV, Frank MJ (2013). *Psychological Review* 120(2):329-355. [DOI: 10.1037/a0031542](https://doi.org/10.1037/a0031542). PMID: 23586447.

---

## What the paper did

Wiecki and Frank present a neural circuit model of inhibitory control instantiated in spiking and rate-coded neural units, intended to explain how the frontal cortex and basal ganglia jointly implement the stopping or suppression of responses. The model extends Frank's earlier BG action selection model by adding a frontal executive layer that integrates sensory information and task rules, and that communicates with the subthalamic nucleus (STN) via the hyperdirect pathway. The model is validated against behavioural and neural data from multiple response inhibition paradigms: the antisaccade task, the Simon task, and the saccade-override task. The core mechanism is a loop in which the ACC detects competing or conflicting response representations, sends a conflict signal to the STN, and the STN raises the decision threshold by globally suppressing striatal drive to motor output -- making it harder for any response to reach the threshold for execution until the conflict is resolved.

## Key findings relevant to ARC-016

The paper's central contribution for ARC-016 is a mechanistic, validated computational account of STN-mediated threshold gating. The threshold is not a fixed parameter but a dynamically adjustable variable, raised by frontal conflict signals and released when conflict resolves. The model successfully reproduces the "pause-then-execute" dynamics seen in response inhibition: when the ACC detects conflict, the STN gate rises (all responses are suppressed), and when the stronger response wins, the gate releases and execution proceeds. This is a direct instantiation of the threshold-gated action commitment mechanism that ARC-016 proposes. Importantly, the model demonstrates that the circuit can implement a genuinely discrete gate -- not a gradual weighting -- by using the STN's global inhibitory effect on striatal outputs: the threshold effectively creates a winner-take-all bottleneck that forces a single committed response once conflict is resolved.

## Translation to REE / ARC-016

ARC-016's circuit runs: E3-derived variance -- threshold -- BetaGate -- action selection. Wiecki & Frank provide the most mechanistically detailed account of the threshold -- BetaGate -- action selection segment of this circuit. In REE terms: E3 plays the ACC role (detecting uncertainty or conflict in the current plan), the hyperdirect pathway corresponds to the mechanism by which E3 raises the commit threshold, the STN-mediated global inhibition is the BetaGate's inhibitory mechanism, and the threshold release is the commitment event. The model's demonstration that this circuit produces discrete committed/uncommitted dynamics (pause-then-execute, not a gradual slope) is direct evidence for the threshold-gated mode interpretation that ARC-016's discrete BetaGate claim requires. This paper provides the computational grounding for the gating mechanism that Forstmann 2008 demonstrated empirically at the neural level.

## Limitations and caveats

The primary limitation is the same one that applies to Forstmann 2008: the threshold-raising signal in Wiecki & Frank's model is driven by response conflict (ACC detecting competing motor representations) rather than by E3-derived prediction variance (the internal uncertainty estimate that ARC-016 specifies). This is an important distinction: conflict-driven threshold adjustment is reactive (responds to external competition) while ARC-016's variance-driven threshold is proactive (generated from internal predictive uncertainty before any competing response is activated). The circuit topology is the same, but the functional logic differs. Additionally, the model operates at the granularity of individual action-selection trials -- each trial has a threshold that is raised and then released as the response executes. ARC-016's commitment threshold operates at a higher level: it determines whether the agent is in committed or uncommitted mode, which persists across many individual action steps. This scale difference means the paper provides mechanistic grounding for one aspect of ARC-016's circuit but does not directly model the sustained cognitive mode that ARC-016 describes.
