# Summary: Borodovitsyna et al. 2018 — Persistent Stress-Induced LC/NE Neuroplasticity

**Entry:** 2026-04-06_mech_178_stress_lc_borodovitsyna2018
**Claim:** MECH-178

## What the paper shows

Borodovitsyna, Joshi, and Chandler review the growing body of evidence that stress -- particularly chronic or traumatic stress -- produces persistent, not merely transient, neuroplastic changes in the locus coeruleus/norepinephrine system. Corticotropin-releasing factor (CRF) released during stress activates CRF receptors on LC neurons, driving sustained increases in tonic firing that persist well beyond the acute stressor and are associated with morphological changes including dendritic remodelling and altered receptor expression. The review notes that prolonged and repeated LC activation leads to maladaptive responses and is implicated in stress-induced neuropsychiatric conditions including depression, PTSD, and anxiety -- conditions characterised by, among other things, cognitive inflexibility and thinking rigidity.

## Why it matters for MECH-178

MECH-178 identifies chronic stress as one of three routes through which tonic LC-NE activity can be driven persistently above the silence threshold required for REM generation. For this claim to be plausible, it must be established that chronic stress does indeed produce sustained (not just acute) LC hyperactivity -- i.e., that the LC does not simply return to baseline once the stressor resolves. Borodovitsyna et al. 2018 directly supports this: the neuroplastic changes they document are specifically persistent, representing a maintained elevation of tonic LC output rather than a transient stress response. This addresses one of the most challenging aspects of MECH-178 -- the word "chronic" in the claim -- by providing mechanistic grounding for why stress-induced LC hyperactivity would be sustained enough to produce cumulative REM suppression.

## Honest evaluation of fit

The fit is partial. The review confirms the upstream portion of MECH-178's causal chain (chronic stress -> persistent LC hyperactivity) directly. However, it does not close the circuit to REM suppression or to behavioural rigidity via the sleep pathway specifically. The cognitive inflexibility and thinking rigidity noted in the review are attributed primarily to waking-state NE dysregulation -- including the inverted-U dose-response relationship between NE and PFC function -- rather than to REM-deprivation-mediated effects. This matters because MECH-178 makes a specific mechanistic claim that the rigidity arises through the sleep pathway, not simply through daytime excess NE. The review's behavioral observations are therefore consistent with but not probative for MECH-178's specific mechanism.

## Epistemic status

A solid 2018 review in Neural Plasticity, covering an active area with substantial rodent evidence and growing human clinical correlates. The limitations are the usual ones for stress neuroscience: most detailed mechanistic work is in rodents, and the relationship between animal stress-induced LC changes and clinical human presentations is imprecisely mapped. The review is most valuable here as confirmation that chronic stress produces the kind of sustained LC hyperactivity that MECH-178 invokes, rather than as evidence for the downstream sleep-mediated rigidity mechanism.
