# Sakai, Kitaguchi, Hikosaka 2003 -- Chunking during human visuomotor sequence learning

The behavioural human-subject paper that operationalises chunk identification through inter-element timing. Sakai et al. test healthy subjects on a 2x10 visuomotor sequence task with extensive practice and identify chunks via the spontaneous emergence of inter-element time gaps -- subjects perform sequences as several clusters separated by longer gaps, with each cluster acting as a single memory unit. The empirical chunk-size constraint is 2-4 items, occasionally 5.

This paper is the load-bearing source for R5 (outcome-consistency threshold and chunk-size budget). The chunk-size budget gives ARC-071 a concrete biological default: chunks of 2-5 primitive elements, not chunks of 15. ARC-071 implementations that produce dramatically larger chunks are over-aggressive; implementations that fail to compose 2-3-element sequences are under-aggressive. The "cluster becomes clearer with learning" trajectory implies the outcome-consistency threshold is gradual rather than step-function -- chunks crystallise progressively as inter-element-time variance drops. ARC-071 implementation should track outcome variance over a sliding window and form chunks when variance drops below a threshold; the threshold itself is implicit in the gradual-clarification dynamic.

The behavioural signature of chunk formation is the inter-element timing pattern. ARC-071 implementations should produce the same signature: faster within-chunk timing than between-chunk timing. This is a direct behavioural test that an REE agent can be evaluated on -- if chunks formed, behavioural latency on within-chunk transitions should drop relative to between-chunk transitions. This is a sharper falsifiable signature than the family doc's pre-registered "rollout deliberation cost drops measurably" prediction; the synthesis should add it as a secondary behavioural prediction.

The spontaneous-chunking-without-instruction finding matters for the ARC-071 substrate-level claim. Subjects in Sakai et al. were not instructed to chunk; chunking emerged automatically as a consequence of repetition. ARC-071 should fire automatically (not require explicit goal-driven chunking instruction) -- the slot's pre-registration is consistent with this, but the synthesis can pin the automaticity property explicitly.

Two caveats. First, Sakai et al. is on finger-sequence learning -- a fine-grained motor task with elements of ~milliseconds each. REE's 'primitives' are coarser than Sakai's 'elements' (single-action units over seconds, not finger-presses over milliseconds). The chunk-size budget (2-5) is biologically robust at one grain; whether it transfers to coarser-grain REE primitives is open. The synthesis should flag this for the child-MECH design pass. Second, the briefing pre-registered Sakai 2003 as Curr Op Neurobiol; the paper is actually Exp Brain Res. Same authors, same year, similar topic -- minor citation correction for the slot's notes.
