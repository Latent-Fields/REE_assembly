# Palagi et al. 2024: Smiling Underwater -- Play Signals and Rapid Mimicry in Bottlenose Dolphins

## What the paper did

Palagi and colleagues filmed 22 captive bottlenose dolphins across two facilities (Zoomarine Rome and Planete Sauvage, France) over 80 hours of video. They characterized a previously undescribed facial display -- the open mouth (OM) -- during social play and tested: (1) whether OM is context-specific (more common in social than solitary play), (2) whether OM is attention-directed (emitted within the partner's visual field), and (3) whether OM triggers rapid facial mimicry (RFM) -- automatic replication within 1 second by the receiver.

## Key findings relevant to the claim

1,288 OM events were recorded during social play; only 1 during solitary play. OMs were emitted within the receiver's visual field in 89% of cases, significantly more than non-OM motor patterns. When the receiver could detect an OM, replication within 1 second was 13 times more likely than in the control condition (receiver could not see the OM). The 33% replication rate matches that of terrestrial carnivore species. OMs never accompanied actual biting attempts, confirming they are ritualized signals, not defensive or predatory motor patterns. Dolphins did not emit OMs toward human trainers at similar rates to conspecifics, suggesting species-appropriate calibration.

## Translation to REE

This is the capstone cross-species finding for the generalizability argument. Bottlenose dolphins are approximately 95 million years evolutionarily removed from primates and terrestrial carnivores, inhabit a completely different sensory ecology (acoustic primary communication, aquatic), and have a very different facial musculature. Yet they have independently evolved an open-mouth background signal with identical properties: social-context specificity, visual-field sensitivity, and RFM. This convergence is the strongest evidence that the level-2 background signal mechanism is an adaptive solution to a universal play-frame maintenance problem rather than a phylogenetic inheritance. For REE's ARC-049, the convergent evidence justifies treating the three-level signal architecture as a general principle rather than a canid or primate feature. The 13x RFM odds ratio is the first quantitative operationalization of mutual frame maintenance intensity: high-RFM dyads maintain the frame more reliably. This provides an implementation metric for REE's experiments: frame maintenance integrity could be measured as the within-1s replication rate of background signals between partners.

## Limitations and caveats

Captive dolphin facilities limit ecological validity. The acoustic communication channel (whistles during play) was not captured, so OM may be one component of a multimodal signal system. The two-facility design increases generalizability compared to a single captive population. The interspecific play data (human-dolphin) suggests species-calibrated signal systems -- which may be relevant for REE's implementation context (AI-human play frames may require explicit signal adaptation).
