# Bekoff 1995: Play Signals as Punctuation -- The Structure of Social Play in Canids

## What the paper did

Marc Bekoff systematically observed play bows in infant domestic dogs, infant wolves, infant coyotes, and adult domestic dogs, using sequence analysis to determine whether bows are randomly distributed throughout play or clustered at specific moments. The key dependent variable was the temporal placement of play bows relative to potentially ambiguous actions -- specifically, biting accompanied by rapid side-to-side head shaking (bite-shake), which also occurs during predatory and agonistic encounters.

## Key findings relevant to the claim

Play bows are non-randomly placed: they occur significantly more often immediately before or after bite-shakes than at other moments during play. In puppies, the most common pattern was bow -> bite-shake -> bow (bowing before AND after the ambiguous action). This temporal clustering supports the interpretation that bows function to bracket ambiguous actions, signaling to the partner that the preceding or following action is playful rather than aggressive. Play bows were highly stereotyped motor patterns, described as "rigid and reproducible." The paper also noted that play sequences overall are more variable than agonistic sequences, and this variability itself serves as a composite play signal -- but the bow is the primary punctuation device.

## Translation to REE

This paper is the empirical foundation for REE's level-3 explicit repair signal concept in ARC-049. The three-level architecture (open/close event + background signal + repair signal) maps directly onto Bekoff's findings: level-1 open events correspond to play initiation signals, level-2 background signals correspond to the body orientation and general play sequence variability that maintains the play mood, and level-3 repair signals correspond precisely to the bows deployed at ambiguity moments. Bekoff's data directly answers Q-035 (is open/close sufficient?): no, because play episodes contain ambiguous actions that require in-session clarification. An architecture with only open/close events and no repair mechanism would allow an episode containing bite-shakes to be misread and terminate as aggression.

## Limitations and caveats

The paper's primary species are canids. Cross-species generalization requires additional evidence (provided by the other entries in this review: gorilla, gelada, hyena, dolphin, raven). Additionally, Byosiere et al. (2017, PLoS One, doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0168570) found that in adult dogs, play bows most frequently follow brief pauses in play rather than bracketing bite-shakes specifically -- suggesting the repair/reinitiation function may shift across development. Bekoff's 1995 data are primarily from infant animals. This developmental modulation is important for REE's DEV-NEED-009 (play-dominant childhood) context: the explicit repair signal mechanism may have a developmental trajectory of its own.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is 0.88. This is the canonical founding paper, with sound behavioral methodology, systematic sequence analysis across three species, and clear non-random placement results. Confidence is not maximal because (a) the developmental modulation found by Byosiere et al. complicates the repair interpretation for adults, and (b) cross-species generalization beyond canids is not established in this paper alone.
