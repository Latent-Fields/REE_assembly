# Davila-Ross & Palagi 2022: Laughter, Play Faces, and Mimicry in Animals

## What the paper did

Marina Davila-Ross and Elisabetta Palagi contributed a major synthetic review to a Phil Trans Royal Society B theme issue on laughter. The paper synthesizes phylogenetic evidence across hominids and multiple carnivore taxa, adds new original muscle-activation coding (using FACS-derived systems) for 6 carnivore species (including meerkats and sun bears), and reviews the rapid facial mimicry (RFM) literature across species. The paper establishes whether open-mouth play expressions in carnivores are homologues or analogues of primate play faces.

## Key findings relevant to the claim

Carnivore open-mouth play expressions are phylogenetic homologues of primate play faces, not independently evolved analogues -- the muscle activation patterns are conserved. Rapid facial mimicry (RFM), the automatic replication of a partner's play expression within 500ms of perceiving it, is documented across primates and multiple carnivore species. RFM is outside voluntary control (like a reflex), operates below 500ms latency, and predicts play bout duration: sessions with more RFM are longer and more balanced. The play face has an evolutionary history from ritualization of the biting intention, and serves to communicate positive emotional state and playful mood to the partner. Human laughter and laugh faces show continuity with this mammalian background signal system.

## Translation to REE

This is the pivotal cross-species evidence that validates generalizing the three-level play signal architecture beyond canids. The phylogenetic homology finding means the background signal mechanism (level-2 in Bekoff's framework) is conserved across mammals, not a canid-specific invention. The RFM mechanism is the neural implementation of INV-059: each partner's brain reflexively tracks and mirrors the other's background signal within 500ms. Frame maintenance in play is thus implemented at a rapid, pre-deliberative layer -- a fast automatic matching process rather than a cognitive theory-of-mind computation. For REE's architecture, this implies: (1) the level-2 background signal channel needs sub-second latency; (2) the implementation is more like a resonance circuit than a reasoning step; (3) background signals that are successfully mirrored extend episode duration, providing an operationalizable metric for REE's frame maintenance integrity. DEV-NEED-009 is supported: the RFM mechanism develops through play experience and neural maturation (as shown by Palagi 2011 in geladas).

## Limitations and caveats

The homology claim for carnivore expressions rests on FACS-derived muscle coding applied to anatomically different faces -- methodological comparability across taxa requires assumptions that may not fully hold. The RFM literature is dominated by captive primate data. Wild population data on RFM timing is sparse. The evolutionary 'homology' designation for play signals has been debated in the ethological literature; some argue for mosaic homology (shared origin of some components, independent elaboration of others).

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is 0.85. Very high source quality (Phil Trans Royal Society B, leading authors), strong cross-species synthesis, new empirical data on carnivore muscle activations, and the RFM-duration relationship provides the mechanistic link between background signals and frame maintenance function. Confidence not maximal because homology claims across very different taxa involve methodological assumptions and the RFM data are predominantly captive.
