# Summary: Yan & Rein (2021)

**Claim tested:** MECH-188 -- PFC-mediated goal persistence under adversity, sustained by 5-HT projections from DRN to dlPFC

**Source:** Yan Z, Rein B (2021). Mechanisms of synaptic transmission dysregulation in the prefrontal cortex: pathophysiological implications. Molecular Psychiatry, 27(1):445-465.
DOI: https://doi.org/10.1038/s41380-021-01092-3 (PMID: 33875802, PMC8523584)

---

Yan and Rein provide the synaptic anatomy that MECH-188 needs but does not supply for itself. MECH-188 claims that 5-HT projections from DRN to dlPFC sustain working memory representations -- specifically the z_goal representation -- against adversarial interruption. The cellular mechanism by which this could happen is found in this review: PFC working memory is sustained by NMDA receptor-mediated recurrent excitation among layer V pyramidal neurons during delay periods. The key serotonin entry point is the 5-HT2A receptor, expressed on these same layer V pyramidal cells, which modulates NMDAR function by disrupting the microtubule/kinesin-based dendritic transport of NMDA receptors. 5-HT2C has the opposing effect. The net serotonergic tone in dlPFC therefore sets the gain of the NMDA-mediated recurrent excitation that underlies persistent z_goal representation.

This anatomy is the proximal mechanism MECH-188 is missing. The claim invokes DRN projections to dlPFC but does not specify how those projections translate into maintained goal representations at the cellular level. Yan and Rein close that gap: adequate 5-HT tone balances 5-HT2A and 5-HT2C effects to permit the NMDA-sustained persistent firing that constitutes working memory maintenance. The z_goal representation in REE terms is the computational description of what these layer V neurons are physically doing.

The review's stress hormone section adds an important temporal dimension. Acute stress -- analogous to a transient z_harm_a spike -- enhances glutamatergic transmission in PFC via glucocorticoid receptors, which could transiently boost NMDA-mediated recurrent excitation and thus briefly stabilise z_goal. Chronic stress -- analogous to sustained elevated z_harm_a -- has the opposite long-term effect: it produces dendritic atrophy, spine loss, and impaired LTP in hippocampus-to-PFC connections. This predicts a time course to MECH-188 failure: brief harm spikes may be tolerable or even briefly stabilising, but sustained harm exposure progressively degrades the PFC substrate itself, eventually making goal persistence impossible regardless of serotonin tone. This is architecturally important for REE: it implies MECH-188 operates reliably only within a harm-exposure time window, and that the V3/V4 distinction (whether z_harm_a elevation is transient or chronic) is biologically load-bearing.

The limitation of this review for MECH-188 purposes is that it does not test goal representation maintenance as a dependent variable. It covers how 5-HT modulates synaptic transmission in PFC circuits -- a necessary but not sufficient condition for MECH-188. The mapping requires accepting that "working memory maintenance" in the delay-period firing literature is the biological substrate of what MECH-188 calls z_goal persistence. That identification is highly plausible but not directly proven in this paper. The receptor-level pharmacology is also complex enough that the direction of 5-HT effects depends on basal activity levels and which receptor subtypes dominate in a given dlPFC context -- the review does not resolve this for the harm-spike condition specifically.

For EXP-0100: this review suggests an important confound check. If the z_goal injection experiment is run with elevated harm terrain, the intervention may partially work via glutamatergic upregulation (acute stress effect) rather than via the maintenance circuit per se. Separating NMDA-mediated persistent activity from acute stress-enhanced excitability in the experiment's dependent measures would strengthen the mechanistic interpretation.
