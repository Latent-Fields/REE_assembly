# Summary: Miyazaki et al. (2020)

**Claim tested:** MECH-188 -- PFC-mediated goal persistence under adversity, sustained by 5-HT projections from DRN to dlPFC

**Source:** Miyazaki K, Miyazaki KW, Sivori G, Yamanaka A, Tanaka KF, Doya K (2020). Serotonergic projections to the orbitofrontal and medial prefrontal cortices differentially modulate waiting for future rewards. Science Advances, 6(48).
DOI: https://doi.org/10.1126/sciadv.abc7246 (PMID: 33246957, PMC7695476)

---

This paper from the Doya lab at OIST is the closest thing available to a direct causal test of the circuit MECH-188 invokes. Using Tph2-ChR2 transgenic mice with optogenetic axon terminal stimulation, Miyazaki and colleagues selectively activated serotonergic terminals in the orbitofrontal cortex (OFC), medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC), or nucleus accumbens (NAc), then measured how each manipulation affected patience -- the willingness to wait for a delayed reward. The results are a clean dissociation: OFC serotonin strongly promotes waiting across all delay conditions; mPFC serotonin promotes waiting selectively when reward timing is uncertain; NAc serotonin does not promote waiting at all.

The mPFC finding is the most directly relevant to MECH-188. The selective effect of mPFC 5-HT under temporal uncertainty maps closely onto the scenario MECH-188 addresses: benefit terrain has temporarily collapsed (making the timing and probability of future benefit uncertain), and the agent must maintain goal-directed behaviour without the bottom-up terrain signal providing continuous reinforcement. Under precisely these conditions, serotonergic input to mPFC is what sustains waiting behaviour. Importantly, this effect is causal -- it is not a correlational observation but the result of optogenetically driven 5-HT release at the axon terminal, confirming that the DRN projection is a functional contributor, not merely an anatomical co-traveller.

The Bayesian decision model the authors build to explain the results provides additional architecture. In their framework, OFC calculates the overall probability that a reward will eventually arrive (analogous to the benefit terrain signal in REE -- is there a goal worth pursuing?), while mPFC evaluates the time committed so far and updates the estimate of whether delivery is still pending (analogous to goal maintenance -- the agent has already invested in the goal path and the question is whether to persist). This double dissociation between OFC and mPFC contributions is relevant to how MECH-188 should be related to MECH-186 and MECH-187: the benefit terrain mechanisms (MECH-186/187) may map more to OFC valuation, while MECH-188's maintenance mechanism maps to mPFC temporal commitment.

The transfer risk is moderate. The task is a positive-reward waiting paradigm, not an aversive harm context. The subjects are not experiencing harm events that threaten to displace the goal -- they are simply waiting, with varying levels of temporal uncertainty. The mapping to MECH-188's harm-spike scenario is analogical: uncertain timing in this paradigm plays the role that harm-induced terrain collapse plays in REE. The rodent mPFC also does not directly correspond to primate dlPFC, and the OFC contribution in this paper may be stronger than MECH-188's single-pathway formulation acknowledges.

For EXP-0100: this paper provides direct support for the intervention design. Injecting a constant z_goal signal bypasses the terrain-based seeding exactly as optogenetic 5-HT terminal stimulation bypasses natural serotonin release -- both inject the maintenance signal without modifying the underlying terrain. The prediction from Miyazaki et al. would be that the intervention should be most effective when benefit terrain is degraded (uncertain timing) and should have smaller effects when terrain provides a strong bottom-up signal (the OFC component is already engaged). This suggests EXP-0100 could usefully vary the degree of terrain collapse to test whether the z_goal injection shows the same uncertainty-dependent benefit that mPFC 5-HT does.
