# Summary: Cools, Roberts & Robbins (2008)

**Claim tested:** MECH-188 -- PFC-mediated goal persistence under adversity, sustained by 5-HT projections from DRN to dlPFC

**Source:** Cools R, Roberts AC, Robbins TW (2008). Serotoninergic regulation of emotional and behavioural control processes. Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 12(1):31-40.
DOI: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tics.2007.10.011(PMID: 18069045)

---

The Cools, Roberts and Robbins review is the most cited synthetic account of what serotonin actually does in behavioural and emotional control, and its value for MECH-188 is both direct and somewhat oblique in equal measure. The paper's central argument is that 5-HT is not a monolithic "mood regulator" but rather modulates two separable processes: the sensitivity of the organism to aversive signals, and the capacity to inhibit responses that have been previously associated with punishment. Depletion of 5-HT, they argue, makes the brain more reactive to punishment and simultaneously impairs the ability to suppress actions despite that reactivity. The two effects can be dissociated experimentally and they correspond to distinct clinical presentations -- exaggerated threat vulnerability on one hand, compulsive perseveration on the other.

This framing maps usefully onto the failure mode that MECH-188 is designed to prevent. The claim is that PFC-mediated goal persistence maintains z_goal through periods of elevated harm -- harm spikes or temporary collapses in benefit terrain that would otherwise extinguish the goal representation. Cools et al. describe exactly the biological mechanism that would produce this failure: low 5-HT tone in PFC circuits means that punishment-related signals (z_harm_a in REE terms) are amplified and the response-inhibition capacity that would normally gate out harm-avoidance behaviours is degraded. The result is that every harm event displaces the current goal state. This is the "cannot-hold-a-plan" phenomenology MECH-188 is meant to address.

What the review does not provide is a direct test of goal-representation maintenance under aversive challenge. It treats the behavioural outputs -- choice behaviour, response inhibition, punishment avoidance -- rather than the internal representational state. The inferential step from "5-HT modulates aversive processing in PFC" to "5-HT in dlPFC sustains z_goal working memory during harm spikes" requires additional anatomical and physiological reasoning that this review motivates but does not complete. The PFC region is somewhat underspecified: the review synthesises work across prelimbic cortex, orbitofrontal cortex, and dorsolateral regions without consistently distinguishing their roles.

Despite this caveat, the Robbins lab framing is foundational for any argument about 5-HT in PFC-mediated behavioural control. The translational breadth -- rodent lesion and depletion studies, non-human primate pharmacology, and human imaging and psychopharmacology -- gives the claim robustness across levels of analysis. For MECH-188 specifically, the paper is best read as establishing the mechanistic plausibility of the DRN-to-PFC pathway as a substrate for goal persistence, and as providing the failure-mode vocabulary (aversion sensitivity and response inhibition) that the REE architecture needs to distinguish.

For the EXP-0100 design (injecting a constant z_goal signal to bypass terrain-based seeding): this paper suggests the intervention is biologically plausible precisely because it mimics what adequate 5-HT tone in dlPFC should do -- maintain a goal representation against aversive interruption, independent of the ongoing benefit-terrain signal. If the experiment PASSes, it will show that the maintenance mechanism is separable from the seeding mechanism, which is what Cools et al.'s two-process framework predicts at the circuit level.
