# Mansouri, Buckley, Mahboubi & Tanaka 2015 — Behavioural Consequences of Selective Damage to Frontal Pole and Posterior Cingulate Cortices

## What the paper does

Mansouri and colleagues make selective bilateral aspiration lesions to frontal pole cortex (FPC) in one group of macaques and to posterior cingulate cortex (PCC) in another, run both groups along with age-matched controls through analogues of the Wisconsin Card Sorting Test and related executive tasks, and compare their behavioural profiles. The methodology matters: this is one of the few studies that cleanly isolates FPC damage in a primate, whereas human frontopolar lesions almost never occur in isolation. The lesions are made after training so pre-existing rule knowledge is preserved.

## Key findings relevant to SD-033e

Three findings matter for REE's V4 scoping of SD-033e. First, FPC lesions do not impair WCST performance or within-task rule-switching. The animals still track rule changes and update their behaviour accordingly. This is a crucial null result — it dissociates frontopolar function from the rule-maintenance and rule-switching functions that SD-033a (lateral-PFC-analog) covers.

Second, FPC-lesioned animals are MORE focused than controls under distraction. When interrupted by a secondary task or by a surprising free-reward delivery, they disengage less readily from the ongoing task and retain the current rule across the distraction. The authors interpret this paradoxically: FPC is not broken executive control; its function is to ENABLE disengagement from the current task. Without FPC, the animal becomes over-focused, cognitively inflexible in a way that looks like improved performance on the narrow metric but represents a loss of the higher-order capacity to consider alternatives.

Third, FPC-lesioned animals are IMPAIRED at rapid learning of novel alternative abstract rules. They cannot efficiently pivot to explore a genuinely new option when one is presented. The authors synthesise the three findings as: FPC is specialised for disengaging executive control from the current task and redistributing it to novel sources of reward — exploration rather than exploitation.

## How this translates into REE

For SD-033e this is the cleanest primate-mechanism entry available. It does two things:

- **Grounds SD-033e as a disengagement-for-exploration substrate, not a rule-holding substrate.** The rule-holding function belongs to SD-033a (lateral-PFC-analog). SD-033e enables the agent to notice that the current task might not be worth continuing and to disengage from it to explore an alternative. That is a computationally distinct function, and ablating it produces a specific failure mode (over-focus + inability to learn novel alternatives) rather than generic executive disruption.

- **Converges with Boorman 2009 and Burgess 2007.** Boorman shows frontopolar tracks the value of counterfactual alternatives; Mansouri shows frontopolar enables disengaging from the current task to pursue those alternatives; Burgess frames the common thread as a gateway between engagement with the current external world and internally-generated cognition about alternatives. The three findings are mutually consistent and jointly specify what SD-033e is for. V4 design should implement all three aspects — not just Koechlin's branching — as a coherent function.

## Limitations and caveats

Macaque FPC is homologous to human BA 10 but substantially smaller and less differentiated. The task is WCST-adjacent rule-switching with monetary-equivalent reward, not naturalistic multi-goal deliberation. How cleanly the disengagement-for-exploration framing extrapolates to V4's ethical-deliberation target is an open question this paper does not address. But it is the best primate-lesion grounding for the function available.

## Confidence reasoning

Source quality very high — PNAS, Buckley and Tanaka labs, selective-lesion primate methodology that is rare and informative. Mapping fidelity high — disengagement-for-exploration is a specific and testable substrate function that V4 SD-033e can implement. Transfer risk modest — primate-to-human homology is clean for BA 10, cleaner than for most lesion substrates. Net confidence 0.82.
