# Bouton 2004 — context and behavioral processes in extinction

The dual-trace anchor design made a strong commitment before this literature was consulted: an anchor that becomes invalid is *marked inactive, not erased*, and its payload is preserved so it can be found again. SD-039 stores the goal snapshot precisely so a blocked goal survives as something more than anonymous stale terrain, and ARC-060 asserts a discrete bank of such preserved traces is the right structure. The question worth asking honestly is whether that was a lucky guess or whether biology actually works this way. Bouton's 2004 synthesis is the canonical answer, and it is almost eerily aligned.

His central claim, built from decades of conditioning work, is that extinction does not destroy the original learning. It creates a *new*, inhibitory, context-dependent memory that competes with the original — and the original remains latent and recoverable. The evidence is the family of return phenomena: renewal (the response comes back when the cue is tested outside the extinction context), spontaneous recovery (it comes back with the passage of time), reinstatement (it comes back after a lone outcome exposure), and rapid reacquisition. The unifying idea is that an extinguished cue is *ambiguous* — it now has two histories — and which meaning is expressed is selected by the retrieval context, where "context" is broad: physical place, interoceptive and mood state, recent events, and elapsed time all count.

For the cue-system reframe this is strong, direct support for the architecture's foundational move. Preserve-don't-erase is not an engineering convenience; it is what nervous systems do. And it sharpens what `recoverability` in MECH-292 should actually mean: not a decay scalar but *context-selection between competing traces* — the bank should be deciding which of two histories the present context licenses, which is a richer and more biologically faithful operation than "how stale is this."

I have to record the other half of this honestly, because it is the part that should change a design decision rather than just validate one. The same theory that vindicates preserve-don't-erase also predicts the pathology of a system built only that way. Inhibition is *more* context-specific than the original excitatory trace, so the maladaptive original generalises back more readily than its suppression. Small triggers — a single outcome re-exposure, one reinforced repetition, merely a change of room — snap the whole inactive trace back at full strength with no new evidence. A bank whose only tool for "letting go" is context-dependent inhibition will, by this model, equilibrate toward intrusion and relapse; rumination is the *predicted steady state* of an inhibition-only design, not an aberration of it. Biology conspicuously has no clean "this trace is now safe to delete" operation. An artificial system does have that option, and this paper is the strongest argument I have seen that we will need it — that `recoverability` cannot be the only thing standing between the bank and rumination, and that an explicit abandon path, which the biology lacks, may be exactly the place where the right design should *diverge* from the biology rather than imitate it.
