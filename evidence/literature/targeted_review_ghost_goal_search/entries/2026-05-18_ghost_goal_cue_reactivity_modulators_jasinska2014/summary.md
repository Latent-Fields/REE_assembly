# Jasinska et al. 2014 — factors modulating neural drug-cue reactivity

Every term in `ghost_priority` is a hypothesis about how a cue's pull should be scaled, and the cleanest place to see those scaling rules fail is clinical addiction, where cue-triggered wanting is the disorder itself. That is why this review belongs in the strand even though — and partly *because* — it describes the pathological extreme. Jasinska and colleagues survey the human neuroimaging of drug-cue reactivity and assemble it into a structured account of the *modulators* that scale the response: treatment status, length and intensity of use, severity, abstinence, stress, drug availability, cue modality, and explicit or implicit cognitive regulation.

Two of those modulators map encouragingly onto our design. Availability and expectancy strongly gate the cue-evoked striatal/limbic response: when the reward is signalled unavailable, healthy cue reactivity is *suppressed*. That is a recoverability gate operating in real nervous systems — exactly the function MECH-292's `recoverability` term is supposed to serve, and the review is fairly direct support that such a gate exists and is load-bearing. Likewise, cognitive regulation can up- or down-modulate the response, which supports treating the gain on re-probing as something a controller can act on rather than a fixed constant.

But the same paper is where the reframe's tidiest assumption breaks, and I have recorded the direction as mixed precisely for this. The bank assumes `staleness` raises priority but is bounded by recoverability, with the implicit prior that an old, long-blocked trace decays in pull unless it is genuinely recoverable. Incubation of craving says the opposite can happen: across abstinence, cue reactivity can *intensify* — a long-blocked entry becomes *more* invigorating with time, not less. Cue reactivity also scales with severity and predicts relapse, meaning repeated retrieval can grow the wanting signal rather than habituate it. A monotonic staleness-decay prior is therefore biologically wrong at the extreme, and a re-probing budget that trusts it will systematically under-weight exactly the entries most likely to capture the agent.

The discipline here is to not import the gain curve. This is the maladaptive regime — the same circuitry is adaptive at low gain and pathological only when chronically over-amplified and insensitive to availability. The clinical literature tells us the *shape of the failure* (unbounded growth with repetition; incubation that inverts staleness; release of wanting when top-down regulation weakens; persistent craving when the availability gate is broken). It does not tell us the healthy setpoint, and treating its curve as the default would be the category error the biology-before-formal-definitions discipline exists to prevent. The honest use of this entry is as a falsification target: the cue-system design should be able to *produce* incubation and severity-scaling as deliberate failure states and should not assume staleness monotonically decays pull.
