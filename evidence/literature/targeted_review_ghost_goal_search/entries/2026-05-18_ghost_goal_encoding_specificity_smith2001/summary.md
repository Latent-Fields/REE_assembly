# Smith & Vela 2001 — environmental context-dependent memory (encoding specificity)

The first two papers in this strand argue that cued retrieval is partial-cue completion and that the cue can be internal. This one asks the question that actually changes our design: *what should the cue be made of?* The ghost-goal bank currently matches on one thing — the cosine between the current `z_goal` and a stored goal snapshot. Encoding specificity says that is too narrow. Tulving's principle, and the behavioural literature Smith and Vela meta-analyse, is that retrieval succeeds to the extent the retrieval cue overlaps the conditions present at encoding — and "conditions" includes the incidental context, not just the target content.

Their synthesis confirms the effect is real and reliable: reinstating the environment in which something was learned improves retrieval. That alone argues SD-039 should be preserving more than `z_goal` in the anchor payload — the world/affective context at the moment of storage is part of what makes the trace later findable, which is why the payload already carries `arousal_tag`, `last_vs`, and a cause tag but does not yet *use* them in the match.

The genuinely load-bearing part, though, is the moderator structure, and it cuts against a naive "add a context term" instinct. The context effect is *moderate*, and three moderators systematically shrink it. Overshadowing: if a strong non-contextual cue is present at encoding, the trace never binds to context. Outshining: if a strong direct cue is present at test, it *outshines* the context — context-dependent retrieval is largest in free recall and small or absent in cued recall. And mental reinstatement of the original context substitutes for the physical one. The lesson is not "context matters, add it." The lesson is that context-overlap retrieval is a *conditional fallback* — it does its work precisely when a strong direct match is unavailable.

That is a design constraint, not just supporting evidence, and I have recorded the direction as mixed for exactly that reason. It tells us how to wire the bank's matching function: a strong direct `goal_match` should dominate, and a context channel (built from the SD-039 payload fields we already store but ignore) should be the weak term that only becomes decisive when the direct match is poor. A bank that always adds context with fixed weight would, by the outshining result, *degrade* retrieval relative to identity matching whenever a clean goal match exists. The honest caveat is that this is human verbal memory with a literal room as "context"; the qualitative gating principle transfers cleanly, the numbers do not, and the exact crossover curve for an artificial bank is something the data cannot hand us — it has to be found empirically.
