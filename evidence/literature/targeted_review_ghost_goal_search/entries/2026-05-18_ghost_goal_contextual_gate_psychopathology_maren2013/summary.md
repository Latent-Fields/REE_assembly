# Maren, Phan & Liberzon 2013 — the contextual brain

Bouton tells us, at the behavioural level, that a blocked trace persists and is selected back by context. The natural next question — and the one that matters most for a psychiatrist reading this architecture — is *what circuit does the selecting, and what does it look like when that circuit fails?* Maren, Phan and Liberzon supply both. (Note for the record: the third author is Liberzon, not Phelps or Quirk as one might half-remember; the citation is verified.)

The circuit they assemble is specific. The hippocampus encodes a configural representation of context and acts as the occasion-setter that decides which competing memory — the original or its extinction — the amygdala expresses. Renewal is hippocampus-dependent; "neuronal renewal" is observable as basolateral amygdala cells that had gone quiet to an extinguished cue resuming firing when the cue appears outside the extinction context. There are two routes for the gate: a direct ventral-hippocampus-to-amygdala projection and an indirect hippocampus-to-medial-prefrontal route, where infralimbic cortex drives inhibitory intercalated cells that suppress the trace and prelimbic cortex expresses it. In humans, vmPFC and anterior hippocampus track extinction *retrieval* (the trace is held down) while amygdala and posterior hippocampus track *renewal* (it comes back).

This is close to a direct biological endorsement of the recoverability idea, and that is how I have scored it. MECH-292's `recoverability` and MECH-293's decision of whether to spend probe budget on a ghost are, in this light, not bespoke heuristics — they are the artificial analogue of a context-keyed selection between competing active and inactive traces, with an explicit prefrontal inhibitory limb. ARC-060's preserved discrete bank is vindicated again: return is *gated*, not free, and the gate has identifiable parts.

The clinical half is why this entry is load-bearing rather than confirmatory. The review maps gate failure directly onto disorder. In PTSD, impaired hippocampal/vmPFC engagement and exaggerated dorsal-ACC activity during extinction recall mean the suppressed trace renews in *safe* contexts — intrusive re-experiencing is the inactive trace expressing itself as though the present context were the original event. The same context-gating failure is extended to addiction (loss of context-specific tolerance; context cues reinstating seeking) and to schizophrenia (degraded context processing yielding retrieval unconstrained by situational appropriateness). For the cue-system design this names the failure mode precisely: rumination and intrusion are *the recoverability gate stuck open*. But the caveat is one I would not bury, because it should shape the build. The gate's power comes from a high-dimensional hippocampal configural representation with its own consolidation dynamics; the authors themselves note that even human fMRI struggles to instantiate genuine context, and impoverished context proxies misfire — over-renewing or under-renewing — in exactly the way a thin internal context tag would. So the entry supports the architecture and simultaneously warns that a recoverability gate keyed on a low-dimensional context vector is not homologous to the circuit that makes this work in brains; the dimensionality of the context representation is plausibly where an under-built gate will fail.
