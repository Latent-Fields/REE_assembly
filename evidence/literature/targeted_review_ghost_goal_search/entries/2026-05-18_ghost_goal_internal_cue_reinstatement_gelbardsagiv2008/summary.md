# Gelbard-Sagiv et al. 2008 — internally generated reinstatement in human hippocampus

If Nakazawa tells us retrieval is completion from a partial cue, the obvious next question is whether the *cue itself* can be internal. The ghost-goal architecture assumes it can: MECH-293 has the agent re-probe deferred goals not because the world presented a matching stimulus but because an internal goal/context state went looking. Is that biologically real, or are we smuggling in a convenience? Gelbard-Sagiv and colleagues are about as direct an answer as the human literature offers, because they recorded single neurons in the hippocampus and entorhinal cortex of epilepsy patients while those patients watched short film clips and later freely recalled them.

The finding has two parts and both matter. First, during encoding a subset of medial-temporal neurons fired selectively for a particular clip, and that selective firing outlasted the stimulus. Second — and this is the part that licenses MECH-293 — during *free recall*, with no external cue present, the very same episode-selective neurons reactivated, and they did so roughly 1.5 seconds *before* the patient said they were remembering that clip. The trace was being reinstated by something internal, and it was reinstated before it surfaced into report.

I take this as the strongest warrant we have that an internally generated vector is a legitimate retrieval cue, which is precisely the operation the awake ghost-probe performs and the ranked bank presupposes. It also gently supports ARC-060's discreteness claim: what reactivates is a specific, item-selective representation, not a smear over a continuous field.

But I would resist over-reading it, and the reasons are the usual ones for human intracranial work. It is correlational. It shows reinstatement *precedes* report; it does not show that the internal cue *caused selection of that trace* rather than both being downstream of something else. The tissue is epileptic. And — most relevant for our design — only a sparse, item-specific subset of selective neurons reliably reactivated, and the paper only characterises the successful cases. The far more common event in any recall task is the omission: you reach for something and nothing comes. That silent-miss regime is exactly where a goal-retrieval bank lives or dies, and this paper is structurally unable to tell us anything about it. So: strong support for the *existence and internal triggering* of cued reinstatement, and an honest blank where we would most like data — the boundary at which an internal cue evokes nothing at all.
