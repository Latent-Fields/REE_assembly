# Flagel et al. 2011 — a selective role for dopamine in stimulus-reward learning

The cue-system reframe has a hidden assumption I want to expose. MECH-292 scores an inactive anchor by `wanting x goal_match x staleness x recoverability`. The first two terms are treated as if they multiply naturally — a well-matched cue is, by construction, a wanted one. Flagel and colleagues are the cleanest demonstration that biology does *not* take that for granted, and that the conversion of a matched predictive cue into something the animal will work for is a separate, gated step.

Their design exploits an individual difference. Rats selectively bred as "sign-trackers" come to treat a reward-predictive lever-cue as an object of desire — they approach and contact the cue itself. "Goal-trackers" learn the identical predictive relationship but treat the cue as mere information and go to the food port. Same contingency, same predictive accuracy; what differs is whether incentive salience is *attributed* to the cue. The neurochemistry tracks the attribution, not the prediction: fast-scan voltammetry showed phasic dopamine transferring from reward to cue onset only in sign-trackers. And the causal test is decisive — a dopamine antagonist blocked acquisition of the sign-tracking response while leaving goal-tracking intact. Dopamine is necessary for the cue-becomes-wanted computation specifically, and not for cue-as-predictor learning.

For us this is strong support with a sharp edge. It supports the claim that "wanting" in `ghost_priority` is naming a real and *separable* computation — there genuinely is a step that takes a matched predictive trace and makes it invigorate action, and MECH-293's re-probing presupposes exactly that drive. That separability is the gift and the warning. If incentive attribution is a distinct gated process that some individuals barely express even for perfectly predictive cues, then `wanting x goal_match` as a fixed product is a simplification the biology does not license. The cleaner reading is that goal-match and wanting are different channels that can come apart.

And the failure signatures are clinically familiar, which is why this paper earns its place rather than just decorating the strand. The sign-tracker phenotype is wanting detached from instrumental value — the cue is pursued even when contacting it does not help, and incentive salience persists past the point where the predicted reward should warrant the effort. That is the mechanistic skeleton of compulsive cue pursuit and impulse-control vulnerability. The goal-tracker is the mirror failure: a fully predictive, fully matched cue that never acquires pull — a deferred goal that is correctly recognised and never re-probed. A bank that hard-couples wanting to match cannot represent either pathology, and both are exactly the regimes the recoverability and wanting terms are supposed to be guarding. I read this as: the term belongs, the multiplicative coupling does not, and the two failure poles it predicts are the ones validation should be built to detect.
