# Nakazawa et al. 2002 — CA3 NMDA receptors and partial-cue recall

This is the load-bearing question for the cue-system reframe: when the ghost-goal bank scores an inactive anchor by matching the current goal against a stored snapshot, is it doing the same *kind* of operation the brain does when a fragment of a situation brings back a whole memory? Nakazawa and colleagues give the cleanest causal answer available. They built a mouse in which the NR1 NMDA-receptor subunit was deleted only in CA3 pyramidal cells of adult animals — the cells whose dense recurrent collaterals had long been theorised to implement an autoassociative attractor. The prediction from that theory is specific and falsifiable: knock out the recurrent plasticity and you should lose *completion from a partial cue* while leaving storage and full-cue recall intact.

That is exactly what they found. With the full original constellation of distal cues, the mutants encoded and retrieved a Morris water-maze location normally, and CA1 place fields were normal — so the machinery of laying down and reading out a trace was untouched. The dissociation appeared only when the cue was degraded: remove a fraction of the landmarks and the mutants could no longer recover the stored location, and their CA1 place fields lost stability precisely under that partial-cue condition. The interpretation is hard to escape. A degraded input is normally pulled back to the full stored representation by NMDA-dependent CA3 recurrence; remove that and you abolish completion specifically, not memory in general.

For REE this matters because it tells us the reframe is not a metaphor being stretched. Biological cued retrieval really is content-addressed completion from a partial cue, which is the premise MECH-292's matched-retrieval step and ARC-060's "discrete addressable bank" rest on. It also quietly argues that SD-039 is storing the right kind of thing: a trace worth preserving is one that can later be *completed into*, not merely matched against.

I want to be honest about where the analogy thins. The brain achieves this with attractor dynamics, and attractors come with a characteristic pathology — a sufficiently degraded cue does not fail gracefully, it can fall into a neighbouring but wrong basin and confidently retrieve the wrong trace. Our bank does a single linear cosine readout. That means we get the desirable behaviour (a partial cue still retrieves) without the biological substrate, and therefore without either its specific failure mode or its protections against it. So the paper supports the *existence* of the operation strongly while leaving open a design question it cannot answer for us: whether a linear similarity readout is an adequate stand-in for completion, or whether the absence of basin structure will show up later as the bank retrieving plausibly-near-but-wrong goals with no internal signal that it has done so. I read this as supporting evidence with an explicit instruction to watch the wrong-but-near retrieval case during validation.
