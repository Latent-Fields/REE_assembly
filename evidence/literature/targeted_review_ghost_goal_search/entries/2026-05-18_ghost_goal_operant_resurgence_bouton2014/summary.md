# Bouton 2014 — why behavior change is difficult to sustain

Bouton's 2004 paper made its case with Pavlovian cues. The objection a careful reader should raise is that ghost-bank entries are not cues — they are *action policies*, deferred goals the agent might take up again. Does preserve-don't-erase hold when the thing being suppressed is a voluntary behaviour rather than a stimulus association? This 2014 review is the answer, and it is the reason this entry earns its own slot rather than collapsing into the 2004 one: it generalises the whole structure to the operant case and, in doing so, delivers the single most design-critical finding in the strand.

The reassuring half first. Everything from 2004 reappears for voluntary behaviour: operant renewal (ABA/ABC/AAB), spontaneous recovery, reinstatement, rapid reacquisition — all demonstrated, including in drug self-administration. Inhibiting an operant response does not erase it; it lays down a context-specific "do not make *this* response in *this* context" memory. So ARC-060 and MECH-292 inherit the biological warrant directly: a deferred goal is a latent, context-recoverable policy, exactly as the bank treats it.

Now the part that should change a design decision. The bank's `recoverability` term is, in its natural reading, something that integrates evidence — the longer and harder a goal has been blocked, the lower its recoverability, until eventually it is safe to stop re-probing it. Bouton shows this is precisely the assumption the data refuses. Extended elimination training does not monotonically reduce return: thirty-six sessions of eliminating a behaviour produced the same resurgence as four. More evidence of hopelessness did not make the trace safer to drop. There is, in the entire experimental record he reviews, no dose of suppression evidence that closes the gate — only schedule and context tricks that *reduce* return without ever abolishing it. A recoverability gate built to accumulate suppression evidence toward "retire this anchor" will therefore be *systematically and confidently wrong*.

Resurgence sharpens this into a concrete, non-obvious failure mode for a ranked bank. When a replacement behaviour is itself discontinued, the old behaviour returns — relapse is triggered by the *loss of the substitute*, not by the original cue. Translated into bank terms: retiring or down-ranking an anchor because some competitor goal is currently winning is unsafe, because the moment the competitor stops paying off the supposedly-resolved goal comes back at full strength. Combined with rapid reacquisition (one lapse restores the whole behaviour) and reinstatement after punishment (knowing the consequences are bad does not durably retire the trace), the picture is consistent and clinically exact — roughly seventy percent of successful quitters relapse within a year. I have scored this mixed for the honest reason: it strongly supports the persistent-recoverable-trace architecture while just as strongly falsifying a specific, tempting implementation of the recoverability gate. The constructive reading is that recoverability must be framed as risk-reduction over an irreducibly persistent trace, that "a competitor is winning" must not be a retire condition, and that if an artificial system introduces an explicit abandon path — which biology never gets — that path cannot be justified by accumulated evidence of hopelessness, because that is exactly the inference the biology shows to be false.
