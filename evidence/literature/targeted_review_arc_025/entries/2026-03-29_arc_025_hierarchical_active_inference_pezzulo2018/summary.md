# Pezzulo, Rigoli & Friston (2018) -- Hierarchical Active Inference: A Theory of Motivated Control

## What the paper did

Pezzulo, Rigoli and Friston develop a hierarchical extension of active inference to address motivated behavior -- the capacity of agents to pursue goals that extend over time and require balancing immediate sensory demands against long-horizon plans. The paper argues that a single-level active inference agent cannot simultaneously handle fast reactive correction (needed for sensorimotor control) and slow prospective planning (needed for goal-directed behavior), because the timescales and the type of computation required are fundamentally different. Their hierarchical active inference (HAI) framework has lower levels performing fast sensory prediction error minimisation through perception and action, while higher levels minimize expected free energy over multi-step action sequences toward distal goals. Motivational states modulate precision weighting across levels, allowing context-dependent shifts between immediate reactivity and long-horizon planning. The paper ties this to frontal-BG architecture: lower levels map onto sensorimotor loops, higher levels onto associative and limbic loops.

## Key findings relevant to ARC-025

The paper's central argument is that hierarchical depth is not just computationally efficient -- it is functionally necessary for motivated control. A flat single-level agent optimising a single prediction error at one timescale cannot simultaneously correct rapid sensory disturbances and plan toward distal goals, because these require different generative models and different update frequencies. This is the core of the irreducibility argument: the levels are not equivalent but slower/faster versions of the same computation; they process qualitatively different types of information. Lower-level states change on the timescale of milliseconds to seconds; higher-level states (goals, intentions) are stable over minutes to hours. The paper shows that collapsing these into a single level produces a system that is either reactive without foresight or planful without responsiveness -- the two-constraint failure modes that ARC-025 also identifies.

## How this translates to REE (ARC-025)

The paper supports ARC-025's E1/E3 irreducibility most directly. E1 (slow LSTM world model, sensory prediction error) corresponds to the lower-level sensorimotor inference in HAI -- fast, reactive, concerned with moment-to-moment world state updates. E3 (harm/goal evaluation, long-horizon planning, hippocampal map) corresponds to the higher-level expected free energy minimisation over multi-step plans in HAI -- slow, prospective, concerned with distal outcomes. The paper's functional irreducibility argument covers this distinction: you cannot reduce E3's harm/goal planning to a faster version of E1's sensory prediction, nor can you reduce E1's rapid world model updates to a special case of E3's long-horizon evaluation. The functional requirements are incommensurable in the sense that their timescales and information types differ qualitatively.

## Limitations and caveats

The paper is primarily a two-level hierarchy (fast sensorimotor + slow goal-directed), with motivation as a modulator rather than a third irreducible engine. REE's E2 (action-consequence forward model, motor-sensory error) occupies a middle level that the paper does not cleanly separate. In the HAI framework, the action-consequence prediction might be either part of the lower-level sensorimotor loop or part of the intermediate level between pure sensory inference and high-level planning. This ambiguity is directly relevant to ARC-025: if E2 can be absorbed into either E1 or E3 without functional loss, the three-engine claim requires separate justification. The paper also frames motivated control primarily through the lens of goal-directed versus habitual behavior, not through the specific ethical evaluation lens of REE. The harm/goal error that drives E3 in REE has no direct analogue in HAI's expected free energy framework.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is set at 0.63. The paper provides good support for the E1/E3 irreducibility (fast sensory vs slow planning) but does not clearly establish the three-way partition that ARC-025 requires. The mapping fidelity for the two-level distinction is good; for the specific E2-level irreducibility it is weak. This paper is best used in combination with papers that directly address E2's distinctness -- particularly MECH-069 (incommensurability of motor-sensory and harm/goal error signals) and EXQ-034's direct ablation evidence. On its own, it supports a two-engine reduction being insufficient (good) but does not establish three engines being necessary (insufficient for full ARC-025 support).
