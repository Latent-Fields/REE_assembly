# Friston et al. (2017) -- Active Inference: A Process Theory

## What the paper did

Friston, FitzGerald, Rigoli, Schwartenbeck and Pezzulo develop what they call a 'process theory' of active inference -- a full computational instantiation of the free energy principle in a Markov decision process formalism. The paper's contribution over earlier free energy work is that it specifies not just what the system minimises but how it does so: what the update equations are, at what timescales they operate, and which brain regions plausibly implement which computations. The paper demonstrates that belief updating under active inference naturally decomposes into three functionally distinct processes: perceptual inference (updating beliefs about the current state of the world given sensory evidence, occurring on the timescale of single observations); policy selection (evaluating expected free energy over alternative action sequences to choose a policy, occurring on the timescale of a decision epoch); and learning (updating the model parameters -- the A matrix of sensory likelihoods, B matrix of transition probabilities, C matrix of preferences, D matrix of priors -- occurring over many trials). Each process has its own update equation and its own timescale, and the paper argues that together they are sufficient to explain a wide range of neurophysiological observations.

## Key findings relevant to ARC-025

The paper's key contribution to ARC-025 is its demonstration of functional irreducibility within the three-process architecture. Perception alone, without policy selection, produces a system that tracks the world but never acts. Policy selection alone, without perceptual inference, produces a system that acts blindly without updating its world model. Learning alone, without inference or selection, produces a system that accumulates statistics but cannot use them in real time. The three processes cannot be collapsed into two: any two-process variant loses either real-time world-model updating, action-consequence evaluation, or the capacity to learn from outcomes. The paper also shows that the three processes operate at different timescales -- fast (perceptual inference within a trial), medium (policy selection at trial onset), and slow (learning across trials) -- and that this temporal separation is not arbitrary but follows from the different types of uncertainty each process is designed to reduce.

## How this translates to REE (ARC-025)

ARC-025 claims that no two-engine reduction of E1/E2/E3 is possible without losing a functional capability. The active inference process theory provides the normative argument for why this is the case. In REE's terms: E1 (sensory world model, LSTM, fast) corresponds to perceptual inference -- it updates the world model from moment-to-moment sensory evidence. E2 (action consequence forward model, medium) corresponds to policy evaluation -- it predicts what will happen if action a is taken from state z, enabling trajectory rollout. E3 (harm/goal evaluation, slow planning) corresponds to learning-from-consequences -- it integrates harm and goal signals over long horizons to update the agent's commitment and trajectory selection. The paper's argument that collapsing any two produces a specific failure mode directly supports ARC-025's claim that E1+E2 without E3 gives coherent extrapolation without commitment, E1+E3 without E2 gives impulsive commitment without temporal depth, and E2+E3 without E1 gives brittle rigidity without responsiveness.

## Limitations and caveats

The mapping is an architectural analogy, not an identity. Active inference's three processes are defined by their role within a single free energy objective -- they are different computational strategies for minimising the same quantity. REE's three engines are defined by their qualitatively distinct error signals and their grounding in the five axioms. The ARC-025 irreducibility claim is stronger than what active inference supports: ARC-025 requires that the three REE engines cannot be merged without losing axiomatic coverage, not merely that a three-process partition is useful. Active inference is also a framework that can in principle absorb different architectural partitions -- a sufficiently clever two-process FEP agent could potentially implement the same functional capabilities by different means. The paper does not rule out this possibility. Additionally, active inference uses variational Bayes and expected free energy, while REE uses prediction error backpropagation and neural network forward models -- the computational substrates differ enough that the analogy carries non-trivial transfer risk.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is set at 0.65. The source quality is very high, but the mapping fidelity is only moderate because the active inference three-process decomposition does not correspond one-to-one with REE's E1/E2/E3 partition. The paper provides a normative argument for why three distinct computational processes at different timescales are necessary for viable cognition, which is the logical structure ARC-025 needs. But the specific claim that REE's three engines implement incommensurable error signals -- and that merging them misattributes credit in the REE sense -- requires additional evidence beyond what a general active inference framework can provide. The transfer risk is moderate-high because active inference is a competing framework that could potentially recast ARC-025 as an instance of FEP rather than an independent architectural claim.
