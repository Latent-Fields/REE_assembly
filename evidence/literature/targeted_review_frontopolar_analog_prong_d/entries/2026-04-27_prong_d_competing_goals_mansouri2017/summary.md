# Mansouri, Koechlin, Rosa & Buckley 2017 — Frontopolar cortex and managing competing goals

According to PubMed, Mansouri, Koechlin, Rosa & Buckley (2017, [Nat Rev Neurosci 18:645-657](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrn.2017.111)) integrate primate FPC physiology, lesion findings, human imaging, and comparative neuroanatomy into a synthesis on FPC's role in managing competing goals. The central thesis has two parts. First, FPC in primates monitors the significance of *current and alternative goals* — this is the basic FPC role observed across primate species. Second, in human evolution, FPC has acquired a further role enabling **parallel monitoring of multiple goals** and switching between them, which the authors argue may underlie many forms of uniquely human cognition (language, judgement, reasoning). FPC is enlarged in humans relative to other animals, and area 10 in primates contains a unique cytoarchitectural field.

For SD-033e Prong D, this is the most direct theoretical support available for MECH-265 (relative-importance monitoring across competing goals). MECH-264 (counterfactual-value tracking) corresponds to the basic primate FPC role; MECH-265 corresponds to the human-specific elaboration. The architectural commitment that the FPC analog should track *multiple goal hierarchies in parallel* and switch between them when their relative importance changes is the explicit subject of this synthesis. The implementation question for V3 is how K-way parallel monitoring is realised computationally — softmax over competing-goal value estimates? winner-take-all with threshold-mediated switching? graded multi-channel readout? The review does not provide a computational answer but establishes the function as the central commitment.

I want to flag three caveats that constrain the transfer to SD-033e. The first is that the review is theoretical synthesis, not primary data. The findings being synthesised are the underlying empirical evidence — Boorman 2009 (counterfactual tracking, separate entry), Mansouri 2015 (FPC lesion phenotype, separate entry), and a wide range of single-unit and human fMRI work cited but not detailed. The strength of the architectural commitment to MECH-265 depends on the strength of the underlying primary evidence; the review's role here is to articulate the synthesis but not to supply additional primary support beyond what is captured in the other Prong D entries.

The second caveat is the human-specific elaboration argument. The review argues that parallel monitoring of *multiple* goals is a human-specific FPC specialisation, with the basic primate FPC role being limited to *current vs alternative* (pairwise) monitoring. If this is right, V3 SD-033e may inherit only the basic primate role (MECH-264 pairwise) and not the human elaboration (MECH-265 K-way). The implication for V3 implementation is that MECH-265 may be inheritable only by analogy — REE is a computational artifact, not constrained by the same evolutionary specialisations as primate FPC, but the architectural function MECH-265 specifies depends on the elaborated form. This is a transfer-risk question that does not have a clean resolution: REE can implement K-way parallel monitoring algorithmically without any evolutionary scaffolding, but whether the function will be useful depends on whether REE's task structure benefits from it.

The third caveat is the subregional disagreement. The review draws together findings that disagree on functional specialisation of FPC subregions (medial vs lateral BA 10, BA 10 vs BA 9). The integrative synthesis the review proposes is itself theoretical rather than empirically resolved. SD-033e should not lock to any particular subregional decomposition — the safer architectural commitment is that the FPC analog supports counterfactual tracking and competing-goal monitoring without committing to a specific medial/lateral allocation.
