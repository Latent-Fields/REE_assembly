# Boorman, Behrens, Woolrich & Rushworth 2009 — Frontopolar cortex tracks counterfactual evidence for switching

According to PubMed, Boorman, Behrens, Woolrich & Rushworth (2009, [Neuron 62:733-43](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2009.05.014)) used fMRI and a Bayesian reinforcement-learning model to ask how the brain tracks the relative advantage of foregone alternatives during voluntary choice. The paradigm is a 4-arm bandit where subjects can switch to alternative options when they accumulate sufficient evidence of higher value. The model decomposes each trial's evidence into (a) the value of the currently-chosen option and (b) the relative advantage of switching to the alternative.

The headline finding is a clean dissociation: **frontopolar cortex (FPC)** tracks the relative advantage of switching to a foregone alternative, while **ventromedial PFC (vmPFC)** encodes the value of the currently-chosen option. FPC functional connectivity changes at the moment subjects decide to switch — i.e., the FPC signal is not just monitoring the alternative continuously, but its readout-into-action becomes effective at the switch decision. Interindividual variation in the FPC signal predicts how effectively a subject adapts behaviour to changing reward contingencies.

For SD-033e Prong D — specifically MECH-264 (counterfactual-value tracking by an FPC analog) and MECH-265 (relative-importance monitoring across competing goals) — Boorman 2009 is foundational. It is the paper that establishes counterfactual tracking as a distinct frontopolar function rather than as a generic prefrontal value-monitoring signal. The architectural commitment in SD-033e that the FPC analog tracks a *continuous* counterfactual-value signal that modulates the switch readiness has its empirical anchor here.

I want to flag the pairwise vs K-way distinction explicitly because it determines whether this paper supports MECH-264 alone or also MECH-265. Boorman 2009's design is pairwise: each trial's relevant decision is "stay with the current choice or switch to the best alternative". The FPC signal tracks the chosen-vs-alternative comparison. MECH-265 specifies K≥2 alternatives monitored *as a set* — counterfactual evaluation across multiple foregone options jointly. The FPC signal in this paper does not directly speak to K≥2-way monitoring; whether the same substrate scales to a K-way set-monitoring computation is empirically open. The paper supports MECH-264 cleanly and MECH-265 only as a generalisation by analogy.

A second caveat is the timing of the functional-connectivity change. The vmPFC↔FPC functional connectivity shifts at the moment of switching, not at the moment of evidence accumulation. This raises a question about whether the FPC signal is a *tracking* signal (continuously updated) or a *triggering* signal (phasic at switch). MECH-264's V3 implementation should hold to the tracking reading (Boorman 2009's main finding is that FPC carries the running counterfactual-advantage estimate), but the connectivity dynamics are a separate signature that may need its own implementation account.

Mapping fidelity good at 0.80 for MECH-264 (direct empirical match) and partial for MECH-265 (pairwise vs K-way). Transfer risk low at 0.25 — the design is well-suited to the counterfactual-tracking question. Net direction is **supports** for MECH-264 with the K-way generalisation flagged for MECH-265.
