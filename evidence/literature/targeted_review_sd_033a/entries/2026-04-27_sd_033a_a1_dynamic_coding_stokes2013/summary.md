# Stokes, Kusunoki, Sigala, Nili, Gaffan & Duncan 2013 — Dynamic coding for cognitive control in prefrontal cortex

According to PubMed, Stokes, Kusunoki, Sigala, Nili, Gaffan & Duncan (2013, [Neuron 78:364-75](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2013.01.039)) recorded single units in macaque lateral prefrontal cortex during a rule-conditional decision task and applied time-resolved population pattern analyses to characterise how rule and stimulus information are jointly encoded. The core findings have a tight three-part structure: an instruction cue triggers a rapid series of state transitions before settling into a stable low-activity state; the post-cue stable state is differentially tuned according to the current task-relevant rule; and during decision-making, the response to a choice stimulus shows an initial stimulus-specific population response that then evolves to different final decision-related states depending on the current rule. The authors propose that this highly flexible tuning could be mediated by short-term synaptic plasticity.

For SD-033a's A1 question — per-candidate bias projection versus uniform scalar gain — Stokes 2013 provides the strongest direct evidence available among the papers I reviewed. Two of the paper's findings bear on A1 specifically. First, the rule-conditional differential tuning of the post-cue stable state means that the rule signal carries candidate-discriminative information, not just a scalar magnitude; the population state under rule A is structurally different from the population state under rule B in a way that interacts with stimulus-specific dimensions. Second, the rule-conditional divergence of the choice-stimulus response into different final decision-related states means that the bias is read out as a candidate-state-specific shift rather than as a stimulus-invariant uniform gain. If the bias were a uniform multiplicative scalar applied to all candidates, the choice-response trajectory would scale up or down identically across rules but would not diverge to different decision endpoints — and yet divergence is precisely what the data show.

I want to flag two caveats. The first is that the experimental design uses a small number of rules and a small candidate set; the per-candidate-vs-uniform-gain question is at the edge of what such designs can resolve. The result is most cleanly described as "rule-conditional, stimulus-specific dynamics" — which is consistent with the per-candidate A1 landing but is not a strict refutation of a sufficiently flexible uniform-gain alternative (a gain that interacts multiplicatively with pre-existing stimulus-specific dynamics could in principle reproduce the same divergence). The discrimination at the strict-test level remains underdetermined by the data; the result is supportive but not decisive.

The second caveat matters more for SD-033a's V3-vs-V4 reconsideration. The short-term-synaptic-plasticity interpretation Stokes and colleagues advance is, on the face of it, an A3-question issue rather than an A1-question issue (A3 is about how the rule signal is *persisted*; A1 is about how it is *projected*). But the deeper implication is that the V3 SD-033a abstraction — a static rule_state held by EMA, projected via a frozen-random Linear head — may be missing the substrate that does the actual computational work in biology. If short-term synaptic plasticity is the mechanism that produces both the rule-conditional persistence and the rule-conditional choice-state divergence, then the per-candidate-vs-uniform-gain dichotomy SD-033a poses at the projection step is operating at the wrong level of description. This is a flag for V4, not a refutation of V3.

Confidence components: source quality is high (Neuron 2013, primate single-unit, time-resolved population pattern analyses, well-cited as the modern dynamic-coding anchor for PFC). Mapping fidelity is good at 0.72 — rule-conditional choice-state divergence is the closest direct empirical analogue of per-candidate bias projection available. Transfer risk is moderate at 0.30 because the population-dynamics framing only partially overlaps with SD-033a's static-bias-head abstraction. The short-term-plasticity interpretation is a flag pointing beyond SD-033a's current substrate. Net direction is **supports** the per-candidate A1 landing.

## A1 synthesis across the three new entries

Wallis 2001 establishes that a stimulus-abstracted rule signal exists in lateral PFC — necessary but not sufficient for A1, because rule-selective single units alone do not discriminate between per-candidate and uniform-gain projection downstream. Mante 2013 demonstrates input-selective gating in PFC at the population-dynamics level — supporting the per-candidate landing by analogy from input-selection to output-selection, with the caveat that the analogy crosses the input/output asymmetry. Stokes 2013 demonstrates rule-conditional choice-state divergence — the closest direct empirical analogue of per-candidate bias projection, supporting the landing while flagging that V3's static abstraction may be missing the short-term-plasticity substrate biology actually uses.

Combined synthesis: the available evidence weakly supports the per-candidate A1 landing over the uniform-gain alternative, while flagging that the V3 SD-033a abstraction is operating at a level of description (static rule_state, single Linear projection) that is one substrate-step removed from the population-dynamics-level mechanism the biology appears to implement. The A1 landing is acceptable for V3; for V4 the open architectural question is whether to elevate SD-033a from a static head to a recurrent module with explicit short-term plasticity dynamics. Combined with the existing Miller & Cohen 2001 anchor entry, A1 now carries comparable lit-pull breadth to A2 (4 entries) and A3 (3 entries).
