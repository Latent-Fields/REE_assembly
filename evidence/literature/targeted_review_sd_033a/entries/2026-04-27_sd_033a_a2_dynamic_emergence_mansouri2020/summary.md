# Mansouri, Freedman & Buckley 2020 — Emergence of abstract rules in the primate brain (SD-033a A2 reading)

[DOI: 10.1038/s41583-020-0364-5](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41583-020-0364-5). Sourced via PubMed (PMID 32929262). Distinct entry from the existing 2026-04-19 indexing of the same paper, which addressed MECH-261 / ARC-035 (where do rules go in PFC?). This entry addresses SD-033a A2 specifically: how does the rule-bias projection form, and does the SD-033a landing's frozen-random head cohere with biology?

## What the review establishes

The review synthesises three decades of single-unit recording, lesion, and imaging work on abstract rule representations in primate lateral PFC, with primary focus on the macaque. Across paradigms — delayed match-to-sample, category judgement, conditional rule-shifting, WCST analogues — abstract-rule-selective neurons in lateral PFC do not arrive fully formed. They develop. Proportions of rule-selective cells climb with training. Latencies of rule-driven activity shorten. The breadth of generalisation across stimulus sets widens. The dynamics of formation, maintenance, revision, and task-dependent recruitment differ between lateral PFC, OFC, and ACC, but the throughgoing claim is that the substrate is shaped by experience in a graded, paradigm-sensitive way.

## How this maps onto SD-033a's A2 question

The SD-033a landing implementation (2026-04-20) carries a bias head whose last `nn.Linear` weights and bias are zeroed at initialisation, so the initial bias output is exactly zero, and head parameters are registered but not added to any optimizer by default. This is a backward-compatibility shim: it preserves bit-identical agent behaviour when `use_lateral_pfc_analog=True` is flipped on, at the cost of leaving signature (iv) of MECH-262 — training-dependent emergence — unaddressed. The A2 alternative is a trained head via a phased protocol (P0 encoder warmup, P1 frozen-encoder head training on detached latents, P2 evaluation).

Mansouri et al. give the strongest existing argument that the trained-head alternative is the biologically aligned choice. A SD-033a that never trains its bias projection cannot by construction instantiate the dynamic emergence the review documents. The frozen-random head matches no biological development trajectory; it is a pre-commitment to never instantiate the signature.

That is the strong half of the reading. The weak half is that the review does not pin down a unique training signal. Single-unit emergence is documented under reinforcement learning paradigms with reward feedback, supervised category-judgement paradigms with binary correct/incorrect signals, and matching-rule paradigms where the training signal is implicit in trial structure. The review notes that representational features (proportions, latencies, generalisation breadth) vary across these training regimes. It does not adjudicate between them as the canonical biological signal.

For the V3 architecture this matters in a specific way. The bias-projection head sits between rule_state and dacc_score_bias. Backprop through E3.select() is non-trivial — the same gradient-flow problem documented at SD-016 cue_action_proj (EXP-0155, where action_bias_divergence stayed at 0.0 even with explicit MSE supervision because something downstream zeroed the signal). A trained head therefore needs both a target and a viable gradient path. Mansouri et al. tell us biology trains the substrate; they do not tell us through what gradient.

## Limitations and caveats

The mapping is approximate at the architecture-vs-projection level. Biological rule emergence shapes population activity in lateral PFC; SD-033a's score_bias is a downstream projection from rule_state to a per-candidate scalar bias on action selection. These are correlated but not identical processes. The review's evidence supports the position that rule representations are trained; the inference that *projections from those representations to action selection are also trained* requires an extra step that the review does not directly test. It is plausible — biological projections from PFC to BG/striatum are learning-dependent (see Johnson 2016 already in this review set) — but plausible is not directly evidenced here.

There is also a residual disconfirming risk. If naive-vs-trained dissociation studies were to show that rule-selective cells are present in naive animals at proportions and latencies indistinguishable from trained animals, the dynamic-emergence framing would collapse. The review surveys substantial evidence against this scenario but explicit naive-vs-trained controls are sparse outside specific paradigms. SD-033a should not over-commit to a phased training protocol on the strength of this review alone.

## Confidence

`0.78`. Source quality is very high — Nature Reviews Neuroscience, three-lab synthesis spanning primate single-unit, lesion, and imaging methods. Mapping fidelity is moderate because the review supports trainable emergence at the substrate level but does not address the specific projection-head training-signal question SD-033a A2 poses. Transfer risk is moderate — the review settles the prior question (yes, train) but leaves the signal-specification question open, so any concrete implementation choice will inherit residual ambiguity. The evidence direction is `mixed`, not `supports`, because the review is informative on whether to train and silent on what to train against. A future entry on a more focused training-signal study (RPE-driven, supervised rule-label, or Hebbian co-activation) would let this be sharpened to `supports` for a specific A2 implementation.
