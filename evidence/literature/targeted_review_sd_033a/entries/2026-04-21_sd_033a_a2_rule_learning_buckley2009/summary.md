# Buckley et al. 2009 — Dissociable components of rule-guided behaviour (SD-033a, A2)

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1172377)), Buckley, Mansouri, and colleagues report a circumscribed-lesion study in macaques performing a Wisconsin Card Sorting Test analog. They dissociate three components of rule-guided behaviour: principal-sulcus (mid-lateral PFC) lesions impair maintenance of abstract rules in working memory; orbitofrontal cortex lesions impair rapid reward-based updating of rule value; anterior cingulate sulcus lesions impair active reference to the value of recent choice-outcomes. Superior dorsolateral PFC and ventrolateral PFC lesions produce different profiles. The overall pattern is a three-way regional dissociation among rule-holding, rule-value-updating, and outcome-tracking.

For SD-033a's A2 question — how is the rule-bias projection shaped by learning and on what training signal? — the relevance is indirect but important. The landing choice in SD-033a is a frozen-random head with the last Linear zeroed, so that initial bias is zero and the module is backward-compatible when activated. The A2 alternative is a trained head via a phased protocol, where training signals for rule-emergence and value-update are applied in sequence rather than simultaneously. Buckley et al.'s dissociation supports the phased-protocol alternative at the architectural level: if rule-maintenance (PS) and rule-value-updating (OFC) are genuinely separable in biology — as the lesion data suggest — then the training signals that shape those circuits are plausibly distinct too, and a phased protocol is biologically grounded rather than an ad-hoc engineering choice.

The honest caveat is that lesion dissociation does not directly demonstrate the existence of distinct training signals. It demonstrates the existence of distinct processes. The inference to training-signal separation requires the additional assumption that each region is trained on a signal aligned with its lesioned function — which is plausible given the reward-prediction-error literature in OFC and the choice-outcome literature in ACC, but it is not shown in this paper.

For SD-033a specifically, the most relevant framing is: does the landing choice (frozen-random head, untrained) satisfy signature (iv) of SD-033a — training-dependent rule emergence? Buckley et al. say that rule emergence in biology is PS-dependent and depends on the preserved integrity of a specific circuit. That argues that something trainable is happening in biological PS, not just that a random projection suffices. If we take the lesion data at face value, the SD-033a landing should at some point acquire a training protocol rather than remain frozen-random; the phased-protocol alternative (A2) then becomes the long-term design target even if the immediate landing is frozen-random for backward compatibility. This is an alignment with the evidence, not a falsification of the landing.
