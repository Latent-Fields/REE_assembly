# Badre & Nee 2018 — Frontal cortex and the hierarchical control of behavior (SD-033a A2 reading)

[DOI: 10.1016/j.tics.2017.11.005](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tics.2017.11.005). Sourced via PubMed (PMID 29229206), PMC5841250 full text. Distinct entry from the existing 2026-04-19 indexing under `targeted_review_pfc_subdivision_architecture` (which addressed the rostro-caudal abstraction question — where rules go in lateral PFC). This entry addresses SD-033a A2: what training signal shapes the rule-bias projection?

## What the review establishes

Badre and Nee revisit two decades of evidence on hierarchical cognitive control. Their headline finding is negative: the unitary-controller view of lateral PFC fails, and the unidimensional rostro-caudal abstraction gradient is also incomplete. They propose three functional zones — sensory-motor, contextual control (mid-DLPFC + prePM), and schematic control (RLPFC) — interacting via local and global hierarchical structure rather than via a single dimension. For the SD-033a A2 question, two of their boxes are directly relevant.

Box 2 (corticostriatal gating) collects evidence that working-memory updating, selection, and learning in hierarchical control tasks are supported by cortico-striatal-pallidal-thalamic loops rather than by direct cortico-cortical credit assignment. The specific passage that bears on A2: "Reward prediction errors related to learning a second order rule modulated fMRI activation in prePMd and a spatially proximate subregion of the caudate nucleus." This is the most direct biological evidence in the review for an RPE-driven training signal at the rule-learning-projection interface.

Box 3 (medial-lateral parallel structure) describes the hierarchical error representation model: dorsomedial PFC learns to predict outcomes given lateral PFC representations, with prediction errors updating both lateral PFC representations and medial PFC outcome predictions in a caudal-to-rostral feedback flow. This is RL-flavoured at multiple network levels rather than a single supervised gradient, and it explicitly couples lateral and dorsomedial PFC into a coupled prediction-error system.

The Outstanding Questions box explicitly flags "How do learning and experience impact the hierarchical functional organization of the frontal lobes?" as an open question — i.e., the review identifies the training-signal mechanism as not yet settled, even within their own framework.

## How this maps onto SD-033a's A2 question

Badre and Nee make two contributions to the A2 design space.

**First contribution — they constrain the implementation away from naive single-target backprop.** The review's central thesis is that lateral frontal control is a system of separable networks with their own learning dynamics, not a unitary controller. A SD-033a phased-training protocol that trains a single score_bias head with a single target (rule-label cross-entropy, scalar policy gradient, etc.) is therefore a known biological simplification. The simplification may be acceptable for V3 — V3 already commits to other simplifications, like a single dACC adapter standing in for the salience-network coordinator — but the review argues that the V3 implementation should be honest about what it is reducing.

**Second contribution — they identify an RPE-via-corticostriatal-loops candidate signal.** Box 2's prePMd/caudate RPE finding, combined with the hierarchical error representation model in Box 3, supports a specific implementation choice: train the rule-bias head on a signal derived from action-outcome mismatch (RPE), routed through a striatal-analogue gating layer rather than through direct cortico-cortical credit assignment. V3 already has the relevant scaffolding. The dACC adaptive control (`use_dacc`, the `DACCAdaptiveControl` module landed 2026-04-19) carries a Shenhav-style payoff/effort/PE bundle that is conceptually close to a corticostriatal training signal. The salience coordinator (`use_salience_coordinator`, MECH-261/SD-032a) already provides per-mode write-gate weights that gate substrate updates by operating mode. A phased-training protocol that uses the dACC-bundle PE as the gradient source, gated by the salience-coordinator's `sd_033a` write_gate, would be biologically grounded in the corticostriatal-RPE picture this review supports.

Against this, the review supports neither a supervised rule-label target in isolation nor an unsupervised Hebbian protocol as the primary signal. Both might play roles in biology — the review acknowledges that working-memory updating and selection mechanisms are plural — but the central training signal is closer to RPE-via-striatum than to either alternative. Composed with Mansouri 2020 (which establishes that rule emergence is trained, not innate) and Johnson 2016 (which establishes that frontal projections are physically remodelled by rule learning), Badre and Nee push the A2 implementation toward a corticostriatal-RPE phased-training protocol that uses the existing V3 dACC + salience-coordinator scaffolding rather than a separate supervised pathway.

## Limitations and caveats

The Box 2 prePMd/caudate finding is referenced rather than primary — Badre and Nee cite one fMRI study to anchor a converging-evidence argument, not as a settled primary finding. The hierarchical error representation model in Box 3 is one of several modelling efforts that the review acknowledges have not been adjudicated against each other.

The transfer from "biology uses corticostriatal RPE for rule learning" to "SD-033a's score_bias head should be trained via dACC adapter on RPE-style targets" requires architectural inference beyond what the review tests directly. The review describes lateral frontal control at a network level. SD-033a's score_bias is a specific projection from a population-level rule_state to a per-candidate scalar bias on action selection. There is no biological one-to-one match for this projection in the review's ontology.

The review focuses on hierarchical control as a class of problem — managing multiple levels of contextual contingency. SD-033a in V3 currently handles a much simpler control regime (single rule biasing E3 trajectory selection, no nested contingencies). The richer hierarchical-control phenotype the review describes is a V4-or-later concern. The A2 implementation choice should be informed by the review's framework but not over-fitted to V4-scope demands.
