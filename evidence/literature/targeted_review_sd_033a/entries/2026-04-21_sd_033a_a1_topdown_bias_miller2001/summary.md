# Miller and Cohen 2001 — PFC as a source of top-down bias (SD-033a, A1)

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev.neuro.24.1.167)), Miller and Cohen propose that cognitive control arises from the active maintenance of goal-representing activity patterns in the prefrontal cortex, which project bias signals onto other brain structures and thereby guide the flow of activity along neural pathways that implement the current task's input-to-output mapping. This is the canonical theoretical statement of the "PFC as rule-bias source" view. The review synthesises neurophysiological, neuroimaging, and computational evidence available by 2001 into a single architectural claim: PFC does not directly execute task operations; it configures downstream circuits by biasing them.

For SD-033a, the mapping to the design claim is clean at the module level. SD-033a asserts a lateral-PFC-analog that holds a stimulus-abstracted rule and projects bias into E3's trajectory-selection machinery. Miller and Cohen provide the architectural warrant for that arrangement. The specific falsifier SD-033a carries — "if E3 reproduces rule-abstracted generalisation without an explicit lateral-PFC-analog, SD-033a is over-specification" — is consistent with their framing: absent explicit maintenance of a rule-specific bias in PFC, the task-to-circuit mapping collapses, and performance on novel-but-rule-consistent stimuli fails to transfer.

Where the paper does less work is at the A1 / alternative boundary. The landing choice in SD-033a is a per-candidate score_bias head — a trajectory-specific top-down bias projected as a separate score contribution for each downstream candidate. The A1 alternative is a uniform state-dependent gain: a single scalar modulated by rule identity that multiplies (or shifts) all candidates equally. Miller and Cohen's framing is compatible with either implementation. Their "bias signal" language is abstract over projection granularity, and the specific neurophysiological evidence they cite — e.g. Asaad et al. on rule-selective PFC neurons — establishes that PFC encodes rules but does not resolve whether PFC output fans out as per-candidate weighting or as uniform gain into the basal ganglia and motor systems. That is a later question and one that more recent trajectory-conditional evaluation literature (Mansouri 2020; see separate entry) is better positioned to adjudicate.

For confidence, this entry is rated 0.78 — strongly supportive at the claim-module level, moderately supportive at the A1 landing-vs-alternative level. The failure signatures I record concern the specific granularity question: if downstream evidence shows biological PFC bias enters recipient circuits as a global gain rather than a per-candidate re-weighting, the SD-033a landing choice is over-specified and the uniform-scalar alternative should be preferred. Nothing in Miller and Cohen forces that failure, but nothing in it rules it out either. The transfer risk to REE is the usual architecture-cross-domain risk: "recipient structures" in biology are basal ganglia, premotor cortex, posterior cortex — all of which have circuit-level constraints (striatal gating, dimensionality, convergence onto motor neurons) that E3 does not share.

The honest read is that this paper earns SD-033a its architectural warrant without resolving the A1 question. The A1 question will be resolved by the downstream empirical literature on trajectory-conditional PFC signalling and by the V3-EXQ-456 diagnostic, not by the 2001 Annual Review.
