# Wallis, Anderson & Miller 2001 — Single neurons in prefrontal cortex encode abstract rules

According to PubMed, Wallis, Anderson & Miller (2001, [Nature 411:953-6](https://doi.org/10.1038/35082081)) recorded from single neurons in macaque dorsolateral and ventrolateral prefrontal cortex while two monkeys performed a delayed match-to-sample task under two abstract rules — same and different. On any given trial, an instruction cue established which rule was in effect; the animal then saw a sample picture, a delay, and a test picture, and had to release a lever for "same" trials when the rule was same and for "different" trials when the rule was different. Crucially, the monkeys generalised to held-out novel pictures, demonstrating that they had abstracted the rule rather than memorising stimulus-response pairs.

The headline finding for SD-033a is that the most prevalent activity pattern across the recorded PFC population was **rule coding** that was largely independent of stimulus identity — i.e., distinct rule states produced distinct, separable population responses. This is the upstream-side claim SD-033a makes about its lateral-PFC-analog: that a stimulus-abstracted `rule_state` is held and is available to project as a bias signal into downstream choice circuits.

For A1 specifically — per-candidate bias vector versus a uniform scalar gain modulated by rule identity — the paper provides what I would call necessary-but-not-sufficient evidence. Rule-selective single units mean that "the rule signal is somehow present in the population," which is a precondition for any rule-conditional projection. But the experimental design cannot discriminate the projection granularity downstream: a uniform-gain readout that scales all motor candidates by a single rule-dependent scalar would produce the same single-unit selectivity as a per-candidate readout that re-weights candidates differentially. The same/different task does not require selecting among multiple candidate trajectories in the way E3 does; it requires a binary lever release, where the bias projection question collapses.

This is a familiar problem in the lateral-PFC literature — the units recording rule signals are upstream of the projection step that A1 actually asks about. To discriminate A1 alternatives we would want simultaneous recordings (or causal manipulations) at the projection target during multi-candidate selection, which Wallis 2001 does not provide. The paper is properly classified as **mixed** for A1 — it supports SD-033a's broader architectural commitment that lateral PFC holds an abstract rule signal, while remaining silent on the per-candidate-vs-uniform-gain question. The mapping caveat is the binding one: treating this as supporting evidence for the A1 *landing* would over-read the paper.

The confidence components reflect this. Source quality is very high (Nature 2001, primate single-unit, generalisation to novel stimuli demonstrated, foundational to the modern PFC-rule-coding literature). Mapping fidelity is the limiting factor at 0.55: rule-selective firing is necessary for A1 but does not discriminate the projection alternatives, and the task design does not exercise multi-candidate selection. Transfer risk is moderate — the rule-as-bias-source framing maps cleanly to SD-033a's architectural commitment, but the projection step where A1 lives does not.

For SD-033a's V4 reconsideration of A1, the next-generation papers to look at are those that record PFC and a downstream selection circuit simultaneously during multi-alternative tasks (Mante 2013 and Stokes 2013, both indexed in this targeted review). Wallis 2001 functions as the canonical anchor establishing that rule signals exist; the discriminative work is downstream of it.
