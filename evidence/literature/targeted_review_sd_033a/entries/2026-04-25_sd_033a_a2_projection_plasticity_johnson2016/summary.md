# Johnson et al. 2016 -- projection plasticity during rule learning (SD-033a, A2)

Johnson, Peckler, Tai, and Wilbrecht use two-photon imaging to track long-range orbitofrontal axons terminating in dorsomedial prefrontal cortex while mice learn an odour-based multiple-choice rule task. The key result is narrower than a generic "PFC is plastic" claim: rule training changed the projection itself. Reward exposure, environmental enrichment, and simple instrumental experience were not enough. Rule training increased bouton turnover in OFC->dmPFC axons, and the pattern of change tracked behaviour. Baseline bouton density and bouton gain were associated with exploitative rule use, while bouton loss tracked exploration and exploratory prediction-error magnitude.

For SD-033a, this is the most directly relevant source yet on the bias-head question. The unresolved A2 issue is whether the top-down bias readout should remain a frozen-random head with zeroed output weights, or whether the projection should eventually be trained. Johnson et al. push against the "leave it frozen" endpoint. If rule learning in frontal cortex remodels the long-range projection that carries rule-relevant influence, then a permanently frozen SD-033a score-bias head is biologically weak as a final design. A bootstrap-safe frozen head can still make sense as a landing strategy, but it should be treated as temporary scaffolding rather than the intended mature mechanism.

The paper also sharpens the training-signal discussion. Projection strengthening was associated with exploitative use of a learned rule, while projection loss scaled with exploratory prediction error. That does not prove a specific phased protocol, but it does suggest that a single undifferentiated training signal is not the only biologically plausible choice. A reasonable REE interpretation is that SD-033a's bias head should eventually see at least two pressures: a rule-stabilizing signal during successful exploitation, and an error-linked restructuring signal during exploratory updating or reversal.

The main caveat is circuit mismatch. Johnson et al. study OFC->dmPFC axons in mice, not lateral PFC -> action-selection bias in primates. So this is not direct evidence for the exact SD-033a implementation. It is still important evidence at the architecture level, because it narrows what counts as a biologically grounded bias mechanism: a frontal bias projection can be a learned, strategy-sensitive substrate, not just a fixed readout layered on top of a separately learned rule state.

I record this as `evidence_direction: mixed`. It supports SD-033a's broader top-down-bias framing and strengthens the case for a trained bias head, but it also increases pressure against leaving the current landing permanently frozen. In practice, this entry shifts my recommendation toward: keep the frozen head only as an initialization and add a bounded training protocol once the behavioural stack around SD-033a is stable.
