# Mongillo, Barak, Tsodyks 2008 — Synaptic theory of working memory (SD-033a, A3)

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1150769)), Mongillo, Barak, and Tsodyks propose that working memory is sustained not by continuous persistent spiking but by calcium-mediated presynaptic facilitation in recurrent cortical networks. Residual calcium in presynaptic terminals acts as a buffer that is loaded, refreshed, and read out by sparse spiking activity. The mechanism has long time constants (seconds), low refresh requirements, and is metabolically efficient. Items can sit "silently" in the network — not producing ongoing activity — and be refreshed by occasional population activity, with stability and duration controlled by the network's spontaneous firing rate.

The mapping to SD-033a's A3 alternative is the core reason this paper is in the pull. SD-033a's landing choice is a gate-modulated EMA of rule_state driven by the SalienceCoordinator's write gate. The design doc lists three persistence mechanisms:

- **Landing (EMA):** rule_state decays toward zero at a fixed rate in the absence of writes; each write refreshes toward the new target.
- **Alternative 1 (GRU recurrence):** rule_state is maintained by a gated recurrent unit that updates on every step and holds by virtue of its internal gating.
- **Alternative 2 (synaptic hold / silent synapses):** rule_state is stored in synaptic weights or short-term plastic variables, with activity only at read/write events.

Mongillo et al. formalise a specific version of Alternative 2 and show that it is biologically plausible and computationally viable. This has two consequences for SD-033a. First, it weakens the case for the GRU / recurrent-activity alternative as the unique biological mechanism — Mansouri et al.'s 2020 review points to recurrent spiking, but Mongillo's mechanism is an equally respectable account of the same empirical phenomena. Second, and more importantly for the landing choice, the EMA in SD-033a is functionally closer to the Mongillo mechanism than to continuous reverberation: both have a decaying trace that is refreshed on write, both allow the held state to persist silently between writes, and both are metabolically cheap in the sense that the network does not need continuous spiking to carry the information.

The honest caveat is that an EMA is not a faithful implementation of calcium-mediated facilitation. The biophysical dynamics differ (exponential decay vs calcium kinetics), and the read mechanism differs (EMA is always on, facilitation requires a spiking event to release). The mapping is coarse. What Mongillo et al. establish is that silent, refreshable persistence is biologically viable — which licenses the general shape of the landing choice, not its specific parameters.

The failure signatures worth recording are clinical in flavour. If primate lateral PFC in a rule-selective persistence task shows strict spiking-dependence — silencing disrupts the hold immediately, with no recovery on restored input — then the silent regime Mongillo proposes is not operative in that context, and by transfer the EMA is a poor analogue. Conversely, if PFC rule-persistence is shown to survive brief silencing and recover on re-excitation (as Mongillo's mechanism predicts), the landing choice is vindicated at the functional level. The field has produced evidence on both sides, and the adjudication is not settled.

Confidence is set at 0.62. The source is strong (Science, canonical paper in the silent-synaptic-WM literature, Tsodyks group). The mapping is genuinely mixed — supportive at the functional level, weakly supportive at the biophysical level, and does not discriminate EMA from other silent-hold schemes. I record this as `evidence_direction: mixed` rather than `supports` because the paper argues against the spiking-persistence alternative as the unique mechanism but does not specifically endorse an EMA abstraction over, say, short-term synaptic plasticity (which would favour the A3 synaptic-hold alternative over the landing).
