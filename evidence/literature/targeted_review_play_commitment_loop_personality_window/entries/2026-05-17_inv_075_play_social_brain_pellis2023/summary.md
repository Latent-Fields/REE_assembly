# Play fighting and the development of the social brain: The rat's tale (Pellis et al., 2023)

According to PubMed ([DOI: 10.1016/j.neubiorev.2023.105037](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neubiorev.2023.105037)), this is the canonical synthesis of the Lethbridge rat play-fighting programme. The argument it consolidates is the one we actually need: depriving a rat of peer-peer play *during the juvenile period* produces an adult with measurable socio-cognitive deficits, and those deficits travel with physiological and anatomical changes in the prefrontal cortex, especially mPFC. The benefit is bounded in time — it is a juvenile-window phenomenon, not something an adult can backfill.

Why does this matter for the candidate cluster? Because it cleanly instantiates the INV-075 *antecedent* in biology. Juvenile play is self-extinguishing — it wanes as the animal matures, by design, like every other developmental setup signal we have catalogued (depolarizing GABA, the BDNF surge, the exploration epoch). And it is load-bearing — what it builds has to persist for the rest of the animal's life, because the adult never gets the window back. A signal that is both self-extinguishing and load-bearing is exactly the configuration INV-075 says cannot maintain itself: it must be locked or handed off, or the acquisition decays. So the paper does not, on its own, tell us *which* arm personality uses; it tells us the question is well-posed — there really is a self-extinguishing, load-bearing developmental signal here, not a steady-state one.

The most useful detail for REE is the negative result the authors stress: play does *not* train specific motor actions. Rats deprived of play are not missing a move; they are missing a transferable skill set that they would otherwise have deployed across social *and non-social* contexts. That is a strong hint about *what* is being calibrated. A substrate that stored play-acquired motor policies would predict context-specific deficits; what is observed is a domain-general arbitration deficit. In REE terms that points away from "play writes habits into the sensorimotor loop" and toward "play calibrates the cross-context weighting and gating that the three commitment loops (ARC-021 / MECH-069) apply when deciding whether and how to commit." That is an interpretation the paper does not make and cannot adjudicate — it is our extrapolation — but it is the reading the data are most consistent with.

The honest limits: this is rat rough-and-tumble play and prefrontal anatomy, not human personality and not basal-ganglia loop gains. The paper is silent on the striatal loops specifically, and silent on the lock-versus-handoff question that is the whole point of scoping this for INV-075. I have set confidence at 0.74 — high source quality and a clean antecedent mapping, discounted because the load-bearing structure the paper measures (prefrontal social cognition) is one inferential step away from the structure the candidate claim is about (the commitment-loop calibration), and because the persistence mechanism is exactly the thing this paper does not observe.
