# Temperament and character development across adolescence (Zohar, Zwir, Wang, Cloninger & Anokhin, 2018)

According to PubMed ([DOI: 10.1017/S0954579418000159](https://doi.org/10.1017/S0954579418000159)), this is a longitudinal study of 752 American adolescents assessed with the Junior Temperament and Character Inventory at 12, 14, and 16. It is the human counterpart to the rat play work, and it is the entry that maps most directly onto the candidate cluster — because the Cloninger framework already does, in its own vocabulary, the thing the cluster proposes.

Cloninger splits personality into *temperament* — harm avoidance, novelty seeking, reward dependence, persistence: early-emerging, heritable, and in his psychobiology tied to monoaminergic drive systems — and *character* — self-directedness, cooperativeness: the self-regulatory layer. The paper's central finding is that these follow different trajectories: harm avoidance and persistence decline while self-directedness and cooperativeness rise from 12 to 16, and novelty seeking, reward dependence and self-transcendence show a *biphasic* pattern, rising to 14 then falling. The authors' interpretation is precise and worth quoting in paraphrase: before 14 the adolescent emancipates from adult authority and aligns to emergent peer norms; after 14 the created identity is *internalized*. Character "develops rapidly in adolescence to self-regulate temperament in accord with personally valued goals shaped by peers."

Translate that into REE and the candidate cluster almost writes itself. Temperament is the early, relatively fixed drive-prior of the commitment loops — the limbic-loop value prior that says what is worth committing to, which Cloninger independently grounds in monoaminergic systems, so the basal-ganglia reading is not a stretch. Character is an adolescently-developing regulatory layer that re-weights how those drive priors gate commitment, and — this is the load-bearing word — it is calibrated by the *peer* environment. This is exactly "a social-developmental window calibrates the commitment system." And the post-14 *internalization of the created identity* is the cluster's LOCK: once the peer-calibration window closes, the calibrated commitment policy is consolidated into something that persists and thereafter resists revision. The prediction structure supports this — character maturity at 16 is predicted by both earlier temperament and earlier character, whereas temperament dependability at 16 is predicted mainly by earlier temperament. The regulatory layer is not a pure readout of the drive layer; it has its own developmental dynamics and its own stabilisation.

The caveats are honest and I have kept confidence at 0.72 rather than higher. This is psychometrics, not neuroscience — the Junior TCI, not recordings from striatal loops. "Internalization" is operationalised as trait stabilisation and predictive structure, not as a measured plasticity-restriction mechanism, so the study evidences that a *window-then-stabilise* pattern exists in human personality, not the lock mechanism itself. And the mapping of temperament/character onto the three commitment loops is REE's overlay on Cloninger's model, not Cloninger's claim. But for the question this pull is meant to settle — is the candidate hypothesis well-posed, and which INV-075 arm does personality stability look like — this is the strongest single piece of human evidence that the answer is "window then lock," not "perpetually maintained by an ongoing signal."
