# Social play shapes prefrontal inhibition in a region-specific manner (Bijlsma, Vanderschuren & Wierenga, 2023)

According to PubMed ([DOI: 10.1093/cercor/bhad212](https://doi.org/10.1093/cercor/bhad212)), this paper does the electrophysiological work that the Pellis review summarises at the behavioural level. The authors deprived rats of social play between P21 and P42 and then recorded from layer-5 pyramidal neurons in mPFC and OFC at three ages. Two findings matter for us. First, play deprivation selectively reduced *inhibitory* synaptic transmission — excitatory input was untouched. Second, and more interesting, the effect was region-specific in *time*: the mPFC reduction was present during the deprivation window, whereas the OFC reduction only became manifest *after* the window had closed.

The bounded-window point is straightforward support for the candidate cluster: there is a specific juvenile interval (here P21–P42) in which an environmental signal — peer play — sets a persistent property of prefrontal circuitry. That is the INV-075 antecedent again, now with a defined window and a measured substrate change, not just a behavioural deficit.

The part I find genuinely informative is the region-specific *timing*. mPFC and OFC do not get calibrated on the same schedule, and they do not even read out their calibration on the same schedule — OFC's change is latent until after the window. In REE these two regions sit on different cortico-striatal loops (broadly, mPFC into the limbic/associative arbitration, OFC into value/outcome evaluation). So the data are consistent with a prediction the candidate cluster makes and that ARC-023 already asserts in another guise: the three commitment loops are not calibrated synchronously — each has its own window and possibly its own consolidation latency. A model that calibrated all loops in one shared critical period would not predict the staggered, region-specific trajectories observed here.

The delayed OFC effect is worth flagging as a half-clue about the lock-versus-handoff question, though it is only a clue. A change that is *induced* during the window but only *expressed* afterwards is the signature of a consolidation step that runs after the setup signal is gone — which is what a lock looks like from the outside. But the paper does not characterise any consolidation mechanism, so this is suggestive at best and I have not let it move confidence much.

Limits are the obvious ones: rodent, prefrontal pyramidal cells rather than striatal loop nodes, and the loop-level reading is inference rather than measurement. I have set confidence at 0.66 — solid electrophysiology and the best available analogue of differential loop windows, discounted heavily because the structure measured is one synapse away from the structure the candidate claim is about, and because the lock/handoff mechanism is exactly what the paper cannot see.
