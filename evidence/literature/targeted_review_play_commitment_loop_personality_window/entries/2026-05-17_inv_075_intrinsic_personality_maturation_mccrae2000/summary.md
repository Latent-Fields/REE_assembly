# Nature over nurture: temperament, personality, and life span development (McCrae, Costa, Ostendorf et al., 2000)

According to PubMed ([DOI: 10.1037//0022-3514.78.1.173](https://doi.org/10.1037//0022-3514.78.1.173)), this is the deliberate counterweight in this pull, and it is included precisely because it pushes back on the user's "environmentally-determined" qualifier. McCrae and colleagues analyse NEO-FFI scores across five cultures (N=5085, ages 14+) and marshal behaviour-genetic, parent-child, structural, animal, and longitudinal evidence to argue that the five-factor traits are biologically based tendencies with *intrinsic* developmental paths — endogenous maturation, with only modest empirical association to the rearing environment. Their resolution of the nature/nurture tension is the key move: environment and culture shape the *characteristic adaptations* that express personality, not the *basic tendencies* themselves.

It would be intellectually dishonest to file the play and Cloninger papers as clean support and quietly omit this one. So I have logged its direction as **mixed**, because it does two things at once that are both decision-relevant. It *strengthens* one half of the candidate cluster and *weakens* the other. The half it strengthens: that personality is a persisting structure with a developmental lock — McCrae's "intrinsic maturation" is itself a lock claim, just an endogenous one. The half it weakens: the "environmentally-determined" part. If basic-trait stability is largely endogenous and schedule-driven, then the peer/play environment is not writing the locked set-points; it is writing a downstream layer.

For REE this is the most useful single finding in the pull for the *registration decision*, because it forces a structural disambiguation the candidate cluster currently blurs. The cluster says "play calibrates the commitment loops and personality is the locked result." McCrae says: be careful which thing you claim is locked and which thing is environmentally calibrated. The defensible REE reading after this paper is a two-layer model: (1) the basal-ganglia commitment-loop *drive-prior set-points* (temperament; REE limbic-loop value prior) are largely **endogenously** locked on a maturational timer — INV-075's lock arm, but the lock is schedule-driven, not experience-written; and (2) a downstream *commitment-policy / characteristic-adaptation* layer is what the play/peer window environmentally calibrates and then consolidates. That is exactly the distinction Cloninger's temperament/character split also implies, from the other direction. Two independent literatures converging on a two-layer structure is a strong signal that any registered claim must be two claims, not one — a parent about the endogenous set-point lock, and a child about the environmentally-calibrated policy layer — rather than a single "play determines personality" claim.

Caveats, stated plainly: this is FFM psychometrics, it says nothing about basal-ganglia loops, and it predates two decades of gene-environment-interaction and differential-susceptibility work that has softened a strong "nature over nurture" reading (the Ham 2026 individual-difference result is itself a crack in pure endogenous determinism). It *bounds* the environmental-determinism claim; it does not refute it. Confidence 0.70: the finding is robust and the disambiguation it forces is exactly what the registration decision needs, discounted because it adjudicates the determinism axis without touching the loop-level mechanism, and because the strong-nativist framing is now contested.
