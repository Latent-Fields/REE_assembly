# Aleman-Zapata, Morris & Genzel 2022 -- Double Necessity: Sleep AND Ripples Required for Memory Consolidation

**Source:** Aleman-Zapata A, Morris RGM, Genzel L. "Sleep deprivation and hippocampal ripple disruption after one-session learning eliminate memory expression the next day." *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* 119(44):e2123424119, 2022. DOI: [10.1073/pnas.2123424119](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2123424119). PMID: 36279444

**Claim evidenced:** SD-017

---

## What the paper did

Aleman-Zapata et al. developed a one-session spatial learning paradigm on a plus-maze where animals identify the goal arm in a single training session and are tested 24h later. They ran three groups: (1) allowed to sleep normally after training; (2) sleep deprived for 4h after training; (3) allowed to sleep with closed-loop SWR disruption for 4h after training. Memory was tested at 24h.

## Key findings relevant to REE

Both sleep deprivation and SWR disruption reduced memory performance to chance level (at 24h test). Animals with normal sleep and intact SWRs showed strong memory retention. The result establishes a double necessity: sleep as a whole is necessary for consolidation of one-session learning (sleep deprivation arm), and SWRs within sleep are necessary for that consolidation (SWR disruption arm). These two conditions are equivalent in their effect on memory.

This is the strongest available evidence that: (a) offline sleep periods are genuinely necessary (not merely beneficial) for hippocampal memory consolidation, and (b) the SWR mechanism within those periods is the critical vehicle, not other aspects of sleep physiology.

## Translation to REE

SD-017 states that without offline SWS-analog phases, hippocampal attribution mapping cannot converge and context representations remain globally undifferentiated. Aleman-Zapata et al. provide the cleanest existing proof of the necessity claim: both eliminating sleep and eliminating SWRs within sleep produce equivalent memory failure. This means:

1. The offline sleep window is not optional -- learning occurs but consolidation requires the sleep period (eliminates the possibility that waking replay alone is sufficient for SD-017's consolidation requirement).
2. SWRs are the necessary mechanism within the sleep window -- not slow oscillations, spindles, or other NREM features per se. The SWR replay vehicle is the essential delivery mechanism for hippocampal-to-schema transfer.

For REE: the SWS-analog phase in V3 must generate functional SWR-equivalent replay events. A V3 system that has offline periods but lacks SWR-equivalent consolidation events would fail consolidation -- even if offline periods are long enough. The V3 EXQ-242 experiment (SWS-analog ablation) directly tests this prediction in the context of two-context discrimination.

The one-session design is particularly relevant because it isolates the consolidation requirement from incremental learning across sessions -- a single exposure requires subsequent SWR-mediated offline consolidation to persist. This maps onto REE's need for single-encounter harm events to consolidate into the residue field (MECH-099).

## Limitations and caveats

The task is spatial navigation in a plus-maze -- it does not test context-attribution discrimination (the specific failure mode in SD-017 that concerns multi-context differentiation). The REM-analog phase of SD-017 (causal attribution replay for slot-filling) is not addressed -- the study focuses on NREM SWR events. Whether the one-session necessity result generalises to complex multi-episode context attribution (where SD-017's accumulation argument applies most strongly) requires additional evidence. The closed-loop SWR disruption method is technically demanding and sample sizes are modest.
