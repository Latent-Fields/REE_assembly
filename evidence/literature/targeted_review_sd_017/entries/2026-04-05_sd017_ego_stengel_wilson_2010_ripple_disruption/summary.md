# Ego-Stengel & Wilson 2010 -- Within-Animal Ripple Disruption Impairs Spatial Learning

**Source:** Ego-Stengel V, Wilson MA. "Disruption of ripple-associated hippocampal activity during rest impairs spatial learning in the rat." *Hippocampus* 20(1):1-10, 2010. DOI: [10.1002/hipo.20707](https://doi.org/10.1002/hipo.20707). PMID: 19816984

**Claim evidenced:** SD-017

---

## What the paper did

Ego-Stengel and Wilson trained rats daily on two spatial navigation tasks with identical procedures (find a food reward on a circular track). Each task was followed by a 1-hour rest period. After one task (counterbalanced across animals), they stimulated hippocampal afferents during detected ripple events to disrupt ripple-associated activity during rest. The other task served as a within-animal control with undisrupted rest. Learning rates were tracked across sessions for the disrupted vs control task.

## Key findings relevant to REE

Animals learned the control task significantly faster than the task with disrupted post-training rest. The within-animal design rules out individual differences in learning ability. Sleep-wake structure was not significantly changed by the stimulation. The result establishes a direct, within-animal causal link between SWR-associated rest activity and subsequent learning rate -- disrupting the offline consolidation window for one task slows its learning while the other task (same animal, same day, intact rest) progresses normally.

The learning rate measure (rather than a single test point) shows that the impairment is gradual -- suggesting that ripple disruption slows the consolidation process without completely eliminating it.

## Translation to REE

SD-017 requires offline SWS-analog phases to be necessary (not merely beneficial) for hippocampal attribution convergence. Ego-Stengel and Wilson independently replicate the necessity result from Girardeau (2009) with a stronger within-animal design. The learning rate slowing confirms that each offline consolidation episode contributes to the gradual convergence of hippocampal representations -- consistent with SD-017's prediction that without offline phases, context representations remain globally undifferentiated rather than converging to stable schemas.

The convergence here is in the learning rate sense: the hippocampus needs repeated offline consolidation events to build up the stable spatial representation over sessions. This maps directly onto SD-017's accumulation claim: offline phases are necessary not for single-episode learning but for the progressive convergence of attribution maps across multiple episodes.

## Limitations and caveats

The disruption affects ripple-associated activity broadly (not specifically NREM SPW-Rs -- the stimulation occurs during both waking rest and NREM). The gradual learning rate impairment rather than complete elimination of memory could indicate that some consolidation occurs outside ripple events. As with Girardeau (2009), the task is positive-valence spatial navigation, not harm attribution. The REM-analog phase of SD-017 (causal attribution replay) is not addressed by this paradigm.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence 0.85. Hippocampus journal, MIT Wilson lab. Within-animal design is a significant methodological strength over Girardeau (2009). Converging necessity evidence from independent lab and paradigm. Confidence docked for broader disruption method (not closed-loop SPW-R specific) and positive-valence task limitation.
