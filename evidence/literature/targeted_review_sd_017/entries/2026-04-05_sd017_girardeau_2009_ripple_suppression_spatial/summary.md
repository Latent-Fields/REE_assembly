# Girardeau et al. 2009 -- Selective SWR Suppression Impairs Spatial Memory Consolidation

**Source:** Girardeau G, Benchenane K, Wiener SI, Buzsaki G, Zugaro MC. "Selective suppression of hippocampal ripples impairs spatial memory." *Nature Neuroscience* 12:1222-1223, 2009. DOI: [10.1038/nn.2384](https://doi.org/10.1038/nn.2384). PMID: 19749750

**Claim evidenced:** SD-017

---

## What the paper did

Girardeau et al. trained rats to navigate an 8-arm radial maze where the same 3 arms were baited daily (spatial reference memory). Using closed-loop detection of hippocampal SPW-R (sharp wave-ripple) events, they delivered electrical stimulation to suppress the ripple whenever one was detected during post-training rest periods, while leaving overall sleep architecture (NREM/REM proportions and timing) intact. Memory performance was compared between sessions with ripple suppression and control sessions.

## Key findings relevant to REE

Selective elimination of SWRs during post-training rest resulted in significant performance impairment on the radial maze task. The impairment was specific to the consolidation-suppression condition -- sleep structure was preserved, so the result cannot be attributed to general sleep disruption. This is direct causal evidence that SWR events themselves (not merely sleep duration) are necessary for spatial memory consolidation.

This paper, with Ego-Stengel and Wilson (2010), constitutes the foundational empirical evidence that hippocampal SWR replay is a necessary mechanism for offline consolidation, not merely correlated with it.

## Translation to REE

SD-017 requires a minimal SWS-analog phase that delivers bidirectional hippocampus-to-cortex replay for schema/slot consolidation. The Girardeau finding establishes the necessity half of this requirement: removing the SWR events that carry the replay signal (while leaving sleep intact) prevents consolidation. In REE terms, the SWR vehicle is the mechanism by which hippocampal attribution-map updates transfer to the neocortical schema layer (E1 world model). Without these events, the E3 hippocampal system accumulates episodic experience but it never transfers to the persistent schematic layer -- the result is context representations that remain globally undifferentiated (i.e., episodic rather than schematic), which is precisely SD-017's predicted failure mode.

The spatial reference memory paradigm (same baited arms each day) is analogous to a stable context-attribution task: the animal must learn which spatial zones are safe/rewarding and generalise across sessions. The impairment when SWRs are disrupted shows that even stable context-spatial associations require offline SWR consolidation to persist.

## Limitations and caveats

Spatial reference memory for food reward is a positive-valence task. SD-017's primary concern is harm-attribution convergence. Whether SWR necessity extends to harm-specific attribution (distinguishing harm-prone vs harm-free contexts across multiple episodes) is not established by this paper alone. The V3 experiment EXQ-242 tests this directly. The stimulation method may have co-stimulated non-SWR hippocampal and entorhinal activity; closed-loop specificity is high but not perfect. Small sample (n=6 rats).

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence 0.88. Nature Neuroscience, Buzsaki lab (definitive SWR authority). Closed-loop design with intact sleep control makes this the cleanest causal necessity demonstration available. Confidence docked for positive-valence task and modest n.
