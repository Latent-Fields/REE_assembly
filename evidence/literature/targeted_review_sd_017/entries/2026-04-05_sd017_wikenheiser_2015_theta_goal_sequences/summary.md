# Wikenheiser & Redish 2015 -- Theta Sequences Encode Goal-Directed Trajectory Content

**Source:** Wikenheiser AM, Redish AD. "Hippocampal theta sequences reflect current goals." *Nature Neuroscience* 18(2):289-294, 2015. DOI: [10.1038/nn.3909](https://doi.org/10.1038/nn.3909). PMID: 25559082

**Claim evidenced:** SD-017 (mixed)

---

## What the paper did

Wikenheiser and Redish recorded CA1 place cells in rats performing a T-maze task with multiple goal locations. During locomotion (running), hippocampal theta sequences (the sequential activation of place cells representing a trajectory, compressed within theta cycles) were decoded to characterise their content and extent. The researchers specifically asked whether theta sequence content and length were modulated by the animal's current goal, and how this varied across different phases of navigation.

## Key findings relevant to REE

Theta sequences projected further ahead in the environment (longer lookahead distance) when animals were farther from their goal and compressed as they approached it. The content of the sequences was systematically biased toward the current goal location -- the hippocampus preferentially coded trajectories leading to the goal the animal was currently seeking. This goal-modulation was dynamic: as the goal changed (different T-maze arms on different trials), the sequences updated accordingly.

## Translation to REE

SD-017 requires that the offline SWS-analog phase consolidates meaningful goal-directed content from the waking period. Wikenheiser and Redish establish what that content looks like during waking: theta sequences already carry goal-directed trajectory structure. For SD-017's SWS-analog phase to do useful work, the waking encoding must produce goal-annotated sequential representations that are worth consolidating. This paper confirms that waking hippocampal encoding produces exactly those goal-directed trajectory sequences.

In REE terms: during active navigation, E3's planning module generates trajectory proposals using hippocampal theta sequences. These sequences are already biased toward goal locations (harm-minimum or benefit-maximum regions of the residue field). The SWS-analog phase (SD-017) then consolidates these goal-directed waking sequences into the schema layer. Without goal-structured waking encoding, there would be nothing for the offline consolidation phase to process -- making this paper a prerequisite evidence for SD-017 rather than direct support for it.

The paper is classified as mixed because it supports the architectural context of SD-017 (goal-directed content exists to be consolidated) but does not address offline necessity. The necessity claim requires the disruption studies (Girardeau 2009, Ego-Stengel and Wilson 2010, Aleman-Zapata et al. 2022).

## Limitations and caveats

The study is entirely about awake theta sequences during active locomotion -- it does not address offline SWR consolidation at all. Evidence direction is mixed because the paper supports the architecture that SD-017 presupposes (goal-directed waking encoding) but not the specific necessity claim. The T-maze goal structure is simpler than the multi-context harm-attribution task SD-017's failure mode involves. Theta sequences in this paper are short (covering a few metres), while the residue field navigation in REE may require much longer lookahead.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence 0.62 (mixed direction). Nature Neuroscience, Redish lab (canonical hippocampal planning group). High source quality. The mixed rating reflects that this paper is evidence for the architectural scaffold of SD-017 (goal-directed hippocampal encoding) rather than for its necessity claim. Included because it establishes that goal-directed sequential content is what the offline phases must consolidate.
