# Tononi & Cirelli (2006) — Sleep function and synaptic homeostasis

**Claim tested:** INV-051 — optimal MEL range; understimulation produces model rigidity, overload produces incomplete update.

## What the paper argues

Tononi and Cirelli's Synaptic Homeostasis Hypothesis (SHY) holds that waking is a period of net synaptic potentiation: every learning episode, every prediction error, every adaptation to novelty strengthens connections across cortical circuits. Sleep -- particularly non-REM slow-wave activity (SWA) -- serves the function of globally downscaling these accumulated synaptic weights back toward an energetically sustainable baseline. The slow-wave signal is itself regulated homeostatically: it tracks prior waking load, being maximal at sleep onset after a demanding day and minimal after low-stimulation waking periods. The core consequence is that sleep's restorative, consolidating function is not divorced from daytime activity -- it is proportional to it.

## Why this is relevant to INV-051

INV-051 claims there is an optimal range of daily Model Error Load (MEL). The understimulation pole of this claim -- that extreme monotony, isolation, or institutional under-stimulation produces progressive model rigidity even with intact sleep architecture -- maps directly onto the SHY framework. If a person's waking environment generates little novel prediction error (low MEL), then little synaptic potentiation accumulates, which in turn provides a weak homeostatic signal for slow-wave activity during sleep. The consolidation and renormalisation function -- which in the REE framework is what keeps the model-update loop exercised and adaptive -- is thus under-exercised not because sleep is structurally impaired, but because there is insufficient waking-phase demand to drive it. This is mechanistically distinct from structural sleep pathology: the architecture may look intact, yet the system progressively entrenches existing representations for lack of update pressure.

## The overload pole

SHY is less directly relevant to the overload pole of INV-051 (excessive MEL producing overload insomnia and incomplete update). The hypothesis is silent on what happens when synaptic potentiation load exceeds the system's capacity to normalise it within a single sleep episode. However, the logic is not inconsistent: if daytime potentiation is extreme, the drive for SWA would be high, but the transition from potentiated wakefulness to the cortical quiescence needed for slow oscillations may itself be impaired by the very arousal that the high-load state produces. This pathway requires complementary hyperarousal evidence.

## Caveats and mapping limits

SHY describes synaptic-level dynamics in animal and human EEG studies; it does not directly study MEL, model rigidity, or chronic understimulation in institutional settings. The REE translation treats MEL as an aggregate of the learning and prediction-error demands that drive synaptic potentiation across a waking period -- a conceptual extension that is plausible but not empirically validated as such. SHY also does not resolve whether chronically reduced slow-wave exercise produces permanent rigidity or merely recoverable hysteresis, which matters for the clinical claim in INV-051. It should also be noted that memory-replay consolidation theories offer a parallel account of sleep function that does not require the same quantity of waking learning to be present -- so alternative accounts of why sleep quality might degrade under monotony remain possible.
