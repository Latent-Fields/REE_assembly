# Haber (2003) -- Primate BG Parallel and Integrative Networks

## What the paper did

Suzanne Haber's 2003 review synthesises the tract-tracing and neurochemistry literature on primate basal ganglia connectivity to establish the canonical three-domain model of the striatum. Drawing on anterograde and retrograde tracing in macaques, the review maps the corticostriatal projections onto three functional territories: the ventromedial (limbic) striatum receiving from orbital and anterior cingulate cortex, amygdala, and hippocampus; the central (associative) striatum receiving from dorsolateral prefrontal and parietal cortices; and the dorsolateral (sensorimotor) striatum receiving from primary motor, premotor, and somatosensory cortices. The review also describes the striatonigrostriatal spiral, a unidirectional feed-forward pathway through which limbic circuits influence associative circuits, and associative circuits influence motor circuits -- providing a substrate for hierarchical integration without collapsing the loops.

## Key findings relevant to ARC-021

The central finding is that the three striatal territories receive qualitatively distinct cortical inputs that encode incommensurable content. The limbic territory receives hedonic valence, motivational state, and contextual memory signals. The associative territory receives goal representations, working memory content, and abstract rule-based signals from prefrontal cortex. The sensorimotor territory receives movement parameters and proprioceptive predictions. These territories project to distinct thalamic nuclei and return to distinct cortical targets, forming closed loops that are anatomically segregated. The spiraling pathway allows influence across loops in a hierarchically ordered direction (limbic -> associative -> motor) without creating a reverse or bidirectional mixture. This architecture physically prevents the credit signals in one loop from being attributed to computations in another -- the substrate for what ARC-021 calls 'distinct learning channels.'

## How this translates to REE (ARC-021)

ARC-021 requires that E1 (sensory prediction error), E2 (motor-sensory error), and E3 (harm/goal error) function as distinct learning channels -- that mixing them misattributes credit. Haber's anatomy provides the biological ground for this claim. The limbic loop (ventromedial striatum, OFC/ACC/NAc) is the biological substrate for E3's harm_eval and goal-approach channels: it processes valence, outcome value, and motivational state. The associative loop (central striatum, dlPFC/caudate) corresponds to E3's planning and evaluation functions: it processes goal-directed action selection and rule representation. The sensorimotor loop (dorsolateral striatum, motor cortex/putamen) corresponds to E2's forward model: it processes action-consequence predictions at the motor execution level. That these three input profiles are anatomically segregated is the biological argument that they carry information that should not be merged at the learning signal level.

## Limitations and caveats

The three-domain model is a simplification. Haber explicitly acknowledges that loop boundaries are gradient-like rather than sharp, with projection neurons having multiple collaterals extending across loop boundaries. The spiraling pathway itself introduces bidirectional information flow at the midbrain level, even if the striatal inputs are segregated. The review is also specific to macaque anatomy -- while the three-loop organization is conserved across mammals, the specific projection targets and functional specializations vary. Critically, the review describes anatomical connectivity, not functional incommensurability of learning signals. The claim that these loops cannot share credit assignment (MECH-069) requires computational or physiological evidence beyond anatomy -- anatomy establishes that the signals are distinct but not that they are incommensurable in the strong sense required by ARC-021.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is set at 0.78. This is the highest-quality anatomical reference for the three-loop model, and the finding is foundational and highly reproducible. The mapping to ARC-021 is good: distinct anatomical loops with distinct cortical inputs is strong evidence for the 'distinct channels' claim. The key gap is that anatomy does not directly prove incommensurability of learning signals -- for that, PBWM (O'Reilly & Frank 2006) provides the computational argument, and the two papers together make a stronger joint case than either alone. The transfer risk from primate anatomy to REE's simulated architecture is moderate but acceptable given that the functional argument -- qualitatively distinct inputs should have distinct credit channels -- does not depend on the specific biological substrate.
