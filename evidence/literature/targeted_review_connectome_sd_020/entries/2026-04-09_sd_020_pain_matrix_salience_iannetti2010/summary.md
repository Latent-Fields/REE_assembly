# Summary: Iannetti and Mouraux (2010) -- From the neuromatrix to the pain matrix (and back)

**Entry:** 2026-04-09_sd_020_pain_matrix_salience_iannetti2010
**Claim tested:** SD-020
**Evidence direction:** supports

## What the study did

Iannetti and Mouraux (2010) wrote a theoretical review revisiting the "pain matrix" -- the widespread cortical network (insula, ACC, secondary somatosensory cortex, frontal areas) reliably activated during pain neuroimaging studies and commonly interpreted as pain's neural signature. Their analysis drew on EEG, MEG, fMRI, and PET evidence to argue that this interpretation was mistaken. The key observation: equally salient but non-painful stimuli (loud sounds, unexpected tactile events) activate the same network with similar topography and magnitude. Context and expectation modulate responses in ways inconsistent with a pure intensity-encoding system.

## Key findings

The central argument is that the pain matrix reflects a supramodal salience detection system -- a cortical mechanism for detecting, orienting attention towards, and reacting to significant or surprising sensory events, regardless of modality. Pain reliably activates this network not because pain is pain, but because painful stimuli are almost always salient and surprising in laboratory conditions. When surprise is controlled for (e.g., repeated presentation until stimuli become fully predicted), pain-matrix responses attenuate without any change in perceived intensity -- the empirical signature of a PE/surprise system, not an intensity system. The insula is explicitly included: insula activity to nociceptive stimuli cannot be dissociated from insula activity to equally salient non-nociceptive stimuli, a finding later confirmed by direct intracranial recordings.

## REE translation

SD-020 claims that z_harm_a (the affective harm stream, biologically anchored in AIC) should be trained on precision-weighted prediction error -- the mismatch between predicted and actual harm accumulation -- rather than on raw accumulated harm. Iannetti and Mouraux provide the theoretical foundation: if the AIC is a salience/surprise detector, its natural training signal is the degree to which incoming harm information exceeds prediction, not the harm level per se. An agent whose E3 urgency is driven by z_harm_a trained on PE will respond most strongly to unexpected threat escalation -- behaviourally the correct response, as this is when the agent's current policy has most clearly failed to anticipate. Training on raw harm accumulation instead codes sustained expected threat as highly as novel unexpected threat, degrading the urgency signal's behavioural utility.

## Limitations and confidence reasoning

This is a theoretical review, not a direct empirical measurement of PE coding. The salience framework is contested: critics argue it underweights pain-specific contributions and does not explain why pain produces more persistent distress than equally salient non-painful stimuli. The review does not distinguish anterior from posterior insula at the resolution that later empirical work (Geuter 2017, Horing 2022) establishes -- that distinction is critical for SD-020. Confidence 0.72: high source quality (>1000 citations, UCL and UCLouvain), framework directly underpins the SD-020 rationale, but capped because the claim is theoretical and contested, and does not formalise PE in the precision-weighting sense that SD-020 requires.
