# Elster 2000 -- Ulysses Unbound: ARC-014 Mapping

**Source:** Elster, J. (2000). *Ulysses Unbound: Studies in Rationality, Precommitment, and Constraints*. Cambridge University Press. Available via: [PhilPapers](https://philpapers.org/rec/ELSUUS-2)

---

## What the Book Does

Ulysses Unbound is Jon Elster's extended study of precommitment: the rational strategy of binding oneself in advance to prevent future irrational reconsideration. The Ulysses metaphor is precise -- Odysseus had himself tied to the mast before passing the Sirens so that he could not act on his in-the-moment desire to swim to them. The precommitment worked precisely because it removed the option: once committed (literally bound), the question of whether to swim was no longer open.

The book revises and extends Elster's earlier Ulysses and the Sirens (1979) with three long essays covering: (1) self-binding and precommitment in personal decision-making; (2) constitutional precommitment in political institutions; (3) constraints and creativity in the arts. The first essay is directly relevant to ARC-014.

## Key Arguments Relevant to ARC-014

**1. Precommitment removes options, and that is the point**: Elster argues that precommitment is not merely a strategy that makes some options less attractive -- it is a strategy that removes options entirely. The removal is the mechanism: Ulysses is not tempted to swim because he cannot, not because he decides against it. For precommitment to work, the option must be genuinely foreclosed, not merely penalised. This supports ARC-014's categorical boundary: the pre/post-commit route switch does not merely attenuate re-simulation; it forecloses it architecturally.

**2. Irreversibility is constitutive of commitment**: actions that can be undone at will are not commitments. For Elster, the irreversibility of a committed action is not an unfortunate side effect but the defining feature of commitment -- without it, the agent is not really bound. This maps directly onto ARC-014's claim: "Committed actions cannot be 'unsimulated'." The post-commit channel exists precisely because the action is no longer hypothetical; it is enacted, and its consequences must be attributed as real rather than imagined.

**3. Constraints can be constitutive of agency, not limitations on it**: in Ulysses Unbound, Elster generalises the precommitment argument: constraints on what the agent can do or reconsider are sometimes what makes genuine agency possible, not limitations on agency. A poet who imposes a strict form (sonnet, haiku) cannot say anything; but the constraint is what makes the poem a poem rather than prose. For Elster, this generalises: an agent without commitment architecture cannot act in the full sense -- they can only adjust preferences continuously. This supports ARC-014's architectural necessity argument.

**4. The separation of imagination from action is the epistemic condition for good planning**: Elster notes (in the context of time-inconsistency) that the agent who can simulate and the agent who must act are in important senses different agents. Precommitment mediates the relationship between them. This maps onto REE's pre/post-commit distinction: the pre-commit channel (simulation, Default Mode) is where the "imaginative agent" operates; the post-commit channel is where the "enacted-consequences agent" operates. Keeping them separate is the condition for coherent learning.

## REE Mapping to ARC-014

ARC-014's commitment architecture implements Elster's precommitment logic at the architectural level. The pre-commit/post-commit boundary corresponds to the moment when the option is removed -- the Ulysses moment when the mast-binding takes effect. Before commit, trajectories are options being evaluated in the simulation channel (Default Mode); after commit, the action is enacted and its consequences are attributed via the post-commit channel (phi(z) write). The categorical character of the boundary -- the route switch -- is the architectural implementation of Elster's claim that genuine commitment forecloses the option rather than merely weighting against it.

The architectural necessity of offline attribution passes in REE (MECH-029, MECH-092) maps onto Elster's insight that the consequences of committed actions must be processed by the "enacted agent" not the "imaginative agent" -- precisely because the action is no longer hypothetical. A system that continued processing committed actions through the simulation channel would confuse imagination with reality in exactly the way that failing the Ulysses precommitment confuses the imagined pleasure of the Sirens with the real consequences of drowning.

## Limitations and Confidence Reasoning

The key structural disanalogy between Elster's account and ARC-014 is that Elster describes voluntary, strategic self-binding while ARC-014 claims architectural necessity that holds regardless of the agent's strategic intentions. An REE agent does not choose to have the pre/post-commit boundary -- it is a built-in architectural feature. Elster's framework supports the functional role of the boundary but does not establish its necessity in the way ARC-014 claims. Additionally, Elster's analysis focuses on temporal self-consistency and weakness of will; the phi(z) write-gating function in REE is about ethical residue attribution, which requires an additional step. Confidence: 0.70.
