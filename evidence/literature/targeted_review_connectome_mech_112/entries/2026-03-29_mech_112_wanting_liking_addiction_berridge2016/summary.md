# Berridge and Robinson (2016) -- Liking, wanting, and the incentive-sensitization theory of addiction

**Claim tested:** MECH-112 (E3 requires a structured latent goal representation distinct from harm avoidance)

## What the paper did

Berridge and Robinson's 2016 review in American Psychologist consolidates two decades of work on the wanting/liking dissociation, extending the framework from food reward to drug addiction and providing a comprehensive account of the incentive-sensitization theory. The review is not purely theoretical: it integrates the original rat dopamine depletion studies (wanting absent, liking intact), the hedonic hotspot work (NAc shell opioid microinjections amplify liking locally without expanding wanting), and the sensitisation studies (repeated drug exposure amplifies the wanting system while liking remains stable or diminishes). The key addition over the 1996 paper is the demonstration that wanting and liking can become inversely correlated in addiction: wanting escalates via sensitisation of mesolimbic dopamine while liking remains flat or declines due to tolerance or allostatic shift.

## Key findings relevant to MECH-112

The sensitisation case is architecturally critical for MECH-112. In addiction, wanting escalates far beyond liking -- addicts can intensely want drugs they no longer like. This confirms that the two signals are not derived from a single underlying value representation (if they were, they would covary). They are genuinely independent computational processes that can be separately amplified or attenuated. For MECH-112, this implies that z_goal (wanting) must be an independently maintained latent, not a transformation of benefit_eval_head output (liking). If z_goal were merely a smoothed or integrated version of liking, the sensitisation dissociation could not be reproduced and the architecture would be unable to model compulsive approach to no-longer-beneficial goals.

The paper also establishes that wanting can be unconscious and can occur in opposition to explicit cognitive preferences -- incentive salience is a motivational signal that can drive approach regardless of whether the agent reflectively endorses the goal. This has a subtle implication for REE: z_goal carries motivational force that is not reducible to conscious goal endorsement, which is the correct architecture for a goal-directed agent (the agent should pursue goals even when meta-cognitive evaluation might not explicitly endorse them in every moment).

## REE translation

MECH-112 requires that z_goal be a persistent, prospective attractor that pulls E3 trajectory scoring toward goal-approach rather than merely registering contact value. Berridge and Robinson 2016 show that biological wanting has exactly these properties: it is persistent (maintained across extended delay periods), prospective (generated by anticipatory cues, not consummatory contact), and independently regulated (not derived from current liking). The sensitisation dynamic also supports the SD-012 requirement that drive-state modulate wanting magnitude: the paper discusses how wanting amplification occurs under states that heighten dopamine reactivity (stress, hunger, excitement), consistent with drive-state scaling. When drive is high, wanting is amplified; when drive is low, wanting returns toward baseline.

## Limitations and caveats

This is a review paper integrating many sources of variable quality. The sensitisation model specifically applies to repeated drug exposure, which is a pathological process not directly relevant to normal z_goal formation in REE. The paper frames wanting as an unconscious motivational signal, which maps to REE's architecture naturally (z_goal is a latent, not a symbolic goal declaration), but this framing should not be taken to imply that z_goal operates outside the agent's computational decision-making -- it is a component of trajectory scoring, not a separate homunculus.
