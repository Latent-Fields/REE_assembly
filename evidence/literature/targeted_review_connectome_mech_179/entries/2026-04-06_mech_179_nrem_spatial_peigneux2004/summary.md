# Summary: Peigneux et al. 2004 — Hippocampal Reactivation during Slow Wave Sleep and Spatial Memory

**Entry:** 2026-04-06_mech_179_nrem_spatial_peigneux2004
**Claim:** MECH-179

## What the paper shows

Peigneux and colleagues from the University of Liege used fMRI to measure brain activity during both a virtual navigation task and subsequent sleep in human participants. They found that hippocampal regions activated during spatial navigation were specifically reactivated during slow-wave sleep (SWS/NREM) in the post-learning night. Critically, the amount of hippocampal activity during SWS was positively correlated with overnight improvement in spatial navigation performance the following day. Subjects who showed greater hippocampal SWS reactivation showed better spatial memory -- providing human evidence that NREM-mediated hippocampal replay is a consolidation mechanism, not merely a correlate of prior encoding.

## Why it matters for MECH-179

MECH-179 predicts that episodic, spatial, and factual prediction errors primarily demand NREM-mediated schema replay (MECH-121) for prior correction. Peigneux et al. 2004 provides the clearest direct human neuroimaging evidence for the NREM pole of this dissociation. Spatial memory -- in REE terms, representing world-state PE (incorrect spatial predictions about the environment) -- is selectively consolidated via hippocampal replay during NREM, and the amount of this replay predicts the degree of prior update (memory improvement). This is precisely the MECH-121 mechanism: hippocampal schema replay during NREM corrects world-model errors by transferring spatial regularities into neocortical representations.

## Honest evaluation of fit

The fit for the NREM-spatial pole of MECH-179 is very good. Spatial memory is the prototypical case for NREM-mediated consolidation, and this study provides human neuroimaging evidence at exactly the right level of specificity: hippocampal replay during SWS, predictive of spatial PE correction. The main limitation is that the study was not designed to test a dissociation -- it does not include an emotional memory condition within the same protocol, so it cannot directly demonstrate that emotional memory is not equally consolidated during NREM in the same subjects. MECH-179's claim requires a double dissociation, one limb of which this study provides. The other limb (REM-for-emotional, with a matched NREM-spatial comparison) requires evidence from studies like Nishida et al. 2009.

## Epistemic status

High-quality human neuroimaging study from the Maquet lab, published in Neuron -- a top-tier neuroscience journal. The within-subjects fMRI approach, linking encoding-phase hippocampal activation to sleep-phase hippocampal reactivation to next-day performance, is methodologically strong and has proven replicable. The limitation is that fMRI during sleep involves significant practical constraints (movement, staging accuracy) and the spatial memory task used (virtual navigation) is among the most strongly hippocampally-mediated tasks available, making it something of a best-case test for the hypothesis. Extrapolation to other world-state PE types (episodic, factual) is supported by the broader literature but not directly tested in this study.
