# Summary: Nishida et al. 2009 — REM Sleep, Prefrontal Theta, and Emotional Memory Consolidation

**Entry:** 2026-04-06_mech_179_rem_emotional_nishida2009
**Claim:** MECH-179

## What the paper shows

Nishida, Pearsall, Buckner, and Walker used a controlled nap paradigm with polysomnography and EEG to test whether REM sleep specifically contributes to emotional memory consolidation. Participants either took a nap or remained awake before testing memory for emotional and neutral stimuli. The nap group showed a specific consolidation benefit for emotional but not neutral memories -- a dissociation within the same protocol and subjects. Within the nap group, emotional memory consolidation correlated with the amount of REM sleep obtained and with right-dominant prefrontal theta power (4-7 Hz) during REM, but not with NREM measures. Prefrontal theta during REM is associated with limbic-prefrontal communication and affective memory integration.

## Why it matters for MECH-179

MECH-179 predicts that emotional prediction errors specifically demand REM-mediated recalibration (MECH-123). Nishida et al. 2009 provides experimental evidence for REM specificity in emotional memory consolidation using a within-subjects design -- the clearest available demonstration that the emotional-memory/REM association is not merely correlational across subjects or studies but is observed as a within-protocol dissociation (emotional but not neutral memory benefits from napping, and the benefit scales with REM). The prefrontal theta correlate is particularly relevant: theta oscillations coordinate limbic and prefrontal circuits during REM, which maps onto the MECH-123 description of REM as a recalibration mechanism integrating affective salience with policy-relevant representations.

## Honest evaluation of fit

This study provides the strongest direct experimental support for the REM-emotional pole of MECH-179, stronger than Walker & van der Helm 2009 (which is a theoretical review) because it demonstrates within-subjects selectivity. The main limitation relative to MECH-179's full claim is that the study does not include a spatial or factual memory condition in the same protocol, so the double dissociation -- REM for emotional, NREM for spatial/factual -- is not directly tested within this study. The nap paradigm also involves shorter and less naturalistic sleep than the full overnight cycle that MECH-179 invokes. The PE-type mapping requires treating the emotional stimuli used (emotionally valenced images or words) as analogous to emotional/social/identity-threatening prediction errors in the REE sense, which is conceptually appropriate but operationally distinct.

## Epistemic status

Strong experimental human study from the Walker lab (Berkeley) and Buckner lab (Harvard), published in Cerebral Cortex. The nap paradigm with polysomnography is methodologically rigorous for isolating sleep stage contributions, and the within-subjects emotional/neutral dissociation is among the cleanest demonstrations in the sleep-memory literature. The prefrontal theta finding provides an electrophysiological mechanism that connects REM physiology to the behavioral outcome. The main epistemic limitation is that this is a single study from one lab using one type of emotional material; the generalisability to diverse emotional PE types (social, identity-threatening, as well as affectively valenced episodic content) requires additional evidence. Together with Peigneux et al. 2004, this study forms the empirical core of the double dissociation that MECH-179 requires.
