# Price 2000 — Psychological and neural mechanisms of the affective dimension of pain

**Source:** *Science*, [10.1126/science.288.5472.1769](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.288.5472.1769). Via PubMed (PMID 10846154).

## What the paper does

Price reviews behavioural, anatomical, lesion, and neuroimaging evidence to argue that pain has two dissociable dimensions with distinct neural substrates: a sensory-discriminative stream (intensity, location, quality; lateral thalamus -> S1/S2) and an affective-motivational stream (unpleasantness, suffering, drive to act; medial thalamus -> ACC / AIC). He adds a crucial second stage: the "affective-motivational" signal is itself layered, with immediate pain unpleasantness generated fast by noxious input and a *secondary* affective response that emerges more slowly from appraisal of what the pain means for future wellbeing.

## Key findings relevant to the claim

- Hypnotic-suggestion studies (Rainville et al, drawn from this review's canon) demonstrate clean dissociation: subjects can retain the sensory dimension while the affective dimension is suppressed -- and ACC activation tracks unpleasantness ratings independent of rated intensity. This is the single most direct human evidence for dual-stream computation.
- Cingulotomy (for refractory pain / OCD) preserves noxious detection but drops suffering and behavioural avoidance. Lesion reverse-engineers the action-selection role of ACC specifically.
- Price explicitly distinguishes "primary" affective pain (immediate unpleasantness) from "secondary" affective pain (threat-to-self appraisal, suffering, rumination about future pain). The secondary stage is generated by prefrontal-cingulate circuits integrating the primary signal with future-outcome inference.

## How this maps onto REE (the translation)

ree-v3's SD-011 split (z_harm_s vs z_harm_a) is the correct shape: the primary sensory/affective dissociation is exactly what the dual encoder gives us. Price supports the architectural decision to keep these separate.

But Price's second stage is where this bites. Affective pain in his framework is not purely a bottom-up read of current nociception -- it also gets a top-down contribution from forecasts of future consequences ("this is going to get worse," "I can't escape this"). A consumer architecture that only reads the *current* z_harm_a and never lets trajectory-level harm forecasting update it would miss the signal that actually drives behaviour in sustained threat. This is a direct argument for at least a component of Option B (forward-rollout affective cost via E2_harm_a or similar) rather than pure Option A (read-and-add-to-trajectory-cost at current step).

The cingulotomy evidence also speaks to the *necessity* of the cingulate consumer stage. Cutting the ACC doesn't remove nociception -- it removes suffering's grip on action. ree-v3's current state, where z_harm_a is produced but not consumed, is functionally equivalent to a cingulotomy: the signal exists, it just doesn't reach behaviour.

## Limitations and caveats

This is a review, not original empirical work, and it predates the computational-decision-making framing by a decade. Price does not say anything about action-value cost functions, precision weighting, or temporal-difference learning -- those bridges have to be built by pairing this paper with Baliki (2010) and Seymour (2019). The "secondary affective" stage is also defined loosely -- a concrete computational form (what exactly gets forecast, on what horizon, by which module) isn't specified. For that, I need the Seymour precision framing.

Additionally, Price's subjective-report framing carries transfer risk for an embodied-agent architecture: "unpleasantness" is measured by self-report, but z_harm_a needs to be an operational action-selection signal. The bridge requires assuming the subjective unpleasantness report is a readout of the same signal that biases action value -- an assumption that Baliki's empirical work supports, but that Price himself does not test directly.
