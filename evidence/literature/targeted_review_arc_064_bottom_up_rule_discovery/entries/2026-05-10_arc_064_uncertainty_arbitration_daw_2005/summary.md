# Daw, Niv & Dayan 2005 -- Uncertainty-based competition between prefrontal and dorsolateral striatal systems

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/nn1560), PMID 16286932), Daw, Niv and Dayan asked the question that has structured the dual-systems-RL literature for the two decades since: if the brain has multiple action-selection systems, how does it decide which one to listen to at any given moment? Their answer is normative and elegant. Each system computes its own prediction (about expected outcome of candidate actions) with its own associated uncertainty. The arbitration mechanism deploys each system in proportion to its inverse uncertainty -- whichever controller is most confident gets dominance, and the agent's behaviour reflects whichever system thinks it knows best at the moment. This Bayesian-optimal arbitration principle explains a wide swath of empirical findings: novice behaviour is dominated by the (initially confident) deliberative system, expert behaviour shifts to the (now-confident) habit system, surprising outcomes return control to deliberation, and so on.

For MECH-312 (REE's dual-channel arbitration mechanism, soon to be three- or four-channel) this paper supplies the arbitration variable. Each rule channel -- ARC-062 top-down rule application, ARC-064 bottom-up rule discovery, ARC-065 always-on diversity, possibly a fourth model-free habit channel -- should compute its own prediction uncertainty or rule-applicability confidence. The arbitration mechanism allocates control in proportion to inverse uncertainty. This is the canonical default. Alternative arbitration variables are biologically attested -- practice length (Smith & Graybiel 2013/2016 in this entry set), instructed-context strength, metacognitive self-rating -- but the uncertainty-weighted approach has the strongest normative claim.

The synthesis-level point this paper makes is that MECH-312 cannot be a single global parameter or a binary switch. The arbitration is per-channel and continuous: each channel reports its own confidence, the arbitrator weights them. That has direct implications for ARC-064's substrate design -- the substrate must produce confidence signals that the arbitration mechanism can read, not just rule labels. A bottom-up rule discovery that produces hard categorical labels without confidence is incompatible with Daw 2005's framework; a substrate that produces labels with confidence (e.g., a softmax over rule-cluster assignment) is compatible.

The mapping caveat: Daw et al. frame the arbitration normatively, as what the brain *should* do under Bayesian-optimal computation. Whether the brain actually implements this is a separate empirical question. Lee 2014 (also in this entry set) provides the empirical confirmation that human prefrontal cortex tracks the kind of reliability signals Daw's framework predicts. There is also a non-trivial mapping question about whether ARC-062 / ARC-064 / ARC-065 map cleanly onto Daw's MB / MF / habit taxonomy. They do not; they are different along a different axis (top-down-instructed vs bottom-up-extracted vs always-on-diverse). The arbitration *principle* (uncertainty-weighting) generalises; the specific empirical mappings do not.
