# Schapiro, Turk-Browne, Botvinick & Norman 2017 -- Hippocampal CLS bi-pathway

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1098/rstb.2016.0049), PMID 27872368), Schapiro and colleagues asked a question that on its face looks awkward for the classical Complementary Learning Systems story: how can the hippocampus support both episode-specific memorisation and across-episode regularity extraction, when the canonical CLS argument was that those two demands cannot share a substrate? Their answer is anatomical. The hippocampus has two main projections from entorhinal cortex into CA1 -- a direct monosynaptic pathway (EC perforant path to CA1) and an indirect trisynaptic pathway (EC to DG to CA3 to CA1). They built a connectionist model of these subfields and exposed it to sequences of items containing temporal regularities. The trisynaptic pathway, with its sparse pattern-separated representations in DG and CA3, learned individual episodes; the monosynaptic pathway, with its denser overlapping representations, extracted regularities across episodes. The CLS tradeoff, it turns out, is not handled by hippocampus-versus-neocortex alone; a smaller version of the same tradeoff is internal to the hippocampus.

For ARC-064 this is the load-bearing R2 paper. The proposed bottom-up rule discovery pathway in REE is doing exactly what the monosynaptic route does in Schapiro's model: pooling across the trajectory record to extract behavioural regularities, rather than separating each episode for later high-fidelity recall. If we believe Schapiro's account, then bottom-up rule discovery is not a downstream readout of the episode-encoding system -- it is its own pathway, with its own write rule, sharing only an anatomical substrate. That has clean architectural consequences. The existing REE hippocampal substrate (anchor sets, MECH-269; ranked ghost-goal bank, MECH-292; awake ghost-goal probes, MECH-293) is patterned on the trisynaptic side: episode-anchored, pattern-separated. ARC-064 needs a complementary monosynaptic-analog write path that pools rather than separates.

The mapping caveat I would not want to bury: Schapiro's task is passive sequence learning, not active behavioural-trajectory clustering. Their stimuli are exposed to the model; the model has no policy generating them. REE's bottom-up rule discovery, by contrast, would be operating on the agent's own trajectory record -- a sequence whose statistics are intertwined with the behaviour generating it. The principle generalises (regularity extraction vs episode separation as architectural tradeoff) but the specific anatomical division of labour may or may not transfer. There is also a non-trivial circularity worry: if the agent's behaviour is monomodal (which Pull 1 just established is the recurring V3 failure mode), then there are no across-episode regularities in the trajectory record for the monosynaptic-analog pathway to extract. Bottom-up rule discovery has ARC-065 (behavioural diversity generation) as a non-negotiable upstream precondition.

What does failure look like? If we register ARC-064 as a separate cluster and the substrate-implementation experiment fails to recover the regularity-extraction signature -- fast acquisition of pattern statistics under conditions where the episode-separation pathway is intact -- then either (a) the ARC-064 substrate is mis-implemented, or (b) ARC-064 is not architecturally distinct from the existing E1 and hippocampal-episode pathways and should be absorbed. The Schapiro paper does not by itself decide that question for REE, but it does establish that the architecturally-distinct reading is biologically plausible and computationally tractable. That is enough to recommend PROMOTE-AS-SEPARATE-CLUSTER under R1, with a substrate that implements a regularity-pooling write rule independent of the episode-encoding substrate.

I rate this confidence 0.86. Source quality is high (Phil Trans B, Schapiro is the field's lead), mapping fidelity is strong for the architectural claim and weaker for the specific anatomical division, and transfer risk is moderate because the passive-sequence-learning paradigm is one step removed from the active-trajectory-clustering REE needs. Subsequent empirical work (Schapiro 2016 Neuropsychologia; the broader Davachi/Turk-Browne lit on hippocampal statistical learning) corroborates the basic dissociation, so the confidence is unlikely to drop on replication.
