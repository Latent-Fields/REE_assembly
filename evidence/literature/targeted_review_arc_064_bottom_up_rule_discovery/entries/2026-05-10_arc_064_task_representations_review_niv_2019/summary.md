# Niv 2019 -- Learning task-state representations

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41593-019-0470-8), PMID 31551597), Niv's Nature Neuroscience Perspective opens with a sentence that could serve as ARC-064's mission statement: 'arguably, the most difficult part of learning is deciding what to learn about.' The classical reinforcement-learning literature has spent decades on the value-learning problem -- given a state-action representation, how do we update value estimates from reward feedback? Niv's argument is that this work has been built on top of an unsolved upstream problem. Where do the states come from? How does the agent decide which features of its experience are state-defining and which are irrelevant noise? She sketches the brain's answer in two complementary parts. First, learning what to ignore -- selective attention and feature-relevance inference, with prefrontal-cortex involvement. Second, augmenting perception with inferred latent causes -- structure-learning and Bayesian latent-cause models, with OFC involvement.

For ARC-064 this is the most aligned R2 review in the entry set. ARC-064's whole reason for existing is exactly the problem Niv frames as central: the agent's trajectory record contains many features, and the bottom-up rule discovery pathway has to extract a state-taxonomy from it. Niv's framing makes the architectural separation crisp -- representation-learning is its own computational stage, distinct from the value-learning and policy-learning stages that the existing REE substrate covers. Without ARC-064, REE's representation-learning is whatever falls out of the E1 sensory predictor and E3 hippocampal pathway as a side-effect; ARC-064 names it as a deliberate mechanism with its own write rule and its own substrate.

The R3 verdict for ARC-064 is whether bottom-up rule discovery is online (continuous statistical learning during waking) or offline (sleep-replay-driven) or both. Niv 2019 is mostly silent on this; her examples are online-during-task scenarios, but she does not explicitly argue that offline consolidation is unnecessary. The Schapiro 2017 hippocampal-CLS paper is the bigger anchor for the online-arm verdict; the offline-arm needs the sleep-replay literature (Karlsson 2009, Stickgold 2013, Wilson & McNaughton 1994 — though I dropped the last from the final entry set in favour of the broader sleep-consolidation review).

The mapping caveat to be honest about: Niv presents several candidate computational implementations -- Bayesian latent-cause inference, structure-learning, attention-modulated RL -- without committing to one. ARC-064's substrate design has to make that commitment. Each implementation choice carries different inductive biases and different failure modes. The lit-pull verdict 'PROMOTE-AS-SEPARATE-CLUSTER' would not, by itself, determine which implementation flavour the substrate should take; that is a separate design call.

Confidence 0.82. Nat Neurosci Perspective venue + Niv as the field's central voice. Mapping fidelity high because the paper directly addresses ARC-064's core mechanism. Transfer risk moderate because of the unresolved implementation choice. The Niv 2019 paper is the closest existing literature has to an ARC-064 manifesto, which is both its strength as a citation and its limitation -- it does not settle the lower-level architectural questions.
