# Smith & Graybiel 2016 -- Habit formation

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.31887/DCNS.2016.18.1/ksmith), PMID 27069378), Smith and Graybiel write a Dialogues-in-Clinical-Neuroscience review that synthesises their primary-paper findings (Smith & Graybiel 2013 in this entry set, plus a decade of related work) into a broader picture of how habits form. The central message is that habit formation is not one process. It is a confluence of dissociable substrate-level changes happening across multiple brain regions and even within single regions, occurring at distinct timepoints during overtraining. The dorsolateral striatum produces action-chunking signatures fast; the infralimbic cortex stabilises chunks into habits slow; the broader basal-ganglia-cortical circuit hosts arbitration and learning machinery. Addictions and OCD, on this view, are not unitary disorders but specific dysfunctions in specific components of this network.

For the synthesis at hand, this paper buys two things. First, it strengthens the R2 verdict that ARC-064 should be a multi-component cluster rather than a single mechanism: bottom-up rule discovery has at minimum an extraction component (striatal-analog) and a stabilisation component (cortical-analog), and possibly more. Second, it strengthens the R4 verdict that MECH-312's arbitration scope cannot be a binary uncertainty-only switch: habits crystallise over training time, with arbitration tracking practice-length and the maturity of the chunked representations. Both verdicts were already supported by the primary Smith & Graybiel 2013 paper; the review supplies the conceptual frame and the broader integration.

The R3 verdict (online vs offline timing of bottom-up rule discovery) gets an implicit boost here too. The slow-stabilisation arm Smith & Graybiel describe is, in animal terms, the kind of process that benefits from sleep-replay consolidation -- the same kind of mechanism the sleep-replay literature (Karlsson 2009 awake replay; Stickgold 2013 sleep-dependent consolidation; both in this entry set) anchors. The two readings are compatible: extraction is online and fast in striatum-analog, stabilisation is offline and slow in cortex-analog, the substrate progression is multi-timescale. R3 verdict is BOTH-CHANNELS-NEEDED, with the channels mapped to substrate components rather than to extraction-vs-stabilisation distinctions.

The mapping caveat: this is a Dialogues review, secondary literature. It synthesises rather than tests; specific substrate-design implications for REE require extrapolation. The clinical applications (addiction, OCD) are intriguing for REE's MECH-312 failure-mode predictions but are not load-bearing for the cluster-registration decision.

Confidence 0.74. Dialogues in Clin Neurosci is a respectable but not top-tier venue for primary work; review papers carry less weight than primary papers in the synthesis. Mapping fidelity moderate. Transfer risk moderate. The review's main contribution is the multi-component dissociability framing; the primary evidence has already been captured by Smith & Graybiel 2013.
