# Momennejad, Russek, Cheong, Botvinick, Daw & Gershman 2017 -- Successor representation in human RL

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41562-017-0180-8), PMID 31024137), Momennejad and colleagues set up a discriminative test for whether human reinforcement-learning behaviour is best explained by model-free RL, model-based RL, or something intermediate. The intermediate candidate is the successor representation -- an algorithm that stores partially-computed values (predictions over future states under the current policy) and combines them at decision time with fresh reward estimates. The SR has a clean diagnostic signature: it should be insensitive to changes in the task's transition structure (because the stored predictions are stale until re-experience updates them) but flexible to changes in reward (because the reward estimate is freshly applied to the SR at decision time). Two behavioural studies in humans confirm exactly this dissociation. People are slow to update when transitions change but fast to update when rewards change -- the SR signature, not the MF or MB signature.

For ARC-064 this is the empirical complement to Stachenfeld 2017's theoretical reinterpretation. If the brain runs an SR-style algorithm, then ARC-064's bottom-up rule discovery has at least one well-grounded implementation candidate: extract predictive summaries from the trajectory record and apply fresh value estimates over them at action selection time. The Momennejad finding tells us this is what humans actually do; the Stachenfeld paper tells us where in the brain it might be implemented. Together they establish SR-style computation as a strong default implementation flavour for ARC-064 -- not the only flavour Niv 2019 names, but the one with both an empirical signature and a candidate substrate location.

This paper also matters for R4 (MECH-312 dual-channel arbitration scope), which is why I tagged it dual-claim. Pull 1's R4 verdict expanded MECH-312 from a 2-way (top-down rule vs bottom-up rule) to a 3-way arbitration with ARC-065 always-on diversity as the third competitor. Momennejad's data point in a related direction: human behaviour is mediated by at least three computational channels (MF, SR-intermediate, MB), and the arbitration mechanism has to allocate control across all three. The clean reading is that REE's MECH-312 arbitration scope is at least three-way and possibly four-way. The arbitration mechanism's anchor literature (Lee 2014 in this entry set; Daw 2005 also) was developed in the binary MF-vs-MB framing, but the field has moved on. MECH-312's design needs to track that.

The mapping caveat to be candid about: Momennejad's tasks are sequential-decision tasks with explicit transition structure and explicit reward feedback. REE's environment is open-ended foraging; the transition structure is implicit and continuously changing, and reward feedback is sparser and more aggregated. The SR signature -- transition-structure-insensitivity vs reward-flexibility -- is empirically robust in their setting, but the analogous test in REE has to be designed carefully. The signature is general; the specific behavioural implementation is task-dependent.
