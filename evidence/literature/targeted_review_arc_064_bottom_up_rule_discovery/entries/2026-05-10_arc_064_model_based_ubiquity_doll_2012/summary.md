# Doll, Simon & Daw 2012 -- The ubiquity of model-based reinforcement learning

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.conb.2012.08.003), PMID 22959354), Doll, Simon and Daw write a Current Opinion review that complicates the dual-systems framework even as it preserves it. The classical RL-and-the-brain story has the dorsolateral striatum and dopamine system supporting model-free temporal-difference learning, and the prefrontal cortex supporting model-based deliberation. Doll et al. survey the empirical literature post-2008 and find that signatures of model-based computation -- outcome-based action evaluation, world-model inference, expected-utility calculation -- show up throughout the supposedly model-free territory. The striatum hosts MB signatures. Dopamine reward-prediction-errors carry MB-derived components. The picture is not 'two anatomically separated systems' but 'two computations that cohabit shared substrates and can be teased apart only with carefully designed contrast tasks.'

For MECH-312 this paper cuts both ways, which is why I tagged it mixed-direction. On the one hand, it weakens any naive two-channel-with-anatomical-separation framing of MECH-312 -- if the biology is shared-substrate-with-soft-arbitration, REE's MECH-312 should not over-commit to a hard channel-switching architecture. On the other hand, it reaffirms that there ARE multiple distinguishable computations being arbitrated -- the dual-systems framework survives the empirical complications. Both readings matter for the design.

The synthesis-level lesson I want to extract is implementation-flavour rather than verdict-level. If the biological evidence is that the same substrates can host both top-down-rule-application and bottom-up-rule-extraction depending on context, then ARC-062 and ARC-064 may share a substrate in REE -- with the arbitration mechanism modulating which computation is currently dominant rather than routing to anatomically separated machinery. This aligns with Lee 2014's finding that arbitration operates via effective-connectivity modulation rather than channel-switching. MECH-312 design should consider this carefully; a strict-channel-separation implementation may be an over-engineering of a cleaner reality.

The mapping caveat is that Doll et al. is a review summarising an emerging consensus, not testing it; subsequent empirical work has nuanced the picture but not fundamentally changed the shape. The conceptual lesson (don't over-engineer channel separation) generalises; the specific substrate predictions are domain-specific.
