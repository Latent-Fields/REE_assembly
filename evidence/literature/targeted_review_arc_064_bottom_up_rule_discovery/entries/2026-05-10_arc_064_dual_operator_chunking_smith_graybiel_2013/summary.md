# Smith & Graybiel 2013 -- Dual operator view of habitual behaviour

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2013.05.038), PMID 23810540), Smith and Graybiel did the experimental heavy lifting that turns the action-chunking story from theory into causally-tested mechanism. They recorded simultaneously from rat dorsolateral striatum and infralimbic cortex while animals were overtrained on a maze task -- the kind of training regime that classically converts purposeful behaviour into habit. Two distinct dynamics emerged. The striatum acquired an action-bracketing pattern early: striatal ensembles fired at the start and end of stereotyped action sequences, marking them as compressed chunks. The infralimbic cortex did something different: it slowly developed habit-tracking activity during late overtraining, with a different timecourse and stability. To establish causality they did optogenetic disruption of infralimbic activity during overtraining and found that habits did not crystallise. Striatum extracts the chunks fast; infralimbic cortex stabilises them into habits slowly.

For ARC-064 this paper anchors the most concrete biological story for bottom-up rule discovery available in the entry set. The striatal chunking signature *is* bottom-up rule discovery in miniature: an animal's behaviour, repeatedly executed, gets compressed by the dorsolateral striatum into a chunk-with-bracket-signal, and that chunk becomes a reusable behavioural primitive. ARC-064 in REE is the same kind of operation but at a level higher in the abstraction hierarchy -- not chunking individual action sequences but extracting behavioural rules across episodes. The principle generalises naturally; the specific signature does not.

The dual-operator architecture also matters for the synthesis-level cluster-shape decision. Smith & Graybiel's striatum-extracts-chunks-fast / cortex-stabilises-them-slow story maps cleanly onto a two-stage REE architecture: ARC-064's bottom-up extraction substrate (striatal-analog) produces candidate rules quickly; a downstream consolidation substrate (cortical-analog, possibly ARC-062's gated-policy machinery or a separate cortical infralimbic-analog) stabilises them into habits over time. The R3 verdict on online-vs-offline gets a third reading from this paper: extraction can be online and fast in one substrate, while consolidation can be slow (and presumably benefits from offline replay) in another. Both/and, not either/or.

For MECH-312 the implication is also non-trivial. Smith & Graybiel show that habit takeover during overtraining is driven by practice-length, not just uncertainty -- the longer the animal does the same thing, the more the striatal chunks dominate behaviour. That suggests MECH-312's arbitration variable cannot be uncertainty-only (Daw 2005); it has to also track repetition-count or time-on-task. This is consistent with the broader habit-RL literature (Wood & Neal 2007, Smith & Graybiel 2016) and supports a multi-variable arbitration scope.

The mapping caveat: the chunking signature Smith & Graybiel record is at the action-sequence level, not the rule level. Extending it to ARC-064's behavioural-rule extraction is a conceptual scaling-up. The principle (substrate compresses repeated patterns into reusable units, with separate stabilisation machinery) is robust; the specific signature (start-end-bracketing) may not have a direct REE analog.
