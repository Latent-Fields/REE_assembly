# Theves et al. 2019 -- The Hippocampus Encodes Distances in Multidimensional Feature Space

**Source:** Theves S, Fernandez G, Doeller CF. *Current Biology* 29(7):1226-1231.e3, 2019. DOI: 10.1016/j.cub.2019.02.035

**Claim tested:** MECH-155 (spatial navigation machinery is a specific instance of general associative indexing operating over different latent domains)

---

## What the paper did

Theves and colleagues asked whether the hippocampus encodes not just the *direction* of movement through abstract space (as Constantinescu 2016 had shown for the entorhinal grid code) but the *metric distance* between positions -- the property that makes a map a map rather than merely a compass. Participants learned a novel concept defined by two continuous visual feature dimensions (properties of butterfly morphology). After learning, they underwent fMRI while viewing stimulus pairs drawn from different positions in this 2D feature space. The key manipulation was whether the hippocampal BOLD signal tracked proximity in abstract feature space, assessed through multivoxel pattern similarity and fMRI adaptation.

## Key findings relevant to MECH-155

Both measures converged: after concept acquisition, hippocampal multivoxel patterns discriminated positions in the abstract feature space according to their Euclidean distance, and univariate hippocampal responses were adapted (reduced) for items that were closer together in the abstract space -- precisely the signature observed for physical spatial proximity. Critically, these distance-encoding effects were learning-dependent: they were absent before participants had formed the concept, which rules out raw perceptual similarity as a confound and implicates structured relational learning. The hippocampus was responding to the learned relational geometry of the feature space.

## REE translation

The Behrens 2018 review and Constantinescu 2016 established that the hippocampal-entorhinal system applies grid-like and place-like coding to abstract domains. Theves et al. 2019 closes a critical gap: they show that the *distance metric* property transfers to abstract feature space. For MECH-155, this matters because an indexing substrate that only encodes direction or categorical membership is not sufficient to support planning rollout, concept traversal, and working memory ordering. A proper general-purpose associative index must compute distances in order to select among candidate states by proximity. The finding that distance encoding emerges with learning is also consistent with the REE architecture, in which the indexed manifold is a trained latent space: the hippocampal geometry is structured by experience, not pre-formed for all possible abstract domains.

## Limitations and caveats

The abstract feature space used is still perceptually grounded -- the dimensions are visual stimulus properties, not amodal symbolic relations. Whether the same mechanism operates over fully abstract domains (planned action sequences, social hierarchical positions) is not tested. The fMRI methodology cannot resolve which cell types are responsible: the same BOLD pattern is consistent with either place-cell-like single-unit tiling of the abstract space or a population-level similarity structure that happens to be distance-graded. The learning dependence is informative but also raises the question of whether this is a specific correlate of concept formation rather than a general-purpose property.

## Confidence reasoning

This paper provides a meaningful increment on existing MECH-155 evidence by extending the abstract-space finding from directional tuning to distance-metric structure. The Current Biology venue and Doeller group's independent tradition give reasonable credibility. The mapping to MECH-155 is direct but transfer risk is moderate: one perceptually grounded domain, fMRI-only. Confidence 0.78 reflects genuine support that does not fully eliminate perceptual-grounding or mechanism-specificity concerns.
