# Kunimatsu et al. (2019) -- MRI Findings in Posttraumatic Stress Disorder

## What the paper did

Kunimatsu and colleagues from the University of Tokyo reviewed the structural and functional MRI literature on PTSD, covering voxel-based morphometry, diffusion tensor imaging, resting-state fMRI, and task-based fMRI. The review synthesises findings across dozens of studies to characterise the reproducible PTSD neural phenotype, identify which findings are most consistent across the literature, and situate the imaging results within theoretical frameworks of PTSD pathophysiology.

## Key findings relevant to MECH-094

The review identifies several highly reproducible imaging findings that are directly relevant to MECH-094's tag-loss mechanism.

Prefrontal hypoactivation: Medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC) and dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (dlPFC) show reduced activation in PTSD, both at rest and during fear-related tasks. This is the region identified by Dijkstra et al. as central to reality monitoring. Its hypoactivation in PTSD corresponds precisely to failure of the circuitry that would otherwise maintain the hypothesis tag and route internal signals away from the residue channel.

Hippocampal abnormalities: Hippocampal volume reduction and functional abnormalities in contextual processing are among the most replicated PTSD findings. The hippocampus is responsible for binding memories to their spatiotemporal context -- the "when" and "where" that would allow a traumatic memory to be recognised as past rather than present. Its compromise in PTSD is the structural correlate of the contextual processing failure described in Schwabe (2024) and Desmedt (2021).

Amygdala hyperactivation: Amygdala overactivity is the affective correlate: the harm signal fires without adequate contextual constraint. This is the output of tag loss -- what the system produces when the prefrontal/hippocampal reality-monitoring circuit fails.

The triad (PFC down, hippocampus impaired, amygdala up) is described in the review as reflecting "disequilibrium among functional brain networks": the salience and affective networks dominate because the contextual/autobiographical networks fail to constrain them.

## Translation to MECH-094

MECH-094 proposes that tag loss is the PTSD mechanism: when the hypothesis tag is lost during a replay event, the post-commit channel opens and residue accumulates from simulated content. The review's three-component phenotype maps onto three components of this failure mode.

The prefrontal hypoactivation corresponds to loss of the tag-maintenance circuit. E3 in REE maintains the hypothesis tag as part of its commitment-state; PFC in the brain maintains the reality/simulation distinction. When PFC activates less, the tag becomes less stable, and simulation content bleeds into the committed channel.

The hippocampal abnormalities correspond to loss of the contextual binding that would allow the system to identify the replay as "past event" rather than "current situation." Without this contextual anchor, a replayed traumatic event is processed as if it were occurring now -- which is the subjective experience of the PTSD flashback.

The amygdala hyperactivation is the output: the harm stream activates strongly because neither the tag (PFC) nor the context (hippocampus) is constraining its expression. In REE terms, this is residue being written from replay content because the tag has been lost.

This three-component account of PTSD, grounded in converging structural and functional MRI evidence, provides the strongest clinical validation of MECH-094's tag-loss failure mode: it is not just a theoretical pathology but the recognised neural phenotype of a major psychiatric disorder.

## Limitations and caveats

The MRI findings describe correlates of PTSD, not causes. The directionality is unclear: does PFC hypoactivation cause tag loss, or does repeated flashback experience (caused by some other mechanism) downregulate PFC over time? The tag-loss interpretation is one coherent account of the data but competes with fear-extinction failure (the most prominent competing account) and other models.

Additionally, PTSD is a heterogeneous disorder with variable symptom profiles; the neural phenotype described is an aggregate that may not characterise all PTSD subtypes. The dissociative subtype of PTSD shows a different pattern (PFC overactivation as a defensive mechanism), which would not fit the simple tag-loss account.

## Confidence reasoning

Solid review from a credible group (University of Tokyo) in a major radiology journal. The three-component phenotype is well-established across the literature. The mapping to MECH-094 requires a specific interpretive lens (tag-loss) rather than being the review's own conclusion. Overall confidence 0.65.
