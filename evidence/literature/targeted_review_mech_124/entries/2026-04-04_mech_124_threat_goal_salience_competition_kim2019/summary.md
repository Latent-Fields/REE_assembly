# Threat Suppresses Goal Salience: Online Evidence for the MECH-124 Competition Precondition

**Kim, A.J. & Anderson, B.A. (2019). Threat reduces value-driven but not salience-driven attentional capture. *Emotion*, 20(5), 874-889. DOI: 10.1037/emo0000599**

---

## What the paper did

Kim and Anderson ran a series of controlled human behavioural experiments to test whether threat processing (potential electric shock) selectively interferes with value-based attentional guidance. Participants first learned to associate a colour with reward through a training phase, establishing value-driven attentional capture for that colour. They then performed a visual search task under either threat or no-threat conditions. Critically, the manipulation dissociated value-driven capture (attention drawn by reward-associated stimuli) from salience-driven capture (attention drawn by physically salient stimuli, i.e., colour singletons). The prediction was that if threat and value processing compete for shared resources, threat should suppress value-driven but not salience-driven capture.

## Key findings relevant to MECH-124

The results confirmed competitive suppression: under threat, value-driven attentional capture was substantially reduced, while salience-driven capture was unaffected or increased. The authors concluded that threat processing "selectively interferes with value-based but not salience-based attentional priority, consistent with a competitive relationship between value-based and threat-based information processing."

This finding is directly relevant to the precondition MECH-124 requires. For the consolidation loop to develop, harm salience must not simply co-exist with goal salience -- it must suppress it. Kim & Anderson provide the behavioural evidence that this competition is real: threat does not simply add a second attentional signal; it actively reduces the influence of goal-related signals. An agent whose residue field is harm-dominant will show reduced z_goal-directed behaviour even before any offline consolidation dynamics have operated.

## Translation to REE

MECH-124's V3 implication is precisely about whether z_goal salience is competitive with z_harm salience during online operation. The EXQ-085 series found goal_vs_harm_ratio values above 0.3 (adequate, not the bottleneck), but this is a ratio of mean salience values rather than a test of competitive suppression under high harm conditions. Kim & Anderson suggest a more demanding test: does z_goal-directed navigation degrade specifically when harm encounters are dense? If high harm salience selectively suppresses z_goal guidance while leaving z_harm prediction intact, this would constitute early MECH-124 risk in V3 data.

The paper also establishes a useful dissociation for REE: value-driven capture (goal salience) and salience-driven capture (physically prominent stimuli) are separated. In REE terms, this might map onto the distinction between z_goal as a learned goal representation (analogous to value) and z_harm as a persistent salient signal (analogous to the salience singleton). Threat suppresses the value-based system but not the salience-based one -- which is precisely what MECH-124 predicts for the offline phase.

## Limitations and caveats

The most significant limitation for MECH-124 is that this is a waking, online attentional study. MECH-124 is specifically about offline consolidation dynamics -- the claim that harm-trace dominated replay progressively amplifies harm predictions over successive sleep cycles. Kim & Anderson establish that threat and goal salience compete during waking processing, but whether this competition extends to replay scheduling during offline phases requires separate evidence (partially provided by Joo & Frank and Pace-Schott et al.). The transfer requires two steps: (1) from online attention to offline replay, and (2) from acute laboratory shock threat to chronic harm-trace accumulation in a training regime. Neither is validated by this paper.

Additionally, the laboratory threat manipulation (shock anticipation) produces a specific acute state that may not model chronic harm-trace dominance in the way MECH-124 describes. Acute threat may engage different neural circuits than chronic harm prediction dominance.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is 0.71: solid empirical paper with multiple replications, directly establishing threat-goal salience competition as a real phenomenon. Mapping fidelity is moderate because the study is online and attentional, not offline and consolidative. Transfer risk is the highest of the four entries in this review. The paper is best understood as providing the motivational competition foundation that MECH-124's consolidation loop requires -- evidence that the precondition (harm salience actively suppressing goal salience, not merely co-existing with it) has biological reality.
