# Sleep and Fear Extinction in PTSD: Consolidation Imbalance as Clinical Ground Truth

**Pace-Schott, Seo & Bottary (2023). The influence of sleep on fear extinction in trauma-related disorders. *Neurobiology of Stress*, 22, 100500. DOI: 10.1016/j.ynstr.2022.100500**

---

## What the paper did

Pace-Schott and colleagues wrote a mechanistic review of the bidirectional relationship between sleep and fear extinction in PTSD and related trauma disorders. The paper synthesises both human clinical studies and animal pre-clinical work to build a model of how sleep disruption specifically impairs extinction consolidation -- the process by which learned safety signals come to suppress fear responses -- while leaving conditioned fear traces largely intact. The review covers REM physiology, the hippocampal-prefrontal-amygdala consolidation circuit, the role of norepinephrine dysregulation in disrupting REM architecture, and the resulting self-amplifying loop: unextinguished fear generates hyperarousal, which fragments REM, which prevents extinction consolidation, which perpetuates fear trace dominance.

## Key findings relevant to MECH-124

The central finding directly relevant to MECH-124 is that sleep consolidation is not neutral -- it is content-weighted by what is salient in the pre-sleep period. When the emotional landscape is dominated by fear traces (as after trauma), consolidation selectively fails to preserve extinction memories while fear memories persist and strengthen. The paper documents that individuals with PTSD show deficient recall of extinction memories compared to controls -- this is not simply a retrieval failure but reflects a consolidation asymmetry established during sleep. The feedback mechanism described (fear -> poor sleep -> failed extinction consolidation -> stronger fear) is structurally the self-amplifying loop that MECH-124 predicts for a REE agent whose residue field is harm-dominated at the start of the offline phase.

## Translation to REE

MECH-124 proposes that if z_harm salience dominates the residue field, the MECH-121 SWR replay system will replay harm traces disproportionately, and normal consolidation dynamics will selectively amplify whatever dominates replay content. Pace-Schott et al. provide the clinical ground truth that this mechanism is not speculative: it operates in humans, is well-documented in the PTSD literature, and produces exactly the progressive option-space contraction that MECH-124 predicts. The key structural insight is that imbalanced consolidation input (fear-dominant) produces imbalanced consolidation output (fear amplified, extinction weakened) through the ordinary operation of salience-weighted plasticity, not through any additional mechanism. For REE, the implication is that ensuring z_goal salience is competitive with z_harm salience before the offline phase (a V3-measurable condition) is a necessary precondition for a balanced V4 consolidation outcome.

## Limitations and caveats

The paper addresses clinical PTSD, where the consolidation imbalance follows an acute traumatic event and is compounded by HPA axis dysregulation, elevated norepinephrine, hippocampal volume reductions, and chronic REM disruption. None of these factors are present in the REE V3 training regime. The MECH-124 failure mode is predicted to develop gradually during normal operation -- a gradual drift rather than a trauma-induced acute shift. This makes the mapping structural rather than mechanistic detail-for-detail: the claim is that salience-weighted consolidation produces asymmetric outcomes regardless of what causes the initial salience imbalance. This is a reasonable translation but carries moderate transfer risk.
