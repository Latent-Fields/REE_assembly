# Olafsdottir, Bush, Barry 2018 — Role of hippocampal replay in memory and planning (MECH-267)

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cub.2017.10.073)), Olafsdottir, Bush, and Barry review the hippocampal replay literature with an emphasis on functional multiplicity. The review covers methodologies for detecting replay and surveys evidence that replay supports multiple distinct functions — systems memory consolidation, recall and spatial working memory, navigational planning, and reinforcement learning. The authors flag apparent inconsistencies in existing primary data about which of these functions replay actually implements, and they propose that replay's function changes dynamically according to task demands and arousal state.

For MECH-267 this is the closest thing in the literature to an explicit architectural statement of the mode-conditioning claim. The review does not use the term "operating mode", but its framing — replay function varies with task demand and arousal state — is structurally identical to MECH-267's proposal that propose_trajectories should condition on operating_mode in addition to world_state. What the review adds is biological plausibility: a synthesis of primary data across labs concluding that the inconsistencies can only be resolved if replay content and function are context-dependent. This is the same conclusion MECH-267 reaches from the architectural side.

The mapping is strong at the architectural-principle level. Where it is looser is at REE's particular four-mode taxonomy (external_task, internal_planning, internal_replay, offline_consolidation). Olafsdottir et al. do not carve the mode space this way; their contexts are more a continuum (task engagement vs rest; high arousal vs quiescent). REE's four-mode categorical decomposition is a specific architectural commitment that the review does not validate. The correct read is that Olafsdottir et al. support mode-conditioning as a principle while leaving the specific mode structure open.

Several observations from the review are worth recording. First, replay during task engagement (awake SWRs) has different content than replay during quiescence — awake replay is more likely to carry goal-directed trajectories (Pfeiffer & Foster style), while quiescent replay more often recapitulates recent experience. This maps onto MECH-267's distinction between internal_planning (generative, hypothesis-tagged) and internal_replay (recall of past-committed successful trajectories). Second, there is evidence that replay is modulated by reward state and by recent experience — both of which are natural "task demand" signals in the review's framing and operating-mode-relevant signals in REE's.

The failure signature I record concerns the internal_planning and internal_replay modes specifically. Olafsdottir et al. emphasise that replay consistently recapitulates recent experience in quiescent contexts. If REE's internal_planning mode is expected to generate trajectories that are counterfactual (not observed in recent experience), the biological analogue is closer to forward-looking preplay than to reverse replay of past sequences. This means internal_planning in REE may be better grounded in the Pfeiffer & Foster preplay result than in the broader replay literature Olafsdottir et al. review.

Transfer risk is moderate because the review is rodent-spatial-focused while REE's application is to more abstract trajectory proposals across multiple operating modes. This is a supporting entry for MECH-267's architectural commitment; the specific four-mode decomposition will need additional validation.
