# Lange et al. (2021): A confirmation bias in perceptual decision-making due to hierarchical approximate inference

**Entry ID:** 2026-04-06_mech_123_hierarchical_primacy_lange2021
**Claim:** MECH-123 (REM recalibrates precision priors after content consolidation)
**Evidence direction:** supports | Confidence: 0.72

## What the paper argues

Lange and colleagues demonstrate that confirmation bias -- the tendency for early evidence to disproportionately anchor subsequent inference -- is not a cognitive quirk imposed on inference from outside but a structural consequence of hierarchical approximate inference itself. The mechanism they identify is the feedback loop between categorical beliefs (what is present in the stimulus) and sensory representations: sensory areas do not simply transduce evidence; they represent evidence in a space shaped by top-down categorical predictions fed back from decision areas.

In exact Bayesian inference, this feedback would be perfectly corrected for -- the decision area would "divide out" the influence of its own prior on sensory representations before computing the update. In approximate inference, this correction cannot be performed exactly. The result is that early evidence enters the update loop multiple times: it shapes the categorical belief, which modifies the top-down signal, which then affects how subsequent evidence is represented. Early frames in their task carry disproportionate weight in the final decision -- a primacy effect that is systematic, directional, and predictable from the task statistics (specifically, from how much categorical information individual frames carry).

## The structural mechanism and its generality

The key insight is that this primacy effect is not an artefact of their particular task or stimulus. It follows from the architecture of hierarchical inference itself: any system in which top-down beliefs feed back to influence how evidence is represented will produce this self-reinforcing anchoring, to the degree that approximate inference cannot fully correct for the feedback. The authors verify this experimentally by showing that temporal bias shifts from primacy to recency when task statistics change, in the direction predicted by their model.

This makes the result structurally general. It applies wherever (a) inference is hierarchical, (b) top-down beliefs influence how evidence is represented, and (c) inference is approximate rather than exact. These are properties of biological hierarchical inference more broadly, not specific to visual psychophysics.

## Relevance to MECH-123: why stale priors anchor the new episode

MECH-123's failure mode 4 claims that entering a new episode with precision priors calibrated to the prior episode produces systematic over- or under-weighting of early evidence in the new context. Lange et al. establish the structural mechanism by which this happens. If an agent begins episode N+1 with priors (including precision priors) from episode N, those priors immediately begin generating top-down predictions. The first evidence encountered in N+1 arrives into a hierarchical system that is already engaged in this top-down prediction. The prediction errors from early evidence are therefore processed against N's prior, which feeds back to affect how subsequent evidence is represented.

Because approximate inference cannot perfectly correct for this, early evidence in N+1 "enters the update multiple times" -- anchoring the context estimate in a direction determined by N's priors. This is exactly the failure mode MECH-123 describes: early evidence from the new context is systematically weighted by the wrong precision parameters, and because early evidence shapes the top-down feedback that affects how all subsequent evidence is represented, the miscalibration propagates through the episode.

## The accumulation argument

MECH-123 also claims that the failure accumulates: each night without REM produces a slightly more miscalibrated prior for the next day's attributions. Lange et al.'s framework supports this accumulation argument: if the top-down feedback from stale priors shapes how evidence in N+1 is represented, then the posterior beliefs formed at the end of N+1 are themselves distorted. These distorted posteriors become the priors for N+2. Without reset (recalibration), each episode's distortions compound.

## Caveats

The paper studies within-episode primacy effects on the order of seconds (sequential frames in a psychophysics trial). The cross-episode timescale of MECH-123 (hours to days, separated by sleep cycles) is not studied. Whether the same feedback mechanism operates across this longer timescale -- specifically, whether top-down predictions generated by prior-episode priors persist into the next waking episode and shape initial evidence processing -- is a plausible but unverified extension. The categorical priors in their model (which stimulus category is present) also differ from the affective and precision parameters (commitment_threshold, precision_ema_alpha, harm_weight) that MECH-123 specifically targets. These differences mean the paper provides structural support for the anchoring mechanism but does not directly test the cross-episode or sleep-related claims.
