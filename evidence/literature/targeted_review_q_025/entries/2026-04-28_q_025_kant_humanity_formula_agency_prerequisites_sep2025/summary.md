# SEP: Kant's Moral Philosophy -- the Formula of Humanity as bracket on Q-025

## What the entry says

The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry on Kant's moral philosophy
documents the Formula of Humanity, the second formulation of the Categorical
Imperative: act so as to treat humanity, whether in your own person or in
that of another, always at the same time as an end and never merely as a
means. From this single structural principle Kant derives a list of acts
that are categorically forbidden because they contradict the prerequisites
of rational agency itself.

The canonical Kantian forbidden list, drawn from the Groundwork and
Metaphysics of Morals, includes: suicide (which destroys the conditions of
one's own agency); lying promises (which exploit the practice of promising
and treat the promisee's rational nature as irrelevant to how they are
manipulated); deception in general (which denies others the information
needed for rational self-direction); and treating others as mere means
(using their rational capacities without their informed consent).

Crucially, Kant's grounding is structural. An act is forbidden when it
would undermine the conditions necessary for exercising one's own rational
will or respecting another's capacity for autonomous choice. This is not
"these acts feel bad" but "these acts are incompatible with the existence
of the agency that performs them."

## Why this matters for Q-025

Q-025 asks: what acts are strictly forbidden by INV-025-029, independent of
context or consequences? The Kantian Formula of Humanity is the closest
extant philosophical machine for answering questions of exactly this shape.
The structural argument -- "an act that destroys the prerequisites of
agency is forbidden by those very prerequisites" -- is the same shape REE
needs.

The mapping onto REE's axioms is partial but real. INV-026 (self as
operational prerequisite for responsible agency) is essentially a
generalisation of Kant's prohibition on acts that undermine one's own
rational agency. INV-028 (shared-world ethics, modelling others as
co-inhabitants of the same consequence space) is essentially a
generalisation of the prohibition on treating others as mere means. So a
first-cut answer to Q-025, anchored on these two axioms alone, would
include something like: do not destroy the conditions of your own
continued model-building; do not act on other minds without modelling them
as co-inhabitants of the consequence space.

## Where the mapping is honest about its limits

Kant's list is shaped by his time and by his choice to ground everything in
rational will alone. REE's axioms are wider. INV-025 (irreducible
uncertainty) introduces an epistemic constraint Kant does not have a clean
analogue for -- it might forbid acts that pretend to certainty one cannot
have, or acts that foreclose the agent's ability to correct error. INV-027
(external world as structural prerequisite) has no Kantian analogue -- it
might forbid acts that destroy the shared physical substrate, foreclose
future minds, or render the world unrepresentable. INV-029 (love as
long-horizon care-investment) extends INV-028: not just refraining from
instrumentalising others, but actively maintaining the conditions of their
long-horizon flourishing.

So the Kantian bracket gives part of the answer to Q-025 -- the part that
falls out of INV-026 and INV-028 -- and tells us what shape the rest of
the answer should take. It does not give the full list, and the full list
needs derivation from REE's axioms directly.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is held at 0.48 -- right at the threshold of "weak/ambiguous."
The reasoning: source quality is high (authoritative encyclopedia, recently
revised October 2025), and the structural analogue is genuinely the
closest match in standard philosophy. But mapping_fidelity is moderate
because Kant's axiomatic basis is narrower than REE's, and Kant's specific
list does not exhaust what INV-025-029 would derive. The honest reading is
that this entry partially answers Q-025 (for the INV-026 and INV-028
components) and brackets the rest. For a low-priority open question with
genuinely thin direct literature, that is a useful but not overclaimed
contribution.
