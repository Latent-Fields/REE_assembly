# Summary: Michelet et al. (2007) — Commitment and Error Evaluation in the Rostral Cingulate Motor Area

**Source:** Michelet T, Bioulac B, Guehl D, Escola L, Burbaud P. Impact of Commitment on Performance Evaluation in the Rostral Cingulate Motor Area. *Journal of Neuroscience*, 27(28), 7482-7489.

## The Question They Were Asking

The anterior cingulate cortex is widely thought to monitor errors and signal the need for behavioral adjustment. But performance monitoring is presumably not a context-free computation: whether an error matters depends on whether the agent was committed to the action that produced it. Michelet and colleagues asked whether the rostral cingulate motor area (CMAr) — a region of the ACC with dense connections to motor cortex and the basal ganglia — modulates its error-evaluation activity according to the agent's commitment state. They trained macaques on a Stroop-like task requiring cognitive control and recorded single-unit activity in CMAr across conditions that varied both performance outcome and the animal's apparent behavioral engagement.

## What They Found

CMAr neurons responded to both positive feedback (success) and negative feedback (error), with stronger responses to errors — consistent with the error-monitoring account of ACC function. But the key finding was the modulation by commitment: the magnitude of the performance-evaluation signal in CMAr was contingent on the subject's task commitment. When the animal was disengaged or had effectively abandoned the task (low commitment), outcome signals in CMAr were weaker or qualitatively different from the signals seen during committed engagement. CMAr constitutes, in the authors' framing, a performance-evaluation system that is conditioned on the subject's investment in the action — it is not a passive error detector but an error evaluator whose gain is set by commitment state.

## Why This Matters for MECH-061

This paper provides the most direct empirical support in the current review set for the error-routing half of MECH-061. MECH-061 does not merely claim that a commit boundary exists; it claims that crossing the boundary *reclassifies* error routing — that what the system does with errors changes as a function of commitment state. Michelet et al. show exactly this, at the level of single neurons in a commitment-relevant area: commitment modulates the neural response to outcome information. In REE architectural terms, CMAr is a plausible biological homologue of the post-commit harm-evaluation circuit, and the commitment-contingent modulation of its error responses is a direct demonstration of commitment-gated error routing.

What is especially useful here is that the paper speaks to *function* rather than *timing*. Most other entries in this review (Schultze-Kraft, Thura & Cisek, Aron & Poldrack) characterize the boundary in terms of when it occurs or how it is enforced. Michelet et al. characterize what changes on the other side: error signals are processed differently. That is the computational claim MECH-061 is making, and this paper provides the closest empirical grounding for it.

## Honest Uncertainty

The commitment manipulation in the paper is based on inferred behavioral engagement rather than trial-level discrete commitment events. The monkeys' commitment was indexed from behavioral patterns (task engagement vs. apparent disengagement) rather than from a controlled event that demarcated "committed" vs. "deliberating" on a per-trial basis. This means the modulation Michelet et al. describe is a tonic effect of commitment state, not a phasic consequence of crossing a commitment boundary within a single trial. MECH-061 posits a specific moment at which a token switches error routing; the paper demonstrates state-level modulation of error routing, which is consistent but not identical to the token hypothesis.

There is also a question about what is carrying the commitment signal to CMAr. The paper does not identify the input that sets CMAr's gain as a function of commitment — it could be prefrontal, thalamic, dopaminergic, or some combination. For MECH-061 to be fully supported, we would want to know not just that CMAr changes its behavior post-commitment, but what neural event conveys the commitment information that produces the change. That question remains open, and its answer would specify the token more precisely.
