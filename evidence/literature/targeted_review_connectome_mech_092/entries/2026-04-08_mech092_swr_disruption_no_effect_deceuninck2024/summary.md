# Deceuninck & Kloosterman 2024 -- Disruption of Awake Sharp-Wave Ripples Does Not Affect Memorization

**Source:** Deceuninck L, Kloosterman F. *Disruption of awake sharp-wave ripples does not affect memorization of locations in repeated-acquisition spatial memory tasks.* eLife 13:e84004, 2024. DOI: 10.7554/eLife.84004

## What was done

Deceuninck and Kloosterman performed a causal disruption experiment methodologically comparable to Jadhav et al. 2012, using online detection of hippocampal sharp-wave ripples and electrical stimulation of the ventral hippocampal commissure to terminate SWR events within milliseconds. The critical difference lay in the task design: instead of an incremental spatial learning paradigm (Jadhav's W-track alternation learned over days), rats performed repeated-acquisition spatial memory tasks in which they needed to memorize novel locations within each trial or session. Three task variants were used, including a non-match-to-sample task on an eight-arm radial maze where rats memorized four visited arms in an instruction phase and then navigated to the four unvisited arms in a test phase. The question was whether awake SWR replay is necessary for this kind of rapid, within-session spatial memorization.

## Key findings

Across all three task variants, SWR disruption produced no detectable behavioral deficit. Rats performed equally well whether their awake SWRs were disrupted, delayed, or left intact. This null result was robust across conditions and adequately powered. The authors interpret this as evidence that the functional impact of awake SWR replay on memory and behavior is "long-term and cumulative over time" rather than immediate -- that is, awake replay does not support the short-term buffering or encoding of new spatial information within a trial, but rather contributes to the gradual consolidation of spatial knowledge across extended experience.

## Mapping to MECH-092

This paper presents a genuine challenge for one reading of MECH-092, and a clarifying constraint for another. If MECH-092's quiescent E3 heartbeat replay is understood as providing immediate, within-episode consolidation of viability map updates -- each replay event meaningfully strengthening the map representation -- then the Deceuninck & Kloosterman null result is uncomfortable. SWR disruption over the timescale of a single task session had no measurable effect on spatial memorization. This suggests that individual awake replay events do not produce detectable consolidation effects at short timescales.

However, if MECH-092's consolidation is understood as cumulative and gradual -- many quiescent replay episodes across extended experience collectively refining the viability map -- then the null result is actually informative rather than damaging. The viability map would not be built by any single replay event but by the statistical accumulation of many replay episodes, each contributing a small increment of consolidation. This interpretation aligns with Jadhav 2012's finding that SWR disruption impaired learning over days (cumulative timescale) while leaving within-trial performance on well-learned components intact.

The deeper question this raises for MECH-092 is one of timescale. The "micro-DMN" framing implies that quiescent replay operates on very short timescales (seconds between actions within an episode). If each micro-DMN replay event contributes only a tiny increment of consolidation, the mechanism may be real but its per-event contribution undetectably small. The consolidation function would emerge only over many such events -- which is still a consolidation function, but one whose behavioral signature requires extended observation to detect.

## Limitations

The task difference between this study and Jadhav 2012 is substantial and may explain the discrepant results without requiring a timescale argument. Repeated-acquisition tasks (memorize new locations each trial) may rely more heavily on working memory buffers that do not depend on hippocampal replay, whereas Jadhav's incremental learning task required building and updating a stable spatial rule over days -- a process more dependent on consolidation mechanisms. If so, the null result may simply reflect task-inappropriate testing rather than a genuine constraint on awake SWR function.

The study also cannot determine whether the disrupted SWRs would have contributed to long-term retention of the memorized locations (they only tested immediate performance). It remains possible that SWR disruption would impair 24-hour retention of the same information -- a prediction that would support the cumulative consolidation interpretation.

## Confidence reasoning

Source quality is good (eLife, rigorous methods, adequate controls and power). Mapping fidelity is moderate -- the null result provides a genuine constraint on MECH-092's consolidation timescale claims but does not refute the mechanism. The task type confound makes direct comparison with Jadhav 2012 difficult. Transfer risk is elevated because the timescale implications are not fully resolved. Overall confidence: 0.70, rated as "mixed" evidence direction because the paper neither clearly supports nor clearly weakens MECH-092 but rather constrains and refines its scope.
