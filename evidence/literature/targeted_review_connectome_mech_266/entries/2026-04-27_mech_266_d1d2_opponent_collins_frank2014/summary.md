# Collins & Frank 2014 — OPAL: Opponent actor learning, D1/D2 striatal asymmetry, and bistable choice incentive

According to PubMed, Collins & Frank (2014, [Psychological Review 121:337-66](https://doi.org/10.1037/a0037015)) develop the OPAL model: opponent actor learning, formalising the dynamics of striatal direct (D1) and indirect (D2) pathways as a dual-actor reinforcement learning system. The architectural core of OPAL is that D1-pathway medium spiny neurons (MSNs) and D2-pathway MSNs respond *oppositely* to tonic dopamine: high tonic DA drives D1-MSN activity (the Go pathway) and suppresses D2-MSN activity (the NoGo pathway), with phasic DA bursts and dips further biasing positive vs negative learning asymmetrically. The model reproduces a wide range of behavioural and neural findings: PD on/off-medication switching effects, individual differences in choice incentive, reversal-learning asymmetries, and the canonical "approach the rewarded, avoid the punished" learning bias.

This paper also appears in the SD-034 review (under the closure-operator question — D1/D2 opponent dynamics as the substrate for release-and-No-Go). The framing here is different: MECH-266 asks whether the operating-mode register has asymmetric enter and exit thresholds, and OPAL is the canonical formal model showing how a dual-pathway striatal architecture produces precisely the kind of asymmetric, bistable dynamics MECH-266 commits to.

The architectural support is straightforward at the high level. MECH-266 specifies that enter-threshold for a given operating mode is not the same as exit-threshold. The natural biological grounding is the D1/D2 opponent pathway architecture: Go-pathway and NoGo-pathway represent the two ends of the mode-state axis, and the same modulating DA level produces *opposite* effects on the two pathways. This intrinsic asymmetry is exactly what produces hysteretic dynamics — the system's tendency to stay in a current mode after the entering condition has passed is the cognitive-control-level analogue of the dual-pathway opponent dynamics OPAL formalises. A SalienceCoordinator with separate enter and exit thresholds, biased by tonic DA, inherits this asymmetry directly from the architecture.

I want to flag the mapping caveat carefully because it is the binding constraint on this entry's confidence. OPAL operates at the **RL-actor level** — action representations during learning. The asymmetry in OPAL is between Go and NoGo pathways *for a single action*, with each action having its own D1-actor and D2-actor representation that compete for choice. MECH-266 operates at a different decomposition: enter and exit thresholds on a *single operating mode*, with the operating_mode register globally affecting which substrates can update. These are mathematically distinct decompositions even if they are computationally related (both produce bistable dynamics under DA modulation, both involve dual-pathway opponent architecture, both predict the same kind of "stay where you are" behavioural signature).

The transfer is therefore by **analogy across levels of description**: the D1/D2 architecture supplies the *kind* of mechanism MECH-266 needs (an intrinsically-asymmetric dual-pathway substrate that produces bistability under DA modulation), but it does not *directly demonstrate* asymmetric hysteresis at the operating-mode-register level the V3 implementation lives at. A V3 implementation that uses a SalienceCoordinator-level Schmitt-trigger gate inherits the architectural commitment but does not implement OPAL's specific Go/NoGo opponent dynamics. This is fine for V3 — the architectural commitment is what MECH-266 cares about — but it should be flagged that the deeper biology is OPAL-style and that V4 elaborations of MECH-266 may benefit from explicitly modelling the dual-pathway substrate rather than parameterising the asymmetry abstractly.

A second caveat is timescale. OPAL's asymmetry is most clearly demonstrated in **learning dynamics** — the D1/D2 opponent pathways differentially update under positive vs negative reinforcement. The hysteresis MECH-266 cares about is at the **decision / control timescale** (a single trial's mode-state, not the learning history of mode preferences). The two timescales are related (decision-time mode-state is influenced by learned mode preferences), but they are not the same operation. OPAL's machinery may support the decision-timescale hysteresis only metaphorically rather than mechanistically. This is one ARC of indirection that the V4 elaboration would need to address explicitly.

Confidence components: source quality is very high at 0.92 (Psychological Review 2014, Frank lab, foundational paper for D1/D2 opponent RL modelling, deeply cited). Mapping fidelity is moderate at 0.62 — the mapping from RL-actor-level D1/D2 opponent dynamics to operating-mode-level enter/exit threshold asymmetry is by analogy across levels of description. Transfer risk is moderate-to-high at 0.40 because the timescale (learning) and the decomposition (Go/NoGo per action) differ from MECH-266's (decision/control, enter/exit per mode). Net direction is **supports** MECH-266 by providing the canonical asymmetric-pathway architecture, while flagging that the V3 implementation lives one level of abstraction up from where OPAL actually operates.

## MECH-266 synthesis with the new entries

The MECH-266 review now spans six entries: Fallon 2016 (asymmetric stability/flexibility from individual differences in DA), O'Reilly 2006 (BG gating as the substrate for stable-vs-flexible dynamics), Cools 2019 (stability/flexibility tradeoff review), Cools & D'Esposito 2011 (inverted-U dose-response curves), and the two new entries — Cools 2008 (paradoxical baseline-dependent DA effects) and Collins & Frank 2014 (OPAL D1/D2 opponent dynamics). The architectural commitment is well-grounded: the dual-pathway striatal substrate produces the kind of asymmetric, hysteretic dynamics MECH-266 needs, and tonic DA modulates the threshold structure in a baseline-dependent way that is consistent with two-threshold gating.

The implementation-level recommendations that come out of the synthesis are: (a) keep enter and exit thresholds independently tunable rather than locking them to a fixed gap (Cools 2008 inverted-U is a smooth modulation, not a fixed-gap Schmitt trigger); (b) acknowledge that the V3 single `operating_mode` register may need to elaborate into per-substrate mode tracking for V4 (Cools 2008 multi-DA-system dissociation); (c) the V3 SalienceCoordinator-level Schmitt-trigger gate inherits the asymmetric-pathway architectural commitment from OPAL but does not explicitly model the dual-pathway substrate -- V4 elaborations may benefit from doing so. Combined synthesis: MECH-266 is biologically grounded and architecturally well-motivated; the V3 implementation is acceptable; the V4 elaboration questions are about substrate-specificity and the timescale at which the asymmetric hysteresis operates.
