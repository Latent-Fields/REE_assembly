# Fallon, van der Schaaf, Ter Huurne, Cools 2016 — Methylphenidate double-edged effect (MECH-266)

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1162/jocn_a_01065)), Fallon and colleagues report a pharmacological fMRI study in healthy adults comparing methylphenidate (a catecholamine reuptake inhibitor) to placebo on a task that dissociates working-memory distractor-resistance from flexible WM updating. The key finding is that methylphenidate improves distractor resistance (stability) but impairs flexible updating (flexibility) — a double-edged effect that reveals these two processes as separable and oppositely modulated. The neural signature shows task-general striatal effects and task-specific opposite effects in PFC, consistent with the Cools framework of dopaminergic output gating.

For MECH-266, this is a supporting entry at the claim-mechanism level. MECH-266 posits asymmetric (enter, exit) threshold pairs per operating mode, with the asymmetry magnitude a clinical-axis variable. Fallon et al. show that a single pharmacological manipulation produces opposite effects on stability and flexibility, meaning these two properties are not collapsible into a single scalar. That supports the broader claim that (enter, exit) asymmetries are biologically real and tunable independently. The mapping is one level of abstraction up from the paper: stability and flexibility at the WM-content level are not literally the same as enter-mode vs exit-mode thresholds at the operating-mode level, but the gating logic is shared in O'Reilly & Frank-style frontostriatal theories, and the phenomenological signature Fallon et al. produce is the kind of signature MECH-266 predicts for per-mode asymmetric hysteresis.

The caveat I record explicitly is the translation cost. The paradigm is working-memory updating vs distractor resistance — both operating within a single task and a single mode. MECH-266 posits that enter and exit thresholds on an operating mode differ, and the mode in SD-032a is a higher-level construct (external_task, internal_planning, internal_replay, offline_consolidation). Whether the same asymmetric-gating logic scales from item-level WM updating to mode-level operating-state transitions is the biological-plausibility question that this paper partially addresses but does not resolve.

The clinical framing MECH-266 uses — under-binding (depression axis) vs over-binding (OCD / rigidity axis) corresponding to different positions on the asymmetry parameter — is consistent with the Fallon et al. finding that individuals at different baseline DA states experience opposite effects of the same drug. This is a classic Cools-lab result and is one of the clearer demonstrations that stability-flexibility is not symmetric; it is governed by two parameters that can move in opposite directions.

Confidence is 0.72. Source quality is high (Cools lab pharmacological fMRI). Mapping fidelity is moderate because of the WM-content to operating-mode abstraction gap. Transfer risk is higher than most: REE does not implement a dopamine analogue or a baseline-dependent inverted-U — the MECH-266 implementation path is explicit (enter, exit) parameters rather than a modulated single parameter. The entry supports the biological reality of asymmetric stability-flexibility gating; it does not validate the specific parametric choice in the SD-032a extension.
