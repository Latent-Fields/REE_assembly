# Cools 2019 — Chemistry of the Adaptive Mind (MECH-266)

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2019.09.035)), Cools's Neuron review frames striatal dopamine as the modulator of the stability-flexibility tradeoff in cognitive control. The review is unified around the claim that dopaminergic projection systems have distinct basal tones reflecting environmental statistics computed in frontal cortex, and that pharmacological manipulations produce task-dependent effects that depend on the baseline state of the targeted region. The result is an inverted-U relationship with direction — improvement or impairment — contingent on where a given individual or context sits on the baseline-dopamine curve.

For MECH-266 this is close to an architectural warrant. MECH-266 claims that SD-032a's mode register should be extended with per-mode (enter, exit) threshold pairs — a Schmitt trigger — and that the asymmetry is mode-specific. The Cools account supports three things. First, that the biological gate is not symmetric: a high-tonic-DA state biases toward stability (resists exit from the current mode); a low-tonic-DA state biases toward flexibility (easier to enter alternative modes). Second, that the asymmetry magnitude varies with a context-dependent signal (the "perceived statistics of the environment"), which in REE terms would be a function of operating_mode and task context. Third, that clinical extremes along the stability-flexibility axis correspond to pathological hysteresis — too-stable is the rigidity phenotype (OCD, perseveration), too-flexible is the distractibility phenotype.

The mapping is clean at the phenomenon level and coarser at the implementation level. Cools describes a global neuromodulatory mechanism; MECH-266 specifies per-mode threshold parameters inside the SalienceCoordinator. These are not the same mechanism — REE does not have a dedicated tonic-DA channel, and the mode-specific asymmetry parameters would be local rather than globally modulated. What Cools provides is a biological existence proof that asymmetric hysteresis at the cortex-striatum gate is a real phenomenon and is modulated by tonic signals; what MECH-266 adds is a specific implementation commitment to represent that asymmetry as explicit (enter, exit) pairs per mode.

The honest caveat I record is that if follow-up empirical work shows that mode transitions in biology are symmetric at the mode level but modulated only by a global gain factor, MECH-266's per-mode-asymmetry commitment is over-specified and the correct implementation is a single global threshold modulated by an independent gain channel. Cools's review does not distinguish these architectures directly; it describes the phenomenon at a higher level of abstraction.

Confidence is set at 0.74. Source quality is high (Neuron, leading author). Mapping fidelity is moderate because of the abstraction gap between neuromodulator-level and local-threshold-level descriptions. Transfer risk is moderate: the stability-flexibility tradeoff is very well established, but its specific instantiation as Schmitt-trigger (enter, exit) pairs per mode is a REE design choice not directly tested in Cools's work. The V3-EXQ-464 and V3-EXQ-467 experiments queued for MECH-266 are the primary falsifiers; this lit-pull establishes the biological warrant without resolving the implementation question.
