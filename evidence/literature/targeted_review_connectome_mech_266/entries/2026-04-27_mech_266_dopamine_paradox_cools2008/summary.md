# Cools 2008 — Role of dopamine in the motivational and cognitive control of behavior

According to PubMed, Cools (2008, [The Neuroscientist 14:381-95](https://doi.org/10.1177/1073858408317009)) reviews the paradoxical role of dopamine in motivational and cognitive control. The paper's central tension is that the same dopaminergic manipulation can produce opposite effects depending on baseline state — what is often called the inverted-U or sweet-spot framing. A patient with low baseline striatal dopamine (advanced Parkinson's, off-medication) may benefit from L-DOPA on cognitive flexibility tasks but be impaired on the same drug on different tasks; a healthy individual at the high end of baseline DA may show the opposite pattern. The paper synthesises three further strands: (a) distinct dopaminergic systems — mesolimbic, mesocortical, and nigrostriatal — have dissociable roles in different aspects of behavioural control; (b) there are large individual differences in response to dopaminergic drugs, with some individuals benefiting and others being impaired by the same compound; (c) these dissociations matter for neuropsychiatry, where some individuals are disproportionately vulnerable to the adverse cognitive effects of dopamine-enhancing drugs.

For MECH-266 (asymmetric mode hysteresis: distinct enter vs exit thresholds per operating mode, providing the basis of bistable mode-state dynamics), Cools 2008 functions as architectural-level support rather than as a strict empirical test. The biological grounding for asymmetric hysteresis has two parts. First, tonic dopamine modulates the cost of switching between modes — DA's role as the regulator of stability-vs-flexibility trade-offs is well-established and is the central thesis of the closely related Cools & D'Esposito 2011 paper (already in the MECH-266 review). Second, the modulation is *baseline-dependent* — the threshold to enter a given mode is not the same as the threshold to exit it, because the same DA level produces different transition probabilities depending on which mode the system is currently in. Cools 2008 supports the second component by establishing the baseline-dependence as a foundational property of DA's role in control. The natural neurobiological mechanism for asymmetric hysteresis follows: the threshold depends on the current mode, which depends on current baseline DA, which is itself sensitive to recent transitions.

I want to flag two boundary conditions on the transfer to MECH-266. The first is that Cools 2008 frames the baseline-dependence of DA effects primarily in terms of inverted-U dose-response curves rather than in terms of explicit Schmitt-trigger threshold asymmetry. A smooth inverted-U sensitivity curve could in principle reproduce many of the same behavioural signatures without requiring discrete enter and exit thresholds with a measurable gap. The strict test for MECH-266 — that enter-threshold and exit-threshold differ in a way that produces hysteretic behaviour over time, with the system staying in a mode after the entering condition has passed — is not directly demonstrated by this review. It is a plausible but not strictly forced architectural inference. The implementation-level recommendation that follows is that MECH-266's V3 implementation should expose both the enter- and exit-threshold parameters as tunable, so that the empirical question of how large the asymmetry needs to be can be tested rather than fixed by stipulation.

The second boundary condition is that the dissociation across mesolimbic, mesocortical, and nigrostriatal DA systems implies that MECH-266's "mode" may need to be elaborated into multiple parallel mode registers, one per substrate, rather than a single global `operating_mode`. SD-032a SalienceCoordinator currently holds a single mode signal. Cools 2008 flags that biology may require per-substrate mode tracking — a mode register specific to flexibility-stability dynamics in striatum, a separate register for working-memory updating in PFC, and so on. This is a V4 elaboration rather than a V3 commitment, but it is worth surfacing now so the V3 implementation does not foreclose on it.

A third caveat is that the individual-difference effects (some individuals benefit, some are impaired by the same dopaminergic drug) imply that MECH-266's threshold parameters may be subject-specific rather than population-canonical. A V3 implementation that fixes thresholds at population means may miss the substantial individual variation the biology shows. For an REE agent with a single fixed parameter set this is fine; for any future ambition to model individual differences (clinical phenotypes, OCD subtypes from the SD-033 governance plan), the parameter would need to vary.

Confidence components: source quality high at 0.85 (Neuroscientist review 2008, Cools is a leading authority on DA + cognitive control, well-cited as the dopamine-paradox anchor for the field). Mapping fidelity moderate at 0.65 — baseline-dependent DA effects support hysteresis-friendly architecture but do not directly demonstrate the two-threshold Schmitt-trigger structure. Transfer risk moderate at 0.30 because of the multi-DA-system dissociation and the individual-difference effects. Net direction is **supports** MECH-266's architectural commitment to asymmetric hysteresis, with implementation-level recommendations: (a) keep enter and exit thresholds independently tunable rather than locking them to a fixed gap; (b) acknowledge that the V3 single `operating_mode` register may need to elaborate into per-substrate mode tracking for V4.
