# van de Leemput et al. 2014 — Critical Slowing Down as Early Warning for Depression

## What the paper did

Van de Leemput and colleagues applied dynamical systems theory to ecological momentary assessment (EMA) data from several hundred individuals across European cohorts, including people transitioning into and out of major depressive episodes. The central question: do the mathematical signatures of bistable systems — specifically, critical slowing down (rising autocorrelation and variance as a system approaches a tipping point) — appear in human mood data before depressive transitions? If mood is organised as a bistable dynamical system with two attractors (normal and depressed), the system should "slow down" as it approaches the tipping point between them, because perturbations take longer to decay when the restoring force is weak.

## Key findings relevant to INV-054

The results supported the bistable mood hypothesis. Individuals who subsequently transitioned into a depressive episode showed elevated temporal autocorrelation and variance in their daily mood fluctuations in the weeks prior to the transition — the critical slowing down signature. Crucially, the same phenomenon was observed in reverse: individuals recovering from depression also showed elevated autocorrelation before their transition back to normal mood. The paper describes this as evidence for "alternative stable states separated by tipping points." The system does not move continuously between depressed and normal; it settles into one attractor and then flips to the other when a threshold is crossed.

## How this maps to INV-054

INV-054's central claim is that the depressive state is self-maintaining — a closed negative feedback loop that keeps the system in the depressed attractor. The van de Leemput paper provides the empirical geometry: there are indeed two attractors (normal and depressed) separated by a tipping point, and transitions are discontinuous threshold crossings, not gradual linear improvements. Both claims map directly. The critical slowing down signature before recovery is particularly relevant to INV-054's prediction about the direction of causation: the system doesn't drift gradually back to normal, it slow-builds toward a tipping point and then snaps across. This is what "phase transition" means in the INV-054 context — and this paper shows it empirically in human mood data.

## Limitations and caveats

The paper establishes the bistable geometry at the phenomenological level (mood autocorrelation patterns) without specifying which feedback loops create the attractor wells. INV-054 claims the well is maintained by a specific benefit_terrain -> VALENCE_WANTING -> exploration -> benefit_exposure loop. The van de Leemput paper does not identify these variables; it observes the attractor structure without explaining the mechanism. The mapping is therefore structural (both claim bistability and threshold crossings) rather than mechanistic (the loop components are inferred by REE, not confirmed here). There is also individual heterogeneity: not all transitions showed the critical slowing down signal, which may indicate that the bistable attractor structure is not universal.
