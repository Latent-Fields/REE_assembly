# Summary: Nesse (2000) -- Is Depression an Adaptation?

## The Central Argument

Nesse asks a genuinely uncomfortable question: if natural selection has had millions of years to eliminate depression, why does it persist? His answer is that low mood is not a malfunction but a coordinated inhibitory state that adaptively blocks investment in futile, dangerous, or wasteful pursuits. Pessimism, motivational withdrawal, and disengagement are the features, not the bugs -- they prevent an organism from persisting in challenges that cannot be won. The analogy is to pain: we do not ask why pain exists, we ask what it signals and when it becomes maladaptive.

## Structural Correspondence to INV-054

What is striking from the REE perspective is that Nesse's adaptive account accidentally describes a self-maintaining loop. An organism in low mood reduces effort and exploration. Reduced exploration means fewer positive reinforcers are encountered. Fewer positive reinforcers means the environmental gradient (which would signal that investment is now worthwhile) remains flat. The inhibitory state therefore persists, not because the environment has not changed, but because the agent is no longer sampling the environment sufficiently to detect that it has changed. This is structurally isomorphic to INV-054's closed loop: the state that reduces exploration prevents the exploration that would dissolve the state.

Nesse does not describe this as a fixed point or attractor. He frames it as a feature that becomes pathological only when triggered inappropriately or maintained beyond its adaptive window. But the mechanism is the same whether framed evolutionarily or computationally: the inhibitory state is self-limiting in adaptive cases (the environment eventually changes, or the goal is abandoned) and self-sustaining in pathological cases (the terrain is the maintaining variable, not the environment).

## What the Paper Does Not Provide

Nesse's paper is theoretical. It provides no quantification of how long depressive states persist, no data on recovery trajectories, and no account of why environmental improvement does not immediately resolve the state. These are the questions that matter most for INV-054's empirical grounding. The paper is silent on threshold dynamics: there is no equivalent to the SD-012 seeding threshold, no distinction between terrain (internal) and environment (external), and no prediction of non-linear recovery. The attractor framing has to be read into Nesse, not read from him.

## Relevance and Limits

This paper supports INV-054 as a theoretical anchor: it establishes that the maintenance loop has an adaptive functional logic, not merely a pathological one. This matters for the REE paper's psychiatric section, which needs to situate the computational model within existing clinical frameworks. Nesse's framing makes INV-054 look less like an ad hoc computational artifact and more like a formalization of a long-recognized clinical observation. The caveat is that evolutionary framing and computational terrain framing are different explanatory levels; the correspondence is structural, not mechanistic.
