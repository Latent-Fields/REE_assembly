# Jacobson et al. 1996 — Behavioral Activation Component Analysis

## What the paper did

Jacobson and colleagues ran a three-arm RCT (n=150 outpatients with MDD) to decompose cognitive-behavioral therapy into its constituent components. One arm received behavioral activation (BA) alone — scheduled contact with potentially rewarding activities, without any cognitive restructuring. A second arm added automatic-thought modification on top of BA. A third arm received the full CT protocol including schema-level work. Outcomes were assessed at acute termination and at 6-month follow-up using standard depression inventories.

## Key findings relevant to INV-054

The headline result is provocative: BA alone was statistically equivalent to full CT at every assessment point, including follow-up. Neither the automatic-thought component nor the schema component added incremental benefit over BA alone. More striking still, all three arms produced equivalent reductions in negative thinking and dysfunctional attributional styles — suggesting that external behavioral scheduling altered internal cognitive content even without explicit cognitive work. The active therapeutic ingredient appears to be the increase in contact with rewarding activities, not the modification of beliefs per se.

## How this maps to INV-054

INV-054's core prediction is that once benefit terrain collapses, the internal motivational system cannot self-reinitiate: VALENCE_WANTING is at zero, benefit-oriented exploration is suppressed, benefit encounters are sparse, and the SD-012 seeding threshold goes unmet. This is a closed loop. The only way out, INV-054 predicts, is external forcing of benefit_exposure — scaffolding reward encounters from outside the system before internal VALENCE_WANTING has recovered. Behavioral activation is precisely this mechanism: a therapist structures the patient's schedule to increase contact with rewarding activities regardless of the patient's internal motivation. The Jacobson 1996 finding supports this: BA alone is sufficient for recovery, and crucially, the cognitive changes followed the behavioral changes, not the reverse. This is consistent with terrain recovery driving internal-state recovery in the REE model.

## Limitations and caveats

The paper does not measure motivational trajectories — it measures symptom inventories. The mapping from "scheduling rewarding activities restores mood" to "externally scaffolded benefit_exposure restores VALENCE_WANTING terrain" is plausible but not directly tested. The paper also does not demonstrate the negative feedback loop mechanism per se — it shows that intervening at the behavior level works, which is consistent with INV-054 but does not rule out simpler accounts (e.g., increased activity simply distracts from rumination). Transfer from clinical populations to REE's grid-world abstraction involves obvious simplification: the richness of human reward landscapes does not map cleanly onto a 10x10 grid.
