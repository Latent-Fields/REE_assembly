# Tang & DeRubeis 1999 — Sudden Gains and Critical Sessions in CBT for Depression

## What the paper did

Tang and DeRubeis conducted a secondary analysis of a large RCT comparing cognitive-behavioral therapy to antidepressant medication for major depression. They identified "sudden gains" — large, abrupt improvements in depression symptoms occurring within a single between-session interval — and characterised their magnitude, timing, precursors, and prognostic value. A sudden gain was operationally defined as meeting three criteria: a large absolute improvement (5+ BDI points), a stable improvement (not reversed in subsequent sessions), and a disproportionately large improvement relative to preceding session-to-session fluctuations.

## Key findings relevant to INV-054

Approximately 50% of CBT patients experienced at least one sudden gain. When they occurred, the average magnitude was 11 BDI points — accounting for roughly 50% of those patients' total improvement across the entire course of therapy. Patients who experienced sudden gains ended treatment significantly less depressed than those who did not, and this advantage was maintained at 18-month follow-up. The sudden gain was typically preceded by a "critical session" in which patients showed unusually elevated insight into their cognitive distortions. The abrupt symptom change occurred between sessions — suggesting the mechanism involved consolidation of insight rather than in-session change per se.

## How this maps to INV-054

INV-054's non-linear recovery prediction is its most distinctive and falsifiable claim: recovery from the depressive attractor is not a gradual dose-response process but a threshold crossing. Once benefit_exposure accumulates to the seeding threshold, VALENCE_WANTING terrain begins recovering through a self-amplifying positive feedback: terrain recovery enables more exploration, which enables more benefit encounters, which enables further terrain recovery. This escape from the depressive attractor has the mathematical structure of a phase transition — an abrupt discontinuous shift, not a smooth linear approach. Sudden gains are the symptom-level observable signature of exactly this kind of threshold crossing. The finding that 50% of total improvement occurs in a single between-session interval is incompatible with simple dose-response linear recovery, and consistent with a system snapping between attractor states.

## Limitations and caveats

The "critical session" precursor is the most significant caveat for the REE interpretation. If sudden gains require a prior session of elevated cognitive insight, this suggests the threshold crossing may involve schema-level reorganisation rather than (or in addition to) motivational terrain recovery. REE currently lacks a schema layer in the INV-054 loop; the benefit_exposure -> terrain -> VALENCE_WANTING account may be incomplete. The ~50% incidence is also worth noting: the other ~50% of patients show more gradual improvement, which is harder to reconcile with a strict bistable model. The sudden gain literature has also been replicated across multiple studies (Tang 2005, Stiles et al., Gaynor et al.), strengthening the finding while also showing variability in precursors.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence 0.76. The paper is a canonical citation in the discontinuous-change literature, well-replicated and methodologically sound. Mapping fidelity to INV-054's non-linear recovery prediction is good but not tight: the symptom-score measure is an indirect proxy for the motivational terrain change INV-054 describes, and the critical-session cognitive-change precursor introduces ambiguity about the mechanism. This is weaker evidence than the van de Leemput dynamical systems paper because it is at a more descriptive level, but it is complementary: van de Leemput establishes the attractor geometry, Tang establishes that recovery in humans is observably discontinuous.
