# Summary: Trivedi et al. (2006) -- STAR*D Step 2 and Response Latency in Depression

## The Critical Numbers

The STAR*D trial is the largest effectiveness trial of depression treatment ever conducted (N=4,041 at enrollment). This Step 2 augmentation paper provides the quantitative anchors that INV-054 most urgently needs. The key figures, sourced from PubMed records of this paper and the companion Gaynes et al. (2008) STAR*D analysis:

- Step 1 non-remitters had received a **mean of 11.9 weeks** of citalopram (mean dose 55 mg/day) before entering Step 2. This is not a brief trial -- nearly three months of adequate SSRI therapy without crossing the remission threshold.
- Step 2 augmentation (bupropion-SR or buspirone added to citalopram) achieved remission in approximately **30%** of those who had failed Step 1. Two-thirds of Step 1 non-remitters remained unresponsive even to augmented treatment.
- For those who did eventually respond to Step 1 citalopram (Gaynes et al. 2008): median time to remission was **6.2-6.9 weeks** and median time to response was **5.4-5.5 weeks**.

These numbers matter. They are the empirical calibration for EXP-0097's recovery threshold. The INV-054 prediction is that once the depressive attractor is established, recovery requires a phase transition -- a period of terrain rebuilding during which benefit_exposure must accumulate above threshold before the gradient re-emerges. The STAR*D latency data (5-7 week median for eventual responders; 12 weeks still not enough for 60-70% of non-remitters) are inconsistent with graded linear recovery and consistent with threshold-crossing dynamics.

## Why the Latency Pattern Matters

Ask the simplest possible question: if antidepressants worked by directly and linearly suppressing depressive symptoms, you would expect partial improvement to appear within the first week or two and to accumulate continuously. This is not what STAR*D shows. Eventual responders show a characteristic delayed onset -- several weeks of minimal apparent change, then a more rapid improvement phase once the threshold is crossed. This non-linear response profile is the clinical signature that INV-054's attractor account predicts and graded-recovery accounts cannot easily explain.

One might object that the delay reflects pharmacokinetic mechanisms -- receptor desensitization takes weeks, neurogenesis requires sustained BDNF signaling, and so on. These are real biological processes. But they do not compete with the terrain interpretation; they may instantiate it. If SSRIs work partly by supporting exploration behavior (reducing avoidance, increasing approach motivation) in a way that allows benefit encounters to accumulate, then the pharmacokinetic delay and the terrain rebuilding delay are the same process described at different levels. The REE framing makes an additional prediction that no pure pharmacokinetic account makes: behavioral interventions that directly scaffold benefit exposure should show a comparable latency window.

## The Non-Responder Data and Attractor Depth

The Step 2 data are at least as informative as the latency data. After nearly three months of adequate SSRI therapy, 70% of Step 1 non-remitters remained depressed and entered Step 2. With augmentation, another 30% achieved remission. This cumulative picture -- progressive attrition of treatment response across steps, with substantial proportions remaining depressed despite adequate sequential trials -- is consistent with a heterogeneous population of attractor depths. Some patients are near the threshold and respond quickly; others are embedded more deeply in the attractor basin, requiring more intervention to accumulate sufficient benefit_exposure to cross threshold. The STAR*D step structure inadvertently maps onto a population-level view of attractor basin depth.

## Calibration for EXP-0097

The empirical window for recovery latency established by STAR*D is 5-7 weeks (median) for eventual responders, with non-response defined as 12+ weeks without remission. In the REE agent's CausalGridWorldV2 environment, EXP-0097's recovery criterion must be calibrated against this timescale. The specific threshold of >50 benefit episodes required should be understood as a model of this accumulation period: the exact number is a parameter, but the qualitative prediction -- that recovery requires a threshold quantity of benefit encounters, not merely time or environmental improvement alone -- is the claim being tested.
