# Shenhav, Botvinick, Cohen 2013 -- Expected Value of Control

The Neuron paper by Shenhav, Botvinick and Cohen offers an integrative theory of dorsal anterior cingulate cortex (dACC) as a computer of the expected value of control. On their account dACC does not itself do the controlled processing; rather, it specifies, moment by moment, how much cognitive control to allocate and where, by weighing the expected payoff of a controlled process against the amount of control required and the intrinsic cost of cognitive effort. The elegant move in the paper is that one region outputs a single scalar-like signal whose meaning is resolved downstream: different cortical and striatal targets interpret the allocation signal according to the task context they are embedded in.

For the question we are asking -- whether a forward model in REE (E2) operates in distinguishable attributional and evaluative modes, or whether the data are better described as a single process read differently by downstream circuits -- this is an interesting piece of neighbouring evidence rather than a direct answer. The direct subject matter is control allocation, not forward-model reuse. But the architectural precedent is suggestive: the brain, at least in this cortical territory, prefers a shared-substrate-with-gated-readout organisation over one where every mode gets its own dedicated region. If we take that preference seriously, the default expectation for E2 is also single-process with a controller that shifts readout weights.

There is a caveat worth stating clearly. EVC is a computational theory resting on fMRI, lesion, and modelling work, not on a direct demonstration that one forward-model population is read in two different modes. The transfer to E2 attribution vs. evaluation is architectural reasoning by analogy. Worse, the EVC account could itself dissolve into a compositional one -- sub-regions of dACC for cost, for conflict, for payoff -- and if that is the case, the single-process inference for E2 would weaken in step.

Practically, the paper matters to our registry decision in this way. If we were going to register MECH-E2-DUAL-FUNCTION as two disjoint E2 substrates with separate training signals, EVC gently pushes us away from that framing. A more defensible claim is: E2 is a single forward-model substrate; there exists a mode-gating signal (candidate homologues: dACC EVC, z_beta, E3 arbitration) that selects whether E2's rollout is read attributionally (compared against observed outcome) or evaluatively (integrated over rollouts for pre-action scoring). Under this framing, the dual-function claim becomes a claim about readout and controller, not about the forward model itself.

The weak spot in using Shenhav 2013 as evidence here is the same caveat every review carries: it is integrative, not discriminative. It makes the single-process+readout hypothesis respectable; it does not force it. That is why I would hold confidence near 0.58 -- enough to lean the registry in one direction, not enough to retire the alternative.
