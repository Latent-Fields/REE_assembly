# Rangel, Camerer, Montague 2008 -- A Framework for Value-Based Decision Making

The Rangel, Camerer and Montague framework in Nature Reviews Neuroscience remains one of the most useful scaffolds for thinking about any decision-making system, biological or artificial. They decompose value-based decision making into five stages -- representation, valuation, action selection, outcome evaluation, and learning -- and they identify three parallel valuation systems (Pavlovian, habitual, goal-directed). The framework is pitched as a lingua franca rather than a specific neural implementation; this makes it broadly applicable but also means any transfer to a specific design question, like ours about E2, requires careful unpacking.

What matters for the attribution vs evaluation question is the role the framework assigns to the forward model inside goal-directed valuation. A model-based forward predictor is used prospectively, during valuation, to estimate the value of rollouts; the very same model contributes to prediction-error signals during outcome evaluation. Rangel and colleagues treat these as two computational stages, not two substrates. That is an important point of support for a single-process view of E2: the framework implicitly assumes, without much argument, that the goal-directed forward model does double duty. One substrate, two computational consumers.

The caveat is that the framework is permissive rather than precise. It does not actually exclude the dual-substrate view. Within model-based valuation, nothing in Rangel 2008 prevents you from positing a prospective forward model and a retrospective forward model with separate training signals. The framework simply does not adjudicate. It is evidence for the single-process view by default rather than by demonstration.

A second caveat is the model-free / model-based distinction the framework emphasises. If that distinction is taken as architecturally hard, then an unwary reader might conclude that goal-directed and habitual systems have separate substrates and generalise that principle to attribution-vs-evaluation inside the model-based system. That would be an over-extension. The framework only commits to separation at the model-free/model-based boundary, not within the model-based stages.

For registry purposes, this is the most mapping-relevant of the three theoretical entries (Shenhav 2013, Shenhav 2016, Rangel 2008). The framework implicitly endorses one forward model with multiple consumers, which is the picture we want to encode in MECH-E2-DUAL-FUNCTION as single-process-plus-readout. I would weight it around 0.62 confidence because the mapping is cleaner, but the evidence is still indirect -- a framework paper does not settle an implementation question.
