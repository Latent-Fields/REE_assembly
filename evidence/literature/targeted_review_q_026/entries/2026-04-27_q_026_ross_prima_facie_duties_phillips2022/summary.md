# Ross's Prima Facie Duties (SEP) -- relevance to Q-026

## What the entry covers

David Phillips's Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry on W. D. Ross sets out
Ross's pluralist deontology. Ross's central distinction is between *prima facie
duties* and *duty proper*. The five canonical prima facie duties (fidelity,
reparation, gratitude, beneficence, non-maleficence) are universal and self-evident:
they identify "considerations counting in favour of or against an act" and are
"objective facts present in a situation," not merely apparent duties. Crucially, they
are not absolute. Each prima facie duty is binding "other things equal" -- it can be
overridden by a competing prima facie duty of greater weight.

Actual duty (duty proper) emerges only from a contextual comparison: it is "the
greatest balance of prima facie rightness... over their prima facie wrongness" given
the full situation. Ross is explicit that this comparison "rests with perception" and
yields only "probable opinion, never certainty." His drowning-child example makes the
point: breaking a promise becomes obligatory because beneficence (saving a life)
outweighs fidelity in those particular circumstances.

## Why this matters for Q-026

Q-026 asks: what actions are strictly required by the five axioms at all times,
independent of context? Ross's framework is the dominant modern philosophical
challenge to the very form of that question. On Ross's view, the universal /
context-independent things are the *prima facie considerations*, not the *actions*.
No specific action is absolutely required across all contexts, because any prima
facie duty can in principle be overridden by another. The question Q-026 asks --
"what action is required regardless of context" -- has, on Ross's reading, a
near-empty answer: only the abstract injunction to act on the all-things-considered
strongest balance of prima facie duties is itself context-independent, and that is
not really an action specification.

This is why I am marking the evidence direction *weakens*. The entry argues, in effect,
that Q-026 may be reaching for something that does not exist in the form requested.

## Possible REE re-framing

Ross also suggests a constructive reframing. INV-042 already specifies a list of
derived ethical objectives -- preserve minds, preserve future options, reduce
unnecessary suffering, increase shared joy, maintain corrigibility, maintain
truth-seeking, maintain the ability to love and be loved, maintain the shared world,
maintain the possibility of future minds. That list functions structurally very much
like a Rossian prima facie duty set: each is a universal consideration, but in any
concrete moment the agent must compare them and act on the strongest balance. REE's
existing harm-stream / goal-salience / residue-field machinery is already a
context-sensitive comparator. So the Ross entry suggests that the natural answer to
Q-026 is: the *axioms* are context-independent, the *derived objectives* (INV-042) are
context-independent considerations, but no specific *action* is required at all times
independent of context -- the agent's job is to compute the weighted balance.

## Caveats and confidence

Mapping fidelity is moderate. Ross's specific duties (fidelity, reparation,
gratitude, etc.) are not derived from REE's axioms and do not transfer wholesale.
What transfers is the structural lesson: universal moral *content* and context-
sensitive action *selection* are compatible, and indeed the latter may be the only
honest answer to questions in Q-026's form. Transfer risk is moderate because Ross
is writing about human moral agents in ordinary social life, not about computational
agents instantiating five formal axioms; some of the framework's force depends on
self-evidence claims that REE would have to redo from its own primitives.

Confidence 0.45 -- the relevance to Q-026's form is high, but the answer is
"the question may need reframing," which is useful but not the kind of evidence that
closes an open question.
