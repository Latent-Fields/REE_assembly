# Kant's Moral Philosophy (SEP) -- relevance to Q-026

## What the entry covers

Robert Johnson and Adam Cureton's Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry is the
standard scholarly summary of Kant's moral framework. It lays out the categorical
imperative, the four-step universalisability procedure, and -- most relevantly for
Q-026 -- the perfect/imperfect duty distinction.

A *perfect duty* on Kant's account "admits of no exception in favour of inclination"
and binds universally: one must "never (or always) phi to the fullest extent possible."
These are derived when a maxim's universalisation generates a contradiction in
conception (the lying-promise example: universal lying-promises destroy the institution
of promising itself). *Imperfect duties* are weaker: they require an agent to "sometimes
and to some extent" pursue certain ends, and arise when a maxim passes the conception
test but fails a contradiction-in-the-will test.

## Why this is the closest classical analogue to Q-026

Q-026 asks: what actions are strictly required by REE's five axioms at all times,
independent of context? That is structurally a question about *categorical*, not
hypothetical, requirements. Kant's perfect duties are the canonical historical example
of such a category in Western moral philosophy. If anything in the secondary literature
gives us a template for "context-independent action requirement," it is here.

The entry is therefore useful as conceptual scaffolding. It tells us what an answer to
Q-026 would *look like* -- a small set of universally binding proscriptions or
prescriptions, derived from the architecture of rational agency, that admit of no
exception. INV-042 already lists candidate derived objectives ("preserve minds,"
"preserve future options," "reduce unnecessary suffering," "maintain corrigibility,"
etc.). The Kantian framing suggests that a subset of these might be cashed out as
perfect-duty analogues.

## Why I am keeping confidence low

The mapping is indirect on two fronts. First, Kant's perfect duties are derived from
universalisability, not from REE's axioms about self-existence (INV-026), world-existence
(INV-027), and shared-world ethics (INV-028). The derivation routes are different and
the conclusions may not coincide -- Kant's "do not lie" and "do not commit suicide" are
not obvious consequences of the REE axiom set, and INV-042's list is not obviously a
restatement of Kant.

Second, the entry itself notes that Kant concedes "judgment is often required to
determine how these duties apply to particular circumstances," and that "moral laws
cannot, after all, have regard to every little circumstance." That is itself a
significant caveat against pure context-independence even on the strongest historical
version of the view. So the very thing Q-026 is asking after may not survive Kant's own
qualifications, let alone the more restrained versions in modern deontology.

## Net evidence direction

I am marking this *mixed*. It supports the existence of the *category* of context-
independent moral requirements that Q-026 presupposes, but it does not answer Q-026
itself, and it weakens any reading of Q-026 that requires *purely* context-free action
specifications. Confidence 0.42, weighted heavily by mapping fidelity rather than source
quality. The right way to use this entry is as a frame for designing an REE-internal
derivation, not as evidence that REE's axioms imply any particular Kantian duty.
