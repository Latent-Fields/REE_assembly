# Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy -- Love (Keller 2023)

## What the entry did

The SEP entry on Love, written and updated by Simon Keller, provides a systematic survey of the major philosophical accounts of what love is. The entry organizes the literature around four main views: love as a kind of emotion (or constellation of emotions), love as a form of appraisal (valuing the beloved's properties), love as a desire for union or identification with the beloved, and the 'robust concern' view (love as caring for the beloved's wellbeing for its own sake). For each view, the entry explains the central claims, the main supporting arguments, and the principal objections. The entry also discusses the constancy of love, the rationality of love, and the special obligations love generates. The robust concern view -- held by Frankfurt, Taylor, Soble, LaFollette, Newton-Smith and others -- receives substantial treatment and is presented as having strong support in the literature.

## Key findings relevant to INV-029

The central finding relevant to INV-029 is that the robust concern view represents a widely-held and philosophically well-supported account of love's essential nature. According to this view, what makes love love -- rather than mere liking, admiration, or attachment -- is a disinterested concern for the beloved's wellbeing for its own sake. This concern is non-instrumental: it is not based on what the agent will get out of the beloved's flourishing, but on genuine orientation toward the beloved's interests. The entry notes that 'caring about your beloved for her sake' is regarded by many philosophers as not merely a feature of love but its defining feature. The constancy of love -- its persistence over time, through changes in the beloved and in circumstances -- is also treated as a necessary feature, not merely a typical one. And the identification of the lover with the beloved's interests -- taking the beloved's welfare as part of one's own interest, without collapsing into self-interest -- is described as characteristic of mature love across multiple frameworks.

These three features (non-instrumental concern for flourishing, constancy over time, identification with the beloved's interests) correspond precisely to the three components of INV-029's definition of love as 'long-horizon care investment in another agent's wellbeing -- a structurally coherent agent disposition.' The SEP entry establishes that this definition is not a philosophical invention but a formulation of the robust concern view that commands significant philosophical consensus.

## How this translates to REE (INV-029)

INV-029 asserts that love as long-horizon care investment is structurally coherent and not reducible to self-interest. The SEP entry provides the evidence that this claim corresponds to a well-established philosophical position, not a stipulative definition. The 'non-instrumental concern for flourishing' = care investment in another's wellbeing. The 'constancy' = long-horizon. The 'stable dispositional structure rather than episodic feeling' = structurally coherent disposition. The 'not reducible to self-interest' = multiple philosophers' agreement that genuine love is disinterested even when the lover benefits from the beloved's flourishing. INV-029 is, in effect, applying the robust concern view to REE's architectural context and asking whether an artificial agent's disposition can satisfy the same conditions. The SEP entry cannot answer that question, but it establishes that the conditions INV-029 specifies are philosophically coherent and well-motivated.

## Limitations and caveats

The SEP entry surveys competing views and does not establish that the robust concern view is definitively correct. The emotion view (love as a kind of emotional response to the beloved's properties) and the union view (love as aspiring to merge with or be constituted by the beloved) are both given serious treatment and are not obviously incompatible with INV-029 in all respects. If the emotion view is correct, love is constitutively an affective state rather than a volitional disposition, which would complicate the 'structurally coherent disposition' framing. The entry also does not address artificial agents at all, and the question of whether a simulated agent can instantiate the robust concern view -- whether it can have genuine non-instrumental concern for another agent's wellbeing -- is left open. REE must supply the additional argument that its architecture can implement functional analogues of the robust concern view's constitutive features.
