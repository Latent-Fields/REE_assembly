# Ploghaus et al. 1999 -- Dissociating pain from its anticipation

**Citation.** Ploghaus A, Tracey I, Gati JS, Clare S, Menon RS, Matthews PM, Rawlins JN. Dissociating pain from its anticipation in the human brain. Science 1999; 284(5422): 1979-1981. [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.284.5422.1979). (According to PubMed.)

**What they did.** Canonical early fMRI study cueing healthy adults for imminent pain, and comparing brain activity during the anticipation window to brain activity during pain delivery itself.

**Key finding.** Anticipation activated sites within medial frontal lobe, insular cortex, and cerebellum that were distinct from -- but close to -- the sites mediating pain experience. The authors argued this was the first direct evidence that pain anticipation has its own neural signature, dissociable from pain experience.

**What this does for the REE question.** Foundational, not decisive. It establishes that the machinery for predicting pain is anatomically separable from the machinery for processing pain once it arrives. That is a precondition for any forward-model architecture, and it is what makes the predictive-coding research programme possible. For REE: the predictor (E2_harm_a / E2_harm_s) should not be the same module as the consumer (SD-032b). We already build it that way.

**Where Ploghaus is silent.** The paper does not distinguish one anticipation machinery from two. It shows anticipation vs experience, not A-delta vs C.

**Limitations.** 1999 is early fMRI; the spatial resolution, analysis methods, and statistical standards are all dated. Modern replications (Ploghaus 2003 and beyond, the entire Buchel and Tracey programmes) cover the ground more rigorously. I include it here because it is cited as the origin point by virtually every subsequent paper in this area.

**Confidence reasoning.** Moderate. Historical weight high; direct relevance to the shared-vs-parallel decision is limited. I include it for corpus completeness and because the user named Ploghaus explicitly.
