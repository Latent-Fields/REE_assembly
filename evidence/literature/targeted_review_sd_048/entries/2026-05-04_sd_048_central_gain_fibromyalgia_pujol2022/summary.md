# Pujol et al. 2022 — central gain enhancement signatures in fibromyalgia and pain-sensitised osteoarthritis

According to PubMed, [Pujol et al. 2022](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13075-022-02942-3) compared cortical resting-state functional connectivity in two clinical pain-sensitisation populations against matched controls, with replication samples for each. Pain-sensitised knee osteoarthritis (pOA; n=31 with n=34 replication) showed weaker local functional connectivity in the insula. Fibromyalgia (FM; n=38 with n=63 replication) showed weaker connectivity distributed across sensorimotor cortex covering the cortical body representation. The authors interpret the pOA pattern as compatible with reduced insula neural activity following repeated activation — a metabolic-recovery signature on a peripherally-originated chronic pain. The FM pattern they read as central gain enhancement: weak sensory input compensated by over-amplification at the central level, producing the broad sensorimotor signature characteristic of fibromyalgia.

For SD-048 the relevance is at Source 2 (sensitisation spikes). SD-048 implements transient multiplicative amplification of `harm_obs_a` via Poisson-onset / exponential-decay events. The substrate-level claim is that gain-style perturbation of the body-state readout produces its own architectural signature distinct from damage-style perturbation. Pujol et al. provide the clinical end of this mechanism: when central gain is chronically elevated, the cortical interoceptive substrate carries an observable connectivity signature, broadly distributed and qualitatively different from the focal-recovery signature of peripherally-driven sensitisation. That dissociation is the cleanest empirical support I have found for the architectural commitment SD-048 makes — namely, that gain perturbation is real, observable, and not reducible to damage.

The mapping caveat is the timescale mismatch. Pujol et al. study patients with months-to-years sensitisation history; SD-048 Source 2 simulates transient events with `sensitisation_halflife` ~15 steps inside a single episode. Both fall in the same architectural family (multiplicative amplification of body-state readout) but the paper does not directly licence the per-episode Poisson dynamics SD-048 chose. A different parameter regime — e.g. high `sensitisation_rate` with very long halflife — would be the SD-048 substrate analogue of the chronic clinical signature. The paper supports the category but not the specific Poisson dynamics, which lowers mapping fidelity.

A second feature worth flagging. The paper distinguishes pOA (peripherally-originated chronic pain) from FM (central-gain-enhancement-dominant) at the cortical level. SD-048 in V3 does not differentiate these — Source 2 is one Poisson process and SD-022 limb damage is the only peripheral channel. If REE later wants to capture the pOA / FM dissociation, it would need either an additional substrate distinction or a parameterisation of Source 2 that decomposes peripheral-driven vs central-gain-driven amplification. SD-048 as currently written collapses these onto one stream. This is consistent with the V3-substrate-as-minimum-viable design philosophy but worth noting as a falsifier path — if REE's comparator cannot recover the pOA/FM dissociation given SD-048 Source 2 alone, that would be a substrate-ceiling diagnosis pointing to V4 enrichment.

Confidence assigned at 0.72. Sample sizes are adequate and replication is included, but the mapping fidelity is bounded by the timescale difference between chronic clinical sensitisation and per-episode Poisson events. The paper supports SD-048 Source 2 at the categorical level; it is not a cleaner mechanistic anchor than that.
