# Stefanov et al. 2020 — inflammation-induced fatigue mediated by insula effective connectivity

According to PubMed, [Stefanov et al. 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bpsc.2020.04.005) ran a double-blind, randomised, within-subject crossover study in twenty healthy men. Each participant received either a Salmonella typhi vaccine or a saline placebo in two separate sessions, then underwent a 3 T resting-state fMRI scan three hours post-injection. The vaccine session raised IL-6 and self-rated fatigue (Profile of Mood States). Spectral dynamic causal modelling on bilateral mid/posterior (viscerosensory) and anterior (visceromotor) insular regions showed augmented effective connectivity from right mid/posterior insula to bilateral anterior insula under inflammation, exceeding a 99% posterior threshold. The right-mid/posterior-to-left-anterior connection significantly mediated the inflammation-to-fatigue association in mediation analysis.

What this paper offers SD-048 is a clean experimental existence proof: a body-state perturbation that the agent did not produce — induced systemic inflammation — drives a detectable signature in the very interoceptive-circuit nodes the SD doc cites as the biological substrate the comparator must read from. SD-048 Source 2 (sensitisation spikes) is intended as a substrate analogue of exactly this kind of perturbation: transient, multiplicative, non-motor, with a temporal envelope (Poisson onset, exponential decay over ~15 steps). Stefanov et al. confirm that the structural shape of the prediction holds in vivo — not in REE's substrate, but in human insula — and that the resulting fatigue is the experiential downstream of the comparator-circuit change, not its input. That detail matters for SD-048's design choice to apply Source 2 multiplicatively to `harm_obs_a` rather than treating it as new tissue damage; the paper's mediation chain (inflammation → connectivity change → fatigue) puts the connectivity change between body-state perturbation and experiential output, the way SD-048 puts the comparator between body-noise and downstream comparator outputs.

Two caveats keep confidence under 0.8. First, the paper's Source 2 analogue is inflammation specifically, induced once at a known time. SD-048 Source 2 is a stochastic Poisson process meant to generalise across inflammatory and non-inflammatory transient amplifications. Mapping a single-induction RCT result onto a multi-event Poisson substrate is principled but adds transfer risk. Second, the sample is healthy males only; sex effects on inflammation-fatigue coupling are documented elsewhere and could in principle change the mediation chain. The paper supports the existence of a comparator-level signature in response to body-noise; it does not speak to the functional form of comparator-competence-vs-noise-amplitude (Asai-style inverted-U), which SD-048's 4-arm sweep is designed to test.

A second-order consequence worth noting: the directional asymmetry in the DCM (mid/posterior insula → anterior insula, not the reverse) supports a viscerosensory-first reading of where SD-048 perturbations should act. SD-048 implements this correctly by applying noise on the readout side of `harm_obs_a` (`_apply_interoceptive_noise` in `_get_observation_dict`), which is the substrate-level analogue of perturbing the viscerosensory input rather than the motor predictions. The paper does not adjudicate between trunk-sharing and stream-separation (ARC-058 vs ARC-033), but it does support the design choice in SD-048 to perturb at the readout layer.

Confidence assigned at 0.78. The RCT design, IL-6 confirmation, and explicit mediation analysis lift source quality. Mapping fidelity is high for the structural shape — body-noise produces detectable comparator-circuit signature — but transfer risk is moderate because SD-048's Source 2 generalisation is wider than what one inflammatory induction directly tests.
