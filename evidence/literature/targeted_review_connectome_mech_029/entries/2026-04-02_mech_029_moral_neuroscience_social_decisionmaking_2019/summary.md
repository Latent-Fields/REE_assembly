# Yoder & Decety (2019) — The Neuroscience of Morality and Social Decision-Making

## What the paper does

Yoder and Decety review the distributed neural networks that underlie moral judgment and social decision-making. They describe systems for reward learning/valuation, mental state understanding (theory of mind), and salience processing, and how these interact during moral cognition. Key regions include mPFC (DMN core), ventromedial PFC, amygdala, insula, and dorsolateral PFC. The review emphasizes that moral cognition is domain-general — using the same neural machinery as non-moral social cognition.

## Relevance to MECH-029

MECH-029 claims the reflective/DMN mode supports moral simulation via gated replay. This review establishes the neural substrates: the DMN (particularly mPFC and precuneus) is consistently activated during theory-of-mind reasoning and moral judgment, which involves simulating others' mental states and evaluating potential outcomes. This supports the biological plausibility of a "moral simulation" mode.

However, the review's framing is competitive dual-process (cognitive control vs emotional response) rather than gated-replay. The neuroscience suggests moral cognition involves real-time competition between systems, not a discrete gated simulation episode.

## A challenge for MECH-029

The domain-general nature of moral cognition raises a question: does MECH-029 need a dedicated "moral simulation" mode, or is moral evaluation simply what happens when the general DMN simulation capacity is applied to morally relevant content? REE's architecture may be overspecifying the mechanism by proposing a distinct mode (with gating), when the biology suggests moral simulation is a natural use case of a general-purpose simulation capacity.

This is worth discussing in governance: should MECH-029 be reframed as "DMN mode supports general simulation including moral reasoning" rather than "DMN mode supports moral simulation specifically"?

Decent source quality. Supports DMN involvement in moral cognition but not the specific gated-replay mechanism. Confidence moderated by the framing mismatch.
