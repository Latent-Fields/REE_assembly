# Ormond, Serka & Johansen 2023 -- Aversive Spatial Learning Produces Harm-Zone Remapping with Enhanced SWR Reactivation

**Source:** Ormond J, Serka SA, Johansen JP. "Enhanced Reactivation of Remapping Place Cells during Aversive Learning." *Journal of Neuroscience* 43(12):2153-2167, 2023. DOI: [10.1523/JNEUROSCI.1450-22.2022](https://doi.org/10.1523/JNEUROSCI.1450-22.2022). PMID: 36596695

**Claim evidenced:** MECH-099

---

## What the paper did

Ormond et al. trained rats on a multi-arm maze spatial navigation task requiring them to reach a food goal while avoiding maze arms where aversive stimuli were delivered. The aversive arms were spatially fixed across sessions. CA1 place cell ensembles were recorded throughout learning using tetrode arrays. Researchers tracked which cells remapped during task learning, compared reactivation rates of remapping vs stable cells during SWR events, and assessed how harm avoidance behaviour correlated with the spatial representation changes.

## Key findings relevant to REE

Task learning induced partial remapping in CA1 place cells. Remapping cells (those that changed their spatial firing fields during acquisition of the avoidance task) were preferentially recruited into SWR events compared to stable cells. This preferential reactivation occurred despite remapping and stable cells having similar firing rates during maze navigation, indicating that the SWR recruitment was not simply driven by activity level but by the representational change associated with aversive learning.

The multi-arm structure with fixed aversive zones means the task approximates ongoing exposure to spatially stable harm regions across sessions -- closer to the residue field accumulation scenario than a single acute shock paradigm.

## Translation to REE

MECH-099 requires that the E3 hippocampal system maintains a persistent spatial map of harm accumulation and that this map is used to generate context-sensitive trajectories. Ormond et al. provide two direct supporting findings:

First, aversive spatial experience causes partial remapping of the hippocampal spatial representation -- the map is modified by harm. Second, the harm-modified representation (remapping cells) is preferentially consolidated via SWR replay. The fixed aversive zone design means the harm-map updates accumulate across sessions, which is the accumulation mechanism MECH-099 requires.

The combination of this paper with Blair et al. (2023) provides convergent evidence from two independent paradigms: (a) single acute shock in familiar environment, and (b) ongoing aversive zone navigation in a goal-directed task. Both show the same mechanism: harm-associated place cells remap and are preferentially replayed.

## Limitations and caveats

Remapping is partial, which could indicate that the hippocampal harm map is noisy or incomplete. MECH-099 may require a more complete representation than partial remapping provides. The multi-session design includes an ongoing food reward goal in addition to harm avoidance -- the residue field update could be confounded with goal approach encoding. The study does not measure whether the remapped representation is causally used for avoidance path planning (the link from harm map to trajectory generation in MECH-099). Rodent CA1 tetrode recording; generalisation to REE's E3/hippocampal architecture requires inference.

## Confidence reasoning

Complementary evidence to Blair et al. with better ecological validity (ongoing aversive zone navigation rather than single shock).
