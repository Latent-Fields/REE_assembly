# Kool, Gershman & Cushman 2017 — cost-benefit arbitration as multi-variable combination

The Kool, Gershman & Cushman 2017 Psychological Science paper extends the model-based vs model-free arbitration literature in a way directly relevant to REE's MECH-312 question. Earlier arbitration work (Daw 2005, Lee 2014, both covered in Pull 2) established that the brain weights model-based and model-free systems by their relative reliability — when one is more uncertain than the other, the more reliable system gets more control. Kool/Gershman/Cushman demonstrate that this is incomplete: the arbitration also depends on a cost-benefit calculation. Subjects increase model-based control specifically when (a) the additional accuracy it provides matters (reward stakes are high), and (b) the cognitive cost of running it is justified by the accuracy gain. Under low-stakes conditions, even high-uncertainty model-free is preferred to costly model-based; under high-stakes conditions, the same uncertainty estimate yields the opposite choice. The combination rule is non-trivially-non-additive — uncertainty alone does not predict arbitration; the interaction with stakes does.

This is the strongest existing-literature anchor for the R4 question this pull is supposed to settle: how does MECH-312 combine multiple modulating variables (uncertainty, practice/maturity, diversity pressure, V_s freshness, cost) into arbitration weights across 3+ channels? Pull 2 R4 surfaced uncertainty as the PRIMARY variable with practice/maturity as SECONDARY but did not specify combination form. Kool/Gershman/Cushman 2017 makes a strong case that the combination is multiplicative-gate rather than additive-logit: variables modulate each other's contribution to the final arbitration, not just sum into a logit. The biological substrate (dlPFC for cost computation, ACC for the comparison) is consistent with the standard arbitration-substrate literature; what is novel in this paper is the empirical demonstration that multi-variable combination is the dominant pattern.

For REE's MECH-312 architecture decision, this entry recommends a multiplicative-gate combination rule with at least the following structure: each channel's arbitration weight is a product of (channel-specific reliability) × (modulating-variable factors that gate the channel's contribution). The modulating factors come from the various dimensions REE has identified across pulls — V_s freshness from the current region (this pull's D3), practice/maturity from training history (Pull 2 R4), affective-stream load (this pull's D2), simulation-mode tag (this pull's D1, modulating whether write rules are activated at all), and uncertainty/reliability per channel (Daw 2005 / Lee 2014). The multiplicative form means that any one factor going to zero can effectively suppress a channel's contribution, while the channel can still be active when all factors are moderate.

There are two transfer risks worth recording. First, the Kool/Gershman/Cushman 2017 result is for two controllers (MB vs MF). REE's MECH-312 has 3+ channels, and the multiplicative-gate combination rule may need to extend in non-trivial ways when there are more competitors — the relevant literature (Kool 2018 follow-up, Boorman et al 2009 multi-option choice) has begun to address this but has not fully settled it. The cleanest extension is per-channel multiplicative gates feeding a softmax across channels, which preserves the multi-variable modulation structure while accommodating arbitrary channel counts. Second, the 'cost' dimension that drives the Kool/Gershman/Cushman experiments is cognitive/metabolic cost; REE's modulating variables (V_s freshness, practice maturity, diversity pressure) do not all map cleanly to cost. The right reading is that 'cost' is one instance of a broader pattern of multi-variable modulation, not the unique driver REE should commit to.

For the R4 verdict in the synthesis, this entry supports MULTIPLICATIVE-GATE as the recommended default combination rule for MECH-312, with the explicit caveat that the V3 substrate experiments should test this against an additive-logit baseline. Confidence is set to 0.74 (supports) — strong on the principle that multi-variable combination is the right pattern, with calibrated transfer risk for the channel-count and modulator-set extensions REE will need to make.
