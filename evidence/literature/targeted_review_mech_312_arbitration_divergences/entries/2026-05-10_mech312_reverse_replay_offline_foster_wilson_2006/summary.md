# Foster & Wilson 2006 — reverse replay as a discriminable simulation-mode signature

The Foster & Wilson 2006 Nature paper is the foundational demonstration of awake-quiescent hippocampal replay in a temporally-reversed direction. Rats ran short linear tracks for food reward; during the brief pauses between laps, place-cell ensembles emitted compressed sequences of activity that recapitulated the just-completed trajectory in reverse temporal order — the place visited last fired first. The phenomenon was repeated across many pauses, was not random in its sequence content, and was temporally compressed (sequences that took 5-10 seconds during waking running were replayed in roughly 100-200 ms during the quiescent state).

The reason this paper anchors REE's MECH-094 / candidate MECH-319 question is narrow but load-bearing. REE's claim is that simulation-driven and replay-driven rule applications carry a categorical tag that downstream write machinery uses to suppress arbitration-weight updates which would otherwise fire if the same rule were applied during waking action. For that mechanism to be biologically plausible, the substrate has to provide a discriminable signal that downstream regions could in principle read off. Foster & Wilson demonstrate that exactly such a signal exists at the level of the hippocampal output: the temporal direction of place-cell firing during awake-quiescent replay is structurally inverted relative to the temporal direction during waking running. A downstream cortical region receiving hippocampal input could in principle decode the temporal-direction signature and gate its synaptic write rules accordingly. The signal is real and persistent.

What this paper does NOT show is the functional half — that any downstream region actually USES the temporal-direction signature as a write-gate. The 2006 paper is a substrate-availability paper, not a functional-utilisation paper. The functional question is open: it is consistent with REE's proposed mechanism (a real categorical write-gate), with a continuous-modulation alternative (the temporal compression simply produces less downstream depolarisation, which produces weaker plasticity), and with an intermediate position (some specific downstream pathways gate writes by replay signature while others do not). The Foster & Wilson result narrows the space of biologically plausible MECH-319 implementations without confirming any specific one.

Two caveats restrict how strongly this entry supports REE. First, reverse-replay is one of several documented SWR-time signatures — forward replay, preplay (replay of trajectories not yet experienced), and mixed-direction sequences also occur. Whether these cluster cleanly into a "simulation mode vs not" binary, as REE's categorical-tag framing assumes, is empirically unsettled. The phenomenology of replay is more heterogeneous than a clean binary tag would predict. Second, the 2006 study was done on a behaviourally-driven 1D track; whether the same temporal-direction signature obtains for the kinds of tasks REE substrate would face (object choice under harm pressure, multi-context arbitration) is an extrapolation that would need direct evidence to support.

For the R1 verdict in this pull's synthesis, this entry counts as the strongest single piece of evidence that MECH-094's substrate-level precondition (a discriminable replay vs waking signature) is real and biologically available. The substrate is there; REE's claim that it is exploited as a write-gate remains REE-novel.
