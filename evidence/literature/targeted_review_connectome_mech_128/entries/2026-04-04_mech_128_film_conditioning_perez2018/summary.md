# Summary: Perez et al. 2018 — FiLM: Visual Reasoning with a General Conditioning Layer

**Entry ID:** 2026-04-04_mech_128_film_conditioning_perez2018
**Claim:** MECH-128 (e1.goal_context_conditioning)
**Evidence direction:** supports | **Confidence:** 0.70

---

The FiLM paper by Perez and colleagues establishes a general ML result that bears directly on MECH-128: an arbitrary conditioning input can be used to apply a learned affine transformation (scale and shift, gamma and beta) to a neural network's intermediate feature maps, and this is sufficient to radically alter the network's behavior as a function of that conditioning input. On the CLEVR visual reasoning benchmark, which requires multi-step compositional reasoning about images in response to natural language questions, FiLM-conditioned networks halved state-of-the-art error at the time of publication. The conditioning signal does not merely bias the output layer -- it permeates the network's computation at every layer touched by a FiLM layer, dynamically reshaping what the network is effectively computing.

MECH-128's proposal is a recurrent instantiation of this exact principle. E1's LSTM hidden state, which tracks world dynamics over time to generate predictive trajectories, should receive z_goal_latent as a conditioning input at each recurrent step -- either by concatenation to the input vector (the simplest implementation) or via an affine modulation of the hidden state (the FiLM-style implementation). Without this, E1 computes predictions that are conditioned only on the observed dynamics of the world, not on where the system is trying to go. The FiLM paper shows that this kind of conditioning, when properly implemented, is not merely a modest regulariser -- it is capable of restructuring the effective computation of the network wholesale.

The honest caveat is that FiLM operates on feedforward CNN layers, not on LSTM recurrent hidden states. These are architecturally distinct: a CNN feature map at layer k is a static intermediate representation, while an LSTM hidden state at step t is a compressed history of the entire trajectory seen so far, iteratively updated by gating mechanisms. Injecting a conditioning signal into a CNN layer is a one-shot operation at that layer; injecting it into an LSTM recurrent step creates a persistent influence that accumulates over the entire sequence. This may be more powerful than FiLM in the limit (the goal context is always present), but it may also create training instability or interference with the LSTM's other gating functions. The FiLM paper provides existence-proof support for the principle but does not address these recurrent-specific dynamics.

There is a secondary caveat about the temporal structure of goal conditioning. In the FiLM architecture, the conditioning signal (question encoded by a separate RNN) is computed once and applied to all layers. MECH-128 requires step-wise conditioning -- the goal state available at each LSTM step may in principle change as z_goal_latent evolves, though in most REE training scenarios it will be relatively stable across an episode. This is not a fundamental incompatibility but it means the FiLM experimental setup is simpler than the full MECH-128 implementation.

Overall, this paper makes the strongest available ML-side argument for MECH-128's architectural feasibility. It confirms that feature-level conditioning by an external context signal is an effective general mechanism for making a network's computation context-dependent, which is exactly what MECH-128 claims E1 requires to generate goal-shaped rather than goal-agnostic predictive trajectories.
