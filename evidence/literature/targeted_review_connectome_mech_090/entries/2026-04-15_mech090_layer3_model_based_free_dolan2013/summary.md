# Summary: Dolan & Dayan 2013 -- Goals and Habits in the Brain

**Citation:** Dolan RJ, Dayan P. Goals and habits in the brain. *Neuron*. 2013;80(2):312-325. doi:10.1016/j.neuron.2013.09.007

## Core Claim

Dolan and Dayan subsume habitual/goal-directed and model-free/model-based distinctions under a unified spectrum of "reflexive versus reflective" decision-making. Both systems compute in parallel, but an arbitration mechanism (proposed to involve vmPFC) weights their contributions to output based on relative reliability, computational cost, and uncertainty. This is NOT a framework of independent parallel channels -- it is a framework of continuous integration with context-dependent weighting.

## Evidence on Parallel vs. Competitive Architecture

The paper explicitly frames these as parallel computational processes: model-free systems compute cached values from prior experience while model-based systems construct and evaluate outcome representations. Both compute simultaneously. However, the critical point for the Layer 3 question is that Dolan and Dayan propose ARBITRATION rather than independent parallel output. The vmPFC integrates both systems' signals, and the weighting of model-based versus model-free control is a function of task context, cognitive load, overtraining, and reliability.

This arbitration framing implies that during committed deliberation (E3-dominated, high beta, high model-based weighting), the model-free/habitual contribution to motor output is diminished -- not absent, but downweighted by the arbitration mechanism. An urgency signal could shift this weighting back toward model-free, but within the arbitration framework this is a graded adjustment rather than a channel-switch.

## Relevance to the Layer 3 Architectural Question

The spectrum-plus-arbitration model is the primary theoretical challenge to Interpretation A (fully independent parallel habitual channel during E3 commitment). If Dolan and Dayan are right about vmPFC arbitration, then MECH-090's beta gate does not merely gate one channel while leaving another free -- it is part of a broader commitment-state that shifts the arbitration weighting system-wide. Habitual responses during committed deliberation would be suppressed not by a direct circuit gate but by the arbitration mechanism that prioritizes model-based planning.

This makes the Layer 3 question more subtle: even without a dedicated hardware gate on the habitual channel, the arbitration mechanism would reduce habitual output probability during committed deliberation. For a sufficiently salient threat, the urgency signal would shift the arbitration, restoring habitual output -- functionally equivalent to an urgency interrupt but implemented through weighting rather than channel-switching.

## Key Limitation for REE

The paper focuses on decision-making and action SELECTION, not on the motor EXECUTION phase of committed sequences. MECH-090 targets the execution phase (beta elevated during committed trajectory, not just during planning). The spectrum/arbitration model may accurately describe pre-commitment arbitration without describing what happens during mid-sequence execution when beta is already elevated. The biological beta elevation during committed motor sequences (as documented in SMA by Hosaka et al. 2016) may reflect a distinct execution-phase commitment mechanism that operates downstream of the pre-commitment arbitration described by Dolan and Dayan.

## Layer 3 Verdict from This Paper

Weakens Interpretation A (independent parallel habitual channel). The spectrum-plus-arbitration framework predicts that during E3 commitment, habitual outputs are downweighted by the same system that implements commitment -- not independently maintained by a separate channel. An urgency interrupt (Interpretation B) would work by shifting the arbitration, which is mechanistically equivalent to releasing a beta gate from a functional perspective. This supports Interpretation B as the biologically simpler account, though it does not rule out the possibility of a separate DLS motor output path bypassing the arbitration mechanism entirely.
