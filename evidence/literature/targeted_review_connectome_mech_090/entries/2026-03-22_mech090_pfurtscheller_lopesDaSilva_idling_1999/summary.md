## Claims Tested

- `MECH-090`

## Source

- Pfurtscheller G, Lopes da Silva FH (1999). *Event-related EEG/MEG synchronization and desynchronization: basic principles*. Clinical Neurophysiology.
- DOI: `10.1016/S1388-2457(99)00141-8`
- URL: `https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1388245799001418`

## Source Wording

Alpha and beta oscillations in sensorimotor cortex undergo event-related desynchronization (ERD) before and during voluntary movement and event-related synchronization (ERS) during rest or task-irrelevant periods. Beta ERS (post-movement beta rebound, PMBR) follows movement completion, reflecting return to the idling state. Under the idling rhythm hypothesis, elevated beta indicates a cortical area is not engaged in active processing -- beta synchronization is the default resting signature, desynchronization marks active engagement. This framework was foundational for the ERD/ERS field and remains influential in sensorimotor beta interpretation.

## REE Translation

MECH-090 claims that beta is elevated *during* a committed action sequence and gates E3 policy output. The idling interpretation creates a direct conflict at the sensorimotor level: if elevated beta = cortical disengagement, then high beta during ongoing commitment would indicate the motor circuit is off-line, not actively maintaining the plan. The post-movement beta rebound (PMBR) in the idling account is simply recovery from active engagement, not a reinstatement of a maintained state. This is the principal empirical challenge to MECH-090's framing.

The weakening is genuinely bounded: the idling account applies most directly to primary sensorimotor cortex, and MECH-090's gating mechanism is located in prefrontal and BG circuits where the idling account is poorly established. Subsequent work (Engel & Fries 2010; Helfrich & Knight 2019) explicitly argues against the idling account for PFC and BG beta and rehabilitates the status-quo/maintenance framing. This entry documents a real but circuit-scoped challenge rather than a broad refutation.

## Caveat

The idling account has been refined and partially superseded in the 25 years since this paper. The distinction between sensorimotor and prefrontal/BG beta is now well established; the idling account is most defensible for primary motor and somatosensory cortex and less defensible at the prefrontal and striatal levels most relevant to MECH-090. The challenge is recorded here because it represents a genuine alternative interpretation of the beta-elevation data that any strong version of MECH-090 must account for.
