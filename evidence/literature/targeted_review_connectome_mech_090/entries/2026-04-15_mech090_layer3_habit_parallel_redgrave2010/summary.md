# Summary: Redgrave et al. 2010 -- Goal-directed and habitual control in the BG

**Citation:** Redgrave P, Rodriguez M, Smith Y, Rodriguez-Oroz MC, Lehericy S, Bergman H, Agid Y, DeLong MR, Obeso JA. Goal-directed and habitual control in the basal ganglia: implications for Parkinson's disease. *Nat Rev Neurosci*. 2010;11(11):760-772. doi:10.1038/nrn2915

## Core Claim

The basal ganglia contain two anatomically segregated corticostriatal circuits: the rostromedial (associative) striatum supporting goal-directed action control, and the caudolateral (sensorimotor) striatum supporting habitual stimulus-response control. These circuits are topographically distinct, receiving inputs from different prefrontal/premotor and sensorimotor cortical regions respectively, and project through segregated BG-thalamo-cortical loops back to their origin territories.

## Evidence on Parallel vs. Competitive Architecture

The paper explicitly describes both parallel operation and dynamic competition. On the parallel side: experienced behaviour commonly recruits both circuits simultaneously. The authors offer the canonical example of an experienced driver steering habitually (caudolateral/sensorimotor circuit) while carrying on a goal-directed conversation (rostromedial/associative circuit). These outputs are not mutually exclusive -- they apply to different motor domains simultaneously. Skills decompose into "sequenced combinations of goal-directed and habitual control working in concert." This directly supports Interpretation A (parallel channels).

On the competitive side: when the same motor domain is contested, the systems compete. Under stress, habitual responding dominates. Unexpected events trigger "immediate switch from habitual to goal-directed control." Lesion studies show that removing dorsomedial striatum (DMS) pushes the system toward habitual expression; removing dorsolateral striatum (DLS) prevents habit formation even after overtraining.

## Relevance to the Layer 3 Architectural Question

The Layer 3 question asks whether MECH-090's beta-gated E3 commitment channel should have a separate E1-direct habitual channel running in parallel (Interpretation A) or whether urgency interrupts are the only escape mechanism (Interpretation B).

Redgrave et al. strongly suggest that the biology does NOT implement pure mutual suppression between deliberative and habitual circuits at the circuit level. The anatomical segregation means the two circuits can co-activate in DIFFERENT motor domains -- steering (habit) and speaking (goal-directed) are genuinely concurrent. This biological organization supports Interpretation A for the case where habitual and deliberate actions target different effectors.

However, for the case where habitual and deliberate channels contest THE SAME motor output -- the most likely scenario in a committed E3 trajectory encountering a threat -- the paper predicts competition rather than free parallel expression. The competition appears to arise through resource contention (shared thalamic output gates, shared motor neuron pools) rather than direct circuit-level suppression.

## REE Implication

The evidence supports nuanced Interpretation A: a habitual channel (dorsolateral/E1-direct equivalent) remains anatomically and computationally active during deliberative commitment, but its behavioral expression depends on whether it contests the same effector domain as the committed sequence. For the threat scenario where the same effector is at stake, Interpretation B (urgency interrupt releasing the beta gate) may be the biologically appropriate mechanism because the competition would resolve through the stronger signal anyway. If a different effector is available, the habitual response runs independently without needing to release the gate. This means Layer 3 (separate habitual channel) is biologically plausible but its functional benefit within the REE threat scenario is conditional on whether the habit and the trajectory target different motor channels.
