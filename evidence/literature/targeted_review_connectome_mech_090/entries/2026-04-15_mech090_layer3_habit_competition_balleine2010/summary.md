# Summary: Balleine & O'Doherty 2010 -- Corticostriatal determinants of goal-directed and habitual action

**Citation:** Balleine BW, O'Doherty JP. Human and rodent homologies in action control: corticostriatal determinants of goal-directed and habitual action. *Neuropsychopharmacology*. 2010;35(1):48-69. doi:10.1038/npp.2009.131

## Core Claim

Goal-directed and habitual action control are supported by homologous corticostriatal circuits in rodents and humans. Prelimbic/medial PFC projects to dorsomedial striatum (DMS; anterior caudate in humans) for goal-directed control; sensorimotor cortex projects to dorsolateral striatum (DLS; posterior putamen in humans) for habitual control. These form anatomically distinct but interacting networks.

## Evidence on Parallel Computation

The key contribution for the Layer 3 question is the finding that both DMS and DLS compute values simultaneously, not sequentially. Human fMRI shows activity in both anterior caudate and posterior putamen covarying with goal-directed (model-based) and habitual (model-free) values respectively, even when only one system's output was selected for behavior. The vmPFC appears to have access to both value signals "consistent with parallel, and independent, operation of model-based and model-free valuation systems." This means the neural computation underlying habit is NOT suppressed during goal-directed behavior -- it runs in parallel and simply loses the competition for motor output.

Behaviorally, the systems appear all-or-none ("their control appears to be all or none") but this is a downstream selection effect: both systems are always computing, and the one that wins action selection produces the observable behavior. The authors explicitly note this behavioral appearance masks underlying parallel computation: "evidence has started to accumulate suggesting that they may at times compete and at others cooperate in the selection and subsequent evaluation of actions."

## Relevance to Layer 3 Architectural Question

Balleine and O'Doherty's framework implies a specific answer to the Layer 3 question. The parallel-computation finding at the valuation stage means the DLS/habitual circuit is always computing. During an E3-committed sequence, the question becomes whether beta elevation suppresses only the DMS-to-action-selection pathway (MECH-090's claim) or also suppresses DLS-to-motor-output.

The paper does not directly test this because the motor execution stage during committed sequences is not their focus. But the parallel valuation finding implies that even if MECH-090's beta gate blocks E3/DMS output, the DLS/habitual circuit continues evaluating stimuli and producing candidate responses. Whether those responses reach motor output depends on whether beta-gated BG mechanisms apply to DLS as well.

This creates an important distinction: beta-mediated gating of E3/DMS output (MECH-090) does not necessarily mean beta-mediated suppression of ALL BG output. The DLS/sensorimotor loop may have different beta dynamics during committed E3 sequences. If DLS beta is NOT elevated during E3 commitment, habitual responses from DLS could reach motor output independently -- the biological substrate for Layer 3.

## REE Implication

Balleine & O'Doherty support Interpretation A at the computation level (parallel neural evaluation) but are ambiguous at the motor output level. For REE Layer 3, the critical unanswered question is whether MECH-090's beta gate applies selectively to the E3/DMS channel or globally to all BG output. If the DLS circuit has its own independent motor output path during elevated beta (as suggested by the driving-during-conversation example in Redgrave et al.), then Layer 3 is biologically justified. The authors' emphasis on cortico-BG network context ("cooperation or competition depends not only on local striatal interactions but on the cortico-BG network") suggests this is a dynamic question -- commitment-related signals propagating through the network could either spare or suppress DLS-to-motor-output pathways depending on PFC gating configuration.
