# LaBar & Cabeza 2006 -- retrieval-phase selectivity of amygdala-mediated emotional memory

The LaBar and Cabeza 2006 review in Nature Reviews Neuroscience is the canonical synthesis of emotional-memory neuroscience in humans, and the reason I reached for it in preference to Mather and Sutherland 2011 is that it treats retrieval as a first-class stage rather than as a downstream consequence of encoding. The central claim is narrower than the headline "arousal enhances memory" slogan: emotional content does not simply sit higher in a uniformly strengthened memory trace. It wins a competition. The amygdala, interacting with medial temporal and prefrontal regions, re-weights which parts of an event are retrieved, and the strongest evidence is the dissociation between central/gist/arousing detail and peripheral detail -- the former is enhanced, the latter is unchanged or actively suppressed.

The selectivity evidence I care about for BLAAnalog is neuropsychological rather than imaging. Patients with amygdala damage retain more-or-less intact declarative memory on average, but they lose the specific advantage for the emotional-central detail of a scene. That is a content-selective deficit, not a global one, and it is the cleanest possible argument that the amygdala's contribution at retrieval is a vector reweighting of which traces come up, not a scalar gain on the entire retrieval pipeline. If the amygdala were a uniform multiplicative gate, the lesion would reduce all memory proportionally; it does not. Behavioural trade-off studies (weapon focus, the Cahill slide-show paradigms) converge on the same point: memory for the high-arousal central element is elevated while memory for concurrent peripheral detail is depressed -- a zero-sum or near-zero-sum pattern that looks much more like arousal-biased competition than a broadcast signal.

On persistence, LaBar and Cabeza collate evidence that the emotional advantage grows with delay rather than shrinking. Several of the studies they cite (and Ritchey, Dolcos and Cabeza 2008 is the direct follow-up on this point) show the amygdala-MTL functional connectivity contribution to recollection becoming proportionally more important over one-week compared to 20-minute delays, while the amygdala activity advantage itself is delay-invariant. The implication for a mechanistic model is non-trivial: the retrieval bias is not simply whatever the BLA is doing right now at retrieval time -- it is also encoded into the trace at write time, so that older emotional traces remain preferentially accessible even as neutral traces decay. This argues against a purely transient retrieval-time gate and in favour of a mechanism where an arousal tag is written into each trace at encoding and then consulted (and re-weighted) at every subsequent retrieval.

Magnitudes are sparser than I would like. The review does not supply a single effect-size table; it synthesises across primary studies with different paradigms. The ballpark that emerges from the cited fMRI and behavioural work is a ~30-100% enhancement of central/gist memory for high-arousal items over matched neutral items, with compensatory depression of peripheral detail on the order of 10-30%. Those numbers are noisy and paradigm-dependent, and REE should treat them as defaults to be tuned rather than as targets. What is robust across the cited work is the sign of the effect, the content-selectivity (central boosted, peripheral depressed or unchanged), and the direction of the delay dependence (advantage grows, not shrinks).

## Quantitative defaults for BLAAnalog retrieval_bias

- **Selectivity (content-specific vs uniform):** Content-selective. The retrieval_bias must be a **vector** that reweights HippocampalModule's proposal set per-trace according to arousal/threat-relevance tags written at encoding, not a scalar that multiplies the whole proposal distribution. Implementing it as a scalar is a design failure mode -- it cannot reproduce the central/peripheral dissociation that is the signature behavioural result and the clean neuropsychological finding from amygdala lesions.
- **Bias magnitude range:** Per-trace weight multiplier in the range `w_i = 1 + alpha * arousal_tag_i`, with `alpha` defaulting to the interval ~0.3 to 1.0 (i.e. high-arousal traces retrieved roughly 1.3x to 2.0x more strongly than neutral baseline). A sum-constrained / zero-sum normalisation should produce mild (~10-30%) suppression of untagged traces, mirroring arousal-biased competition.
- **Persistence:** Not transient within a single retrieval cycle. The arousal tag is written into the trace at encoding and consulted at every subsequent retrieval; additionally, the relative contribution of BLAAnalog to retrieval weighting should grow modestly with trace age (amygdala-MTL connectivity contribution increases from short to long delays), so BLAAnalog's weight vector should be a function of both current arousal state AND trace-age, not arousal alone.
