# Dolcos, LaBar & Cabeza (2004) — Amygdala-MTL fMRI: Human Evidence for MECH-074 Write-Strength Modulation

**Claims tested:** MECH-074, MECH-073
**Evidence direction:** supports | **Confidence:** 0.78

---

## What the paper did

Dolcos, LaBar, and Cabeza used event-related fMRI to measure neural activity while healthy participants encoded emotional and neutral pictures from the International Affective Picture System. The key design feature was the explicit modulation of arousal: pictures were rated on emotional intensity both before and during scanning, allowing the researchers to test whether the amygdala's activation during encoding predicted subsequent memory performance specifically for emotional material.

The results confirmed the modulation hypothesis. During encoding of emotional pictures, both amygdala and hippocampus showed greater activation than during neutral encoding. More importantly, the *functional connectivity* between amygdala and hippocampus during emotional encoding — measured as the correlation of their activation patterns across trials — predicted subsequent memory performance for emotional events. Arousal, not just valence, drove this effect: highly arousing pictures produced the strongest amygdala-MTL coupling and the best subsequent memory.

## Key findings for MECH-074

This paper is the human fMRI counterpart to McGaugh's (2004) animal pharmacology work. It provides four things MECH-074 needs:

**Write-strength modulation in humans:** The amygdala does not simply activate during emotional events — its activation predicts hippocampal encoding success. The coupling between BLA and HPC at encoding is the mechanism by which arousal translates into stronger hippocampal memory traces. In REE terms: a high-harm or high-benefit event produces amygdala activation, which then augments hippocampal encoding strength via the BLA→HPC projection, producing deeper attractor basins in the viability map.

**Arousal, not valence, as the key:** The enhancement effect is driven by arousal (how intense the emotion is), not just by valence (positive vs. negative). This is critical for REE: both threatening and highly rewarding events should produce strong amygdala write-head activation because both are high-arousal. The asymmetry in REE's architecture (harm signals are more salient than benefit signals) would require a separate mechanism beyond arousal per se — this paper does not provide that asymmetry but also does not contradict it.

**Human evidence:** Unlike McGaugh's animal pharmacology, Dolcos et al. directly confirm the amygdala-MTL coupling mechanism in human participants. This closes the species transfer gap and establishes MECH-074 as a human-relevant mechanism.

**The "write not store" architecture:** Dolcos et al. demonstrate that the amygdala's role is to *modulate* HPC encoding, not to store the emotional content itself. Patients with amygdala lesions lose the emotional memory enhancement but retain the ability to form neutral memories. This confirms MECH-074's characterisation of the amygdala as a write head (modulator), not a storage device — consistent with ARC-007's "hippocampus stores trajectories" specification.

## Limitations

fMRI connectivity is correlational — the paper demonstrates that amygdala-MTL coupling at encoding correlates with subsequent memory but cannot establish amygdala causation. The MTL region of interest includes hippocampus, perirhinal cortex, and parahippocampal gyrus; hippocampus-specific effects are not isolated. The temporal resolution of fMRI cannot separate online encoding modulation from post-encoding consolidation consolidation — it is possible the amygdala's influence operates primarily via post-encoding NE-mediated consolidation (as McGaugh's pharmacology suggests) rather than during the encoding event itself. The question of how the BLA→CA1 write signal is implemented at the cellular level (via BTSP plateau potentials, per Bittner 2017) is not addressed here — this paper establishes the systems-level effect; the cellular mechanism comes from animal electrophysiology.
