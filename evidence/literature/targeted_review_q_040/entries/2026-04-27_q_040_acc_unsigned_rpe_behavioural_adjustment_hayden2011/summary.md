# Hayden et al. 2011 -- Surprise signals in dACC drive behavioural adjustment, and depend on upstream expectation clarity

Hayden, Heilbronner, Pearson and Platt recorded single units from macaque dorsal anterior cingulate cortex (dACC) while two monkeys performed a probabilistic choice task. The behavioural finding is straightforward: trial-to-trial preference shifts tracked outcome surprisingness rather than valence -- both unexpectedly large and unexpectedly small rewards moved the next choice. The neural finding mirrored this. dACC neurons responded more strongly to rewards (and reward omissions) when those outcomes were surprising, and the magnitude of the neural surprise signal predicted the magnitude of the upcoming behavioural adjustment. The authors argue this is best read as an unsigned reward prediction error signal -- distinct from the signed RPE carried by midbrain dopamine neurons -- and they explicitly contrast it with conflict-monitoring accounts of dACC.

The manipulation that matters most for Q-040 is the "hidden probability" condition. When the upstream cues that allowed monkeys to form an expectation about reward probability were withheld, dACC responses to the same reward outcomes were reduced. Surprise signal scales with how well-formed the upstream prediction is; when there is no clear prediction, there is no clear surprise. Behaviourally, the trial-to-trial adjustment signature also weakens. This is the empirical pattern Q-040 is asking about, almost in a different vocabulary: a downstream control structure that *can* drive behaviour but whose drive is contingent on whether the upstream prediction was credibly formed in the first place.

For REE, the translation is to SD-032b (dACC adaptive-control consumer) and the MECH-269b extension. Q-040 articulates a candidate failure mode -- dACC is wired, the override fires, PAG releases up, and yet approach_commit remains zero across all arms of EXQ-483/483a. The architectural prediction is that without V_s gating reaching E1/E2 forward predictions, E2's harm-rollouts ground on stale streams, and the precision-weighted PE that dACC sees ends up self-consistent enough that no policy revision fires. Hayden's "probabilities hidden" condition is not literally V_s gating, but it is the closest experimental analogue I have found in primate electrophysiology to the situation where the upstream prediction is not credibly anchored. The consequence in Hayden's monkeys is exactly the consequence Q-040 predicts in V3: a damped surprise signal and a flatter behavioural-adjustment curve.

The mapping caveat is that this is an extrapolation. Hayden manipulated *cue availability*, not the staleness of a recurrent forward model. If the dACC response collapse in the hidden condition is driven by something more peripheral than V_s-style state validity (e.g., simple cue-evoked activity), the analogue weakens. I have set mapping_fidelity at 0.65 to reflect this. The data also do not speak to whether *adding* V_s gating to E1/E2 rescues approach_commit -- that is a forward inference Q-040 itself proposes to test as a factorial.

Confidence is 0.72 overall. The source is high quality (single-unit primate, canonical paper), the mapping is conceptually clean for the "wired-but-inert" failure pattern, but the gap between a probability-cue manipulation and a forward-model anchor signal is real and should not be papered over. This entry counts as supporting evidence for the architectural reading of Q-040 -- it does not by itself license promotion of MECH-269b.
