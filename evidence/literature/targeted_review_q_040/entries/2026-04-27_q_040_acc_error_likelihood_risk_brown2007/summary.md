# Brown & Braver 2007 -- ACC encodes expected risk as a function of upstream predictions

Brown and Braver tested an extension of their earlier error-likelihood model of ACC function. The original model predicted that ACC would scale with the perceived probability of making an error on the upcoming trial. The extension predicts that ACC should scale not just with error likelihood but with the *product* of error likelihood and predicted error magnitude -- a quantity they call expected risk. They ran an incentive-change signal task and showed that ACC BOLD activity matched both predictions: it tracked error likelihood, and it scaled with predicted error consequence magnitude when likelihood was held fixed.

The reason this paper matters for Q-040 is structural rather than mechanistic. Brown and Braver's model is explicit: ACC is computing a function of *upstream* predictions about future task events. The ACC signal is not a primary observation; it is a derived quantity that depends entirely on whether the upstream predictions feeding into it are credible. If those upstream predictions are stale, the ACC signal will be stale in a way that looks internally consistent. There will be no detectable inconsistency for the system to flag -- because the inconsistency is not in ACC's computation, it is in ACC's input.

This is the architectural pattern Q-040 is articulating. The notes for Q-040 say it directly: "dACC sees a consistent-looking precision-weighted PE, no policy revision fires, and freeze persists." Brown and Braver provide a template for why that would be the natural consequence of stale upstream predictions in a system whose downstream consumer is computing expected risk or some equivalent function.

The mapping caveat is large. Brown and Braver did not perturb upstream predictions; they manipulated task contingencies that the subjects already knew. The supporting inference for Q-040 is purely structural -- *if* dACC computes a function of upstream predictions, *then* stale upstream predictions corrupt dACC output without producing a flag. The paper itself does not test whether the dACC signal becomes "wired but inert" under stale upstream predictions, only that it is sensitive to upstream variables when those are well-formed. The error-likelihood framework has also been partially superseded by later work (Alexander and Brown's PRO model, Vassena's expected value of control variants, Holroyd and Yeung's hierarchical RL accounts).

I have set confidence at 0.55. This is foundational anchor evidence for the structural reading of Q-040 -- it establishes that dACC is a downstream consumer of upstream predictions in the first place, which is a precondition for the wired-but-inert hypothesis to even make sense. It does not advance the case for MECH-269b specifically. Source quality is moderate-to-good for its era; mapping fidelity is the limiting factor.
