# Mink 1996 — Focused selection and inhibition of competing motor programs

## What the paper does

Mink's 1996 review proposed that the basal ganglia don't generate movement at all. Movement is generated by cortex and cerebellum; the BG's contribution is selection by inhibition. When a voluntary movement is initiated, the BG broadly inhibit motor mechanisms that would compete with the chosen one, and simultaneously remove inhibition focally from the desired mechanism. This is a single mechanism — the BG output stage (GPi/SNr) is a tonically inhibitory veto field, with one selected target getting its veto lifted while everything around it stays clamped down.

The supporting case integrates monkey single-unit electrophysiology, lesion data, and clinical neurology. Bradykinesia, abnormal postures, and involuntary movements in Parkinson's, Huntington's, and dystonia all map onto failures of focal disinhibition or failures of surround inhibition. The MPTP-treated monkey data Boraud et al. published a few years later (J Neurophysiol 2000) provided direct empirical support: in the parkinsonian state the inhibited-to-activated ratio of pallidal cells during passive movement collapses, exactly what focused-selection predicts under dopaminergic depletion.

## Why this matters for Q-019

Q-019's question — three gates or one — has had its existing literature corpus pulled toward the multi-loop view. Mink 1996 is the canonical empirical-anatomical statement of the single-mechanism alternative. The BG output stage shows a unified signature: tonic inhibition with focal disinhibition. That signature does not respect a tidy partition into three independent gates, each with its own selection rule. It is one selection rule, applied to whatever input channel is currently driving the selection.

REE's current architecture treats E3 as a single gate evaluating multiple criteria — closer to Mink's model than to a strict three-loop architecture. If Mink's focused-selection account is correct in its strong form, the existing 11 supporting Q-019 entries describe the input topography of a unified selector, not three independent selectors.

## Limitations and caveats

Mink 1996 was framed specifically for motor program selection. Its claims about cognitive selection, working memory gating, or attentional selection are by analogy, not by direct measurement. So while Mink weakens Q-019 option (B) clearly for the motor commitment loop, its weakening of the sensorium and thought loops is conditional on the assumption that the same focused-selection circuit operates across cognitive domains. That's a non-trivial extrapolation. A hybrid view is in principle possible: focused selection in the motor loop, multi-gate architecture in the non-motor loops, with the arbitration changing as one moves from sensorimotor to associative to limbic territories. Q-019's pinned reading list (Hazy/Frank/O'Reilly, Aron, Brittain & Brown) leans toward this hybrid; Mink's account leans toward unified focused-selection. Resolving which is correct is the remaining empirical question.

The other significant caveat is that Mink's review predates the recent thalamocortical-flexibility work (Scott 2024, Zheng 2024 in the existing Q-019 entries). The mediodorsal thalamus's gating role in context inference is not addressed in Mink 1996 because it wasn't yet characterised. So Mink's silence on thalamic gating is structural, not adversarial.
