# McAlonan, Brown & Bowman 2000 — Thalamic reticular nucleus activation reflects attentional gating during classical conditioning

**DOI:** [10.1523/JNEUROSCI.20-23-08897.2000](https://doi.org/10.1523/JNEUROSCI.20-23-08897.2000) | *Journal of Neuroscience*, 2000 | **Claim:** Q-019

---

## What the paper did

McAlonan and colleagues used the blocking paradigm in rats to dissociate attentional from non-attentional processing of conditioned stimuli, and measured TRN activation via Fos immunohistochemistry. In a blocking design, rats first learn to respond to CS1, then receive compound CS1+CS2 conditioning. Because CS1 already predicts the outcome, CS2 is informationally redundant -- attention is blocked from the second stimulus. This allows comparison of TRN responses to an attended CS1 versus an unattended CS2 within the same animal, controlling for stimulus-level differences. After a final compound session, brains were processed for Fos protein, and Fos-positive cell counts were compared between the TRN sector corresponding to the modality of CS1 versus CS2.

## Key findings

Fos-positive neuron density was significantly greater in the TRN sector corresponding to the attended CS1 than in the sector corresponding to the unattended CS2. The effect was spatially specific to the relevant TRN sectors -- not a global TRN activation. This topographic selectivity is the key result: TRN does not simply activate when the animal is awake or engaged; it selectively activates in the sector corresponding to the stimulus that is being attended.

The authors concluded that TRN reflects selective attention as an attentional gate, consistent with Crick's (1984) searchlight hypothesis: TRN gates which sensory representations from thalamus reach cortex, and this gating is organised topographically according to the current focus of attention.

## How findings translate to REE

Q-019's sensorium loop proposes that TRN (or an equivalent reticular gating structure) routes which inputs the E3 complex processes, selecting from both environmental stimuli and ongoing sensorimotor loops. McAlonan 2000 provides the neurobiological foundation for that proposal: TRN sector activation is not driven by stimulus salience alone but by learned attentional allocation. When attention is directed to CS1 (the attended stimulus), the TRN sector for CS1 is more active -- consistent with TRN actively suppressing the unattended channel at the thalamic level.

For REE, this maps onto the sensorium gate's function: when the sensorium loop is locked to a particular input channel (e.g., processing a salient environmental event), that channel's TRN sector should be strongly engaged, preventing competing channels from passing through to the E3 complex. This is the biological mechanism underlying the REE principle that the sensorium gate determines what reaches the thought loop -- not all sensory inputs are eligible for E3 processing at all times.

This paper also speaks to the Q-019 question about anatomical distinctness: if the sensorium gate were part of a single unified BG selector (Redgrave 1999), there would be no reason to expect topographic TRN activation that tracks attentional allocation selectively. The topographic specificity is inconsistent with a single non-topographic gate.

## Limitations and caveats

The blocking paradigm induces conditioned attentional orienting, which is a procedurally driven reflex rather than the voluntary, goal-directed top-down attention that REE's sensorium loop is principally concerned with. Whether conditioned TRN activation generalises to deliberate top-down attentional control of input selection requires further extrapolation. The Fos measure is also necessarily cumulative across an entire session, preventing moment-to-moment analysis of gating dynamics. These are associative data -- the intervention to confirm causality did not arrive until Ahrens et al. (2015). Finally, rat-to-human transfer introduces some risk, though TRN anatomy is conserved across mammals.

## Confidence: 0.68 — supports Q-019 option (B)

Clean neurobiological evidence that TRN activation is topographically selective for the attended stimulus, supporting the attentional gate hypothesis that underpins Q-019's sensorium loop. Confidence is moderate because the data are associative (no intervention), the measure is lagged (Fos), and the task captures conditioned rather than voluntary attention. Together with Ahrens 2015, these two TRN papers provide convergent support for the sensorium gating mechanism Q-019 predicts.
