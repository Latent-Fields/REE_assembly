# Brittain & Brown (2013) — Beta Oscillations and the Basal Ganglia

**Q-019 relevance: oscillatory grounding for sensorium and action commitment gates**

*Note: Original plan cited "Oscillating to a halt" (Trends Neurosci, PMID 25035364) which could not be confirmed in PubMed. This record uses Brittain & Brown (2013) NeuroImage review covering the same thesis. A related 2014 paper (Brittain, Sharott & Brown, Eur J Neurosci, PMID 24890470) covers additional detail.*

Brittain and Brown establish that beta oscillations (13–30 Hz) in cortico-basal ganglia circuits function as a context-sensitive dynamic gate rather than a simple movement-related epiphenomenon. The core mechanism is an inverted-U relationship between beta synchrony and task performance: optimal performance requires a specific synchrony level, with both pathological excess (Parkinson's disease: bradykinesia, rigidity) and excess suppression degrading output.

**Key mechanisms:**
- **Beta synchrony as "hold current state"**: High beta constrains encoding space to temporally predictable, low-entropy signaling, suppressing novel response selection.
- **STN hyperdirect stop signal**: During motor conflict, cortical conflict signals drive STN beta bursts that globally inhibit ongoing motor programs via GPi, preventing commitment.
- **Cue-driven beta suppression**: Salient environmental cues suppress striatal beta, enabling new encoding — the sensorium admission mechanism.
- **Post-suppression state**: When PD medication suppresses pathological beta, theta and gamma replace it, consistent with distinct oscillatory carriers for cognitive (theta) and execution (gamma) states.

**Three-gate mapping for Q-019:**
- Beta suppression triggered by cue salience → **sensorium gate opening**: environmental content admitted to E3 attention when beta is suppressed
- Theta replacing beta during cognitive processing → **thought loop register**: distinct oscillatory carrier (thought loop ≠ sensorium loop)
- STN beta burst via hyperdirect pathway → **action commitment gate halt signal**: physiological implementation of commit boundary enforcement during conflict
