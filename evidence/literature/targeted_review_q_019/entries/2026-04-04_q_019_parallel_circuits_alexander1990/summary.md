# Summary: Alexander & Crutcher 1990 — Parallel Circuits Review

**Source:** Alexander GE, Crutcher MD. "Functional architecture of basal ganglia circuits: neural substrates of parallel processing." *Trends in Neurosciences*, 1990, 13(7):266-71. DOI: [10.1016/0166-2236(90)90107-l](https://doi.org/10.1016/0166-2236(90)90107-l)

**Evidence direction for Q-019:** supports (three anatomically distinct gates)

---

This is the paper that established the dominant framework for thinking about BG architecture. Alexander and Crutcher's central argument is a departure from earlier serial-convergence views of the basal ganglia: rather than being a single funnel through which all cortical information converges toward a common motor output, the BG are organised as a family of structurally distinct parallel circuits. Each circuit links a different region of the frontal cortex through striatum, pallidum, substantia nigra, and thalamus, and back to the same cortical region. The motor circuit alone had been traced continuously in individual animals at that time; the parallel circuits for oculomotor, prefrontal/associative, and limbic domains were inferred from comparisons across experimental animals, but the authors considered the converging lines of evidence compelling enough to generalise the principle.

What makes this directly relevant to Q-019 is that the parallel architecture claim is made both across circuits and within each circuit. The motor circuit, used as the primary example, shows that even within a single loop there is a parallel rather than purely serial flow of information. If this principle holds across circuits -- and subsequent evidence strongly suggests it does -- then the three functional territories (sensorimotor, associative/cognitive, limbic) are not merely functionally distinct but anatomically segregated through their respective relay stations. The thalamic targets differ: VL/VA for the motor loop, mediodorsal for the associative and limbic loops. The striatal targets differ: putamen for sensorimotor, caudate body for associative, ventral striatum/nucleus accumbens for limbic. This is what the REE three-gate model requires.

The paper is honest about its evidential limitations in a way that has aged well. Alexander and Crutcher note that definitive tests of segregation require double-label within-subject tract-tracing, and that the patchy, divergent terminal fields within each circuit suggest local interdigitation rather than point-to-point cable topography. This caveat matters for REE: the three gates have graded rather than crisp borders, which has downstream implications for how commitment thresholds should be modelled. But the caveat does not undermine the primary claim; it refines it. Segregation and graded borders are compatible.

From the REE perspective, this paper provides the foundational anatomical warrant for the three-gate architecture. The motor circuit maps onto the action commitment loop; the prefrontal/associative circuit maps onto the thought loop and E3 complex; the limbic circuit maps onto the sensorium/harm-goal gate. The authors' language of structurally and functionally distinct circuits with different frontal cortical targets is almost exactly the language the REE model uses for its three loops. The question Q-019 poses -- are these gates distinct or convergent? -- is answered here primarily on the side of distinct, with the qualification that the evidence for non-motor circuits was incomplete in 1990.
