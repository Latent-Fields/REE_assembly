# Summary: Alexander, Crutcher & DeLong 1990 — Segregated Circuits Hypothesis

**Source:** Alexander GE, Crutcher MD, DeLong MR. "Basal ganglia-thalamocortical circuits: parallel substrates for motor, oculomotor, 'prefrontal' and 'limbic' functions." *Progress in Brain Research*, 1990, 85:119-46. DOI: [10.1016/S0079-6123(08)60678-3](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0079-6123(08)60678-3)

**Evidence direction for Q-019:** mixed (supports distinctness; explicitly acknowledges incomplete evidence)

---

This companion review names and fully articulates the "segregated circuits hypothesis." The central proposition is stated with unusual clarity: structural convergence and functional integration happens *within* each circuit, not *between* circuits. Information from motor cortex stays in the motor circuit; information from DLPFC stays in the prefrontal circuit; limbic information stays in the limbic circuit. The anatomical evidence supporting this claim comes from topographic specificity of corticostriatal projections, pallidal and nigral topography, and thalamic relay station specificity, with each circuit having its own thalamic target.

The review extends the four-circuit partition in detail. The motor circuit projects from motor cortex and SMA to putamen, through pallidum and SNr to VLo thalamus, and back to SMA and MC. The oculomotor circuit links frontal eye fields and posterior parietal cortex to the caudate tail, with return through SNr to mediodorsal and VA/VL thalamus to FEF. The prefrontal circuit connects DLPFC and lateral OFC to the caudate body, through pallidum and SNr to mediodorsal thalamus, and back to DLPFC. The limbic circuit connects ACC, OFC, and hippocampal projections to ventral striatum and nucleus accumbens, through ventral pallidum and ventral SNr to mediodorsal thalamus, and back to OFC and ACC. The four-to-three compression the REE model uses (collapsing oculomotor into the motor/action commitment category) is architecturally defensible: both use putamen-like structures and project to VL/VA thalamus, whereas the prefrontal and limbic circuits share mediodorsal thalamus as a relay.

Where this review is irreplaceable is in its candor about the evidential state. The authors note that only the motor circuit has been demonstrated as a continuous loop in individual subjects using simultaneous anterograde and retrograde labelling. The other circuits have been assembled by comparing separate tracing experiments across different animals, which carries real risk given the topographic compression as information passes through BG relay stations. Single-unit recordings in behaving animals do show preservation of functional specificity within the motor and oculomotor circuits, and the authors argue this functional specificity is difficult to explain without strict topographic specificity in the underlying connectivity. But the argument is inferential. This honest acknowledgment is why the evidence direction is rated mixed rather than a clean supports: the three-gate architecture is well-supported for the motor domain, strongly inferred for the associative domain, and least certain for the limbic domain in 1990.

For Q-019 the key implication is that even the canonical source of the three-gate model concedes incomplete anatomical proof. Subsequent work -- including Haber's tract-tracing and human neuroimaging -- has substantially filled these gaps. But the 1990 reviews should be cited as the origin of the segregated hypothesis, not as its definitive anatomical confirmation.
