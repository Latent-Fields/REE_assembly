# McConnell, "Moral Dilemmas" (SEP 2002, rev. 2022) -- relevance to Q-028

## What the entry covers

Terrance McConnell's long-running Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry
on moral dilemmas is the canonical synoptic review of the philosophical
question Q-028 asks. McConnell distinguishes two shapes:

- **Obligation dilemmas** -- more than one feasible action is obligatory,
  and only one can be performed. Sartre's student case (whether to stay
  with one's mother or join the Resistance) is the standard example.
- **Prohibition dilemmas** -- every feasible action is forbidden. Sophie's
  Choice is the standard example.

The entry then walks through the philosophical machinery surrounding
dilemma-resolution. Two principles of deontic logic (Principle of Deontic
Consistency: an action cannot be both obligatory and forbidden; Principle
of Deontic Logic: if an action necessarily produces another, obligating
the first obligates the second) jointly imply that genuine moral dilemmas
generate logical contradictions. So the entry says: either deontic logic
needs revision, or genuine dilemmas do not exist, or some other premise
must give.

McConnell surveys three resolution strategies. **Hierarchical ordering**
proposes that moral rules can be lexically ordered so that conflicts are
always resolvable in principle; W. D. Ross argued explicitly against this,
noting that promise-keeping and harm-prevention do not preserve a fixed
priority across contexts. **Disjunctive requirements** propose that the
agent's obligation in a dilemma is to perform any one of the conflicting
options. **Williams and Marcus** argue that even when the agent chooses
well, the unchosen obligation leaves a moral residue that shows up as
appropriate guilt or remorse -- emotions that are tracking moral structure,
not just psychological discomfort.

## Why this matters for Q-028

Q-028 is the special case of axiom-conflict in which preserving self
(INV-026) and preserving others (INV-028) cannot be jointly achieved. The
McConnell entry tells REE three useful things.

First, the structural shape of the problem is familiar. It is a
near-prohibition-dilemma in McConnell's typology -- both available actions
violate something, and the question is which violation REE should accept.

Second, simple lexical priority is not a defensible answer. If REE
hard-codes "self over other" or "other over self" it inherits Ross's
objection: contexts vary, and a fixed priority across all contexts is
insensitive to the morally relevant features of the situation. The
clinical analogue is familiar -- a patient who cannot trade off self-care
against care for others except by always picking one is exhibiting a
pathology, not exemplifying ethical clarity.

Third, the Williams/Marcus moral-residue position implies that whichever
choice REE makes, the unchosen obligation should leave a trace. The
philosophical point is that an agent who simply picks one axiom and
discards the other is not actually resolving the dilemma -- it is
side-stepping it. A genuine resolution preserves the defeated obligation
in some downstream form, which the agent's later behaviour reflects.

## Possible REE re-framing

The McConnell entry argues for an architecture with two parts. (1) A
context-sensitive comparator: when INV-026 and INV-028 conflict, REE
weighs the situation rather than applying a fixed priority. The
salience-coordinator + harm-stream + residue-field machinery REE already
has is structurally suited to this. (2) A residue mechanism: when REE
chooses (say) preservation-of-other over preservation-of-self, the
defeated INV-026 obligation persists as a downstream signal -- something
analogous to agent-regret in Williams's sense. REE's existing
residue-field representation may be the right home for this, but the
specific instantiation (what residue is laid down, what it gates
downstream) is design work the entry does not do.

A second observation: the entry's silence on self-vs-other as a special
case is itself informative. The specific philosophical literature on
self-versus-other moral asymmetry sits more in the agent-relative /
agent-neutral debate (Parfit 1984, Nagel, Scheffler, Hammerton) than in
the moral-dilemma literature. A future Q-028 lit-pull might fruitfully
target that thread.

## Caveats

The entry treats human moral agents responding to deontic obligations
formulated in classical logic. REE's axioms are not deontic constraints
in that sense -- they are existence claims and structural commitments
that ground a harm signal. The translation from "moral obligation" to
"axiom-derived constraint" is interpretive, and McConnell does not address
the meta-level conflicts characteristic of REE. The self-vs-other case
that Q-028 specifically asks about is one shape of conflict among many
the entry treats; the entry is not specifically about it.

I am marking this *mixed* with confidence 0.46. The entry both supports
candidate answers (context-sensitive comparator + residue mechanism) and
constrains them (Ross's objection rules out strict hierarchy; deontic
logic problems rule out naive disjunction). It is exactly the kind of
philosophical scaffolding Q-028 needs without being the kind of evidence
that closes it.
