# Williams, "Ethical Consistency" (1965 / 1973) -- relevance to Q-028

## What the paper does

Bernard Williams's 1965 paper "Ethical Consistency", reprinted as Chapter 11
of Problems of the Self (Cambridge University Press, 1973), is the
foundational anglophone treatment of the claim that moral conflict is not
always eliminable by argument and that the right resolution still leaves a
moral remainder.

Williams's central move is structural. He compares moral conflict with
conflict of belief and conflict of desire. Conflicting beliefs cancel: when
I learn that p is true, my belief that not-p is simply withdrawn, leaving
no trace. Conflicting desires do not cancel that way: I can want to be in
two places at once, and choosing one leaves the other intact as a desire
not satisfied. Williams argues that moral conflict is structurally more
like the desire case than the belief case. When two oughts conflict and
the agent acts on one, the other is *defeated*, not *dissolved*. The
appropriate response to genuine conflict is not "see, only one of those
oughts was real" -- it is the recognition that both were real, that the
unsatisfied one persists, and that the agent has a class of negative
emotion called *agent-regret* directed at it.

Williams explicitly targets the philosophical move of arguing that one of
the conflicting oughts was "only prima facie" or "pro tanto" and so
disappears once defeated. He treats that move as a way of making the
conflict vanish that misses what is morally important about it. The
phenomenology of agent-regret is itself the evidence: a person who chose
well still has appropriate negative emotion, and a moral theory that
cannot accommodate this fact is missing something.

## Why this matters for Q-028

Q-028 asks how REE should behave when its axioms conflict, paradigmatically
when INV-026 (preserve self) and INV-028 (preserve others) cannot be
jointly satisfied. McConnell's SEP entry (separately filed in this
directory) gives the typology and the survey of resolution strategies.
Williams adds a sharp constraint on what an adequate resolution looks
like. Whatever REE does, the defeated axiom must leave a residue rather
than disappearing.

This rules out a class of architecturally-tempting answers. A simple
priority order (INV-026 always over INV-028, or the reverse) is the kind
of answer Williams targets directly: it makes the conflict vanish by
saying one ought was never really binding. A simple weighted-sum that
collapses both axioms onto a single scalar is similar -- once the sum is
computed, the unchosen alternative leaves no trace. Both architectures
would be Williams-rejectable.

What is left is an architecture in which conflict is resolved
contextually but the defeated axiom persists in some downstream form. In
REE's terms this looks like a residue laid down in z_world that biases
later action toward repair: after preserving-other-over-self, REE's
subsequent actions weight self-preservation more heavily until the
imbalance is corrected; after preserving-self-over-other, subsequent
actions weight repair to the other. The residue is functional, not
phenomenological; it is the architectural translation of Williams's
agent-regret.

## Caveats

Williams himself would likely resist this functional translation. His
argument runs through the phenomenology of regret, and a REE architecture
that captures only the functional consequences of regret without anything
felt is an answer that Williams's framework treats as incomplete. The
move from "the defeated obligation leaves a felt remainder" to "the
defeated axiom leaves a residue signal" is a real interpretive step.

Williams also does not specify any mechanism. He gives the constraint --
the residue must persist -- but does not say what generates it, where it
lives, or how it gates future action. Implementing Williams's lesson in
REE is therefore design work that the paper supports but does not
provide.

The paper is also entirely about human moral psychology in ordinary
social life. The question of axiom-conflict in a computational agent
that has no phenomenology is not a question Williams asked. Transfer
risk is real.
