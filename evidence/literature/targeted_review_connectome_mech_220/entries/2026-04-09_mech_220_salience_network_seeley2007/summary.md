WHAT THE PAPER DID

Seeley et al. (2007) used task-free resting-state fMRI to identify intrinsic connectivity networks in the human brain. The key methodological innovation was to use seed regions derived from two distinct neurological syndromes (behavioural-variant frontotemporal dementia seeding the salience network; corticobasal syndrome seeding the executive network) to derive unbiased network templates, then apply these to healthy controls. This allowed the authors to demonstrate that the ACC-insula axis and the dorsolateral prefrontal-parietal axis are genuinely dissociable networks, not aspects of a single attentional system.

KEY FINDINGS

The salience network, anchored by dorsal anterior cingulate and orbital frontoinsular cortices, showed robust connectivity to subcortical and limbic structures including the amygdala and hypothalamus. The executive-control network connected dorsolateral prefrontal cortex to posterior parietal cortex. Critically, prescan self-reported anxiety correlated specifically with intrinsic functional connectivity of the dACC node of the salience network -- not with any executive network region. This double dissociation establishes that the salience network is selectively responsive to affective state, not simply to general arousal or attentional load.

TRANSLATION TO MECH-220

The dissociability result directly supports the structural premise of MECH-220: the harm hub is a coherent coordinating module, not a byproduct of general executive engagement. If ACC-insula coupling were simply a manifestation of cognitive effort, it would show up in the executive network too -- it does not. The affective specificity (anxiety tracks salience network connectivity, not executive network connectivity) is the resting-state analogue of the z_harm_a urgency gating mechanism. Elevated affective state amplifies the ACC-insula hub's intrinsic coherence, operationally corresponding to MECH-220's claim that z_harm_a urgency modulates z_harm_s precision. What Seeley et al. observe at the level of dispositional trait, Wiech et al. (2010) later demonstrate for phasic threat anticipation.

LIMITATIONS AND CAVEATS

The affective state measured here is dispositional anxiety (self-report before scanning), not acute nociceptive urgency. MECH-220 frames the hypervigilance pathway as a state-level mechanism -- urgency during an active harm encounter -- whereas this paper shows a trait-level correlation. The two are structurally analogous but temporally very different. It is possible that the trait-anxiety correlation is a different phenomenon from phasic urgency-gated precision, even if both engage the salience network. The paper also predates most of the large-scale resting-state replication literature, so while the dissociation result is well-established, some of the specific subcortical connectivity estimates may have been revised.

CONFIDENCE REASONING

The dissociability result is robustly replicated and is the foundational empirical basis for the salience network framework that MECH-220 draws on. That the anxiety-dACC relationship holds in this early study, and has been confirmed in subsequent work, provides genuine support for the affective-amplification mechanism. The confidence ceiling is set by the trait-vs-state gap and by the absence of nociception-specific stimulation. This entry provides the network-level structural dissociation argument; Wiech 2010 and Wager 2013 provide the pain-specific corroboration.
