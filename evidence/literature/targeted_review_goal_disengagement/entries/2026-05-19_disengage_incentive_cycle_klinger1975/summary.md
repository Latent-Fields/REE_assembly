# Klinger 1975 — consequences of commitment to and disengagement from incentives

The reframe left one question deliberately open: if the ghost-goal bank is to have an explicit abandon path that biology lacks, what should *trigger* it? We already established one firm negative — it must not be accumulated "evidence of hopelessness" (Bouton). Klinger 1975 is the canonical positive account, and it is worth taking seriously precisely because it predates the formal-modelling era and so describes the phenomenon rather than a convenient parameterisation of it.

Klinger's structure is this. Committing to an incentive opens a "current concern" — a sustained motivational state with a definite onset (commitment) and a definite offset (consummation *or* disengagement) that biases attention, thought, and dreaming toward the concern until it is released. What triggers release-by-disengagement is *obstruction or non-attainment of the committed incentive* — not a running tally of failures. The response is a stereotyped cycle: invigoration (intensified effort, inflated valuation of the blocked goal), then protest/aggression toward the obstruction, then a depression-like phase of apathy and anhedonia that *actively dissolves the commitment*, then recovery and redirection to new incentives. The depression phase is framed as functional: an evolved disengagement mechanism that detaches the organism from an unreachable incentive so the concern can offset and resources reallocate.

For ARC-078's C3 this is double-edged, which is why I have recorded it as mixed. It strongly supports the half we had already inferred: the trigger is an *appraisal of obstruction of a committed goal*, not accumulated-hopelessness evidence. But it sharply qualifies the other half — the implicit "one-shot flag" picture of an abandon operation. In Klinger the trigger does not flip a bit; it opens a slow, affectively-signed *phase* that is itself the disengagement mechanism. The design reading is that a REE abandon trigger should open a brief disengagement *state* (ideally with reengagement coupled in), not instantaneously delete the bank entry.

The caveat is the obvious one for a 1975 integrative theory: it is structural and behavioural, with no neural localisation, and the depression phase is a process measured in the literature in days, not timesteps. Collapsing it into a single-step flag would discard exactly the stage Klinger says does the work. The failure signatures it predicts are, however, immediately recognisable and bracket the design from both sides: stuck in invigoration is perseverative striving (the rumination pole); premature commitment-collapse is under-persistence; and a cycle that arrests in the depression phase without redirection is clinical depression — abandonment that is not paired with reengagement. That last point is the one I would carry forward most firmly: in Klinger, releasing the goal and acquiring a new one are not the same operation, and a system that can abandon but not redirect has reproduced a pathology, not solved one.
