# Wrosch, Scheier, Miller, Schulz & Carver 2003 — adaptive self-regulation of unattainable goals

If Klinger gives the canonical *theory* of the disengagement trigger, Wrosch and colleagues give the construct that operationalised it and showed it matters for wellbeing. Their move is to separate two capacities that are easy to conflate: goal *disengagement* (withdrawing effort and commitment from a goal) and goal *reengagement* (taking up new goals). Across three samples — undergraduates, a mixed young/older adult sample, and a stress sample that included parents of children with cancer — both capacities are positively associated with subjective wellbeing, and they interact: the healthiest profile is high on both.

The part that matters for ARC-078's C3 is the triggering condition. Disengagement is adaptive *specifically when the goal is appraised as unattainable*. It is not "give up when discouraged," not "give up after N failures," not "give up when effort is high." The construct is gated by an appraisal of unattainability, which is exactly the shape of trigger the Bouton constraint pushed us toward — an appraisal, not an accumulated-evidence tally. This is the cleanest direct support in the strand for the trigger's *input semantics*.

The second contribution is, I think, the more design-changing one, and it echoes Klinger's "cycle arrest" failure mode from the other direction. Disengagement and reengagement are *separable*, and disengaging without reengaging removes distress but leaves a purpose vacuum — relief without restored wellbeing. Translated into the architecture: a REE abandon operation implemented as a bare delete of a bank entry is not the adaptive operation the biology describes. The adaptive operation is *abandon-and-redirect*; the abandon path must be architecturally coupled to a reengagement step, or it reproduces a documented pathology rather than preventing one.

I have recorded this as supports, but the honest caveat bounds how far it can carry us. The construct is a stable individual-difference capacity measured by self-report (the Goal Adjustment Scale), and "unattainability" enters the model as a given upstream input — the paper establishes *that* an unattainability appraisal triggers adaptive disengagement, not *how* a system computes unattainability. That computation is precisely the function a REE abandon trigger has to implement. So this entry firmly constrains the trigger's interface (appraisal-gated, reengagement-coupled) while leaving its mechanism — the thing the eventual abandon Q-claim must adjudicate — explicitly unspecified by the data.
