# Brandstätter, Herrmann & Schüler 2013 — the action crisis

Klinger described the disengagement trigger as a phase rather than a switch; Wrosch gave us the appraisal that gates it. Brandstätter and colleagues are the paper that instrumented the thing in between — the pre-disengagement state itself — and it is the entry that should most discipline how we design the REE abandon path.

They name and measure the **action crisis**: a critical phase, entered when setbacks accumulate to the point that abandonment becomes a *live option*, characterised by an intrapsychic conflict in which the agent is genuinely torn between continued pursuit and giving up. Across two longitudinal field studies and a marathon-runner study they show the action crisis (i) degrades psychological wellbeing, (ii) elevates salivary cortisol and predicts worse race performance two weeks later, and (iii) is accompanied by an *automatic dampening of the cognitive evaluation of the goal itself*. They explicitly align with Klinger: disengagement is a process, and the action crisis is its onset.

For ARC-078's C3 this is the sharpest constraint in the strand, and it is why I have logged it as mixed rather than supports. It supports the appraisal-not-tally reading — the crisis is a conflict appraisal, not an evidence counter. But it actively *weakens* the tidy "one-shot abandon flag" picture. The strongest, most instrumented data we have say the trigger is an extended, metabolically costly, contested struggle, not a threshold crossing. A REE abandon mechanism modelled as a costless single-timestep flag would discard exactly the conflict-resolution dynamics the cortisol and performance data show are the actual mechanism.

There is one more finding here that I would treat as a hard design warning. Goal-devaluation occurs *during* the crisis — the dampened evaluation of the goal is a *symptom* of the unresolved conflict, not proof that the goal is unattainable. An abandon trigger that fires on "the goal's value has dropped" would therefore abandon precisely when the agent is mid-struggle and has not yet resolved whether the goal is genuinely lost. That is the premature-disengagement pole, and it is reachable from a signal that looks superficially reasonable. The honest caveat is that this is healthy goal pursuit in students and runners, not clinical or neural data — but the structural lesson (the trigger is a brief contested state; within-crisis devaluation is not the trigger) transfers cleanly and should constrain the eventual abandon Q-claim directly.
