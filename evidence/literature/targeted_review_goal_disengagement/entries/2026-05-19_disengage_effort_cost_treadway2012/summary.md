# Treadway, Bossaller, Shelton & Zald 2012 — effort-based decision-making in MDD

Husain and Roiser give the over-disengagement pole as a synthesis; Maier and Seligman give its circuit-level inversion. Treadway and colleagues add the thing both of those lack — a quantified clinical measurement of the exact decision point where an agent declines an available, valued goal. That is precisely the point a REE abandon trigger sits at, so it is worth having the empirical pin and not only the theory.

Using the Effort Expenditure for Rewards Task, they compared major-depression patients with healthy controls on willingness to choose a high-effort/high-reward option over a low-effort/low-reward one, across varying reward magnitude and probability. Two findings matter. First, patients were significantly less willing to expend effort for reward — the deficit is at the motivational/decision stage, not in the capacity for pleasure. Second, and this is the part that constrains the design, patients were also *less able to integrate reward magnitude and probability* into their choices: the effort-allocation computation was both biased toward low effort and less sensitive to the value information that should justify effort. Within the patient group, longer current-episode duration predicted worse effort allocation — the over-disengaged phenotype deepens with sustained low-reward exposure.

For ARC-078's C3 this confirms the lower-bound bracket empirically rather than theoretically, and it sharpens it. It is not enough to say "don't trigger on effort cost" in the abstract; Treadway shows *why* a scalar effort-cost cutoff is insufficient. The pathological agent is not simply one with a high effort threshold — it is one whose threshold is shifted *and* whose value-integration is degraded. An abandon trigger that watches only an effort-versus-value scalar cannot distinguish a rationally cost-sensitive agent (who should sometimes disengage) from a pathologically disengaged one (who should not). The design corollary is that the trigger must be *conditioned on value-integration integrity* — the system has to be demonstrably still integrating magnitude and probability correctly before any cost-based disengagement is licensed at all.

I have scored this supports at a deliberately modest confidence. It is a clinical case-control with modest N and a single task, and the EEfRT has its own measurement debates. But it triangulates with the 2009 validation and the 2013 review, and its specific contribution — the *dual* deficit, cost-weight plus value-integration — is exactly the distinction the eventual abandon Q-claim needs and would not get from the theoretical reviews alone. The honest limit is that it tells us a condition the trigger must satisfy (value-integration intact), not how to compute the trigger itself; like the rest of this strand it bounds the design without supplying it.
