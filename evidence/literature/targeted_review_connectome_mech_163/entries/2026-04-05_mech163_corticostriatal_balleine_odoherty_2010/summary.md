# Balleine & O'Doherty 2010 -- Corticostriatal Determinants of Goal-Directed and Habitual Action: MECH-163 Mapping

**Source:** Balleine, B.W. & O'Doherty, J.P. (2010). Human and Rodent Homologies in Action Control: Corticostriatal Determinants of Goal-Directed and Habitual Action. *Neuropsychopharmacology*, 35, 48-69. DOI: [10.1038/npp.2009.131](https://doi.org/10.1038/npp.2009.131)

---

## What the Paper Does

Balleine and O'Doherty 2010 review behavioural and neural evidence for the dual-system architecture of action control across rodents and humans, focusing on the corticostriatal mechanisms that mediate goal-directed versus habitual action. The review establishes cross-species homologies in the anatomical substrates of both systems, using two behavioural tests (outcome devaluation and contingency degradation) as dissociation tools.

**Outcome devaluation**: after training an animal to perform an action for a food reward, the food is devalued (by pre-feeding or pairing with illness). Goal-directed actions are immediately suppressed; habitual actions continue at the same rate. This demonstrates that the goal-directed system maintains an online model of action-outcome contingencies, while the habitual system does not.

**Contingency degradation**: outcomes are delivered non-contingently (same rate regardless of action). Goal-directed actions are suppressed (the contingency is detected and the action deemed unnecessary); habitual actions continue. Same dissociation, different manipulation.

## Key Anatomical Findings

The double dissociation is anatomically localised:

- **Goal-directed action**: requires dorsomedial striatum (anterior caudate in humans; prelimbic PFC / medial orbital cortex in humans). Lesions of dorsomedial striatum or prelimbic PFC convert goal-directed behaviour to habit -- animals continue responding after devaluation.
- **Habitual action**: requires dorsolateral striatum (posterior lateral putamen in humans; infralimbic cortex facilitates habit expression). Lesions of dorsolateral striatum prevent habit formation and maintain goal-directed sensitivity even under extended training.

## Key Findings for MECH-163

The devaluation insensitivity of the habitual system is the direct neurobiological analogue of MECH-163's claim that the habit system cannot attribute harm across contexts. Devaluation creates a new outcome contingency (food -> aversive). The habitual system is architecturally blind to this change: it continues responding because its S-R cache does not update with the new contingency information. This is the same architectural limitation that prevents habitual approach from becoming contextually appropriate harm attribution: the cache stores what worked before, not what is currently true about the outcome.

The goal-directed system, by contrast, immediately integrates the new outcome value (via dorsomedial striatum + mPFC) and suppresses the action. This is the model-based sensitivity that allows contextual harm attribution: when circumstances change (new harm association, novel context), the goal-directed system re-evaluates and adjusts.

The cross-species homologies established in this paper are significant for REE: the corticostriatal dual architecture is phylogenetically conserved, suggesting it reflects a fundamental computational solution rather than a mammalian quirk. This strengthens the argument that MECH-163 is capturing a necessary architectural feature rather than a design option.

## REE Mapping to MECH-163

The habit system in REE (SNc/dorsal-lateral striatum) maps directly onto Balleine/O'Doherty's habitual system. The devaluation-insensitivity mechanism is precisely the architectural limitation MECH-163 identifies: the habit system cannot attribute harm across novel contexts because its S-R associations do not update with new outcome contingency information.

The REE planned system (E3 hippocampal + dorsomedial striatum + mPFC) maps onto Balleine/O'Doherty's goal-directed system. The hippocampal contribution MECH-163 adds (multi-step trajectory generation for long-horizon harm/benefit evaluation) extends beyond what Balleine/O'Doherty study, but is consistent with subsequent hippocampal planning literature. The dorsomedial striatum anatomy in MECH-163 aligns precisely with Balleine/O'Doherty's findings.

## Limitations and Confidence Reasoning

The main mapping gap is that Balleine/O'Doherty study simple instrumental conditioning and devaluation paradigms, not multi-step planning or long-horizon harm/benefit evaluation. The extension from devaluation insensitivity to contextual harm attribution inability is conceptually valid but represents an inference beyond the paper's scope. The hippocampal contribution to goal-directed action in MECH-163 is supported by other literature but not foregrounded here. Confidence: 0.83.
