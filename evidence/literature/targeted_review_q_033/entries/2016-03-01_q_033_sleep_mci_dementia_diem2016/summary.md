# Summary: Diem et al. 2016 — Sleep-Wake Patterns and Risk of MCI or Dementia in Older Women

## Study Overview

Diem and colleagues analysed data from 1,245 cognitively normal older women (mean age 82.6 years) enrolled in the Study of Osteoporotic Fractures (SOF), a large prospective cohort that included actigraphy-based sleep measurement. Participants were followed for approximately 5 years. By the end of follow-up, 38% had developed cognitive impairment: 23.3% mild cognitive impairment and 14.7% dementia. The core question was whether any actigraphy-derived sleep measure predicted who would develop cognitive decline.

## The Duration Non-Finding and What Did Predict

Total sleep duration was not predictive of cognitive decline. This non-finding is as important as the positive results: it establishes that simply measuring how long someone sleeps does not capture the clinically relevant signal. What did predict MCI or dementia was sleep efficiency (the percentage of time in bed actually spent asleep), sleep latency, and sleep variability. Women in the lowest quartile of sleep efficiency had 1.5-fold higher odds of developing MCI or dementia compared to those in the highest quartile. Greater variability in both sleep efficiency and total sleep time also conferred elevated risk.

## Relevance to Q-033

This study extends the evidence base for Q-033 into the cognitive decompensation domain and provides perhaps the cleanest demonstration of the core principle: sleep quality measures (efficiency, variability) derived from actigraphy predict clinical decompensation over a multi-year horizon, while total sleep time does not. Q-033 asks whether a sleep-phase sufficiency index will outperform total sleep time in predicting decompensation type and timing. This study is consistent with that framing — though the specific measures here (efficiency, variability) are proxies for architecture quality rather than phase-sufficiency measures in the full Q-033 sense.

## Where the Mapping Strains

The population is elderly women at mean age 82.6, an age range where sleep architecture is already substantially compressed: slow-wave sleep is markedly reduced, REM becomes less regular, and circadian amplitude is diminished as a baseline condition. It is possible that the effects observed here would be attenuated — or would show different patterns — in younger or working-age clinical populations. More critically, the study includes no measure of waking cognitive or prediction error burden, so the 'mismatch' component of Q-033 is entirely absent. The study tests one side of the Q-033 equation (sleep quality signals decompensation) without the other (waking load modulates the threshold).

## Broader Context

The value of this paper for Q-033 is not in validating the mismatch index per se, but in establishing that the sleep quality signal generalises across clinical decompensation domains — psychiatric (bipolar, depression) and cognitive (MCI, dementia) — and in confirming the total-sleep-time limitation across domains. If the Q-033 hypothesis holds, one would expect similar superiority of phase-sufficiency over duration measures in cognitive populations, and this paper is consistent with that expectation even though it predates the Q-033 framing.
