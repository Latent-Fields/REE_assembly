# Summary: Yuan et al. 2024 — Actigraphy Performance in Sleep Stage Measurement

## Study Overview

Yuan and colleagues at the University of Oxford conducted a systematic review asking a simple but clinically important question: can wrist actigraphy reliably classify sleep stages? They searched MEDLINE, Web of Science, Google Scholar, and Embase and identified only eight studies that compared sleep architecture estimates between wrist-worn actigraphy and polysomnography (PSG). That the field has produced only eight such comparison studies after more than three decades of actigraphy use is itself a finding worth pausing on.

## Key Finding: Qualified Capability with High Heterogeneity

The reviewers found that actigraphy-based sleep staging "had some ability to classify different sleep stages compared with polysomnography" — but the qualification matters as much as the capability. Methodological heterogeneity across studies was large enough to prevent any quantitative synthesis. Studies differed in how they grouped sleep stages, which metrics they reported, and which actigraphy algorithms they used. The authors' formal conclusion is that "current evidence does not support or refute the validity of sleep staging from actigraphy" — a deliberately agnostic verdict that reflects genuine uncertainty rather than a finding of failure.

## What This Means for Q-033

Q-033 asks whether actigraphy data combined with other passive signals can infer "latent sleep-phase sufficiency." The Yuan review establishes the foundational constraint: actigraphy alone, used as a direct sleep-stage classifier, is not a validated substitute for PSG. REM and slow-wave staging depend on EEG, EOG, and EMG signals that wrist movement does not capture. Any inference of sleep-phase adequacy from actigraphy must therefore work indirectly — through proxies such as circadian phase, intra-day variability, sleep efficiency, and fragmentation — rather than through explicit stage labels.

## The Multi-Stream Possibility

The review's limitation is that it evaluates actigraphy in isolation. Q-033 proposes combining actigraphy with behavioural, physiological (e.g., heart rate variability), and subjective signals — a fusion approach not examined in any of these eight studies. The review therefore establishes a lower bound on single-sensor performance but says nothing about whether latent-state inference across multiple streams could recover phase-related information that single-sensor staging cannot. The honest reading is that Q-033's multi-stream approach is not contradicted by this evidence, but its added value relative to the staging gap remains undemonstrated.

## Contextual Note

The authors are from the Big Data Institute and Sir Jules Thorn Sleep and Circadian Neuroscience Institute at Oxford — groups with strong expertise in both large-scale wearable data and sleep neuroscience. Their cautious conclusion reflects methodological rigour rather than pessimism about the technology. The field's progress toward validated multi-stage actigraphy algorithms (e.g., AI-driven approaches) makes this an active area where the evidence base is likely to develop rapidly.
