# Summary: Ferrand et al. 2022 — Actigraphy Dimensions Predict Bipolar Disorder Recurrence

## Study Overview

Ferrand and colleagues recruited 69 euthymic bipolar disorder patients and had each wear an actigraphy device for 21 days. They then followed participants for a median of 3.5 years (range 2-4.5), recording every mood episode recurrence. The question was not just whether actigraphy predicts relapse — several earlier studies had addressed that — but which dimensions of actigraphy data carry the predictive signal. Using principal component analysis, they identified two core dimensions: one capturing sleep quantity and variability, and one capturing the amplitude, stability, and fragmentation of circadian rhythms. Only the second — the circadian dimension — significantly predicted time to recurrence.

## The Key Metric: Intra-Day Variability

Within the circadian dimension, intra-day variability (IV) emerged as the strongest individual predictor. IV measures the hourly fragmentation of rest-activity patterns — it is high when there is erratic, poorly structured activity and rest across the day, rather than a stable sleep-wake cycle. A patient with high IV is essentially showing circadian disorganisation. This variable alone achieved AUC of 0.75 for predicting who would relapse first; combined with six clinical variables it rose to AUC of 0.82.

## What This Tells Us About Q-033

The structural lesson from this study is that circadian architecture quality — not sleep quantity — carries the predictive signal for psychiatric decompensation timing. Total sleep time was part of the first PCA dimension and was not the predictor that survived. This directly supports Q-033's premise: a mismatch index that goes beyond duration to capture phase adequacy should outperform TST-based measures. The Ferrand study did not measure waking prediction error load, but its findings are consistent with the interpretation that the sleep-phase component of Q-033's mismatch index is the load-bearing part.

## Critical Limitations for Q-033's Broader Claims

Two important gaps limit how far this paper takes us. First, the actigraphy window was 21 days at baseline — a snapshot, not the long-run continuous monitoring Q-033 envisions. Whether intra-day variability measured over weeks or months remains stable enough to serve as a continuous biomarker is a different question. Second, all episode types were pooled: the study does not test whether the IV signal discriminates the type of upcoming episode (manic vs depressive vs mixed), which is one of Q-033's central subhypotheses. Lim et al. 2024 addresses type specificity but uses different features.

## Honest Assessment

This study provides the clearest direct support for the decompensation-timing component of Q-033: a circadian fragmentation index, derivable from wrist actigraphy, predicts when a bipolar patient will relapse over a multi-year horizon, and does so better than clinical factors alone. The sample is small and the design is baseline-snapshot rather than continuous, but the effect is large enough and the finding specific enough (circadian dimension, not sleep quantity) that it meaningfully constrains the Q-033 hypothesis space.
