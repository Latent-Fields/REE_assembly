# Precursors to morality in development — Cowell & Decety (2015)

## Relevance to Q-006

Q-006 asks whether ethical capacity is developmental rather than additive: whether it must be built through staged progression or can be bolted onto a complete system as a late constraint. Cowell and Decety's PNAS study provides the sharpest empirical anchor yet available for the developmental side of that argument, and it does so from a level that is hard to dismiss — recorded neural dynamics in infants who cannot yet speak.

## What the study shows

Seventy-three infants and toddlers (12-24 months) watched prosocial and antisocial agents in a standard helper-hinderer paradigm while continuous EEG, event-related potentials, and gaze were recorded. Children showed clear neural differentiation between the two agent types across both spectral power and time-locked responses. Those neural differences — not the behavioural preference scores alone — predicted subsequent approach toward the prosocial character. And they were modulated by parental endorsement of fairness and justice, measured independently.

The picture that emerges is a staged system: an early automatic emotional-evaluative response (approach/avoidance, amygdala-adjacent, present even at 12 months) that forms the foundation on which later cognitive reappraisal and norm-referencing is built. The authors argue explicitly that moral sensitivity in later development "is a careful integration of both an early automatic emotional component and a later cognitive reappraisal of stimuli." The two are not independent; the earlier layer scaffolds and gates the later one.

## Why this matters for ARC-015 and REE

ARC-015 posits that ethical capacity cannot be added to an agent after the core computational structure is fixed -- that it must be woven into the architecture from the start. Cowell and Decety give us a biological existence proof: in the only naturally occurring system we know produces genuine moral agents, the scaffold is not additive. The evaluative and the purely predictive are coupled from the earliest observable stage.

A critic might object that this reflects a contingent biological constraint -- humans evolved this way, but nothing stops an engineer from adding an ethics module to a trained agent. That objection is worth taking seriously. What the paper cannot show, by design, is whether the staged coupling is necessary in the abstract or merely how evolution happened to solve the problem. The mapping caveat here is real: gradient descent over training episodes is not the same as synaptic pruning and myelination over months of embodied social experience.

## Calibration note

Confidence is set at 0.74 rather than higher because the transfer gap is substantive. The study is about moral perception in pre-linguistic children, not ethical agency in adult decision-makers. REE's E3 and its harm channel are analogues to the later cognitive-reappraisal layer, not to the early automatic response. The question Q-006 actually asks -- whether a fully trained predictive system could acquire ethics additively without developmental staging in the training curriculum -- is not what this paper tests. What it does provide is convergent evidence that staged emergence, rather than late-stage addition, is the observed pattern across all biological cases we can study.
