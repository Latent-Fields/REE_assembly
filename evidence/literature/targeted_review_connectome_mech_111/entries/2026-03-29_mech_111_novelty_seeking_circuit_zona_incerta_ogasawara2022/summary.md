# A primate temporal cortex-zona incerta pathway for novelty seeking

**Ogasawara et al. (2022) -- Nature Neuroscience -- DOI: 10.1038/s41593-021-00950-1 -- PMID: 34903880**

## What the paper did

Takaya Ogasawara and colleagues set out to identify the neural circuit controlling novelty-seeking in primates. The key behavioral observation is that monkeys will voluntarily make eye movements to familiar objects in order to trigger the opportunity to view novel objects -- not because the novel objects have reward value, but simply because they are novel. This is information-seeking for its own sake. The authors used a combination of single-unit electrophysiology, pharmacological inactivation, and optogenetic stimulation to identify the zona incerta (ZI), a subthalamic nucleus, as the critical control point. ZI neurons fire specifically in response to predictions of novel stimuli -- before the gaze shift that would produce novelty exposure -- and do not respond to predictions of familiar rewarding stimuli. Low-intensity electrical stimulation of ZI facilitates gaze shifts to produce novelty; pharmacological inactivation suppresses them. The anterior ventral medial temporal cortex (AVMTC), a region involved in object vision and memory, provides novelty-prediction signals to ZI. Crucially, the circuit is functionally independent of dopaminergic substantia nigra neurons and lateral habenula neurons, which encode reward prediction errors and punishment predictions respectively.

## Key findings relevant to MECH-111

Three findings directly support MECH-111. First, the ZI circuit encodes predictions of novelty, not predictions of reward -- the signal is anticipatory, computed before the novelty-seeking action, and graded by the expected novelty of the target. This is the computational analog of MECH-111's intrinsic positive valence at moderate prediction error magnitudes: the system generates a motivational signal from the anticipated information gain, not from anticipated reward. Second, the circuit is anatomically and functionally separate from dopaminergic reward circuits. The substantia nigra dopamine neurons, which encode reward prediction errors, do not show the novelty-selective pattern; their manipulation does not affect novelty seeking in the same way as ZI manipulation. This is the critical architectural point: novelty-seeking and reward-seeking use different hardware. Third, the AVMTC input to ZI carries familiarity predictions: temporal cortex object recognition signals feed into the ZI novelty-seeking motivator, and familiar objects suppress ZI activity. This is exactly the architecture MECH-111 requires: E1's world model (temporal cortex analog) provides a novelty signal that drives a separate information-seeking motivator (ZI analog), bypassing reward/harm channels.

## REE translation

MECH-111 predicts that E1 prediction error at moderate magnitudes generates intrinsic positive valence -- a curiosity signal that motivates exploration independently of harm avoidance. The Ogasawara finding provides the biological existence proof and the architectural template. The existence proof: novelty-seeking is real, dedicated, and distinct from reward-seeking. The architectural template: a temporal cortex (world model) -> subcortical gating nucleus pathway that converts novelty predictions into behavioral drive without routing through the reward or harm channels.

In REE, this maps to a required architectural element not yet implemented: a dedicated channel from E1's prediction error to a novelty-seeking motivator that modulates the commit threshold (lowering it for novel states, as noted in MECH-111) without routing through z_beta (affective channel that also carries harm signals) or z_goal (goal attractor channel). The ZI/AVMTC architecture suggests this should be a separate latent channel or modulator. The finding that ZI inactivation reduces but does not eliminate novelty seeking (some novelty seeking persists) also suggests graded rather than all-or-nothing modulation -- consistent with MECH-111's claim that moderate prediction error generates moderate positive valence, not a binary switch.

## Limitations and caveats

The study is in macaque monkeys performing a specific visual novelty-seeking task (gaze shifts to novel objects). REE operates in a grid-world where 'novelty' is state-novelty rather than object-novelty. The behavioral analog is plausible but the specific mechanisms may differ: primate temporal cortex object recognition is not directly instantiated in REE's E1 LSTM world model. The zona incerta has no identified REE analog, and implementing a dedicated novelty circuit would require new architectural elements not currently present. The stimulation and inactivation experiments are in healthy animals performing a well-practiced task; extrapolation to an agent learning from scratch in a novel environment introduces additional uncertainty. The paper also focuses specifically on visual novelty in a constrained paradigm, not on exploration of abstract state spaces.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is set at 0.76. Source quality is very high (Nature Neuroscience, rigorous methods, multiple converging lines of evidence). The finding is directly relevant to MECH-111's core claim: a dedicated novelty circuit exists and is distinct from reward circuits. Mapping fidelity is moderate because ZI has no REE analog and the transfer from primate visual novelty to grid-world state novelty requires architectural decisions. Transfer risk is moderate -- the architectural principle transfers (dedicated circuit, not repurposed reward signal) but the specific implementation does not. This paper, together with Wang 2024, provides the strongest available evidence for MECH-111.
