# Redondo et al. 2014 — Bidirectional switch of hippocampal contextual memory engram valence

## What they did

The Tonegawa group used activity-dependent ChR2 labelling to tag neurons active during either contextual fear conditioning or reward conditioning in two structures: the dentate gyrus (DG) of the hippocampus and the basolateral amygdala (BLA). They then asked: can you reverse the valence of a memory by reactivating the original engram cells during a conditioning session of the opposite valence?

## Key findings

The answer depends entirely on which structure holds the engram. DG engrams could be switched: mice whose DG engram was labelled during fear conditioning, and then reactivated during reward conditioning, subsequently showed appetitive rather than aversive responses to optogenetic engram reactivation. The reverse also worked (reward-to-fear). BLA engrams could not be switched — they maintained their original valence regardless of reconditioning.

At the cellular level, the valence switch in DG was accompanied by a change in functional connectivity between DG engram cells and BLA engram cells. After switching, the DG engram recruited different BLA populations than it had originally — as though the hippocampal context representation had been re-routed to a different emotional output.

## REE translation

This result speaks directly to the question of how the hippocampal terrain should handle valence. The finding says: hippocampal representations are valence-flexible containers. They store context, and the valence associated with that context can be updated. The amygdala, by contrast, stores valence more rigidly — its engrams resist revaluation.

For REE, this supports a model where the hippocampal terrain (ARC-007) is the updatable cognitive map, and valence information is written into it through associations with amygdala populations. When the environment changes (a dangerous place becomes safe, or a rewarded location stops rewarding), the hippocampal representation can be re-associated with different valence signals. The amygdala doesn't change its tags; the hippocampus changes which tags it connects to.

This has a direct implication for the wanting/liking distinction (MECH-229). Wanting — as a sustained, navigable approach drive — should live in the hippocampal terrain because it needs to be updatable (resources move, gradients shift). Liking — as a consummatory valence tag — is more naturally amygdala-like: it fires at contact, creates a fixed association, and doesn't need to be revised.

## Limitations

The valence switch was achieved by forced optogenetic reactivation during reconditioning, not through natural experience. Whether the same flexibility operates during normal navigation and reward-seeking is not directly tested. The paper also doesn't address the dopaminergic modulation pathway — the VTA-hippocampal return loop that might mediate the wanting-related plasticity in natural conditions. The DG/BLA dissociation is clear, but the mechanism by which the switch occurs at the synaptic level remains open.

## Confidence reasoning

High confidence (0.82) primarily because of the exceptional source quality and the directness of the structural finding. The mapping to REE is moderately faithful — the paper confirms that hippocampal representations are valence-flexible while amygdala representations are not, which is the core architectural principle at stake. The main caveat is that the paper doesn't test navigation or wanting per se.
