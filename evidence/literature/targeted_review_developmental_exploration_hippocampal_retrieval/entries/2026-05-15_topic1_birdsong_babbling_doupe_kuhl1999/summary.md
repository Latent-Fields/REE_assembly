# Birdsong and human speech: common themes and mechanisms
**Doupe & Kuhl (1999), Annual Review of Neuroscience** | DOI: 10.1146/annurev.neuro.22.1.567

## What the paper did

Comprehensive comparative review establishing that human speech and songbird vocal learning share an early BG-driven motor exploration epoch (subsong/babbling), critical period structure, auditory feedback dependence, and a hierarchical forebrain motor-auditory integration circuit.

## Key findings

BG-driven variability is necessary: pharmacological silencing of LMAN in juvenile birds arrests subsong and produces a permanently impoverished, stereotyped song -- the monostrategy signature. Both species have a critical period whose closure is partly self-driven (learning itself narrows subsequent plasticity).

## REE translation

Establishes two claims for ARC-065: (1) the BG-driven motor exploration epoch is causally necessary for repertoire formation -- not noise but a structured variability-generating system; (2) there is a developmental window during which this epoch must be sufficiently broad -- shortening or suppressing it produces permanently restricted behavioral repertoires.

## Limitations

Language semantics and compositionality are not analogous. BG-LMAN circuit has no direct mammalian homologue for non-vocal motor learning; functional principle transfers, mechanism does not.

## Confidence reasoning

Landmark Annual Review paper; high source quality. Good mapping to developmental bootstrapping principle; moderate penalty for vocal-to-spatial transfer. 0.78.
