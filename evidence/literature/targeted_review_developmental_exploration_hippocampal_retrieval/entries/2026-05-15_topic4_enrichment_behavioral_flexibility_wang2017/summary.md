# Differential effects of social and novelty enrichment on individual differences in impulsivity and behavioral flexibility
**Wang, Marshall & Kirkpatrick (2017), Behavioural Brain Research** | DOI: 10.1016/j.bbr.2017.03.028

## What the paper did

Systematically dissected early-life rearing environment into two components -- social (paired vs. isolated) and novelty (novel objects vs. none) -- and tested their independent and interactive effects on impulsive choice, impulsive action, and behavioral flexibility in Sprague-Dawley rats.

## Key findings

Social enrichment and novelty enrichment produced distinct and partially opposing effects. Social enrichment alone decreased impulsive choice but increased regressive errors in the behavioral flexibility task (more perseveration on the previously-rewarded option). Novelty enrichment reduced never-reinforced errors (reduced inflexibility about untried options). Both together produced complex interaction effects. Individual differences in timing sensitivity predicted both impulsive choice and behavioral flexibility, suggesting shared temporal processing as an underlying factor.

## REE translation

The dissociation is a key qualification for ARC-065: the type of early experience matters, not just its quantity. Novelty enrichment -- exposure to varied sensory/motor challenges -- specifically reduces the 'never tried this option' failure mode, which maps to expanding the trajectory store by populating it with diverse option templates. Social enrichment alone does not expand the option store in the same way; it may even increase perseveration on the currently-active strategy.

For the monostrategy problem specifically, novelty enrichment is the relevant developmental intervention. An agent that experiences only social interaction but limited environmental novelty may be socially adapted but behaviorally rigid when novel contexts arise.

## Limitations

Operant task (choice under delay/probability) differs substantially from spatial navigation. The mechanism by which novelty enrichment reduces never-reinforced errors is unknown -- hippocampal, PFC, or basal ganglia accounts are all plausible. Mixed findings complicate a simple 'more exploration = more flexibility' account.
