# Telling Apart Motor Noise and Exploratory Behavior, in Early Development
**Gliga (2018) -- Frontiers in Psychology**

## What the paper did

This invited review by Teodora Gliga (then at Birkbeck) synthesizes the empirical literature on infant motor variability and poses the question: how do we operationally distinguish active exploration from the activity of an immature motor system? Gliga reviews evidence across reaching, mouthing, and vocalization contexts, with a particular focus on whether variability is modulated by information-gathering opportunities.

## Key findings

The central proposal is that infants increase motor activity and variability specifically when faced with opportunities to gather new information -- about their own bodies or about the world. This contextual modulation of variability is the proposed criterion for distinguishing exploration from noise. Gliga further identifies multiple forms of variability generation in early development: (1) deliberate hypothesis testing, (2) increasing the variability of environmental actions to probe affordances, and (3) sensorimotor contingency learning (discovering how one's own movements change sensory input). Each form corresponds to different developmental contexts and different potential learning mechanisms.

## REE translation

Gliga's review is conceptually important for REE because it maps the diversity space. Three distinct variability forms -- hypothesis testing, environmental variance probing, and contingency discovery -- are not the same mechanism, and REE's diversity mechanisms (MECH-313, MECH-314, MECH-320) appear to correspond roughly to these three categories. MECH-313 (novelty/exploration bias) drives hypothesis-testing type variability. MECH-314 (dACC-based monitoring) tracks outcome variability. MECH-320 (lateral PFC meta-control) governs deliberate strategy switching.

The most practical REE implication is the principle that healthy exploration variability is context-modulated: it increases precisely when the agent faces information-rich contexts and decreases in familiar, mastered contexts. A flat-entropy diversity mechanism that does not respond to context cannot replicate healthy infant exploration.

## Limitations and caveats

This is a theoretical review, not an empirical study; the proposed guiding principle has not been tested in a large-scale, preregistered study. The mechanisms generating context-sensitive variability remain underspecified at the neural level. The review conflates variability forms across different motor domains (arm reaching, mouthing, vocalization) that may have distinct regulatory mechanisms.
