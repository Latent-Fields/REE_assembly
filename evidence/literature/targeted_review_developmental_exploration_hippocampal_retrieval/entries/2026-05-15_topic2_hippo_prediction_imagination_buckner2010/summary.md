# The role of the hippocampus in prediction and imagination
**Buckner (2010), Annual Review of Psychology** | DOI: 10.1146/annurev.psych.60.110707.163508

## What the paper did

Buckner's 2010 Annual Review paper reframes the hippocampus away from its traditional role as a passive memory archive. Drawing on rodent replay/preplay electrophysiology and human neuroimaging studies of future-event imagination, he argues that the hippocampal system is better understood as a prediction/simulation device that facilitates upcoming-event modelling during decision-making and at rest.

## Key findings

Hippocampus and associated cortex are active when people envision future events; hippocampal damage impairs this ability. In rats, hippocampal ensembles preplay event sequences before goal-directed navigation -- sequences that, if strung together in novel combinations, could provide the building blocks for planning. The hippocampal system is spontaneously active during task-free rest and sleep, suggesting idle-state generation of new representations.

## REE translation

This is the conceptual anchor for ARC-007. Buckner's argument is that hippocampus is not a store indexed by past event labels but a generator of possible sequences indexed by context. In REE terms: the hippocampus provides the trajectory template library, and different goal/context states query different subsets of that library. The 'novel combinations' framing is also directly relevant to the cue-differentiation requirement: different PFC cues should retrieve different trajectory combinations from the same underlying substrate.

## Limitations

The 2010 review predates Pfeiffer & Foster 2013, which provides more direct evidence that preplay events are specifically directed toward remembered goal locations. The mechanistic account of how novel combinations are generated remains underspecified.
