# Summary: Gergely and Watson (1996) -- Social Biofeedback Theory

**Source:** Gergely, Gyorgy and Watson, John S. (1996). The social biofeedback theory of parental affect-mirroring: the development of emotional self-awareness and self-control in infancy. International Journal of Psycho-Analysis, 77(6), 1181-1212. PMID:9119582.

**Claim IDs:** ARC-049, INV-059, Q-035

**Evidence direction:** supports | **Confidence:** 0.73

## Key Finding

Infants possess a dedicated contingency detection and maximising mechanism that tracks conditional probability of environmental responses to their behavior. Applied to parental affect-mirroring, this mechanism mediates emotional self-awareness development. The mechanism operates preverbally, presymbolically, and is biologically primary. Caregiver expressions must be 'marked' (perceptibly different from ordinary expressions) to allow the infant to tag them as communicative rather than realistic.

## REE Relevance

Provides the mechanistic grounding for continuous frame monitoring as a primitive: the contingency detection module is not a developmental achievement but a core biological capacity. The frame monitoring signal in REE is this contingency computation running over social interaction.

For ARC-049: the play frame tag does not require high-level social cognition -- it is grounded in a primitive contingency-detection loop that runs continuously.

For INV-059: the contingency detection module is always active during social interaction; its disruption (non-contingent responding) immediately generates a mismatch signal. Frame maintenance is necessary because the monitoring mechanism runs whether or not agents intend it.

The 'marked' mirroring concept (expressions similar but perceptibly different from infant's) prefigures the REE idea of frame-acknowledgment signals that must be distinguishable from ordinary behavior -- a potential design constraint for the play frame tag signal.

## Limitations

- Primarily theoretical; empirical support from cited studies rather than novel data.
- Addresses dyadic caregiver-infant contingency, not bilateral peer play.
- Neural substrate and precise developmental origin of the contingency-detection module are left unspecified.

## Action

Supports ARC-049, INV-059, Q-035. Suggests missing claim: a MECH-level claim is needed for the contingency detection module as the substrate of frame monitoring -- the biological mechanism that generates the frame-state signal in REE. Also suggests design constraint: the play frame acknowledgment signal must be 'marked' (perceptibly different from non-acknowledgment) for the monitoring system to use it reliably.
