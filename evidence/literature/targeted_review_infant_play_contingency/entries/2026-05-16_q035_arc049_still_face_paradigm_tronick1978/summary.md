# Summary: Tronick et al. (1978) -- Still-Face Paradigm

**Source:** Tronick, Als, Adamson, Wise, Brazelton (1978). Infants response to entrapment between contradictory messages in face-to-face interaction. JAACAP.

**Claim IDs:** Q-035, ARC-049, INV-059

**Evidence direction:** supports | **Confidence:** 0.85

## Key Finding

The original still-face paradigm demonstrates that 3-4 month old infants immediately detect and respond to the collapse of normal interactive contingency. When a caregiver adopts a neutral, unresponsive face mid-interaction, infants show reduced positive affect, increased social bidding attempts, and eventual withdrawal. The effect carries over into the reunion episode (partial residual negative affect even after normal interaction resumes).

## REE Relevance

Foundational evidence that continuous frame monitoring is operational from early infancy. Infants monitor within-episode contingency in near-real-time and generate a violation signal when expected contingent responding fails. The carry-over effect shows the monitoring signal persists and generates lasting state changes.

For Q-035: the heartbeat interpretation is validated -- infants respond to within-episode monitoring failures, not just frame boundaries. Continuous monitoring is the primitive architecture.

For ARC-049: even the simplest sensorimotor play requires ongoing bilateral contingency tracking, not just open/close bookends.

For INV-059: mutual frame maintenance disruption immediately degrades the interaction.

## Limitations

- SFP disrupts all contingency simultaneously (not subtle mid-game content change).
- Cannot distinguish continuous monitoring from very frequent periodic sampling.
- Conflates contingency monitoring with affect regulation and stress responses.

## Action

Supports existing REE claims Q-035, ARC-049, INV-059. Also suggests experiment: test whether a partial contingency reduction (not total still-face) generates proportional monitoring response in REE -- a gradient experiment for frame confidence.
