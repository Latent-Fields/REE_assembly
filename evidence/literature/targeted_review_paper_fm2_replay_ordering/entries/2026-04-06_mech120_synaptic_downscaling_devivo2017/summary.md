# de Vivo et al. 2017 -- Ultrastructural Evidence for Synaptic Scaling Across the Wake/Sleep Cycle

**Science, 355(6324), pp. 507-510. DOI: 10.1126/science.aah5982. PMID: 28154076**

de Vivo et al. provide the most direct ultrastructural evidence for the synaptic homeostasis hypothesis (SHY) to date. Using three-dimensional electron microscopy in mouse motor and somatosensory cortices, the team measured 6920 individual synapses and found that axon-spine interface (ASI) area -- a proxy for synaptic strength -- decreased approximately 18% after sleep compared to after waking. This is not a uniform subtraction: the decrease was proportional to synapse size, which is the signature of scaling. Synapses that were already large (presumably representing the most strongly-potentiated, salient waking experiences) showed the greatest absolute reduction. A small subset -- approximately 20% of synapses, the very largest -- were spared, possibly corresponding to highly consolidated long-term memories that should not be erased.

This paper directly instantiates the MECH-120 mechanism. MECH-120 claims that slow-wave sleep performs weight normalisation before replay, specifically to prevent dominant high-salience experiences from monopolising the weight landscape. The de Vivo data are physical evidence that this normalisation actually occurs: the synapses most enlarged by waking Hebbian potentiation (the dominant traces in weight-landscape terms) are precisely those most reduced during SWS. The result is a flatter landscape -- not uniform, because large synapses are still relatively larger, but with smaller curvature differences. Into this flatter landscape, subsequent replay (MECH-165) can write more equitably across diverse memory content.

A question that the paper cannot answer is the causal ordering question: does the downscaling happen before or after the SWR-triggered replay events during NREM? The paper measures ASI after sleep versus after wake as a global comparison, not at individual synapses relative to their participation in SWR events. This is an important gap for the FM2 argument: MECH-120 requires that normalisation precedes replay at the relevant synapses, not merely that normalisation occurs during the same sleep stage. It is plausible that slow oscillation-driven downscaling and SWR-triggered replay events are interleaved, and the relative ordering at individual synapses is not yet established.

The paper also does not address the functional consequences of sparing the largest synapses. One reading is that these represent 'protected' memories -- the most important ones should not be downscaled away. Another reading is relevant to FM2: if the largest synapses (dominant traces) are the ones least normalised, they still retain a relative advantage in driving SWR initiation during the same night. This would mean that even with SHY normalisation, some dominant-trace bias persists -- the normalisation reduces but may not eliminate the replay priority bias documented by Huelin Gorriz et al. 2023.

Despite these caveats, de Vivo et al. remains the strongest direct evidence for the physical reality of the MECH-120 weight normalisation step. The proportional scaling mechanism, the selectivity for recently-potentiated synapses, and the temporal placement during SWS all align with MECH-120's functional requirements for the FM2 prevention mechanism.
