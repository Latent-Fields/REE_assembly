# Robins 1995 -- Catastrophic Forgetting, Rehearsal, and Pseudorehearsal

**Connection Science, 7(2), pp. 123-146. DOI: 10.1080/09540099550039318**

Robins 1995 is the foundational computational paper on why rehearsal (replay) effectiveness depends on the prior weight landscape. The central demonstration is this: when a neural network has been trained on task A, the weight space contains attractors that represent those patterns. If the network is then trained on task B and subsequently attempts to rehearse task A items, the rehearsal gradient descent flows along directions shaped by the task-B-dominated landscape, re-potentiating task B structure rather than fully restoring task A. The result is not recovery but further interference. The paper proposes pseudorehearsal -- generating representative samples from the network's output distribution before it is overwritten -- as a way to capture the shape of the prior landscape and rehearse against that rather than against the corrupted post-B landscape.

The relevance to REE Failure Mode 2 is structural: the mathematical problem is identical. Gradient descent from replay writes preferentially along the directions of highest curvature in the current loss landscape; if those directions are dominated by recently-potentiated (high-salience, high-frequency) experiences, replay flows along those directions and the dominant trace is reinforced. Weaker, older, or less-frequently-encoded content -- which produces smaller gradient signals in a dominated landscape -- is not consolidated, because the stronger attractors absorb most of the gradient update. MECH-120's claim that SHY normalisation must precede replay is the biological solution to exactly this problem: by proportionally downscaling the most-potentiated weights, NREM sleep reduces the curvature asymmetry and creates a landscape where replay can consolidate diverse content.

It is worth being precise about what Robins 1995 does and does not show. The paper does not study sleep, nor does it study hippocampal dynamics, Hebbian plasticity, or anything like SWR-triggered replay. The networks are simple feedforward or recurrent architectures trained with backpropagation -- a supervised learning algorithm that has no direct equivalent in biological neural circuits. The 'catastrophic forgetting' that Robins studies is also somewhat different from the failure mode in MECH-165: in Robins, the problem is sequential task learning (learn A, then B destroys A); in REE FM2, the concern is within-night consolidation selectivity (all experiences are from the same waking period, but high-salience ones monopolise replay). These are related but not identical problems.

Nonetheless, the core insight -- that the weight landscape at the time of replay determines what replay can and cannot consolidate -- transfers cleanly. Robins does not claim his solution (pseudorehearsal) has a neural correlate, but subsequent commentators have noted that SHY-normalisation + offline replay is the biological system that implements roughly this logic: the downscaling step creates a landscape where all memory traces have been proportionally reduced, analogous to having a more balanced landscape from which replay can write without dominant-attractor interference. This framing makes Robins 1995 a conceptual anchor for the FM2 argument, even though the biological system is substantially more complex.

The paper is foundational for the continual learning literature and is still cited in contemporary work. Its contribution to the REE argument is as a principled demonstration that ordering matters: replay must happen into an appropriately-structured landscape, and if the landscape has not been normalised, replay does not deliver what consolidation needs.
