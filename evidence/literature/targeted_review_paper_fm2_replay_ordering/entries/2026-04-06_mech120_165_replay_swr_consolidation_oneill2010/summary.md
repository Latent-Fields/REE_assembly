# O'Neill et al. 2010 -- Play it again: Reactivation of Waking Experience and Memory

**Trends in Neurosciences, 33(5), pp. 220-229. DOI: 10.1016/j.tins.2010.01.006. PMID: 20207025**

O'Neill, Pleydell-Bouverie, Dupret, and Csicsvari review the evidence for hippocampal memory reactivation during sleep and rest, and propose a mechanistic account centred on sharp wave/ripple (SWR) events. The paper describes how episodic and spatial memories encoded in hippocampal neuronal circuits can be stabilised via 'reactivation' -- replay of the original network firing patterns during sleep and rest. The key proposal is that SWR events in the hippocampus coordinate this reactivation and direct the reinstatement of memory traces in cortical circuits. The hippocampus 'organises' the reactivation process, meaning that the internal hippocampal state at the time of SWR generation determines what gets replayed.

This last point is the critical link to Failure Mode 2. If the hippocampus organises replay from its internal state, then the internal state at replay time determines replay content. That internal state reflects the cumulative Hebbian potentiation from waking experiences. High-salience experiences -- those involving more exploration, stronger prediction errors, or emotional valence -- drive more potentiation in the relevant hippocampal assemblies, giving those assemblies a lower firing threshold and stronger internal connectivity. When SWR events occur, these over-potentiated assemblies are preferentially recruited. The dominant trace drives replay; the replay further potentiates the dominant trace; weaker traces are marginalised.

The FM2 argument requires this mechanistic step: that the weight landscape at replay time determines which assemblies are recruited into SWRs, and therefore determines what gets consolidated. O'Neill et al. do not state this explicitly -- they are reviewing replay mechanisms, not SHY-ordering effects -- but the mechanism they describe implies it. The hippocampus organising replay from its internal state means the internal state (i.e., the weight landscape) is the causal upstream variable. MECH-120's normalisation step intervenes at this point: by proportionally reducing synaptic weights before SWR events fire, the dominant assemblies' advantage is reduced and diverse content has a more equal opportunity to be recruited into replay.

It is important to acknowledge what the paper does not show. O'Neill et al. do not address synaptic homeostasis, the NREM-then-REM ordering, or what would happen to replay if homeostasis were disrupted. The paper is primarily a mechanistic review of the SWR-replay system, and its relevance to FM2 is inferential: the mechanism it describes is the one that SHY normalisation needs to regulate. The paper also predates the ultrastructural SHY evidence (de Vivo 2017) and the replay priority quantification (Huelin Gorriz 2023), so it lacks the empirical connection between synapse size and replay probability that would make the FM2 causal chain complete.

Taken together with de Vivo et al. and Huelin Gorriz et al., O'Neill et al. provides the mechanistic channel (SWR-driven reactivation organised by hippocampal internal state) through which the weight-landscape bias operates. The three papers together make a coherent argument: (1) salient experiences over-potentiate hippocampal assemblies (implied by O'Neill, confirmed by Huelin Gorriz); (2) those assemblies preferentially drive SWR-replay (O'Neill mechanism); (3) SHY normalisation during SWS reduces this potentiation before replay fires (de Vivo evidence); (4) therefore, normalisation before replay is functionally necessary, not optional (MECH-120 / FM2 argument).
