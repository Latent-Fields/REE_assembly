# Huelin Gorriz, Takigawa & Bendor 2023 -- The Role of Experience in Prioritizing Hippocampal Replay

**Nature Communications, 14(1), 8157. DOI: 10.1038/s41467-023-43939-z. PMID: 38071221**

Huelin Gorriz and colleagues provide direct empirical evidence that hippocampal replay during sleep is biased toward high-salience, novel, and more-frequently-encoded experiences. Male rats ran spatial trajectories on two linear tracks differing in novelty and number of laps. During subsequent sleep, hippocampal place cell assemblies were monitored. The core finding: sleep replay rate for a given track was proportional to the number of spatial trajectories run and decreased with increasing familiarity. More novel and more-practised tracks received substantially more sleep replay events. The cumulative number of awake replay events during waking behaviour was the best predictor of which memories received prioritised sleep replay.

This is the empirical demonstration of the failure mode that MECH-165 names. In the absence of any corrective mechanism, hippocampal sleep replay is not egalitarian: it preferentially consolidates what has already been most heavily encoded during waking. The system's natural tendency is to reinforce dominant traces. MECH-165 claims that this becomes a pathological failure when synaptic homeostasis (MECH-120) does not first flatten the landscape; without normalisation, the replay bias documented here is not corrected, and diverse but weaker memories remain under-consolidated.

The proposed mechanism is nuanced and worth understanding carefully for the mapping caveat. The authors propose that awake replay events 'tag' memories via neuromodulatory pathways (e.g. dopamine), increasing cortical circuit excitability for those memories during subsequent sleep. This increased excitability biases hippocampal SWR events toward those circuits. This mechanism is distinct from -- but compatible with -- the SHY weight-landscape mechanism. Both mechanisms predict the same directional bias: higher-salience, more-encoded experiences get more sleep replay. The SHY mechanism says the bias is embedded in synaptic weights; the tagging mechanism says it is encoded in post-translational excitability changes. In the intact system, both probably operate in parallel.

The normative question is important: is this prioritisation adaptive or pathological? The authors treat it as adaptive -- the system is prioritising what mattered most during waking, which is functionally reasonable for survival-relevant learning. The REE FM2 argument is not that prioritisation is always pathological, but that without a homeostatic mechanism to bound it, the dominant trace can monopolise consolidation entirely, producing the monostrategy failure mode. MECH-120 (SHY) is the bounding mechanism: it reduces but does not eliminate the replay priority advantage of high-salience experiences (de Vivo shows that the very largest synapses are partially spared, consistent with protecting genuinely important memories). The FM2 failure is what happens when MECH-120 fails to operate -- either from insufficient sleep or disrupted slow-wave activity -- in which case the naturally-occurring bias documented here runs uncorrected.

The paper does not address what happens when SWS is disrupted or homeostasis is impaired, so it cannot directly test the MECH-120 ordering constraint. It establishes the baseline bias that MECH-120 must counteract, making it a key empirical anchor for framing both claims. The strength of this paper is its quantitative precision: replay rate as a function of lap count and novelty gives a parametric description of the bias, which could in principle be used to predict the degree of monostrategy failure as a function of SWY normalisation efficiency.
