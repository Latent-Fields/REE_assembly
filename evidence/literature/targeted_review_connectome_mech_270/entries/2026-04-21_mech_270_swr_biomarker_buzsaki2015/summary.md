# Buzsáki (2015) — "Hippocampal sharp wave-ripple: A cognitive biomarker for episodic memory and planning"

**Hippocampus** 25:1073–1188. [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1002/hipo.22488). PMID 26135716.

*(According to PubMed.)*

## What the paper does

Buzsáki — the senior authority on sharp-wave ripples — provides a comprehensive review of the SWR literature from the 1980s forward. He frames SWRs as "the most synchronous population pattern in the mammalian brain" and walks through their generation (CA3 recurrent excitation drives a sharp-wave; this drives a fast ripple oscillation in CA1), their coordination (a consortium of interneurons temporally and spatially arranges pyramidal spike output), their behavioural context (consummatory behaviours and non-REM sleep), and their functional role (compressed replay of waking sequences, memory consolidation via transfer to distributed cortical circuits, decision-making, planning, possibly creativity).

The review also touches on pathology — "p-ripples" as epileptogenic markers, SWR alterations in rodent models of schizophrenia and Alzheimer's — and the neuromodulatory context that gates SWR occurrence. For present purposes the most relevant section is the discussion of SWR coordination mechanisms.

## Findings relevant to MECH-270

MECH-270 proposes ephaptic field coherence in CA1/CA3 pyramidal layers as the biological substrate for per-stream verisimilitude readout. That proposal is only plausible if SWRs are precisely the kind of event where ephaptic field effects should dominate over noise — large-amplitude, tightly synchronised population firing in a densely packed pyramidal layer. Buzsáki's review establishes exactly those conditions. The characterisation of SWRs as "the most synchronous population pattern in the mammalian brain" is not just colourful — it is the physical regime where sub-threshold field effects become a substantial fraction of the coordination budget, which is what Anastassiou et al. 2011 measured at smaller amplitudes.

The review attributes SWR coordination primarily to interneuron consortia and to recurrent CA3 excitation driving CA1. Those are synaptic-level mechanisms. MECH-270 does not contest them; it proposes that ephaptic field effects sit alongside synaptic coordination and carry additional information — specifically, per-stream alignment quality. The two mechanisms superpose rather than compete.

## How it translates to REE

For REE the useful take is that SWRs are the architecturally correct event class for MECH-270 to live in. Other hippocampal rhythms (theta, gamma, irregular activity) involve much smaller synchronous populations and much weaker fields; the ephaptic-readout hypothesis would be harder to defend in those regimes. SWRs are the event where the hypothesis has the best physical footing.

The review also reinforces MECH-271's routing story by emphasising that SWR excitatory output affects "a wide area of the cortex and several subcortical nuclei." The downstream fan-out is not scattershot — it is the specific set of targets MECH-271 identifies (subiculum to EC to neocortex; PFC; amygdala; NAc; mammillary bodies via fornix). Buzsáki's framing that SWR-disruption interferes with memory is consistent with MECH-271's prediction that routing matters: selectively preventing replay from reaching consolidation targets would produce exactly the memory deficits he describes.

## Limitations and caveats

The review does not discuss ephaptic coupling as an SWR coordination mechanism. Buzsáki frames the coordination as synaptic and interneuron-mediated; MECH-270's field-coherence-as-readout proposal is an extrapolation beyond what he asserts. Counting this paper as direct MECH-270 evidence would be overreach — it provides the context (SWRs are the right event class, the synchrony is real) rather than the mechanism (ephaptic contribution to coordination).

The review is primarily rodent; human SWR literature is less developed and more indirect. MECH-270 as stated is substrate-level and should be cross-species conserved, but the quantitative details that would discriminate synaptic-only from synaptic-plus-ephaptic coordination are not established in any species yet.

The p-ripple / pathology discussion is a useful reminder that SWR mechanisms can be pushed off their healthy operating point; this matters for REE's account of confabulatory failure modes (MECH-271 tag erosion) but Buzsáki does not unpack the specific failure signatures REE would care about.

## Confidence reasoning

Source quality is essentially maximal — this is the definitive SWR review by the senior figure in the field. Mapping fidelity is the main discount: the review provides enabling context for MECH-270 but does not address the ephaptic-coupling proposal directly. Transfer risk is low because SWRs are cross-species conserved and the review is architectural rather than task-specific. Net confidence 0.68.
