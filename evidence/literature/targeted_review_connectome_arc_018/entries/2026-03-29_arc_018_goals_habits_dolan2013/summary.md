# Dolan & Dayan (2013) — Goals and habits in the brain

**Source:** Neuron, 80(2):312-325. DOI: [10.1016/j.neuron.2013.09.007](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2013.09.007)
**PubMed:** PMID 24139036

## What the paper did

Dolan and Dayan review four decades of work on the distinction between goal-directed and habitual decision-making in animals and humans. They trace the theoretical lineage from early Tolman/Hull animal learning debates through the modern model-based/model-free reinforcement learning framework, and then through human neuroscience paradigms that have tested and complicated the dichotomy. The review covers lesion studies, neuroimaging, computational model fitting, and the emerging evidence for simultaneous engagement of both systems in normal behaviour. Particular attention is paid to when goal-directed behaviour breaks down (habit formation, drug addiction, OCD) and to the brain regions implicated in each mode.

## Key findings

The review establishes several theoretical points directly relevant to ARC-018. Goal-directed behaviour requires an internal model -- a representation of the transition structure and outcome values of the environment that supports prospective simulation. Hippocampus is identified as a key substrate for this model, particularly for encoding the relational structure that allows flexible value updating. The crucial property is that goal-directed systems can update behaviour after outcome revaluation without re-experiencing the re-valued state: the viability map is consulted prospectively, not built only from experienced transitions. The paper also reviews evidence that the goal-directed system is computationally expensive and fails under cognitive load, time pressure, or extended training -- all conditions that shift control to the habitual system. This shift has direct clinical implications (addiction, compulsion).

## REE translation -- ARC-018

Dolan & Dayan provide the conceptual architecture within which ARC-018's viability map claim is situated. If goal-directed behaviour requires a hippocampal world model whose values can be updated prospectively, then the hippocampal viability map is the implementation of exactly that model. ARC-018's specific claim -- that the map is indexed by E2 action-object coordinates and updated by E3 harm/goal error -- is a mechanistic proposal for how that model is built and maintained. The Dolan-Dayan framework validates the need for such a structure without specifying its implementation.

One particularly relevant thread in the review is the failure-mode analysis. If the goal-directed system fails under load (shifting to habit), this is consistent with ARC-018's commitment boundary architecture: when E3 harm/goal evaluation is disrupted or overwhelmed, the system falls back on habitual (E1-driven) responses, bypassing the viability map entirely. The clinical pathologies the paper describes -- addiction as excessive habitisation, OCD as maladaptive goal-directed rumination -- map onto REE failure modes worth considering in harm-signal architecture.

The paper's discussion of hippocampal lesion effects is relevant: hippocampal damage impairs outcome revaluation (flexible goal-directed updating) but not simple habit formation -- a double dissociation that directly supports ARC-018's claim that hippocampus is specifically required for viability map maintenance, not for all learning.

Where the corrected V3 framing finds support: the theoretical necessity of a hippocampal model for prospective value computation is well established here. Where it is agnostic: the paper does not address the coordinate system (place vs action-object), the specific error signal (E3), or the harm-specific component of viability.

## Limitations and caveats

This is a review paper -- the evidence it synthesises comes from many sources with varying methodological quality. The goal-directed/habitual dichotomy is now known to be more of a spectrum with complex interactions; some of the cleaner lesion dissociations reviewed here have proven harder to replicate in newer paradigms. The hippocampal role in model-based planning, while well-supported, is a simplification: prefrontal cortex also plays a major role, and the division of labour is still debated.

## Confidence reasoning

High-quality theoretical grounding that supports the general framework within which ARC-018 makes sense. Confidence is held at 0.68 because this is a review providing conceptual scaffolding rather than direct mechanistic evidence, and because the mapping from the abstract goal-directed/habitual distinction to the specific V3 claims (E2 indexing, E3 error, action-object coordinates) requires additional theoretical work that the paper does not perform.
