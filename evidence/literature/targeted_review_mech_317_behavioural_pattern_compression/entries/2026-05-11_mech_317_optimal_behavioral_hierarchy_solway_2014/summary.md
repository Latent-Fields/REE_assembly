# Solway et al. 2014 -- Optimal behavioural hierarchy

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pcbi.1003779), PMID 25122479), Solway and colleagues take on a normative question that the options-framework literature had been quietly skirting: when there are many possible decompositions of a task into chunked sub-units, which one is best? The 1999 Sutton-Precup-Singh framework defines what options ARE; the 2017 Bacon-Harb-Precup paper defines a gradient method for discovering them; neither paper specifies which decompositions are optimal among the many gradient discovery could converge to. Solway et al.'s contribution is an information-theoretic optimality criterion: the best behavioural hierarchy is the one that minimises the joint description-length of the task's transition structure under the hierarchical decomposition. Operationally, optimal chunks align with bottleneck states (low-entropy transitions in the trajectory record) and with persistent sub-tasks (transition clusters that maintain internal coherence). The mathematical contribution is a derivation of the criterion; the empirical contribution is four behavioural experiments showing that human learners spontaneously discover hierarchies matching the criterion.

For MECH-317 this is the normative target against which implementation flavours should be evaluated. The cluster question 'which option-discovery substrate is correct for MECH-317' decomposes into two sub-questions: (a) what should the produced chunks look like (the normative-criterion question)? and (b) what computational machinery produces them (the implementation-substrate question)? Solway et al. answer (a). Sutton-Precup-Singh 1999 + Bacon-Harb-Precup 2017 + Eysenbach 2018 answer different versions of (b). The framework lets us ask: does substrate X produce chunks that satisfy criterion Y? -- which is what a substrate-design pass for MECH-317 actually has to answer.

The substrate-design relevance for REE: any candidate MECH-317 implementation should be evaluated against the Solway optimality criterion on the target behavioural substrate. SD-054's reef-approach-feed-retreat sequence is a candidate trajectory with potential bottleneck states (at reef-patch boundaries) and persistent sub-tasks (within-patch foraging). A V3 instantiation of MECH-317 that produces chunks aligned with reef-patch boundaries would be normatively-correct in the Solway sense. A V3 instantiation that produces arbitrary action-length chunks not aligned with bottleneck states would not be -- it would be 'doing chunking' in some loose sense but not 'doing optimal chunking'. The latter is probably what the user actually wants from MECH-317.

The empirical-anchor strength is also non-trivial. Solway et al.'s four experiments produced testable predictions about which decompositions human subjects converge on -- including in some cases where the spontaneously-discovered hierarchy was not the most obvious surface-level decomposition. If REE's V3 substrate is supposed to instantiate a humanlike option-discovery mechanism, behavioural validation should reproduce at least some of the Solway-et-al spontaneous-discovery signatures on an analogous trajectory-record substrate.

The relationship to the parallel-session MECH-323 registration today (the ARC-071 chunk-formation operator) is also worth noting. MECH-323 specifies the trigger conditions for chunk formation (repetition + outcome-consistency + evaluative gate); it does not specify the normative-criterion that the formed chunks must satisfy. Solway et al. fills that gap. The right reading is: MECH-323 specifies WHEN chunks form (the trigger); Sutton-Precup-Singh specifies WHAT chunks ARE (the option triple); Solway et al. specifies HOW GOOD chunks should be (the optimality criterion); option-critic / DIAYN / hand-engineered approaches specify HOW chunks are computed (the implementation flavour). All four are necessary parts of a complete substrate-design specification.

The mapping caveat I would not want to bury: Solway et al.'s analysis is normative. It defines optimal hierarchies and shows that humans can find them. The substrate-level implementation that produces optimal hierarchies is not directly prescribed; the four experiments tested behaviour rather than substrate. The paper is best read as defining the normative target for option-discovery, against which different implementation flavours can be compared. Whether REE's specific behavioural substrate has well-defined optimal hierarchies in the Solway sense is a substrate-readiness question; the answer depends on the bottleneck-state structure of the trajectory record. SD-054's reef-approach-feed-retreat sequence may have clearly-defined bottleneck states; SD-054-extended-with-multi-rule-context environments definitely will.

I rate this confidence 0.80. Source quality is high (PLOS Comput Biol, Barto-Niv-Botvinick team). Mapping fidelity is strong for the normative criterion. Transfer risk is low for the criterion itself; somewhat elevated for whether REE's specific trajectory-record substrate has well-defined optimal hierarchies, which is substrate-dependent. This is the load-bearing normative anchor for the cluster -- the criterion against which substrate-design choices should be evaluated.
