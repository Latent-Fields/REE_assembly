# Distinct electrophysiological potentials for intention in action and prior intention for action

**Vinding, Jensen & Overgaard (2013) — Cortex**
DOI: [10.1016/j.cortex.2013.09.001](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cortex.2013.09.001)
*Based on articles retrieved from PubMed*

## What the paper did

Vinding and colleagues asked a deceptively simple question: is there something neurally distinctive about forming a plan to act *before* the moment of action, as opposed to deciding to move *at* the moment of movement? They recruited healthy adults and had them perform a voluntary finger movement task under three conditions: (1) act as soon as the intention arises (proximal), (2) form the intention and then wait 2.5 seconds before moving (distal, self-paced), and (3) receive an external cue to wait 2.5 seconds then move (distal, externally-cued). EEG was recorded throughout, with analysis of both the readiness potential (RP) window near movement and a longer window spanning the moment of intention formation.

## Key findings

The late RP differed significantly across conditions -- consistent with known literature -- but the more interesting finding was the slow negative deflection over mid-frontal electrodes that appeared exclusively during the self-paced distal intention condition, labelled the 'intention potential'. This signal arose at the moment participants consciously formed the plan to move later, not at the moment of movement. Crucially, it was absent when the same temporal delay was externally imposed. This dissociation shows that the neural commitment to act has at least two separable temporal components: a prospective planning signal tied to *deciding* to do something, and a proximal preparatory signal tied to *initiating* it.

## REE translation

This finding maps directly onto Q-015's question about the minimal contract for a commit token. If the token only carries a single timestamp (the moment of gate opening, analogous to the late RP), it loses information about when the agent *formed the intention* to reach that gate. For REE, this matters because E1 and E3 operate at different timescales: E3's planning horizon (the distal register) and E1's sensorium loop (the proximal register) may have made separate commitments at different moments. A commit token carrying only the proximal trigger cannot support attribution across both registers -- it cannot answer "was this outcome anticipated by the long-horizon planner or was it a last-minute impulse?" The intention potential suggests that the distal register is a genuine, observable commitment with its own neural substrate, not merely a description of behavior in retrospect.

## Limitations

The 2.5-second delay is a laboratory artefact -- clean enough to show the dissociation but too short to model E3-scale planning horizons. The self-paced vs. externally-cued manipulation is important for REE: in REE, authority-triggered commits (E3 responding to an external ethical override) may bypass the distal register and collapse to a purely proximal token. The paper does not address what happens to multi-timescale attribution when the distal planning phase is pre-empted. EEG spatial resolution also limits inference about which regions are actually implementing the two registers -- the mid-frontal label is consistent with pre-SMA/mPFC but not definitive.

## Confidence reasoning

I rate this 0.72. The experimental dissociation is clean and the human EEG data are credible. The structural insight -- that intention has at least two separable temporal registers, each with its own neural signature -- transfers well to REE. The transfer risk is moderate: the commit token in REE is a discrete categorical object, not a continuous EEG waveform, and the timescale mapping is approximate. The key inferential step (that a minimal token must carry both registers) is supported but not proven by this paper alone.
