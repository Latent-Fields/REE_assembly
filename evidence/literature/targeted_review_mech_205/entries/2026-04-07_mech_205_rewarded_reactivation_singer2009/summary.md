# Rewarded Outcomes Enhance Reactivation of Experience in the Hippocampus

**Singer & Frank, 2009, Neuron**

## What the paper does

Singer and Frank recorded single units in hippocampal CA3 while rats performed a spatial sequence-switching task that allowed direct comparison of identical trajectories yielding or not yielding reward. This isolates the reward effect from confounds of trajectory, location, or running speed.

## Key findings relevant to MECH-205

Place cells were significantly more active during SWRs following reward receipt: more SWR events, higher activation probability per ripple, and more spikes per event. Crucially, the reward-related enhancement of reactivation was *strongest during learning* -- when animals first encountered a new reward contingency (Sequence 2), SWR reactivation increased beyond levels seen for familiar rewarded sequences. This learning-phase amplification is precisely what one would expect if *surprise* (reward prediction error) drives the prioritisation rather than reward value alone: reward is most surprising when the association is new.

## Mapping to REE

The finding maps onto MECH-205's surprise buffer: salient outcomes with high prediction error (here, novel reward) receive enhanced hippocampal replay. The learning-dependent enhancement is consistent with the claim that replay allocation is proportional to unexplained prediction error -- as the association becomes familiar, prediction error decreases, and the enhancement attenuates.

However, the study measures reward salience, not prediction error directly. Reward and surprise are confounded during learning. The study also does not address whether the enhanced reactivation is generative (producing variations) or rote (re-presenting the experienced trajectory). MECH-205 claims both surprise-gating AND generative variation; Singer & Frank provide evidence only for the former, and only in the reward domain.

## Limitations

Rodent spatial navigation; reward is the only salience signal tested. MECH-205 claims surprise-gating across all prediction error domains (E1 sensory, E2 motor-sensory, E3 harm/goal). The study cannot speak to whether non-reward prediction errors (e.g., surprising sensory events without reward) produce the same reactivation enhancement. The reactivation is measured during awake post-reward SWRs, not during sleep -- the offline consolidation context MECH-205 primarily targets.

## Confidence reasoning

Strong experimental design isolating reward effects; high-quality electrophysiology. Mapping fidelity moderate because the study addresses reward salience rather than prediction error directly, and does not test the generative aspect. Overall confidence 0.72.
