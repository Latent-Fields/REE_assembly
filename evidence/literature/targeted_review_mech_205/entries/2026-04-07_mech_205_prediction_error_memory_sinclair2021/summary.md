# Prediction errors disrupt hippocampal representations and update episodic memories

**Sinclair, Manalili, Brunec, Adcock & Barense, 2021, PNAS**

## What the paper does

Sinclair et al. used fMRI to measure hippocampal activity while participants watched familiar narrative videos. Some videos were interrupted before their expected endings -- creating "mnemonic prediction errors." The study then tested memory 24 hours later to see whether prediction errors triggered memory updating.

## Key findings relevant to MECH-205

Hippocampal representations that normally stabilise during narrative viewing were immediately destabilised by unexpected interruptions. The degree of destabilisation predicted memory updating: after surprising interruptions, stronger hippocampal activation predicted *more* false memories (integration of new information into the old memory trace). After expected endings, the opposite: stronger activation predicted *fewer* false memories (memory preservation). The updating effect only appeared after a 24-hour delay, consistent with sleep-dependent reconsolidation.

The cholinergic dimension is notable: the prediction-error-driven destabilisation required concurrent basal forebrain activation. This suggests neuromodulatory gating of when memories become labile -- one cannot simply "surprise" the hippocampus into updating; the cholinergic system must be engaged.

## Mapping to REE

The study supports the broader principle underlying MECH-205: prediction errors flag episodes for hippocampal model-updating. The destabilisation of hippocampal representations by surprise is consistent with episodes being cached in the surprise buffer. The 24-hour delay before updating manifests is consistent with the updating occurring during offline sleep phases rather than immediately online. The cholinergic gating suggests that the surprise buffer may have a neuromodulatory write-gate -- not all prediction errors are cached, only those occurring during cholinergically-permissive states.

However, the study measures reconsolidation of existing episodic memories, not replay scheduling or the generation of counterfactual variations. MECH-205's core claims -- that surprise determines replay *frequency* and that replay is *generative* -- are not directly tested here. What Sinclair et al. show is that surprise destabilises memories, making them available for updating. This is a necessary precondition for MECH-205's mechanism but not a demonstration of it.

## Limitations

Human fMRI cannot resolve SWR-level replay events. The paradigm uses narrative video (episodic memory updating), which is a different domain from the spatial navigation tasks where replay is typically studied. The 24-hour delay confounds sleep with simple time passage (no sleep deprivation control reported).

## Confidence reasoning

PNAS, well-designed prediction error manipulation in humans. Mapping fidelity moderate -- the study addresses memory destabilisation by surprise, which is a precondition for MECH-205 but not a direct test. Overall confidence 0.68.
