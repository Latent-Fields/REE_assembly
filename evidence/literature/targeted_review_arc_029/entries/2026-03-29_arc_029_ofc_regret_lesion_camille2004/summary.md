# The Involvement of the Orbitofrontal Cortex in the Experience of Regret

**Camille et al. (2004) -- Science**

## What the paper did

Camille and colleagues tested patients with orbitofrontal cortex (OFC) lesions and neurologically healthy controls on a gambling paradigm designed to induce regret. After each committed choice (a gamble was selected and played out), subjects were shown the outcome of both the chosen and unchosen options. In normal subjects, seeing that the unchosen option would have paid more generated regret -- a negative emotional response proportional to the gap -- and over time subjects modified their choices to avoid situations where they expected large counterfactual gaps. Crucially, OFC lesion patients failed on both counts: they did not report regret after committed choices, and they did not learn to anticipate and avoid regret in future choices. On the same task, when no counterfactual information was provided (so regret was not possible), OFC lesion patients performed comparably to controls. The deficit was specific to committed choices with visible counterfactual consequences.

## Key findings relevant to ARC-029

This lesion study provides a causal double-dissociation argument for the claim that committed and uncommitted modes are computationally distinct. When the neural system dedicated to evaluating committed outcomes against counterfactuals is disrupted (by OFC lesion), agents make measurably worse committed choices over time -- they fail to accumulate the regret signal that would normally update their decision strategy. Normal agents, by contrast, progressively improve their committed-choice performance by learning from post-commitment counterfactual evaluation. This is a behavioral outcome difference between agents with intact vs. disrupted committed-mode processing, and it arises specifically from committed choices (not from non-counterfactual tasks). ARC-029 claims that committed and uncommitted operating modes produce measurably distinct harm outcomes. The Camille paper establishes the causal foundation: post-commitment evaluation is a separable computational step (implemented by OFC) whose presence or absence changes decision outcomes in measurable ways.

The paper also establishes the directionality of the effect: agents with disrupted post-commitment evaluation accumulate worse outcomes from committed choices. This maps onto ARC-029's finding (EXQ-063) that gate-active (committed-mode-aware) agents outperform ablated agents on harm accumulation. The OFC circuit does for biological agents what the commit gate does for REE agents: it ensures that post-commitment consequences are routed through a different evaluative channel that appropriately weights the irreversibility of the choice.

## Mapping to REE

ARC-029 is implemented in REE as a commit gate that changes error routing when an agent crosses the commit boundary. The commit boundary corresponds, in Camille's paradigm, to the moment at which the gamble is resolved and the outcome is real. Before this moment, the agent is deliberating (pre-commit, simulated evaluation). After this moment, a real outcome has been produced and counterfactual evaluation becomes possible. Camille's study shows that the neural system mediating post-commitment evaluation (OFC) is causally required for adaptive behavior following committed choices. When it is absent, agents do not update their policies appropriately based on committed-choice harm -- exactly the failure mode that ARC-029 argues will occur if the commit boundary is ablated in REE.

## Limitations and caveats

The study measures subjective regret (felt emotional state, verbal report) and choice modification in a gambling paradigm, not objective harm accumulation in a control loop. The harm in Camille's study is financial loss from a foregone opportunity -- a very specific form of harm that is irreversible in an informational sense but does not map cleanly onto the physical or social harm that motivates REE's commit boundary. The OFC lesion is a blunt instrument affecting a broad region with multiple functions; the specific computational component disrupted may not be exclusively the regret circuit. Small clinical N is a standard limitation of lesion studies. The mapping from OFC lesion effects to REE's commit gate ablation requires the assumption that the commit gate implements the same computational function as the OFC regret circuit -- an assumption that is motivated but not proven.

## Confidence reasoning

A Science paper with a clean lesion design that establishes causal necessity of OFC for post-commitment evaluation. This is stronger evidence than correlation: the lesion establishes that the post-commitment evaluation circuit is necessary for adaptive committed choice. Confidence is set at 0.64 -- somewhat lower than the Coricelli fMRI paper for ARC-029 because the lesion study's clinical N is small, the financial gambling paradigm is a more distant analogue to REE's harm concept, and the subjective-to-objective mapping gap is present throughout. Together, Camille (2004) and Coricelli (2005) form a complementary pair: causal necessity (lesion) and neural mechanism (fMRI), both supporting the claim that committed-mode evaluation is a distinct computational step with measurable behavioral consequences.
