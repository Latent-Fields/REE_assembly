# Regret and its avoidance: a neuroimaging study of choice behavior

**Coricelli et al. (2005) -- Nature Neuroscience**

## What the paper did

Coricelli and colleagues used fMRI to study the neural basis of regret in human decision-making. Subjects chose between two financial gambles, and after choosing, they were shown the outcome of both the chosen and unchosen gambles. When the unchosen gamble would have paid more, subjects experienced regret -- a subjective state proportional to the counterfactual gap. The key neuroimaging finding was that medial orbitofrontal cortex (OFC), anterior cingulate cortex, and hippocampus activated in proportion to the size of this counterfactual gap: the worse the unchosen alternative relative to the chosen one, the stronger the OFC/ACC signal. Critically, the same circuit was recruited prospectively during subsequent decision-making: subjects became progressively more regret-averse over the experiment, and OFC/amygdala activity during pre-choice deliberation predicted how strongly subjects would weight counterfactual risk. The study established that the brain has a dedicated circuit for evaluating committed-choice outcomes against counterfactual alternatives, and that this circuit modifies future committed choices.

## Key findings relevant to ARC-029

The paper establishes two things relevant to ARC-029. First, committed choices (where the agent has acted and outcomes are realised) recruit a distinct neural circuit -- the OFC/amygdala regret system -- that is not recruited during pre-commitment deliberation. This is the neural correlate of what ARC-029 calls distinct operating modes: pre-commit (simulation, deliberation) and post-commit (realised outcomes, counterfactual evaluation). The committed and uncommitted modes are distinguished by whether counterfactual evaluation of realised harm is possible, and Coricelli's paper shows that the brain treats these as fundamentally different computational situations. Second, the regret circuit is not a post-hoc emotional reaction but an active error-routing mechanism that modifies subsequent decisions. The OFC activation during anticipation of a new choice tracks the agent's accumulated regret history, implementing something like a running harm-accumulation signal. This is structurally similar to the post-commit error channel in REE, which accumulates realised harm differently from pre-commit simulated harm.

## Mapping to REE

ARC-029 claims that committed and uncommitted operating modes produce measurably distinct harm outcomes. The Coricelli paper provides the neural evidence that the brain makes exactly this distinction: committed outcomes activate a dedicated evaluative circuit (OFC/ACC/amygdala) that computes the gap between what was obtained and what could have been obtained. In REE, the commit boundary corresponds to the moment at which a simulated action becomes real -- after which errors are realised-outcome errors rather than simulated errors. The Coricelli paper shows that biological agents have a specific mechanism for processing post-commitment errors differently from pre-commitment deliberation, and that this difference produces measurably distinct behavioral consequences (increasing regret aversion, choice modification). The REE claim that committed vs. uncommitted modes produce distinct harm outcomes is supported by this broader evidence that mode distinction at the commit boundary has functionally significant consequences.

## Limitations and caveats

The mapping requires holding several things in mind simultaneously. Coricelli measures subjective regret as a felt state with a neural signature; ARC-029 claims objective harm accumulation differences. These are different levels of description. The gambling paradigm uses financial stakes where all outcomes are known -- a highly controlled setting very different from the open-ended environments where REE would operate. The OFC regret signal in Coricelli is specifically about the counterfactual gap (what was missed), not about accumulated harm to others or to the environment; REE's harm concept is broader and includes external harm, not just self-harm from a foregone opportunity. The transfer from human emotion neuroscience to REE's computational harm evaluation requires multiple conceptual steps that are not directly bridged by this paper.

## Confidence reasoning

Nature Neuroscience paper from a leading group (Dolan lab, UCL Wellcome), landmark in decision neuroscience. The paper is directly relevant to the question of whether committed and uncommitted modes are distinct, and it provides strong evidence that they are -- at the neural level and at the behavioral level. Confidence is set at 0.66 rather than higher primarily because of the subjective/objective gap: demonstrating that humans feel differently about committed vs. uncommitted outcomes is not the same as demonstrating that a computational agent accumulates harm differently. The paper supports the mode-distinction claim at the neural level but provides only indirect support for the harm-outcome claim that ARC-029 specifically makes.
