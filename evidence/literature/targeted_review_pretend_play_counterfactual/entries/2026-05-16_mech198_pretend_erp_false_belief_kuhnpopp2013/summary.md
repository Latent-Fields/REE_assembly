# Kuhn-Popp, Sodian, Sommer, Dohnel, Meinhardt 2013: Same or Different? ERP Correlates of Pretense and False Belief

## What the paper did

Kuhn-Popp et al. (doi:10.1016/j.neuroscience.2013.06.017) used event-related potentials (ERPs) to compare the neural signatures of pretense reasoning and false belief reasoning in 28 children aged 6-8 years. Children were shown scenarios requiring them to reason about either (a) what a character is pretending, or (b) what a character falsely believes, alongside non-mental control conditions. The study aimed to test whether pretense and false belief -- both of which require separating real from non-real content -- share the same neurocognitive mechanism.

## Key findings

Pretense and false belief produced distinct ERP signatures in polarity, latency, and scalp topography. Pretense elicited an early parietal P2 component (attributed to incongruence detection) and a negative frontal slow wave at 290-600ms (attributed to intention processing). False belief elicited the characteristic late positive fronto-central slow wave at 290-920ms, associated with metarepresentational processing. The authors concluded that pretense reasoning in 6-8 year olds relies on simpler intention-processing rather than full metarepresentational machinery, and that pretense and false belief rely on distinct neural substrates.

## Translation to REE

This finding is mixed but ultimately consistent with REE's two-tag decomposition. If pretend play and false belief shared a single neurocognitive mechanism (Leslie's unitary PRETEND operator), ERP signatures should be identical. The observed dissociation suggests instead that the two tags REE proposes -- ARC-049 (bilateral play frame, intention-level) and MECH-094 (hypothesis tag, full metarepresentational decoupling) -- are neurally distinguishable. The early negative frontal slow wave for pretense (intention processing, ~simpler mentalistic reasoning) corresponds to what ARC-049 requires: recognizing that a partner's action is play-framed, which can be achieved via behavioral/intentional cues without full metarepresentation. The late positive slow wave for false belief corresponds to what MECH-094 requires: maintaining a full propositional metarepresentation of a state different from one's own.

This study therefore refines rather than contradicts MECH-198: the two tags are neurally separable in 6-8 year olds, which REE predicts should be exploitable -- ARC-049 (play frame recognition) can develop partially independently from the full MECH-094 metarepresentational capacity, with MECH-198 (full pretend play competence requiring both co-operating) representing the convergence of these two developmental lines.

## Limitations and caveats

The study measures reasoning *about* pretense, not naturalistic pretend play production. The 6-8 year age range is past the pretend play acquisition window (18 months to ~4 years for pretend play foundations); whether the same neural dissociation exists at the acquisition window is unknown. ERP components are temporally precise but spatially coarse, and the cognitive interpretation of the frontal negative slow wave as "intention processing" rather than "failed metarepresentation" is partly inferential. N=28 is relatively small for an ERP study.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is 0.68 for the claim mapping. The study provides genuine neural evidence for the dissociation between intention-level (ARC-049) and metarepresentational (MECH-094) processing during pretense. However, the mapping from ERP components to specific REE mechanisms is inferential, the age range is post-acquisition, and the task taps reasoning about pretense not pretend play production. The finding is best used as evidence for REE's two-tag decomposition rather than direct evidence for MECH-198's developmental training function.
