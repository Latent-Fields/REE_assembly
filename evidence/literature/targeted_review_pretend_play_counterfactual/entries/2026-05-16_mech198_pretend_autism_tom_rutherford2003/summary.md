# Rutherford & Rogers 2003: Cognitive Underpinnings of Pretend Play in Autism

## What the paper did

Rutherford and Rogers (doi:10.1023/a:1024406601334) compared spontaneous and prompted pretend play in three groups: 28 young children with autism, 24 children with other developmental disorders, and 26 typical children. They tested two competing accounts: a theory-of-mind (ToM) account (pretend play deficits in autism stem from metarepresentational impairment) versus an executive function (EF) account (pretend play deficits stem from EF deficits). Both pretend play scores and the cognitive measures (ToM tasks and EF measures including generativity) were obtained from all three groups.

## Key findings

Children with autism showed significantly delayed pretend play scores. They also had significant deficits in the ToM measure, but not the EF measures. Regression analyses showed that the ToM measure predicted pretend play performance; of the EF measures, only generativity (the ability to generate novel action sequences) showed a secondary significant role. This pattern was interpreted as supporting the ToM account: pretend play deficits in autism stem primarily from metarepresentational impairment (the inability to decouple from reality) rather than from executive function deficits.

An important secondary finding: even with ToM deficits, some children with autism produced simple object substitution pretense. This suggests that very basic pretend play (object substitution without full role enactment or frame maintenance) can occur without metarepresentational competence -- consistent with ARC-049 (behavioral/intentional frame opening) operating partially independently from MECH-094 (full hypothesis tag).

## Translation to REE

Autism maps onto selective impairment of the hypothesis tag system (MECH-094): the agent lacks the metarepresentational capacity to simultaneously maintain a primary reality-tracking representation and a decoupled synthetic representation marked as counterfactual. The pattern of results supports REE's two-tag decomposition: ARC-049 (behavioral frame-opening via social/intentional cues) is partially intact even with ToM deficits (hence simple object substitution is preserved), while MECH-094 (full hypothesis tag, metarepresentational decoupling) is selectively impaired (hence elaborated role play and mutual frame maintenance fail).

The generativity role -- generating novel action sequences within play -- is consistent with REE's requirement that E3 trajectory selection must function in synthetic mode during pretend play: an agent that cannot generate novel trajectories within the hypothesis-tagged frame cannot exercise the full pretend play circuit even if the frame-opening mechanism (ARC-049) is intact. This points to generativity (E3 trajectory generation in synthetic mode) as a third prerequisite for full MECH-198 pretend play, beyond the two tags.

The failure signature for MECH-200 and MECH-201 can be grounded here: autism-spectrum profiles with ToM impairment but intact EF show exactly the pattern REE predicts for MECH-094 impairment -- difficulty maintaining the synthetic/real boundary, manifesting as either literal interpretation of pretend scenarios (synthetic-reads-as-real: MECH-201) or failure to recognize pretend frames as distinct from real frames.

## Limitations and caveats

Autism is a complex multi-factor developmental condition; using it as the model for isolated hypothesis-tag impairment risks attributing multiple confounds to a single mechanism. The ToM-EF dissociation in autism is contested -- more recent studies using more sensitive EF measures (fine-grained inhibitory control paradigms) do find EF deficits in autism. Sample sizes are small for a three-group comparison (N=28 autism, 24 DD, 26 typical). The EF measures may not have been sensitive enough to detect relevant EF contributions.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is 0.74. The finding that ToM predicts pretend play deficits better than EF in autism provides directional evidence for the hypothesis tag (metarepresentational decoupling) as the primary prerequisite. The partial preservation of simple pretense with ToM deficits supports the two-tag decomposition. Confidence is limited by the clinical population confounds, small sample sizes, and the contested ToM-EF dissociation in more recent autism research.
