# Vasilopoulos et al. 2026: Longitudinal Evidence of Pretend Play and Mental Health in Early Years

## What the paper did

Vasilopoulos et al. (doi:10.1007/s10643-026-02150-7) used the Longitudinal Study of Australian Children (LSAC) birth cohort (N=1,426 children born in 2004) to examine whether pretend play ability at age 2-3 years predicts mental health outcomes at ages 4-5 and 6-7 years. Pretend play was assessed via standardized developmental tools at wave 1 (age 2-3). Internalising problems (anxiety, withdrawal, somatic complaints) and externalising problems (aggression, hyperactivity, conduct problems) were measured at waves 2 and 3. Potential confounders including socioeconomic status, maternal mental health, language ability, and parent-child attachment were statistically controlled. Emotional regulation was tested as a potential mediator.

## Key findings

Higher pretend play ability at age 2-3 predicted significantly fewer internalising problems at ages 4-5 and 6-7, and significantly fewer externalising problems at ages 4-5, after controlling for all confounders. The critical null finding: emotional regulation did NOT significantly mediate the relationship between pretend play and mental health outcomes. The authors propose embodied cognition mechanisms as a possible alternative explanation -- pretend play may engage motor brain regions involved in attention and anxiety regulation -- though this is speculative and untested.

## Translation to REE

This is population-level prospective evidence for REE's prediction that pretend play training (MECH-198) protects against commitment architecture failure modes (MECH-200, MECH-201, MECH-202). Internalising outcomes (anxiety, withdrawal) map onto MECH-201: agents whose hypothesis tag system is undertrained process simulated or ambiguous stimuli through the real-consequence pipeline, producing excess harm signal (anxiety is z_harm_s firing on synthetic/ambiguous input without hypothesis tag suppression). Externalising outcomes (aggression, impulsivity) map onto MECH-202 (commitment gate undertrained: commits without evaluation) and MECH-200 (real-reads-as-synthetic: real consequences discounted, disinhibiting action).

The non-mediation by emotional regulation is the finding REE would most want to emphasize. The simplest alternative to REE's frame-distinction account would be that pretend play teaches children to regulate their emotions, and this emotional regulation is what protects mental health. The empirical finding rules this out: emotional regulation is not the mechanism. The protective effect of pretend play must operate through some other pathway -- and the REE account (pretend play calibrates the real/synthetic frame distinction, preventing frame confusion failure modes in later life) is a candidate that the null mediation finding cannot rule out. REE predicts an experiment: measure frame-confusion tendency (tendency to process ambiguous situations as real when they are synthetic, or as synthetic when they are real) as a mediator; this should account for variance that emotional regulation does not.

## Limitations and caveats

The pretend play measure at age 2-3 is a composite parental/observational measure from the broader developmental assessment; the specific pretend play capacities that drive the effect (object substitution, bilateral role play, mutual frame maintenance) are not decomposed. The 2-3 year window is early pretend play emergence, before the full MECH-198 pretend+hypothesis_tag co-operation that requires more sophisticated play at 3-4 years. The outcome measures (internalising/externalising categories) are broad clinical constructs that do not map one-to-one onto specific REE failure modes. The null mediation by emotional regulation is an absence of evidence, not evidence of absence.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is 0.76. The large cohort, prospective design, and strong covariate control make this one of the most methodologically rigorous longitudinal studies of pretend play outcomes. The dual-direction finding (both internalising and externalising protected) is consistent with REE's two failure-mode prediction. Confidence is limited by the coarse pretend play measurement, broad outcome categories, and the inferential step from population-level mental health outcomes to specific REE architectural failure modes.
