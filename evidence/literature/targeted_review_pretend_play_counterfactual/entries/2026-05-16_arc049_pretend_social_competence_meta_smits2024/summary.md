# Smits-van der Nat, van der Wilt, Meeter, van der Veen 2024: The Value of Pretend Play for Social Competence -- A Meta-analysis

## What the paper did

Smits-van der Nat et al. (doi:10.1007/s10648-024-09884-z) conducted a systematic meta-analysis of 34 empirical studies examining the relationship between pretend play and social competence in early childhood (ages 3-8). The meta-analysis synthesized evidence across various social competence measures (prosocial behavior, conflict resolution, peer acceptance, theory of mind) and moderated the overall relationship by pretend play measurement type (quality vs. quantity), play social context (solitary vs. social), and child age.

## Key findings

The overall meta-analytic relationship between pretend play and social competence was positive across all social competence measures. Two critical moderating findings: (1) Studies measuring the *quality* of pretend play (sophistication of frame maintenance, role elaboration, partner responsiveness) found stronger correlations with social competence than studies measuring *quantity* (hours of play engagement). (2) The relationship weakened with age (stronger in younger cohorts), consistent with competence approaching asymptote during the pretend play acquisition window. A secondary finding from companion studies in the search: social pretend play (with a partner) showed stronger associations with inhibitory control than solitary pretend play.

## Translation to REE

The quality-quantity dissociation directly evidences ARC-049's functional role. What matters for social competence is not how much a child plays but whether they can successfully maintain a shared bilateral play frame -- whether they monitor the partner's frame state, repair frame violations (correcting a partner who breaks the pretend scenario), and negotiate shared frame content across episodes. This is precisely what ARC-049 requires: a bilateral frame tag that both agents must open, maintain, and close in co-ordination. Children who can do this well (high quality) develop the social capacities that require bilateral frame maintenance in adult life (negotiation, shared intentionality, perspective-taking during conflict). Children who play alone frequently but cannot maintain bilateral frames do not develop these capacities.

The age moderation is consistent with REE's developmental curriculum: ARC-049 bilateral frame competence should approach asymptote during the pretend play window (3-6 years). Cross-sectional correlations weaken once the competence is established in most children, because variance in social competence is then driven by other developmental factors rather than pretend play exposure.

The social vs. solitary finding is directly relevant to a key question the user specified: whether pretend play requires mutual frame maintenance or can be solitary. The meta-analytic answer appears to be: for the social-competence benefits specifically, social (bilateral) pretend play is what matters. Solitary pretend play may train the hypothesis tag (MECH-094) independently, but the ARC-049 bilateral frame maintenance capability requires a partner to exercise.

## Limitations and caveats

Most included studies in the meta-analysis are cross-sectional; causal direction is not established. The specific effect size (overall r) for the pretend play -- social competence relationship is not reported in available excerpts; exact quantitative mapping to REE claims is therefore limited. 'Social competence' covers multiple constructs that do not map one-to-one onto ARC-049. The moderating effect of social vs. solitary play was reported from companion studies rather than directly from all 34 meta-analytic studies.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is 0.73. The meta-analytic design with 34 studies gives robustness for the directional claim (pretend play quality predicts social competence). The quality-quantity and social-vs-solitary moderators are directly relevant to ARC-049. Confidence is limited by the absence of the specific effect size in available text, the cross-sectional limitation of most included studies, and the breadth of the 'social competence' construct.
