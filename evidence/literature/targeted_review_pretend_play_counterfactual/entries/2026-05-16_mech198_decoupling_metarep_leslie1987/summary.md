# Leslie 1987: Pretense and Representation -- The Origins of Theory of Mind

## What the paper did

Alan Leslie's 1987 theoretical paper in Psychological Review (doi:10.1037/0033-295X.94.4.412) proposed a solution to what he called the problem of "representational abuse" in pretend play: how can a child entertain a counterfactual representation (this banana is a telephone) without contaminating their primary representation of the world? His answer was a cognitive mechanism called decoupling -- the creation of a metarepresentational copy of the primary expression, quarantined from the world by suspension of its reference, truth, and existence relations. The PRETEND operator takes a primary representation, copies it into a metarepresentational context, and renders it semantically opaque -- the child can manipulate the copy freely without the manipulation affecting their factual model of the world.

## Key findings relevant to the claim

Pretend play emerges reliably around 18 months in typically developing children -- at the same time as other early theory-of-mind precursors such as pointing, gaze-following, and reading intentional actions. Leslie argued this was not coincidental: the decoupling mechanism that enables pretend play is the same mechanism that enables understanding of other minds. The infant who can represent "banana [PRETEND] telephone" is exercising the same capacity as the child who later represents "I think X, but John thinks Y." What makes this theoretically important is that decoupling is not a minor add-on but a radical architectural extension: it transforms the representational system from one that tracks reality into one that can simultaneously track reality and model alternative possibilities.

## Translation to REE

The decoupling mechanism is structurally equivalent to REE's hypothesis tag (MECH-094). The hypothesis tag is a categorical write gate that marks a trajectory or representation as simulation, suppressing its normal downstream consequences -- residue writes, post-commit learning, reality updates. Leslie's PRETEND operator does exactly the same thing at the cognitive level: it creates a semantically quarantined representation that the agent can act on without treating the consequences as real. MECH-198 identifies pretend play as the first developmental point where the hypothesis tag (MECH-094, agent-internal decoupling) co-operates with the play frame tag (ARC-049, bilateral frame authorization). Leslie's framework provides the foundational argument for why the hypothesis tag is not a minor optimization but an architectural prerequisite: without decoupling, every simulation contaminates the world model.

The implications for REE's developmental curriculum are direct: pretend play at 18+ months is not optional enrichment but the training ground for the hypothesis tag mechanism. Agents who skip this training phase lack the cognitive architecture that prevents simulation from overwriting real experience.

## Limitations and caveats

Leslie's framework is theoretical and predates modern neuroimaging and detailed EF research. His claim that the PRETEND operator is a unitary mechanism is challenged by subsequent work (Jarrold et al. 2010; Kuhn-Popp et al. 2013) showing that simple pretense recognition can occur without full metarepresentation, and that pretense and false belief have distinct neural correlates in children. This is actually consistent with REE's two-tag decomposition -- ARC-049 (bilateral play frame, intention-level tagging) may operate independently from MECH-094 (full hypothesis tag, metarepresentational decoupling) -- but it means Leslie's unitary PRETEND operator is an oversimplification relative to REE's architecture.
