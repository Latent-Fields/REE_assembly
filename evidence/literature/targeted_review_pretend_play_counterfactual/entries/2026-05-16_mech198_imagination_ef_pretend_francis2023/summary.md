# Francis & Gibson 2023: A Plausible Role of Imagination in Pretend Play, Counterfactual Reasoning, and Executive Functions

## What the paper did

Francis and Gibson (doi:10.1111/bjop.12650) used structural equation modelling with 189 children (mean age 4.8 years) to empirically test the Weisberg-Gopnik hypothesis that pretend play (PP) and counterfactual reasoning (CFR) share a common imaginative representational capacity. Measures included standardized PP assessments (object substitution, referring to absent objects, verbal property attribution, sequenced imaginary actions), CFR tasks (counterfactual statements generation), and comprehensive executive function batteries (inhibition, working memory, cognitive flexibility).

## Key findings

Confirmatory factor analysis showed that PP and CFR measures loaded onto distinct but significantly correlated latent constructs (r=.51, p=.001). Complex executive function (the combination of inhibition + working memory + cognitive flexibility) accounted for unique variance in both PP (beta=.21, p=.02) and CFR (beta=.22, p=.01) when controlling for age and receptive language. A structural equation model supporting a second-order latent factor representing general imaginative capacity showed satisfactory fit (CFI=.94, TLI=.91, RMSEA=.08). Working memory alone showed no significant associations with either measure.

## Translation to REE

The shared 'imaginative capacity' latent is REE's hypothesis tag system (MECH-094) at the cognitive level: the agent-level capacity to generate and maintain counterfactual/synthetic representations decoupled from the real-consequence stream. The SEM finding directly supports MECH-198: pretend play and counterfactual reasoning are not the same thing (distinct latent constructs) but they both exercise and depend on the same underlying decoupling/imagination mechanism.

The EF finding is also significant for REE: complex EF (not single-component EF) predicts both capacities. This is consistent with what REE requires from the hypothesis tag system: simultaneously maintaining two representations (one real, one synthetic) in working memory while inhibiting the real-consequence pathway for the synthetic representation and remaining flexible enough to switch between them. No single EF component is sufficient; the joint system is what matters. Working memory alone being non-significant rules out a simple "capacity to hold more information" account.

The behavioral indicators of PP in this study -- object substitution, referring to absent objects, verbal property attribution, sequenced imaginary actions -- map directly onto what REE's E3 trajectory selection must do in play mode: substitute a synthetic action-object for a real one, project its consequences, attribute properties to synthetic states, and sequence those into a temporally coherent trajectory, all while keeping the hypothesis tag active.

## Limitations and caveats

The 'imagination' latent is inferred from SEM model fit rather than independently measured. The model shows good but not excellent fit (RMSEA=.08 is at the conventional acceptability threshold). Cross-sectional design prevents causal inference about developmental order. The PP measures are standardized tasks rather than naturalistic observations, potentially underestimating the richness of real pretend play.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is 0.79. This is the most methodologically rigorous direct test of the imagination-as-shared-substrate hypothesis. The SEM methodology and adequate sample size give confidence in the structural finding. Confidence not higher because the imagination latent is model-inferred and the EF decomposition is composite (complex EF) rather than component-specific.
