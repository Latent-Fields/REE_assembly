# Craig (2003) -- A new view of pain as a homeostatic emotion

**Source:** Trends Neurosci. 2003 Jun;26(6):303-7. DOI: 10.1016/s0166-2236(03)00123-1. PMID: 12798599.

## What the study did

Craig's 2003 paper in Trends in Neurosciences synthesises anatomical, electrophysiological, and neuroimaging evidence to argue that pain -- particularly slow, burning, C-fiber mediated pain -- should be reclassified as a homeostatic emotion rather than as a somatosensory percept. The paper builds directly on Craig's 2002 Nature Reviews Neuroscience interoception framework (PMID 12154366) and extends it to make the homeostatic motivation claim explicit. The central argument is that the lamina I / C-fiber / VMpo thalamus / posterior insula / ACC pathway is architecturally homologous to the pathways for temperature, itch, hunger, and thirst -- all are C-fiber-mediated homeostatic signals that motivate corrective action rather than merely reporting where and how intense a stimulus is.

## Key findings

The paper identifies two anatomically and functionally distinct ascending nociceptive pathways:

1. The neospinothalamic pathway: A-delta fibers -> lateral spinothalamic tract -> VPL thalamus -> S1/S2. Fast, topographically precise, encodes stimulus location and intensity (the sensory-discriminative dimension). This pathway has a counterpart in the tactile system and is phylogenetically older in its cortical projection target.

2. The paleospinothalamic/homeostatic pathway: lamina I neurons (predominantly monosynaptic C-fiber input) -> lateral spinothalamic tract (but targeting VMpo/MDvc thalamus) -> posterior insula -> ACC. Slow, diffusely organized, encodes physiological condition of tissues (the affective-motivational dimension). This is phylogenetically newer in primates and unique in projecting to anterior insula and ACC -- the substrate for felt body-state and motivational urgency respectively.

Craig's key claim for SD-011: the C-fiber/homeostatic pathway signals persistent deviation from homeostasis. It accumulates input from the sustained nociceptive barrage (C-fiber traffic from inflamed, damaged, or persistently noxious tissue), integrates this over time in posterior insula, and generates a motivational urgency signal in ACC. It is NOT suited for precise stimulus localization or forward prediction on short timescales.

## REE translation

This is the most direct published statement of the biological grounding for z_harm_a in SD-011. The C-fiber lamina I pathway is the biological substrate of z_harm_a: it carries accumulated homeostatic deviation as a motivational signal. Craig's framing also explains why z_harm_a cannot be forward-modeled on the same timescale as z_harm_s: it is a body-state signal, not a stimulus-event signal. The A-delta pathway, by contrast, generates fast localizable signals that can be anticipated and predicted (ARC-033).

Craig's model also raises a scope question for REE: he groups pain under the same homeostatic family as temperature, itch, hunger, and thirst. This suggests z_harm_a and the SD-012 homeostatic drive may share architectural substrate -- REE should specify whether these are distinct latents or the same latent with different semantic labels.

## Limitations and caveats

This is a framework review, not a primary experiment. The homeostatic emotion framing is influential but remains contested: some researchers argue the hedonic and motivational properties of pain are better explained by interactions between somatosensory and limbic systems rather than by a distinct labeled-line pathway. The VMpo thalamic nucleus (Craig's proposed relay) has been disputed as a target of lamina I neurons in humans by some anatomists. Transfer risk to REE: Craig's framework concerns somatic nociception and body-state monitoring, not harm-in-world as an ethical-relational signal, which is what REE instantiates.
