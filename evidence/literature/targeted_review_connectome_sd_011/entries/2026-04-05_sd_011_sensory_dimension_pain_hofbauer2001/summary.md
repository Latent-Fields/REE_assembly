# Hofbauer, Rainville, Duncan, Bushnell (2001) -- Cortical representation of the sensory dimension of pain

**Source:** J Neurophysiol. 2001 Jul;86(1):402-11. DOI: 10.1152/jn.2001.86.1.402. PMID: 11431520.

## What the study did

This PET study is the intentional companion to Rainville et al. (1997, Science). Where Rainville 1997 selectively modulated pain unpleasantness (affective dimension) using hypnotic suggestion and observed ACC changes without S1 changes, Hofbauer et al. 2001 selectively modulated pain intensity (sensory-discriminative dimension) using hypnotic suggestion and asked which brain regions tracked intensity but not unpleasantness.

Participants experienced painful thermal stimulation (hot water bath at constant temperature) while under hypnosis. Hypnotic suggestions were used to increase or decrease the perceived intensity of the sensation without changing its emotional quality. PET blood flow was measured before and after the suggestion manipulation.

## Key findings

Hypnotic modulation of perceived pain intensity produced significant changes in pain-evoked blood flow in primary somatosensory cortex (S1), specifically in the contralateral hemisphere. In contrast, ACC activation was not significantly changed by modulation of intensity alone (unlike in Rainville 1997, where it was modulated by unpleasantness).

This constitutes a bidirectional double dissociation:
- Unpleasantness up/down (Rainville 1997) -> ACC changes, S1 unchanged
- Intensity up/down (Hofbauer 2001) -> S1 changes, ACC unchanged

The two studies together provide the clearest experimental evidence available that S1 and ACC encode separable dimensions of the pain experience: S1 is the cortical substrate for the sensory-discriminative dimension, ACC for the affective-motivational dimension.

## REE translation

For SD-011, this double dissociation is critical. z_harm_s (the sensory-discriminative stream) finds its cortical expression in S1: it encodes intensity, location, and quality of the nociceptive input -- the "where and how strong" information that is fast, localizable, and forward-predictable (ARC-033). z_harm_a (the affective-motivational stream) finds its cortical expression in ACC: it encodes unpleasantness and behavioral urgency -- the "what to do about it" information that reflects accumulated homeostatic deviation.

The bidirectional dissociation confirms that the two REE streams are not simply two labels for the same neural process but are genuinely separable computations. The brain can vary one while holding the other constant, which is exactly what the SD-011 architectural separation requires.

## Limitations and caveats

The dissociation is at the cortical level (S1 vs ACC). Below the cortex, the streams converge in spinal cord, parabrachial nucleus, and thalamus. The REE architectural separation thus maps onto cortical-level processing, not peripheral.

The hypnotic modulation paradigm has been questioned on the grounds that hypnotic suggestions may produce demand characteristics -- participants may report what the suggestion implies rather than genuine perceptual change. However, the PET data show objective neural changes, not merely self-report differences, which partially addresses this concern.

S1 responses to pain are complex: there is evidence that S1 also responds to pain location (somatotopic), to attention, and to expectation, not solely to intensity. The S1 activation observed here is consistent with the sensory-discriminative claim but does not exclude other interpretations.
