# Rainville et al. (1997) — Pain affect encoded in human anterior cingulate but not somatosensory cortex

**Source:** Science 277(5328):968-71. DOI: 10.1126/science.277.5328.968. PMID: 9252330.

## What the study did

Rainville and colleagues exploited the dissociability of pain's two primary dimensions — sensory intensity and affective unpleasantness — by using targeted hypnotic suggestion in healthy adult volunteers. Hypnotic instruction was designed to selectively increase or decrease the unpleasantness of a standardized noxious heat stimulus (immersion in hot water) without altering its perceived intensity, or alternatively to alter perceived intensity while leaving unpleasantness unchanged. PET imaging captured regional cerebral blood flow changes during these conditions. This is not a blunt pharmacological manipulation: hypnosis, when carefully calibrated, provides a clean dissociation between the two pain dimensions in ways that pharmacological approaches rarely achieve. The subjects confirmed via verbal rating scales that the selective modulations were subjectively real.

## Key findings

The central result is clean and dramatic: when unpleasantness was selectively modulated by hypnotic suggestion, activity in anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) tracked the change in unpleasantness but activity in primary somatosensory cortex (S1) did not change. Conversely, when intensity was modulated, S1 activity changed but ACC did not alter in parallel with the unpleasantness manipulation. The functional dissociation was robust across subjects and converged with the clinical lesion literature, in which cingulotomy reliably reduces the distressing quality of chronic pain ("it still hurts but it doesn't bother me") while leaving the ability to locate and quantify the sensation intact. The paper explicitly frames this as direct experimental support for the Melzack-Casey (1968) sensory-discriminative versus motivational-affective model, grounding a previously theoretical construct in functional neuroimaging evidence.

## REE translation

This paper provides the strongest available human neuroimaging evidence for the functional premise of SD-011. The ACC corresponds functionally to what REE encodes as z_harm_a: it processes harm in terms of motivational urgency and unpleasantness — the dimension that drives prioritisation, avoidance scheduling, and what REE calls the affective-motivational stream. The S1/S2 complex corresponds to z_harm_s: it encodes the sensory-discriminative content of harm — where it is, what it is, how intense — without directly encoding motivational weight. SD-011 proposes that z_harm_s and z_harm_a are computed by parallel streams within the architecture, and this study demonstrates that the two dimensions are neurally dissociable at the cortical level in humans under real experimental conditions.

## Limitations and caveats

The most important caveat is anatomical: this study demonstrates cortical-level dissociation but does not directly resolve where the streams diverge anatomically. SD-011 posits divergence at the level of the spinothalamic tract (lateral STT to VPL/S1/S2 for z_harm_s; medial STT to intralaminar nuclei/ACC/anterior insula for z_harm_a). The PET data are consistent with either a single ascending stream that projects to both areas with differential weighting, or two genuinely separate ascending streams from the spinal cord. The cortical dissociation does not compel the subcortical stream architecture. A second limitation: hypnosis is an unusual cognitive state, and it is possible that suggestion modulates downstream cortical processing rather than revealing a pre-existing structural separation. Practically, this is a strong concern — selective modulation of one dimension might reflect differential attentional gating rather than truly independent streams. Thirdly, the study uses acute experimental pain in healthy adults; generalisation to chronic harm states and non-somatic harm signals (the wider scope of REE's z_harm) requires an inferential step.

## Confidence reasoning

Despite these caveats, this paper deserves the highest confidence of the five entries. The experimental design is elegant, the effect is robust, the source venue is Science, and the direct connection to the Melzack-Casey distinction (which SD-011 explicitly cites) is explicit in the paper's framing. The caveat about upstream anatomy does not diminish the functional evidence — it only limits the mechanistic interpretation. Confidence: 0.92.
