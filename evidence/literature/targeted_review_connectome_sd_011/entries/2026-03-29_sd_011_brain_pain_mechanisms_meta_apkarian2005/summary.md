# Apkarian et al. (2005) — Human brain mechanisms of pain perception and regulation in health and disease

**Source:** European Journal of Pain 9(4):463-84. DOI: 10.1016/j.ejpain.2004.11.001. PMID: 15979027.

## What the study did

Apkarian, Bushnell, Treede, and Zubieta conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis of human brain imaging studies of pain from 1988 to 2003, spanning PET, fMRI, EEG, and MEG methodologies. Sixty-eight hemodynamic studies of experimental pain in normal subjects and 30 in clinical pain conditions were selected through consensus across all four authors based on methodological quality. An additional 24 studies examining neurochemistry of pain were included. The review explicitly sought to characterise the brain network underlying pain perception, identify its somatotopic organisation, describe its psychological modulation, and distinguish acute from chronic pain mechanisms. The authorship — combining Apkarian's expertise in chronic pain neuroplasticity, Bushnell's in cortical pain processing, Treede's in spinal/thalamic mechanisms, and Zubieta's in neurochemistry — gives this review unusually broad mechanistic coverage.

## Key findings

The review identifies the core pain matrix as comprising primary somatosensory cortex (S1), secondary somatosensory cortex (S2), insular cortex (IC), anterior cingulate cortex (ACC), and prefrontal cortex (PFC). Somatotopic organisation, established in 10 studies, confirms that S1 and S2 encode sensory-discriminative information: stimulus location and intensity are mapped spatially and quantitatively. Psychological modulation, reviewed across 20 studies, consistently implicates ACC, anterior insula, and PFC in the affective-motivational processing of pain — the component that varies with attention, expectation, emotional context, and catastrophising.

A particularly significant finding is the dissociation between acute and chronic pain: chronic pain conditions engage brain regions critical for cognitive and emotional assessment (PFC, ACC, hippocampus) in ways that acute pain does not, suggesting that persistent harm signals recruit a progressively more affective/evaluative network. The authors characterise this as implying that the affective component of pain may be a distinctive feature differentiating chronic from acute experience. They also document the role of descending modulation (opiate and catecholamine systems) in modulating pain experience, confirming that the affective dimension is actively regulated rather than passively computed.

## REE translation

This meta-analysis provides the broadest empirical base for the pain matrix and its functional organisation. For SD-011, the key contribution is population-level validation: across 68 independent studies, the sensory-discriminative function of S1/S2/posterior insula and the affective-motivational function of ACC/anterior insula/PFC hold up consistently enough to survive meta-analysis. This is evidence that the two-stream claim is not an artefact of a single study's experimental design — it is a reproducible feature of the human pain system.

The chronic pain finding is architecturally provocative for REE. If sustained exposure to harm signals progressively shifts the dominant representation from z_harm_s (sensory-discriminative) to z_harm_a (affective-motivational), this is a failure mode that a well-designed REE system should model and potentially guard against. Chronic harm signal dominance by z_harm_a — without adequate z_harm_s grounding — would produce maladaptive avoidance patterns unmoored from actual harm location and intensity. This is the architectural analogue of catastrophising or harm-generalisation, which REE explicitly wants to avoid through accurate causal attribution.

## Limitations and caveats

The most important caveat is the salience network critique. Iannetti and Mouraux (published after this review, 2010 in PNAS) argued that the 'pain matrix' is better understood as a general salience and prediction-error detection network, activated by any attended salient stimulus, not by nociception specifically. If the ACC and anterior insula activations reflect salience processing rather than pain-specific affective encoding, the clean z_harm_a mapping would be weakened — these regions would be detecting any salient event, not just harm-specific motivational urgency. Apkarian et al.'s 2005 review predates this challenge and does not engage with it.

A second limitation is the methodological heterogeneity of the meta-analysed studies: different pain modalities (heat, cold, mechanical, visceral), durations, intensities, and subject populations. The consensus-based selection procedure reduces some of this noise but cannot eliminate it. Pooled activation coordinates from heterogeneous paradigms may obscure important functional distinctions within the pain matrix.

## Confidence reasoning

The Apkarian et al. meta-analysis is a highly authoritative population-level account. Its value is primarily in confirming the generalisability and reproducibility of the two-stream network organisation — it is not the right paper to read for mechanistic dissociation, but it is essential for demonstrating that the S1/S2 vs. ACC/insula functional partition is not an experimental artefact. The salience network caveat is a genuine concern that modestly reduces confidence relative to the Rainville and Craig entries. Confidence: 0.82.
