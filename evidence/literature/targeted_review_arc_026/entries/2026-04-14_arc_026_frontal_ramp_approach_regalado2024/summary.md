# Neural activity ramps in frontal cortex signal extended motivation during learning
**Regalado et al., 2024 | eLife**
**DOI:** 10.7554/eLife.93983.3

## What the paper did

Regalado and colleagues used fiber photometry, single-cell 2-photon imaging, and chemogenetic inhibition to characterize how the orbitofrontal cortex (OFC) and anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) jointly sustain approach motivation across unrewarded intervals in mice. The paradigm required animals to initiate trials and maintain approach behavior through a sequence of cues, only some of which predicted reward. The key discovery was that OFC-to-ACC projection neurons display ramping neural activity: signal rises monotonically across unrewarded cues and peaks when a reward-predictive cue is reached. Inhibiting these projections disrupted extended approach behavior -- animals were less willing to persist toward distant reward.

## Key findings and what they imply

The ramp is a graded, temporally extended approach signal. It does not merely fire at reward contact; it encodes the progressive narrowing of distance to benefit. This is the neural implementation of what ARC-026 calls benefit-gradient sensitivity: a circuit that converts spatial or temporal proximity to benefit into an analog motivational drive. Crucially, the ramp persists even in the absence of intermediate reinforcement -- the system is sustaining approach motivation from prediction, not from moment-to-moment reward. This is the hallmark of long-horizon planning: behavior organized by a distant attractor rather than local reward.

## REE mapping to ARC-026

ARC-026 argues that love -- operationalized as sustained approach investment toward a distant beneficiary -- depends on a computational capability that is harder to learn than binary harm avoidance. Harm avoidance needs only a contact detector (harm encountered -> stop); approach toward a distant benefit requires a gradient detector (benefit state at distance -> orient and move toward it through time). The Regalado et al. finding establishes that a dedicated circuit for exactly this function exists in the frontal cortex, that it is anatomically distinct from contact-range detection circuits, and that it is causally necessary for extended approach behavior. If this circuit scales with frontal hierarchy depth and capacity -- which frontal development literature suggests -- then more capable agents would have proportionally larger or more refined approach-gradient signal, consistent with ARC-026's prediction.

## Limitations and caveats

This is a mouse study with a seconds-to-minutes time horizon. The ramp encodes approach within a learned task structure; it remains unknown whether the same mechanism scales to novel benefit gradients (learning a new person's needs) versus merely enacting known ones. The paper does not compare approach-gradient circuits to harm-avoidance circuits in any capacity-scaling test, so the differential scaling prediction of ARC-026 is not directly tested. Extrapolating from a cue-reward ramp in ACC to love-as-long-horizon-investment involves multiple inferential steps. The mechanistic support is genuine; the ecological translation is speculative.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is 0.55. The paper provides strong mechanistic evidence for the existence and necessity of a graded approach-gradient circuit in frontal cortex -- directly naming the substrate ARC-026 predicts. But it does not test intelligence-scaling, is in mice, and the time horizons are categorically different. This is an important piece of mechanistic corroboration but is not confirmatory evidence for the full ARC-026 prediction. It belongs in the evidence record as substrate-level support for the approach-gradient computational claim.
