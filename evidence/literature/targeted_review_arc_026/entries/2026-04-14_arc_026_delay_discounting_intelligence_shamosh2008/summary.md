# Delay discounting and intelligence: A meta-analysis
**Shamosh & Gray, 2008 | Intelligence, 36(4), 289-305**
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.intell.2007.09.004

## What the paper did

Shamosh and Gray conducted a quantitative synthesis of 24 studies (26 effect sizes) examining whether cognitive ability predicts delay discounting -- the tendency to prefer smaller immediate rewards over larger delayed ones. Using a random-effects model, they found a weighted mean correlation of r = -0.23 between intelligence and discounting rate, meaning more intelligent individuals are less likely to sacrifice future value for immediate payoff. The direction was consistent across nearly all included studies. Moderator analyses showed the effect was weaker when probabilistic reward schemes were used, suggesting the association is strongest for pure temporal (rather than risk-laden) future valuation.

## Key findings and what they imply

The headline finding is deceptively simple: smarter people wait longer for better outcomes. What is less obvious is what this implies computationally. The ability to hold a future reward state in working memory long enough to allow it to compete with an immediate option is precisely the kind of operation that would make approach-gradient signals readable across distance. If I cannot represent a reward reliably at t+10, any gradient pointing toward it will be drowned out by local immediacy -- I will behave as if only contact-range signals exist. Lower discounting means the value function flattens out across time, which is structurally equivalent to expanding the effective radius within which approach gradients can guide behavior. The paper does not frame it this way, but the implication is direct: higher g extends the temporal reach of benefit detection.

## REE mapping to ARC-026

ARC-026 claims that love -- operationalized as benefit-gradient sensitivity -- expands under intelligence rather than being crowded out by it. Harm avoidance is a contact detection problem (binary signal, no gradient required); approach toward a distant beneficiary is a gradient problem that requires integrating weak, graded, temporally extended signals. Lower temporal discounting is the cognitive signature of exactly this capacity. The Shamosh & Gray finding thus provides indirect empirical support: the cognitive machinery that makes long-horizon benefit real to an agent grows with g. One does not need to posit a special "love faculty" -- the same capacity that makes intelligent agents better at intertemporal choice is the capacity that makes love-like long-horizon investment computationally tractable.

## Limitations and caveats

The effect size (r = -0.23) is real but modest, leaving substantial variance unexplained. A 2021 replication by Yeh, Myerson, and Green (n=1206, Human Connectome Project) found that after controlling for socioeconomic status, the intelligence-discounting correlation shrank to near-zero; discounting loaded on its own factor largely independent of cognitive measures. This is a genuine complication: it suggests that temporal preference may be more stable and trait-like than g-dependent, with SES confounding earlier findings. The REE translation also involves a step that the meta-analysis itself does not support: it measures explicit monetary choice, not the graded spatial or relational manifold over which ARC-026's approach gradient operates. The mapping is structurally plausible but not direct.

## Confidence reasoning

Source quality is high for a meta-analysis with no detected publication bias, but the 2021 large-sample replication introduces genuine uncertainty. The mapping is the weaker link: delay discounting is a reasonable but imperfect proxy for long-horizon benefit gradient sensitivity. Overall confidence: 0.68. This paper contributes directional support for ARC-026 but should be read alongside the Yeh et al. 2021 finding as a cautionary note about assuming the intelligence-discounting relationship is robust across SES.
