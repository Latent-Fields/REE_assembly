# Bratman (1987) -- Intention, Plans, and Practical Reason

## What the book did

Michael Bratman's 'Intention, Plans, and Practical Reason' argues that intentions are a distinct kind of mental state that cannot be reduced to combinations of beliefs and desires. An intention to do A is not simply a strong desire plus a belief that doing A is the best available means to some desired end. Intentions are practical attitudes with specific functional roles: they persist over time, resist reconsideration except when new defeating information arrives, structure deliberation by settling the question of what to do and redirecting planning energy toward how to do it, and coordinate action sequences hierarchically so that short-term actions serve longer-term plans. Bratman introduces the concept of the 'plan' as the broader structure within which intentions are embedded -- a partial specification of what the agent intends to do across an extended timeframe, which can be elaborated, revised, and hierarchically nested.

## Key findings relevant to ARC-026

Bratman's central insight for ARC-026 is that plans serve as the scaffolding on which increasingly complex action can be built. A settled intention provides a stable terminal constraint around which further planning can organise. As an agent's planning capacity deepens -- as it can consider more steps ahead, more alternative routes, and more complex contingencies -- the practical reach of its settled commitments expands. An agent with a terminal commitment to promoting someone's flourishing, but with only a shallow planning horizon, can identify only immediate means to that end. The same agent with deeper planning can identify long-horizon strategies, anticipate obstacles, and coordinate complex multi-step interventions that serve the same commitment more effectively. The commitment itself does not expand; its functional consequences in behaviour do. This is the structural argument for ARC-026: love, understood as a settled long-horizon care commitment, expands in its behavioural reach as intelligence increases, not because the commitment strengthens but because the agent's ability to pursue it improves.

## How this translates to REE (ARC-026)

ARC-026 claims that love as long-horizon care investment expands under intelligence rather than being crowded out. Bratman's planning theory provides the structural argument that complements Frankfurt's volitional account. Frankfurt tells us that love is a settled volitional disposition (not a competing preference). Bratman tells us that settled dispositions expand in functional reach as planning capacity increases (more means can be identified, more complex coordination can be achieved). Together, the two accounts ground ARC-026's 'expansion under intelligence' claim philosophically: love is not a fixed-bandwidth resource (Frankfurt), and its practical consequences grow with planning depth (Bratman). In REE terms: an E3 engine that has encoded a stable care commitment can pursue that commitment more effectively as E2's trajectory modelling improves and E3's planning horizon deepens. The care is not diluted by additional capability; it acquires additional reach.

## Limitations and caveats

Bratman's framework is for human agents with cognitive constraints appropriate to human planning horizons. The transfer to an artificial agent with arbitrary planning depth requires the assumption that Bratman's insights about settled intentions structuring deliberation generalise to architectures radically different from human practical reason. There is a real question about whether a sufficiently powerful artificial planner would retain stable commitments or whether its greater capability for reconsideration would destabilise them -- Bratman's account requires that intentions resist reconsideration 'without sufficient reason,' but what counts as sufficient reason for a very capable planner is not specified. The specific empirical prediction in ARC-026 -- that approach-gradient detection grows faster than contact detection as a function of training depth -- is a behavioural prediction that Bratman's philosophical framework does not directly generate. A computational experiment (EXQ-033 and its successors) is the appropriate test of the empirical claim; Bratman provides only the philosophical motivation for expecting expansion rather than crowding-out.
