# Cooperation and the evolution of intelligence
**McNally, Brown & Jackson, 2012 | Proceedings of the Royal Society B**
**DOI:** 10.1098/rspb.2012.0206

## What the paper did

McNally, Brown, and Jackson used artificial neural networks -- whose neuron count served as a proxy for intelligence -- to model the co-evolution of cooperative strategies and cognitive capacity. They simulated iterated prisoner's dilemma and snowdrift games across populations with heritable neural architectures. The key question was whether the cognitive demands of cooperation could themselves select for greater intelligence. They found that transitions toward cooperative equilibria generated sustained selection pressure for increased cognitive capacity, and conversely, that more intelligent agents developed more sophisticated and effective cooperative strategies.

## Key findings and what they imply

The central result is a bidirectional feedback loop: cooperative demands select for intelligence; intelligence enables greater extremes of cooperation. Populations do not stabilize at simple equilibria -- intelligent strategies cycle between cooperation and defection at greater amplitude, suggesting that high-intelligence populations are not simply more cooperative at all times, but are more capable of deep cooperation when conditions allow. The paper also notes that "intelligence facilitates greater extremes of cooperation" -- a phrasing that directly addresses the question of whether intelligence crowds out altruism. It does not. The intelligent strategy set contains richer cooperation, not less of it.

## REE mapping to ARC-026

ARC-026 holds that love -- long-horizon benefit investment -- is not crowded out by intelligence but rather becomes more tractable as capability grows. The McNally et al. finding supports this direction at the population/evolutionary level: the most intelligent agents are not the most self-interested; they are capable of the most sophisticated cooperation. If we allow that love is a form of sustained cooperative investment, then the paper provides evolutionary-game-theoretic evidence that intelligence and love-analogous behavior co-evolve rather than trade off. This is weaker than the specific ARC-026 claim about approach-gradient learning scaling faster than contact avoidance, but it addresses the surface claim -- love expands rather than contracts -- from an independent direction.

## Limitations and caveats

The mechanism is Machiavellian intelligence, not benefit-gradient sensitivity. The neural networks in the model are selecting for strategic contingency -- the capacity to track partner history and deploy tit-for-tat variants -- not for the ability to detect graded benefit signals across spatial or temporal distance. Cooperation in the iterated prisoner's dilemma does not require any analog of love's distinctive computational feature: sustained approach toward a distant other's good. It could be entirely strategic, cold, and instrumental while still registering as "more cooperative" in the model. The intelligence proxy (neuron count) is also underspecified: it captures network capacity but not the specific architectural properties (frontal hierarchy depth, planning horizon) that ARC-026 implicates. Populations cycle between cooperation and defection rather than monotonically expanding altruism, which complicates a simple "love grows with intelligence" reading.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is 0.50 -- mixed direction. The paper supports the broad claim that intelligence and cooperation are not antagonistic and that greater cognitive capacity enables more sophisticated cooperative strategies. It does not support the specific asymmetric-scaling claim that is the mechanistic core of ARC-026. Evidence direction is marked "mixed" because while the surface implication aligns with ARC-026, the underlying mechanism differs and the model dynamics (cycling, not monotonic growth) do not straightforwardly map onto "love expands under intelligence." This paper is most useful as one convergent signal among several, not as primary support.
