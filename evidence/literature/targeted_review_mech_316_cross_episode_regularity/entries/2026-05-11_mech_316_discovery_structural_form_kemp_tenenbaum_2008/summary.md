# Kemp & Tenenbaum 2008 -- The discovery of structural form

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.0802631105), PMID 18669663), Kemp & Tenenbaum ask a question that even now feels somewhat audacious: can a learning algorithm recover the right kind of structural representation for a given dataset -- not just the parameters of a fixed structural form, but the form itself? Their answer is a hierarchical Bayesian framework that maintains a hypothesis space over structural forms (tree, ring, chain, partition, multidimensional space, dominance hierarchy, and others) and infers which form best describes the input via marginal-likelihood maximisation. The validation tour-de-force is that the algorithm recovers known scientific discoveries from the raw data: Linnaean taxonomy when given species-feature co-occurrences, the periodic table's 2D grid when given chemical properties, hierarchical kin relations from social-cognition data. The framework is normatively a complete account of how structure-discovery could work computationally, and the paper is the foundational reference for the structure-learning research programme in cognitive science.

For MECH-316 this is the boundary case of what 'cross-episode regularity extraction' could maximally mean. Three of the four candidate implementation flavours surfaced in Pull 1 (latent-cause inference, successor-representation, attention-modulated RL) all assume some structural prior is in place -- the agent already knows that the world is organised by hidden causes, or by discounted state-occupancies, or by attention-relevant feature dimensions. Kemp & Tenenbaum's structure-learning flavour does not assume this. It asks the agent to also discover the structural prior itself: is the trajectory-record organised by a tree of rule-contexts, a chain of sequential stages, a partition into episode-categories, a dominance ordering of strategies? Each form prescribes a different way of generalising across episodes.

The case for the strong structure-learning reading: it is the only flavour that lets the agent qualitatively switch between organising principles as evidence accumulates. The other three flavours assume the organising principle and parameterise within it. If REE's V3 substrate is going to handle qualitatively-different rule-regime environments -- not just multiple instances of the same kind of rule, but environments where the rule-space has a different shape -- then something like structure-learning is necessary in principle.

The case against the strong reading: it is biologically expensive and the empirical evidence that brains explicitly maintain a hypothesis space over discrete structural forms is thin. What is empirically defensible is the WEAK reading: structure-learning operations exist implicitly, in the form of priors over which latent-cause or attention-allocation patterns are admissible. Under the weak reading, the agent does not maintain an explicit hypothesis space; it has architectural commitments that bias toward certain structural forms, and those biases can be updated by experience. This weak reading is compatible with simpler implementation flavours (Bayesian latent-cause inference, attention-modulated RL) without requiring an explicit form-discovery substrate.

For the substrate-design pass that follows this lit-pull, Kemp & Tenenbaum 2008 is best read as the boundary case rather than the target. The strong reading is probably overdetermined for what MECH-316 needs to do in V3. The architectural commitment REE actually needs is closer to: 'the substrate supports cross-episode regularity extraction with enough flexibility that it can adapt to qualitatively different rule-regimes when they arise'. Whether that means explicit form-discovery (strong reading) or just sufficient parametric flexibility within a single structural form (weak reading) depends on how varied the V3 substrate's rule-regimes are intended to be. SD-054 single-context plus the proposed multi-rule-context extension does not obviously demand the strong reading; the weak reading suffices.

The mapping caveat I would not want to bury: Kemp & Tenenbaum's framework is normative. It shows that structure-learning is doable computationally given enough data and a well-specified hypothesis space. It does not show that brains do this. The biological evidence on whether humans or animals maintain explicit hypothesis spaces over discrete structural forms is genuinely contested. The natural reading for REE is to treat the framework as inspiring an architectural commitment (build the substrate to be flexible across structural forms) without committing to the explicit-hypothesis-space implementation.

I rate this confidence 0.62 with direction MIXED rather than supports. Source quality is high (PNAS, Tenenbaum is the foundational figure for the research programme). Mapping fidelity is moderate -- the framework is clearly relevant to the structure-learning candidate flavour, but the strong reading is probably overdetermined for MECH-316. Transfer risk is elevated because the paradigm (recovering scientific discoveries from co-occurrence data) is far from REE's behavioural-trajectory-record substrate. The MIXED direction reflects the dual position: foundational for the algorithmic flavour, biologically over-strong for the substrate-design call as it actually has to be made. Direction would shift to supports if V3's substrate-design pass concludes that explicit form-discovery is necessary; it would shift to weakens if the pass concludes that the simpler flavours suffice.
