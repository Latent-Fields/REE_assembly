# Gershman & Niv 2010 -- Latent structure as carving nature at its joints

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.conb.2010.02.008), PMID 20227271), Gershman & Niv survey an emerging research programme that reframes learning -- across categorisation, Pavlovian conditioning, structure-learning tasks, and reinforcement learning -- as inference over a structured generative model with hidden causal variables. The slogan is borrowed from Plato: the agent's job is to carve nature at its joints, discovering the latent categories or contexts or causes that the environment is actually organised around, rather than memorising the surface statistics of observations. The review's relevance for MECH-316 is that it is the foundational citation for one of the three candidate implementation flavours Pull 1 surfaced -- Bayesian latent-cause inference -- alongside successor-representation and attention-modulated RL.

The argument that matters for the substrate-design call is that a latent-cause inference mechanism is qualitatively different from a tabular successor-representation update. Under the Bayesian framing, what the agent acquires across episodes is not just a discounted-state-occupancy table; it is a posterior distribution over hidden context variables, and the posterior actively gates behaviour. That distinction has clean falsifiable consequences. Latent-cause inference predicts context-switching behaviour where reappearance of an old contextual cue restores a previously-suppressed policy (Pavlovian renewal is the canonical case). A pure SR substrate predicts smooth interpolation, not snap-back. If MECH-316 has to reproduce snap-back behaviour, the SR flavour is insufficient -- the substrate needs an inference step. Conversely, if MECH-316 only needs to support gradual policy adaptation across stationary statistical regimes, the inference machinery is overkill and the simpler SR flavour suffices.

For REE specifically, the relevant question is what kind of regularities the agent has to extract from its trajectory record. If V3's intended substrate work involves multi-rule-context environments (cf. MECH-318's falsifier on multi-reef configurations, or Q-044's MECH-314 three-arm ablation with novelty / uncertainty / learning-progress as independently manipulable axes), then the substrate has to support inference over which rule-context is active -- and Gershman & Niv's Bayesian framing is the natural fit. If V3 stays with single-context SD-054 reef foraging plus monotone curriculum extensions, the simpler SR / attention-modulated-RL flavours are more parsimonious.

The mapping caveat I would not want to bury: Gershman & Niv 2010 is a review of an algorithmic family, not a mechanistic proposal. The paper does not commit to one neural substrate for the inference. Multiple substrates could realise it -- hippocampal pattern separation + cortical readout, frontopolar uncertainty-bonus over hidden-cause posteriors, OFC discrete state-labels, even attention-modulated-RL given enough flexibility in the attention weights. The paper's contribution to MECH-316 is at the algorithmic-flavour level (which inference family the substrate should belong to), not at the substrate-implementation level. The actual choice of substrate for MECH-316 has to be made by a separate substrate-design pass that this lit-pull is meant to inform, not settle.

I rate this confidence 0.80. Source quality is high (Curr Opin Neurobiol, both authors are field leads). Mapping fidelity is good for the framework-level claim but loses some bite because the paper anchors the framework rather than a specific substrate proposal. Transfer risk is moderate -- the substrate-level translation requires a downstream choice this paper does not by itself decide. The bigger uncertainty is whether REE's regularity-extraction demands actually require latent-cause inference, or whether the simpler SR/attention-modulated flavours are sufficient. That is what the substrate-design pass has to resolve.
