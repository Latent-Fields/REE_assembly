# Rizzuto et al. 2003 -- Reset of human neocortical oscillations during a working memory task

## What the paper did

Rizzuto and colleagues recorded intracranial EEG from 551 electrodes implanted in human neocortex and hippocampus while patients performed a Sternberg short-term recognition memory task. They examined whether stimulus presentation caused genuine phase reset of ongoing oscillations -- as opposed to merely adding a new evoked signal on top of background activity. This distinction is critical: additive evoked responses would increase both phase coherence and power post-stimulus, whereas genuine phase reset would produce cross-trial phase alignment without a corresponding power increase.

## Key findings

The study demonstrated convincing evidence for genuine phase reset in the 7-16 Hz range (peaking around 8 Hz). Following stimulus events, oscillatory phases locked across trials without accompanying increases in oscillatory power -- the signature that distinguishes true phase reset from superimposed evoked responses. Different stimulus classes (list items vs. memory probes) produced topographically distinct reset patterns across cortical regions, with inferior temporal and hippocampal electrodes showing particularly robust effects. This selectivity suggests that phase reset is not a blanket cortical response but is organized by stimulus type and functional relevance.

## Mapping to MECH-091

MECH-091 claims that salient events phase-reset the E3 heartbeat clock, ensuring that updated harm estimates from the salient event enter E3 at the start of a fresh processing cycle. Rizzuto et al. provide perhaps the strongest available evidence that phase reset is a real biological mechanism in human cortical circuits -- not merely a theoretical construct or measurement artifact. The intracranial recordings bypass the ambiguities that plague scalp EEG phase analyses.

The topographic selectivity of reset patterns is particularly relevant: if different stimulus types reset different cortical regions, this supports the REE architectural claim that salient events could selectively reset E3 timing without necessarily disrupting E1 or E2 cycles. The hippocampal involvement is also noteworthy, given E3's architectural relationship to hippocampal systems in REE.

## Limitations and honest uncertainties

The mapping from this study to MECH-091 requires several inferential steps. First, the task context is working memory encoding, not salience detection or harm evaluation -- the "salient event" here is a memory item, not an unexpected outcome or threat. Second, the dominant frequency band (7-16 Hz, alpha range) is higher than the delta-theta oscillations most commonly associated with salience processing and the P300 response. Third, while the study establishes that phase reset exists as a cortical mechanism, it does not directly test whether phase reset serves the specific computational function MECH-091 proposes -- namely, ensuring fresh harm estimates enter a processing cycle at its start rather than mid-cycle.

## Confidence reasoning

Source quality is very high (0.92): this is a PNAS paper with intracranial recordings from a large electrode array, using rigorous methodology that directly addresses the phase-reset-vs-evoked-response question. Mapping fidelity is moderate (0.60): the mechanism is demonstrated, but in a different functional context. The transfer risk is moderate (0.45): phase reset appears to be a domain-general cortical mechanism, but the specific application to E3 heartbeat cycling in a salience/harm context requires bridging assumptions. Overall confidence: 0.72, weighted toward the strong mechanistic demonstration but tempered by the contextual distance.
