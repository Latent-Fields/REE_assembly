# Kennedy and Shapiro (2009) -- Motivational states activate distinct hippocampal representations to guide goal-directed behaviors

**PNAS 106(26):10805-10810 | DOI: 10.1073/pnas.0903259106 | PMID: 19528659**

## What the paper did

Kennedy and Shapiro recorded single-unit activity from the same population of dorsal CA1 hippocampal neurons in rats performing two tasks: a hippocampus-dependent contextual memory retrieval task (requiring the animal to use motivational state to select the correct goal arm) and a hippocampus-independent random foraging task (where hunger or thirst was irrelevant to behavior). Crucially, they alternated sessions between food-deprivation and water-deprivation states in the same physical environment, so they could directly compare how the same neurons represented the same locations under different motivational conditions.

## Key findings for SD-014

The key finding is a clean dissociation: hippocampal ensemble representations were significantly more distinct between hunger and thirst states during the goal-directed memory task than during random foraging. Put differently, when motivation mattered for behavior, the hippocampus activated qualitatively different map configurations for the same physical space -- food-relevant and water-relevant locations were represented differently depending on which drive state was active. During random foraging, where the motivational state was incidental, ensemble representations were more similar across drive states. The paper also showed prospective coding: CA1 firing at a given location predicted the animal's intended goal (food vs. water arm), but the predictive signal was about the non-spatial target (the reward type) rather than the spatial trajectory -- suggesting the hippocampus was encoding drive-state-goal associations, not just routes.

## REE mapping: drive-state gating of hippocampal valence

This paper speaks directly to the w (wanting) component of SD-014's valence vector and to the replay prioritisation claim. SD-014 says that wanting is drive-state-scaled -- a hungry animal should find food-location nodes more salient than a satiated one. Kennedy and Shapiro show that this is literally true at the hippocampal ensemble level: the map reconfigures under different motivational states such that the currently relevant reward locations are differentially activated. This is not just a downstream readout effect; the hippocampus itself participates in representing the drive-state-modulated relevance of locations. For replay prioritisation, this means that the drive state at the time of consolidation should influence which hippocampal map nodes are "hot" and therefore more likely to be replayed in SWRs -- which is exactly what SD-014 proposes.

## Limitations

The experiment conflates drive state (hungry/thirsty) with reward type (food/water). It cannot distinguish whether the hippocampal reconfiguration is driven by the internal state or by the expectation of a specific reward identity. A cleaner SD-014 test would hold reward type constant (e.g., always food) and vary drive level (hungry vs. satiated) to see whether wanting intensity alone modulates node firing. The paper also does not dissect wanting from liking -- both would shift together when the animal transitions from hunger to thirst. So the l component of the valence vector remains untested. Finally, the spatial scale (dorsal CA1, linear tracks and open field) may differ from the more abstract location encoding relevant to SD-014's action-graph nodes.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is 0.80. This is among the most direct available evidence that hippocampal spatial representations are modulated by motivational drive state -- exactly the right phenomenon for SD-014. The confound between drive state and reward type is a genuine limitation but does not undermine the core point. The paper is published in PNAS with a clean within-subject design. It grounds the drive-state-gated aspect of SD-014's w component and the replay prioritisation claim with direct hippocampal electrophysiology.
