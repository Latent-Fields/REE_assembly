# Beauchamp & Ruxton 2010 — Reassessment of the risk allocation hypothesis

According to PubMed: Beauchamp & Ruxton 2010, *American Naturalist* 177(1):143–6 (with discussion 147–51). [DOI 10.1086/657437](https://doi.org/10.1086/657437) (PMID 21117942).

## What the paper does

A theoretical comment in the *American Naturalist* responding to Lima & Bednekoff 1999 (sibling entry). The authors challenge several aspects of the canonical risk-allocation hypothesis on theoretical grounds, raising questions about the assumptions underlying the state-dependent decision-making structure and the conditions under which the hypothesis's qualitative predictions hold. The paper is a brief commentary rather than primary empirical or theoretical work; its load-bearing function is to flag that even the canonical theory's theoretical foundations have been contested.

## Why this matters for R4 (tolerance-window calibration)

The acceptance threshold for the Phase 2 falsifier should not pre-register criteria that match a strict reading of Lima-Bednekoff which even the canonical theorists' reassessors do not endorse. The risk-allocation literature has retained the *spirit* of the hypothesis (animals modulate antipredator effort by relative risk pattern) while weakening the specific quantitative predictions. For ARC-062, this argues for a tolerance window: ARM_1 should reproduce *some* risk-pattern dependence (the architecturally load-bearing finding) rather than *all* of Lima-Bednekoff's specific quantitative predictions.

This complements two other entries in this pull:
- Sundell 2004 (empirical partial-replication in voles) — only 1/5 predictions replicated under field conditions; even canonical-theory-supportive results in real animals are partial.
- Eccard 2020 (individual variation in voles) — within-population behaviour is consistent-but-different across individuals, so within-population calibration must accommodate spread rather than a single ratio.

Together with the Lima-Bednekoff canonical theory entry, these four pieces argue convergently for a tolerance-window-not-single-ratio approach to the Phase 2 falsifier acceptance threshold.

## Why this matters for MECH-309

MECH-309 makes a *strong* logical-necessity claim: parametric-policy learners cannot invent rules; without a rule-creator, the trainer collapses to a single regime. Beauchamp & Ruxton remind that even the *empirical* version of the rule-creating story (animals are good at risk-allocation in field conditions) has theoretical caveats. The architectural commitment is therefore that ARC-062 / ARC-063 enable rule-allocation *in principle*; whether the agent achieves it in any specific substrate is a function of architecture + substrate signal quality + training adequacy. A single-prediction PASS in the Phase 2 falsifier is consistent with MECH-309's logical-necessity claim being right and the empirical execution being noisy — both readings can be honest about uncertainty in the same way Beauchamp & Ruxton are honest about caveats in the canonical theory.

## Mapping caveat

This is a theoretical comment, not primary data. It does not provide quantitative calibration. Its load-bearing function is the *caveat* itself — caution against narrow Phase 2 acceptance criteria — rather than a positive prediction. The architectural-falsifier acceptance window should be derived from the convergence of the four Pull B entries together (Lima-Bednekoff's theoretical structure, Sundell's empirical partial-replication, Beauchamp & Ruxton's theoretical caveats, Eccard's individual-variation findings) rather than from any single one in isolation. The Crowell 2016 species-difference entry adds a fifth voice arguing for cross-instance variation as biologically expected.
