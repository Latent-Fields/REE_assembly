# Garvert et al. 2023: Hippocampal spatio-predictive cognitive maps adaptively guide reward generalization

Garvert and colleagues present fMRI evidence that the human hippocampus simultaneously
encodes two types of cognitive map -- one based on spatial relationships, another based
on experienced transition structure (the predictive map). In a virtual navigation task
where spatial location determines reward magnitude, participants' choices are initially
influenced more by the predictive map but progressively shift toward the spatial map.
The orbitofrontal cortex arbitrates between the two maps, tracking which is more
consistent with observed outcomes.

The relevance to MECH-216 is the demonstration that predictive representations -- not
just direct spatial or reward proximity -- guide reward-related behavior. In MECH-216
terms: E1 schema activations that predict resource encounters are the analog of the
predictive cognitive map, and these predictive signals influence approach behavior
before the direct wanting signal from resource proximity (the "spatial map" equivalent)
is available. The finding that predictive map influence is strongest early in learning
is particularly interesting: it suggests that MECH-216's schema-conditioned wanting
may be most important during initial exploration of novel environments, before the
VALENCE_WANTING landscape has been populated by direct resource contact events.

The paper also reveals a competitive dynamic between spatial and predictive maps --
strengthening one weakens the other. If this translates to REE, it would predict that
as direct contact wanting (Level 1) accumulates, E1 schema-conditioned wanting
(Level 2, MECH-216) may diminish in influence. This is not necessarily a problem --
it suggests that MECH-216 serves as a bootstrap mechanism for navigation in novel or
sparse environments, handing off to direct wanting as experience accumulates.

The caveat is that Garvert et al. measure choice behavior, not motivational or
wanting signals. The paper does not address whether predictive maps generate
motivational approach per se -- only that they influence value-based decisions. The
step from "predictive map influences choice" to "predictive representation seeds
wanting" is the additional claim that MECH-216 makes, drawing on the incentive
salience framework rather than on this paper alone. Additionally, the OFC
arbitration mechanism has no current REE analog, which limits the architectural
specificity of the translation.
