# Wikenheiser and Schoenbaum 2016 -- Over the river, through the woods: cognitive maps in the hippocampus and orbitofrontal cortex

**Source:** Nature Reviews Neuroscience 17(8):513-523 (2016). DOI: 10.1038/nrn.2016.56. PMID: 27256552.

---

The question Wikenheiser and Schoenbaum pose is structural: the hippocampus appears to hold a map of the world and the OFC appears to hold a map of values, so what does the joint system actually do? Their answer is a division of labour that maps almost precisely onto the MECH-056 claim. The hippocampus encodes the topology of the environment -- spatial and abstract relationships between states, the lay of the land -- while the OFC encodes expected outcomes and values at positions within that map. The two are anatomically interconnected but functionally separable: lesioning the hippocampus disrupts map-dependent navigation; lesioning the OFC disrupts the ability to use value information to bias route selection, without destroying the map itself.

The key implication for MECH-056 is that the value/outcome layer (OFC) reads off the structural map (hippocampus) rather than rewriting it. An animal avoids a shock-associated location not because its spatial representation of that location changes -- place fields do not vanish; the spot is still in the map -- but because the OFC overlay assigns a negative expected value to trajectories that pass through that location, making them less likely to be selected. The harm signal constrains trajectory selection at the output stage of the hippocampal-OFC joint system without requiring the hippocampal map to be restructured.

Rodent electrophysiology supports this: place cells associated with aversive locations do sometimes show altered firing -- the map is not perfectly rigid -- but the structural topology of the map is largely preserved even when outcome associations change substantially. Human fMRI evidence adds a parallel picture in abstract state spaces: hippocampus tracks structural relationships between states regardless of their current value; OFC responds to value changes while hippocampal correlates of state structure remain stable.

The limitation for MECH-056 is the one common to all spatial navigation work: the claim is about trajectory planning over a residue field, not merely about spatial route avoidance in a familiar environment. The OFC-hippocampus dissociation is clearest for spatial and well-learned abstract maps; extension to online trajectory planning through novel harm terrain, as REE requires, is not directly demonstrated. The review also synthesises across quite varied paradigms, some of which involve feedback-driven map modification (extinction, reversal learning) where the separation is less clean. Nevertheless, the framework it describes -- a structural map layer kept separate from a value overlay layer, with value information gating route selection -- is the closest neuroscientific instantiation yet found of what MECH-056 requires at the architectural level.
