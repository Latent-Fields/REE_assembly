# Huang et al. 2024 — SafeDreamer: Safe Reinforcement Learning with World Models

**Source:** ICLR 2024. arXiv: 2307.07176. URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.07176.

---

SafeDreamer asks a practical engineering question -- how do you make a DreamerV3-based agent reliably satisfy safety constraints in complex environments -- and in answering it, arrives at an architecture that embodies the MECH-056 principle without explicitly naming it. The design choice that matters for our purposes is where in the computational pipeline safety constraints are applied: not in the state encoder, not by modifying the RSSM recurrent dynamics, but at the trajectory planning stage.

The world model retains its standard DreamerV3 architecture: a Recurrent State Space Model (RSSM) that learns latent representations of the world primarily through reconstruction objectives (image decoder, reward decoder). To this, SafeDreamer adds a cost decoder -- a separate prediction head operating on the same shared RSSM state, trained to predict expected safety cost. The key step is what happens at planning time. The OSRP (Online Safety-Reward Planning) procedure generates candidate trajectories in imagination, evaluates each for expected cumulative cost, and restricts the action-selection step to trajectories that fall within the safety threshold. Only within that feasible set is reward maximisation applied. The harm constraint shapes trajectory selection; it does not reshape the encoder.

This is a partial rather than complete instantiation of MECH-056. The cost decoder loss does backpropagate through the shared RSSM representations, which means safety supervision is not fully isolated from core representation learning at the gradient level. SafeDreamer does not claim representational integrity as a design goal; it claims near-zero safety violation as an engineering outcome. The separation is architectural -- separate heads on shared features -- not gradient-blocked. Whether the cost gradient subtly modifies the learned representations in ways that would count as "distortion" in the REE sense is not tested.

What SafeDreamer does demonstrate is that the trajectory-first approach is computationally tractable and practically effective: keeping harm assessment as a read operation over stable world-model representations, and applying the harm criterion as a filter at action selection time, achieves strong safety performance. The paper achieves near-zero cost violations across Safety-Gymnasium benchmarks. For REE the lesson is that the algorithm exists, it works, and the engineering choice to place harm constraint at trajectory selection rather than at representation learning is neither artificial nor fragile -- it is robust enough to hold up across diverse continuous-control tasks.
