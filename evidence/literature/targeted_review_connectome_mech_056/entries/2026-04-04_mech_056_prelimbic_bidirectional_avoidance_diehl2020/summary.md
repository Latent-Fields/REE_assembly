# Diehl et al. 2020 — Divergent projections of the prelimbic cortex bidirectionally regulate active avoidance

**Source:** eLife 9:e59281 (2020). DOI: 10.7554/eLife.59281. PMID: 33054975.

---

The question Diehl et al. were asking is a practical one with deep architectural implications: when an animal faces genuinely competing drives -- the pull of food reward and the push of threat avoidance -- how does the brain resolve the conflict without simply amplifying the stronger signal and corrupting the other? Their answer, arrived at through careful optogenetic dissection, is that the prelimbic (PL) prefrontal cortex resolves the conflict not by rewriting its own internal representation of the situation, but by routing output through two antagonistic pathways that project to different downstream targets.

The finding is clean. Photoactivating PL terminals in the ventral striatum reduces avoidance -- it suppresses threat-driven action selection. Photoactivating PL terminals in the basolateral amygdala increases avoidance -- it promotes it. Both effects are driven by the same cortical region, operating at the same time, in the same task. The ventral striatum receives opposing pressure from PL (approach-promoting) and from the BLA directly (avoidance-promoting), making it the site where the competition is resolved. What the PL cortex itself appears to be doing is not changing its representation of the threat, but modulating the relative gain of its two output channels.

The implication for MECH-056 is that trajectory-level constraint can be implemented architecturally without representational distortion. The stable cortical state -- the PL representation of the world, including the threat -- can remain intact while pathway weighting determines which trajectory gets selected. This is trajectory-first placement in a circuit sense: the harm signal constrains which action trajectory wins (via BLA and VS pathway competition) before, and without requiring, any change to the cortical representation that holds the world model.

One should be careful not to overextend this. The paradigm is a binary choice -- platform or reward zone -- not a graded multi-step trajectory over a spatial map. And this study does not independently verify that the PL representation is unchanged under high threat: we observe the output pathway behaviour, not the representational stability. It is logically possible that amygdala input to PL is simultaneously modifying the cortical state while also routing output -- the two could co-occur. The stronger version of MECH-056, which requires active preservation of representations, is not directly tested here. What is tested, and confirmed, is that the trajectory selection can be fully modulated by circuit routing without requiring the planning stage to be aware of the route taken -- the selection happens downstream.
