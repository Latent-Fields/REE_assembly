# Jercog et al. 2021 — Dynamical prefrontal population coding during defensive behaviours

**Source:** Nature 595, 690-694 (2021). DOI: 10.1038/s41586-021-03726-6. PMID: 34262175.

---

The central puzzle Jercog et al. set out to solve was a timing problem: the amygdala signals threat early and persistently, yet animals do not simply react to every threat signal -- they sometimes avoid and sometimes do not, and the difference between these outcomes is not predictable from the amplitude of the initial threat response. If harm signals were directly writing onto action representations, you would expect the strength of the threat encoding to predict what the animal does. It does not. That is the first result: sustained dmPFC population activity at stimulus onset, clearly driven by amygdala inputs, encodes threat robustly but does not distinguish avoided from non-avoided trials.

What does predict action outcome is a second, temporally distinct signal -- a transient surge in dmPFC population activity that appears immediately before action initiation and that reliably separates avoided from non-avoided trials in single-session decoding. This is not the same neural population reactivating; it is a different dynamical regime within the same prefrontal tissue, separable by principal-components analysis of the population trajectory. Optogenetic inhibition of dmPFC activity at the moment this transient arises -- but not at the time of the sustained threat encoding -- impairs adaptive defensive response selection. The causal necessity is therefore located at the action-selection computation, not at the threat-encoding computation.

For MECH-056 the implication is direct. The sustained amygdala-driven threat representation in dmPFC is a reasonable functional analogue of the REE residue field: a persistent, harm-landscape signal that is present in the background, real, and informationally rich, but not itself the proximal driver of which trajectory gets selected. The transient pre-action code is the analogue of trajectory-level constraint: the moment at which harm-state information is converted into a selection pressure on action, without requiring the underlying threat representation to change shape. The two are temporally and functionally dissociated in the recorded neural data, and the dissociation is causally confirmed by the optogenetic timing experiment.

It would be too strong to say this paper shows how distortion is *prevented*. It shows that the two computations are separable, not that they are guaranteed to remain separate under all conditions -- for instance, chronic stress is known to alter dmPFC representations, and the paper does not address that regime. The honest claim is more modest: the brain implements something structurally analogous to MECH-056 in the context of acute threat-driven avoidance, dissociating persistent harm-state encoding from the transient action-selection computation that reads off that state. Whether the same architecture generalises to multi-step trajectory planning over complex environments remains an open question.
