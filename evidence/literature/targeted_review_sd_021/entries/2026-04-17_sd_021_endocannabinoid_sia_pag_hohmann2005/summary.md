# Hohmann et al. 2005 -- An endocannabinoid mechanism for stress-induced analgesia

## Source
Hohmann AG et al. *Nature* 435(7045):1108-12. DOI: 10.1038/nature03658

## What the paper did

Hohmann and colleagues addressed a long-standing puzzle in pain neuroscience: acute stress produces analgesia in rodents and humans, and while the opioid-mediated component of stress-induced analgesia (SIA) is well established, a substantial portion of SIA is naloxone-resistant -- it persists after opioid blockade. What mediates this non-opioid component? Using a continuous foot-shock model in rats, this paper showed that CB1 cannabinoid receptor blockade within the periaqueductal gray (PAG) -- but not elsewhere -- prevents the non-opioid SIA. The paper then used mass spectrometry to demonstrate that stress produces rapid, local release of two endogenous cannabinoids (2-arachidonoylglycerol, 2-AG, and anandamide) specifically in the midbrain PAG. Pharmacological inhibition of the enzymes that degrade each of these lipids, when microinjected into the PAG, augmented SIA in a CB1-dependent fashion. The conclusion was clean: the PAG is the site where endocannabinoid release mediates non-opioid SIA.

## Key findings for SD-021

SD-021 requires the PAG to function as the relay point where E3 commitment signals engage the descending inhibitory pathway to reduce z_harm_s PE precision. This paper establishes something important beyond the canonical opioid story: the PAG is capable of generating analgesia via locally released endocannabinoids in response to aversive context. The implication for SD-021 is that the PAG relay is not a passive conduit for descending opioid tone -- it has local molecular machinery that responds to the organism's current state (aversive vs neutral) and modulates its own output accordingly. When the organism is in a high-stakes context that involves anticipated harm (proximal to what REE calls commitment to a harm-accepting trajectory), the PAG is biochemically primed to increase descending inhibition.

The opioid independence is particularly relevant. It means that SD-021 cannot be reduced to 'E3 commitment releases endogenous opioids.' The descending analgesia during commitment-gated harm acceptance likely recruits multiple molecular mechanisms at the PAG -- opioidergic, endocannabinoidergic, and possibly others -- all converging on the same RVM relay and dorsal horn gate.

## REE mapping

In REE architecture, E3 planning involves forward simulation of trajectories including harm-laden ones. When E3 commits to such a trajectory, the SD-021 mechanism reduces z_harm_s PE precision to prevent sensory alarm signals from interrupting execution. The cortical initiation (pgACC -> PAG) is documented in Petrovic 2002. What Hohmann 2005 adds is evidence that the PAG itself is an active signal processor, not merely a relay: aversive context activates local endocannabinoid synthesis, which then gates output to the RVM and dorsal horn. This is consistent with the idea that commitment-state representations (from E3 via pgACC) find a PAG that is already biochemically responsive to the high-stakes context, potentially allowing E3-derived signals to tip the PAG into inhibitory output mode.

## Limitations

The most significant limitation for SD-021 is the gap between stress and commitment. Stress-induced analgesia is triggered by inescapable or high-intensity aversive stimulation -- it does not require goal formation or trajectory planning. The REE claim is specifically about commitment-gated analgesia, which requires E3 to have resolved a planning horizon that passes through expected harm. These may recruit overlapping but not identical PAG mechanisms. Additionally, this is a rodent study; the relevance of PAG endocannabinoid-mediated SIA to human voluntary pain tolerance during goal pursuit is inferred, not demonstrated. The paper also does not address the sensory-discriminative vs affective-motivational distinction -- whether endocannabinoid-mediated SIA selectively suppresses the A-delta input (z_harm_s) while sparing C-fiber affective signaling (z_harm_a) is an open question.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is 0.75. The paper is high quality (Nature, causal pharmacological design with direct PAG microinjections and mass spectrometry validation) and it directly supports the PAG as the analgesia relay. The mapping fidelity is moderate because the triggering condition (uncontrollable stress) differs from E3 commitment, and the paper does not address the sensory/affective selectivity that is the key mechanistic claim in SD-021. Nevertheless, it robustly establishes the PAG molecular substrate and its context-sensitivity, which is an important component of the SD-021 biological analogy.
