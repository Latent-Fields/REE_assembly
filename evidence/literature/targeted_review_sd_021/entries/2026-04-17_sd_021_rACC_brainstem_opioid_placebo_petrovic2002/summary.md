# Petrovic et al. 2002 -- Placebo and opioid analgesia: imaging a shared neuronal network

## Source
Petrovic P, Kalso E, Petersson KM, Ingvar M. *Science* 295(5560):1737-40. DOI: 10.1126/science.1067176

## What the paper did

Petrovic and colleagues used PET imaging with H2O15 in 16 healthy male volunteers to compare the neural correlates of opioid analgesia (remifentanil infusion) versus placebo analgesia. Importantly, they also included a pain-only condition. The central question was whether the analgesic effects of an exogenous opioid and a psychologically induced expectation of relief share common neural substrates -- or whether these are mechanistically distinct phenomena that happen to produce similar behavioral outputs. The answer they found was unambiguous: both conditions recruited increased activity in the rostral anterior cingulate cortex (rACC), and -- critically -- both showed functional covariation between the rACC and the brainstem, a pattern that was absent in the pain-only control.

## Key findings for SD-021

The finding that rACC-brainstem connectivity is specifically elevated during analgesia -- whether pharmacologically or cognitively induced -- and absent during unmodulated pain experience is precisely what SD-021 requires. The rACC (or perigenual ACC, pgACC) is the cortical initiator of the PAG-RVM descending inhibitory pathway in the canonical anatomy. What Petrovic et al. provide is direct functional evidence: when top-down suppression is engaged, you can measure it as a correlated increase in rACC and brainstem activation, not just as a reduction in self-reported pain. The shared network across opioid and placebo conditions argues that the cortical gating mechanism is not opioid-specific -- it is a general top-down modulation channel that can be opened by multiple triggers, including expectation and commitment.

## REE mapping

SD-021 proposes that E3 commitment to a trajectory passing through expected harm reduces z_harm_s PE precision via a descending modulation pathway analogous to pgACC -> PAG -> RVM -> dorsal horn. Petrovic 2002 provides the functional imaging correlate of the cortical-initiation step: when the organism adopts an analgesia-expectant state (an analogue, in REE terms, of E3 accepting harm as trajectory-consistent), the rACC becomes more active and its activity co-varies with brainstem activity. This is the neural signature of commitment-gated precision reduction being initiated at the cortical level. The paper does not speak directly to the sensory-discriminative versus affective-motivational distinction (that falls to Hofbauer 2001), but it locates the control node -- the rACC -- whose projections to the PAG constitute the gateway of the SD-021 mechanism.

## Limitations

The PET spatial resolution cannot distinguish the PAG from the RVM within the brainstem signal; both are collapsed into a single 'brainstem' label. The analogy between placebo expectation and E3 trajectory commitment is imperfect: expectation is a passive belief update, whereas E3 commitment involves a resolved planning horizon with allocated resources. Whether the same rACC-brainstem pathway mediates both is plausible but not demonstrated. The sample is male-only, which is relevant given known sex differences in endogenous opioid function.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is 0.82. The paper provides strong, clean evidence for the cortical initiation angle of SD-021 -- the rACC-brainstem co-activation during top-down analgesia. The convergent replication across pharmacological and cognitive analgesic conditions is particularly compelling. Deductions come from the transfer gap between placebo expectation and committed trajectory acceptance, and from the spatial resolution limitation preventing brainstem structure identification.
