## Rationale for source selection

MECH-102 claims that violence is a terminal error-correction mechanism, selected architecturally when low-energy coordination pathways are unavailable. This is a multi-level claim: it applies at individual, institutional, and civilizational scales, and it depends on the prior claim (INV-029) that the default trajectory-selection bias favours coordination-preserving futures.

The literature search targeted four domains where this claim has empirical or theoretical purchase:

1. **Frustration-aggression tradition (Berkowitz 1989):** The most direct empirical literature on goal-path blockage as a trigger for violence. Berkowitz's 1989 reformulation establishes that blocked pathways generate aggression via negative affect, providing individual-level grounding for the pathway-exhaustion mechanism.

2. **General Aggression Model (Anderson & Bushman 2002):** The unifying framework for aggression research. Relevant for MECH-102 via its appraisal/reappraisal cycle (thoughtful vs. impulsive action) and its developmental feedback loops. The GAM also documents the failure modes that MECH-102 is designed to prevent.

3. **Just war philosophy (Aloyo 2015):** Direct philosophical analysis of the 'last resort' criterion, which MECH-102 superficially resembles. Included as a stress-test of the conceptual structure of the claim. Aloyo's critique of temporal last-resort framing sharpens the distinction between sequential-exhaustion and forward-model-projection readings of MECH-102.

4. **Game theory of cooperation (Axelrod 1984):** The most rigorous formal model of when cooperative equilibria collapse and escalation follows. Provides structural grounding for MECH-102 at social and institutional scales, with direct mapping between the 'shadow of the future' (cooperation accessible), 'shadow of the present' (cooperation inaccessible), and REE's pathway-closure conditions. Also provides a formal account of escalation spirals corresponding to MECH-102's condition (c).

Together these sources cover the individual psychological, systems/architectural, normative-philosophical, and formal game-theoretic dimensions of the claim.
