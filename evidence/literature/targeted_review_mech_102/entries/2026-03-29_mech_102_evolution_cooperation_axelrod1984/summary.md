## What the paper did

Axelrod's *The Evolution of Cooperation* (1984) is a foundational study in game theory and evolutionary biology. The core experimental contribution is a series of iterated prisoner's dilemma (IPD) tournaments in which computer strategies competed over many rounds. The winning strategy -- Tit for Tat (TFT), submitted by Anatol Rapoport -- was nice (never defects first), provocable (retaliates immediately to defection), forgiving (returns to cooperation after punishment), and clear (behaves predictably). Axelrod extended this result into an evolutionary analysis showing that TFT could invade populations of defectors when introduced in sufficient clusters, and that cooperation could emerge as a stable equilibrium under the right structural conditions. The key structural condition is the 'shadow of the future': when agents expect continued future interaction, the cooperative equilibrium becomes self-sustaining because the long-run cost of retaliation exceeds the short-run gain from defection.

## Key findings

The most important finding for MECH-102 is structural: cooperation is not the universal default but the equilibrium strategy when low-cost coordination futures are accessible. Defection (escalation to conflict) becomes the equilibrium strategy precisely when those futures are unavailable -- when the shadow of the future is short, when one party has irreversibly defected, or when noise corrupts the coordination signal such that the agents can no longer reliably distinguish cooperation from defection. Axelrod also demonstrates the danger of escalation spirals: in noisy environments, TFT can become trapped in long sequences of mutual retaliation because each agent interprets the other's retaliatory defection as a fresh provocation. This corresponds to MECH-102's condition (c) -- commitment architecture failure -- where the agent cannot model consequences beyond the immediate step and the escalation becomes self-sustaining.

The 'forgiveness' property of successful strategies is also noteworthy: the best-performing strategies return to cooperation after punishing defection, which means they treat escalation as targeted error-correction rather than terminal termination of the relationship. This is structurally consistent with MECH-102's framing: even when violence is selected as a high-energy corrective, it is not the destruction of the coordination possibility -- it is a corrective intervention aimed at restoring the cooperative equilibrium.

## Limitations and caveats

The primary limitation is mechanistic: Axelrod's agents are strategic payoff-maximisers, not architecturally constrained error-correction systems. MECH-102 claims violence is selected because the forward model finds no accessible coherent future via low-energy paths -- this is a computational claim, not a strategic payoff claim. In the IPD, cooperation collapses when defection yields higher expected payoffs; in MECH-102, violence is selected when the forward model fails to project coherent futures through coordination. These are formally different conditions that often co-occur but are not equivalent.

A second limitation: Axelrod's model is dyadic and symmetric. MECH-102's framing is about an agent's unilateral error-correction decision in a world with many possible coordination partners. The dyadic structure of the IPD cannot fully capture institutional or civilizational dynamics where the 'other party' is a distributed system of many agents.

Finally, Axelrod's model operates at the level of observable behaviour (cooperate/defect) without any internal architecture. MECH-102 requires an internal architecture capable of forward-modelling pathway accessibility. The formal results are consistent, but the level of description is different.
