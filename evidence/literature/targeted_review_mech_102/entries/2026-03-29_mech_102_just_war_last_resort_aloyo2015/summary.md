## What the paper did

Aloyo's 2015 paper in *Ethics & International Affairs* is a careful philosophical analysis of the 'last resort' criterion in just war theory. Last resort is one of the classical *jus ad bellum* conditions: war (or more broadly, political violence) is justified only when peaceful alternatives have been tried and failed. Aloyo systematically examines four formulations of this principle -- exhaustion of all alternatives, reasonable alternatives, alternatives with a reasonable chance of success, and others -- and argues that each formulation either produces counterintuitive policy recommendations or reduces to conditions already captured by proportionality analysis. His conclusion is that just war theory would be more coherent without the last resort criterion: what matters morally is minimising severe harms to innocents and achieving just causes, not temporal sequencing of violent and non-violent means.

## Key findings

Aloyo's key argument is that strict last resort can require allowing greater harm in order to preserve the temporal sequencing of non-violent before violent means. If a violent intervention at time T would prevent far greater harm than a non-violent intervention followed by a violent one at time T+n, then insisting on temporal sequencing makes the outcome worse. He also shows that the most defensible versions of last resort either become indistinguishable from proportionality assessment or rely on counterfactual probability estimates that are not uniquely captured by the last-resort framing. The paper is philosophically precise and resists easy summary, but the core claim is: the moral work attributed to last resort is either not real or is done by other, more fundamental criteria.

## REE translation: grounding MECH-102

This paper is the most philosophically uncomfortable entry in this review for MECH-102, and it earns its place precisely because of that. MECH-102's claim that violence is triggered 'only when low-energy coordination pathways have been exhausted or are unavailable' sounds formally like a last-resort criterion at the level Aloyo is critiquing. If Aloyo's argument is correct, then MECH-102's sequential framing may inherit the same philosophical problems.

The REE response, however, is structural rather than temporal. MECH-102 does not claim that an agent must try non-violent options in sequence before violence becomes permissible. It claims that the agent's forward model must project that low-energy pathways are unavailable -- meaning the architecture evaluates accessibility in trajectory space, not in a temporal queue. This is closer to Aloyo's preferred proportionality-based standard than it might initially appear: in both cases, the decision criterion is based on projected outcomes rather than procedural sequencing. Aloyo's preferred question -- 'which policy minimises severe harms to innocents?' -- translates in REE as: 'which trajectory preserves the most coherent coordination futures?' (INV-029). These are not identical, but they are structurally analogous.

The philosophical contribution here is to sharpen what MECH-102 is actually claiming. If the claim is temporal ('try non-violent means first'), then Aloyo's critique applies directly. If the claim is structural ('violence is selected when the forward model finds no accessible coherent future via low-energy paths'), then the claim is more defensible but also more demanding -- it requires a forward model sophisticated enough to accurately evaluate pathway accessibility, including counterfactual assessment of whether non-violent alternatives would have succeeded.

## Limitations and caveats

The paper operates entirely within normative political philosophy and interstate war ethics. The mapping to REE's architectural claim requires treating the philosophical analysis as a stress-test rather than as empirical evidence. Aloyo's argument is not an empirical finding about when violence occurs -- it is an argument about when violence is justified. MECH-102 is not primarily a normative claim about justification; it is a mechanistic claim about architectural selection. The domains are different, but the philosophical pressure on the 'sequential exhaustion' framing is real regardless of the domain difference.

A secondary limitation: Aloyo's paper does not engage with the computational or cognitive literature on decision-making under conflict. His analysis is purely normative-philosophical. This limits the extent to which his critique can be addressed by pointing to descriptive evidence.

Confidence is 0.58, reflecting the mixed character of this evidence. The net effect is to raise the bar for what MECH-102 needs to mean in order to be philosophically defensible.
