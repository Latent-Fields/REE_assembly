# Price, Harkins & Baker 1987 -- Sensory-Affective Relationships in Clinical and Experimental Pain

**PubMed:** https://doi.org/10.1016/0304-3959(87)90065-0

## What the paper did

This is one of the foundational empirical studies establishing that pain has separable sensory-intensity and affective-unpleasantness dimensions that can be independently modulated. Price, Harkins, and Baker asked multiple patient groups -- cancer pain patients, chronic pain patients (various aetiologies), and labour patients -- as well as experimental pain subjects to rate their pain on two visual analogue scales: one for sensory intensity (running from "no sensation" to "the most intense sensation imaginable") and one for affective unpleasantness (from "not bad at all" to "the most unpleasant feeling possible"). They compared the ratio of affective to sensory ratings across groups, and additionally tested whether attentional focus (attending to the birth vs to the pain) modulated the two dimensions differentially in labour patients.

## Key findings relevant to SD-011

The dissociation is striking. Cancer and chronic pain patients gave VAS affective ratings that were substantially higher than their VAS sensory ratings -- the affect was disproportionate to the reported intensity. Experimental pain subjects (controlled thermal stimulation) showed the reverse: sensory ratings exceeded affective ratings. Labour patients fell in between, with stage-dependent variation. Critically, when labour patients were instructed to focus on the birth rather than on the pain, VAS affective ratings dropped significantly but VAS sensory ratings did not change. This is a within-subject manipulation that selectively modulates the affective dimension while leaving sensory intensity largely untouched.

The implication is that the two dimensions are genuinely distinct psychological constructs, not simply two measures of the same underlying variable. The affective dimension is sensitive to perceived threat, accumulated distress, health consequences, and attentional context in ways the sensory dimension is not.

## The REE mapping

SD-011 proposes z_harm_s (sensory-discriminative) and z_harm_a (affective-motivational) as two distinct latent streams. Price and Harkins 1987 provides a direct behavioural grounding for this split at the phenomenological level. The VAS sensory measure operationalises what z_harm_s tracks: stimulus intensity as a momentary property, relatively directly tied to the nociceptive signal and thus forward-predictable from action (move away from heat, intensity drops). The VAS affective measure operationalises what z_harm_a tracks: accumulated distress, threat appraisal, health-consequence weighting -- the homeostatic deviation that SD-011 assigns to the slower, C-fiber-mediated stream.

What is especially interesting for REE is the cancer/chronic pain finding: when the body's homeostatic integrity is under sustained threat (cancer, chronic condition), z_harm_a becomes chronically elevated relative to z_harm_s. This is exactly the "accumulated homeostatic deviation" pattern SD-011 predicts for z_harm_a -- it does not reset rapidly when the proximal stimulus is removed, because the underlying homeostatic state remains disturbed.

## Limitations

The study uses self-report VAS measures, which creates shared method variance and limits mechanistic inference. The paper cannot tell us whether the two ratings reflect two neural streams or a single underlying signal that is contextually reweighted. There is also considerable sample heterogeneity in the chronic pain group (lumbar, causalgia, myofascial, etc.). The attentional manipulation in labour pain is a potential confound: focus on birth may also reduce the perceived intensity through distraction, though the authors argue against this given the selectivity of the effect on the affective scale.

## Confidence reasoning

This is a well-designed foundational study with good across-group replication and an within-subject manipulation. The mapping to SD-011 is conceptually tight at the phenomenological level. The main limitation is that it cannot distinguish between two separate neural streams (the SD-011 claim) and a single stream with context-dependent affective weighting. Confidence 0.82 -- strong behavioural evidence for the separability, with honest uncertainty about the neural architecture.
