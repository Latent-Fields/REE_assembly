# Apkarian, Hashmi & Baliki (2011) — Pain and the brain: specificity and plasticity in chronic pain

## What the paper does

Apkarian and colleagues review roughly a decade of human neuroimaging work on chronic pain, focusing on how the brain in a patient with sustained noxious input differs from the brain in an acute laboratory nociception paradigm. They compare chronic back pain, complex regional pain syndrome, osteoarthritis, and fibromyalgia cohorts against healthy controls across fMRI BOLD, resting-state connectivity, voxel-based morphometry, and diffusion tensor imaging. Their central claim is that chronic pain is not the prolongation of acute pain — it is a partially distinct network phenomenon, with reorganization that is best modelled as a learning process rather than a persistent sensory state.

## Key findings relevant to SD-011 context-stability

The relevant question for SD-011 is not "does dual-stream architecture exist?" — that is settled by Rainville 1997, Craig 2002/2003, and the anatomical literature already in the folder. The question is whether the dissociation survives sustained context change. Apkarian's answer is subtle:

1. **The anatomical substrates persist.** A-delta/S1 and C-fiber/medial-thalamic/ACC-insula projections remain structurally intact in chronic pain patients. There is no evidence that the streams merge.
2. **Functional engagement drifts.** In chronic pain, S1 (the z_harm_s analogue) is less engaged by equivalent nociceptive input than in healthy controls. The affective/medial stream (z_harm_a analogue) expands its downstream consumers to include mPFC, NAc, and amygdala — mesolimbic learning circuits that are not central to acute pain.
3. **The transition is learning-mediated.** The authors explicitly frame subacute-to-chronic transition as reinforcement-driven: the nociceptive signal becomes bound to predictive states in mesolimbic circuitry. This is an important REE-relevant hypothesis: the dual-stream weights are not fixed but drift with experience.

## How this translates to REE

SD-011 as written treats z_harm_s and z_harm_a as structurally distinct with distinct downstream consumers (z_harm_s feeds E2_harm_s forward model for SD-003 counterfactual attribution; z_harm_a feeds E3 as motivational/urgency signal gating ARC-016). Apkarian et al. supports this dual-stream architecture as robust to the most extreme sustained-context perturbation we can observe in humans (months-to-years of chronic pain) — the streams do not collapse into one. That is the strongest form of context-stability evidence the literature can provide.

But the review also warns that the functional weighting is plastic. z_harm_a's downstream coupling expands under sustained context. For REE this predicts that ARC-016 harm-variance gating should not be hard-wired to a single consumer — under sustained exposure, z_harm_a should be consumable by additional downstream circuits (in V3 terms: mesolimbic-analogue modules that were not originally wired to it). This is a design nudge rather than a contradiction.

## Limitations and caveats

The review concerns clinical chronic pain with timescales of months to years, whereas V3 experiments operate on episode-level timescales of seconds to minutes. The existence proof for architectural robustness is therefore extreme-case — strong evidence that the architecture does not collapse, weaker evidence about short-timescale drift. The framing is also pathological: chronic pain is a failure mode, and it is not settled whether chronic-pain reorganization reflects the normal operation of the system under sustained context or a distinct clinical failure that SD-011 should explicitly not instantiate. I lean toward reading it as "the architecture is stable but the weighting drifts," which is a calibration signal for V3 rather than a validation or refutation.

## Confidence reasoning

I put this at 0.72 — moderate-to-strong support for context-stability of the dual-stream architecture per se, with an honest discount for the timescale mismatch and the pathology framing. The paper is the best available direct test of whether dual-stream nociception survives sustained context; the answer is "yes, structurally, but the downstream integration reorganizes." Both halves of that finding are informative for SD-011.
