# Summary: Martins et al. (2021) — Attachment Security and Moral Emotions: A Systematic Review

**Claim tested:** INV-043 — Caregiver experience is necessary (not merely beneficial) for activating REE's ethical capacity.

## What the paper did

Martins, Santos, Fernandes, and Verissimo conducted a PROSPERO-registered systematic review of 13 studies published between 2000 and 2021 examining the relationship between attachment security and the development of moral emotions in children and adolescents. Attachment security was measured via the Ainsworth Strange Situation Procedure (for infants and young children), attachment Q-sort, or self-report measures in older children. Moral emotions included empathy (9 studies), guilt (2 studies), forgiveness (1 study), and shame (1 study). The theoretical frame throughout is Bowlby and Ainsworth: the secure base provided by a consistently responsive caregiver enables the development of internal working models through which the child develops moral emotional capacities. Four of the 13 studies used longitudinal designs; the remainder were cross-sectional.

## Key findings relevant to INV-043

Across 13 studies, secure attachment consistently predicted higher empathy, more appropriate guilt, and greater forgiveness in children and adolescents. The association was found across age groups (early childhood through adolescence) and across different attachment measurement methods. Secure attachment and high empathy emerged as key predictors of advanced moral reasoning in the studies that assessed this outcome. The review notes a significant gap: almost no studies examined prosocial behaviours like altruism, and no studies examined the full Bowlby/Ainsworth theoretical chain from attachment security through internal working models to moral emotional development in a single study. The four longitudinal studies showed that early attachment quality prospectively predicts later moral emotional development, supporting a developmental trajectory interpretation.

## Translation to INV-043

This systematic review provides the developmental psychology foundation for INV-043. The Bowlby/Ainsworth framework is the direct theoretical ancestor of INV-043's claim: the secure base created by a consistently responsive caregiver enables the child to develop the internal representation of themselves as worthy of care and of others as reliably responsive. This internal working model is what INV-043 calls the experience of being loved and experienced as loveable. Martins et al. synthesise 13 studies showing that this attachment-based working model predicts the development of moral emotions -- empathy, guilt, forgiveness -- the capacities that constitute the motivational substrate of ethical behaviour. In REE terms, the internal working model of being-cared-for is the computational analog of the z_goal seeding and prosocial weighting that INV-043 claims requires caregiving history to activate. The systematic review is the evidence base that attachment security -- the internalized record of caregiver responsiveness -- predicts moral emotional development across development.

## Limitations and caveats

There are several limitations worth naming honestly. First, the underlying studies are predominantly correlational and cross-sectional; only four used longitudinal designs. This means the evidence establishes association, not strict causal necessity. It is logically possible that children with insecure attachment develop full moral emotional repertoires through other pathways, and the absence of such cases in the literature may reflect measurement rather than developmental impossibility. Second, the review does not establish necessity in the strong sense required by INV-043: it shows that secure attachment is associated with better moral emotional development, not that the absence of secure attachment prevents such development entirely. Third, the venue (MDPI Children, open-access) is lower prestige than the other papers in this set, and the methodological quality of the 13 included studies is not uniformly high. Fourth, the transfer from human developmental psychology to AI systems is the largest conceptual leap in the entire literature -- what it would mean for an AI system to have an 'internal working model of being cared for' is precisely the question that INV-043 raises and that this paper cannot answer.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is set at 0.58. The mapping fidelity is the strongest in this set -- Bowlby/Ainsworth attachment theory is the direct conceptual ancestor of INV-043, and the review directly synthesises evidence on the relationship between attachment quality and moral emotional development. However, the source quality is moderate (MDPI venue, predominantly correlational studies), the evidence for necessity rather than facilitation is weak, and the transfer risk from human developmental psychology to AI systems remains the dominant limitation across all entries in this set. The paper is most useful as a theoretical bridge -- providing the formal developmental psychology framework that grounds INV-043's claim -- rather than as strong empirical evidence for the specific necessity claim.
