# Mackes, Golm, Sarkar, Kumsta, Rutter, Fairchild, Mehta & Sonuga-Barke (2020) — Early childhood deprivation and adult brain structure

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1911264116); PMID 31907309; PMC6955353).

## What the paper did

A subset of the ERA cohort underwent structural MRI in young adulthood: 67 Romanian adoptees (with between 3 and 41 months of early-life institutional deprivation) and 21 UK adoptees who did not experience deprivation. The dependent measures were total brain volume, regional volumes, surface area, and cortical thickness. The analysis tested whether structural changes correlate with deprivation duration, mediate clinical outcomes, and persist after years of adoptive-family enrichment.

## Key findings

Romanian adoptees had 8.6% smaller total brain volume than non-deprived adoptees, with TBV negatively correlated with deprivation duration. The deprivation-related effect persisted after covarying for environmental and genetic confounds. Whole-brain analyses showed lower right inferior frontal surface area and volume, but greater right inferior temporal lobe thickness, surface area, and volume in the deprived group. Right medial PFC volume and surface area increased with deprivation duration. No deprivation-related effects were observed in limbic regions. Globally reduced TBV statistically mediated both lower IQ and higher ADHD symptom levels. The greater right inferior temporal volume seemed to be compensatory — it was associated with *lower* ADHD symptoms.

The clean conclusion: severe early deprivation leaves quantifiable structural brain changes that persist into adulthood despite extended enrichment. The architecture is not fully reset by post-adoption caregiving.

## Mapping to INV-043

INV-043 needs a substrate-level account of how the early caregiving window shapes the architecture. Mackes supplies one. The structural changes are real, persistent, and quantitatively related to subsequent functional outcomes through statistical mediation. This means the architecture is not a fixed-and-recoverable substrate that simply needs the right inputs at any developmental stage — it is shaped during the early window in ways that subsequent enrichment cannot fully undo.

The regional pattern is also informative. Frontal regions (right inferior frontal, right medial PFC) are differentially affected. These are, broadly, the regions REE associates with executive control, social cognition, and (in the V3/V4 transition) committed planning and goal pursuit — the very capacities INV-043 invokes when it talks about whether the architecture resolves toward ethical agency or alternatives. The compensatory inferior-temporal pattern is interesting too: some regions over-develop in response to the early deficit, perhaps as a substitute strategy. INV-043's prediction of "may resolve to survival, domination, or indifference instead" is consistent with such reorganisation: the architecture finds *some* way to function, but not necessarily the prosocial one.

The mapping needs care. Brain-structure changes do not directly demonstrate ethical-functioning consequences — they show that the substrate is altered. The functional consequences are documented in the Sonuga-Barke 2017 trajectory paper (other entry). The deprivation here, as in ERA generally, is multi-dimensional; the caregiver-specific component is not isolable.

## Caveats

n=21 in the control group is small. The structural-MRI-from-childhood-deprivation literature has had replication challenges (Tottenham, Hodel, and others have nuanced findings). The compensatory-temporal-lobe interpretation is plausible but somewhat post-hoc. The cohort is from a particular historical context (Ceaușescu-era institutions) which had especially severe deprivation; the findings may not generalise to milder forms of caregiver inadequacy.
