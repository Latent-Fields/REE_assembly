# Sonuga-Barke, Kennedy, Kumsta, Knights, Golm, Rutter, Maughan, Schlotz & Kreppner (2017) — Child-to-adult trajectories after early life deprivation: ERA young-adult follow-up

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(17)30045-4); PMID 28237264).

## What the paper did

The English and Romanian Adoptees (ERA) study is the longest-running longitudinal natural experiment on early institutional deprivation: children adopted from Ceaușescu-era Romanian orphanages into UK families in the early 1990s, followed across childhood through young adulthood (22–25 years at this assessment). The 2017 paper reports the young-adult wave. Romanian adoptees were stratified by exposure: less than 6 months of institutional deprivation (n=67 at age 6; n=50 at young adulthood) versus more than 6 months (n=98 at age 6; n=72 at young adulthood). UK adoptees who did not experience deprivation served as controls.

## Key findings

The <6mo deprivation group looks like UK controls across nearly all outcomes at every age. The >6mo group shows persistent elevation in autism spectrum disorder symptoms, disinhibited social engagement, inattention/hyperactivity through young adulthood. Cognitive impairment (IQ < 80) was high at age 6 and 11 but had remitted to control levels by young adulthood — the IQ recovery is part of the architectural-capacity-intact reading. By contrast, self-rated emotional symptoms had a *late-onset* pattern: minimal differences at 11 and 15, then marked increases by young adulthood. Educational attainment, employment, and mental health service use were all worse for the high-deprivation group. About one-fifth of the >6mo group (n=15) were problem-free at all assessments — heterogeneity, not refutation.

The headline conclusion is that extended early deprivation leaves long-term deleterious effects that years of nurturance and support in adoptive families do not erase.

## Mapping to INV-043

INV-043 says that REE architecture is necessary but not sufficient for ethical development; without a developmental phase of received caregiving, the architectural capacity may resolve to survival, domination, or indifference rather than to ethical agency. The ERA cohort is the cleanest available human signature of that prediction. These children received normal-or-better post-adoption caregiving for the next 15+ years. Their architecture was intact (IQ recovers to normal). Yet the early deprivation effects persist — and the late-onset emotional pattern is exactly the kind of resolution-failure INV-043 predicts: the architecture works, but it has been shaped during a critical window into something that does not subsequently re-organise toward typical social-emotional competence.

The mapping needs care on two fronts. First, the outcomes ERA measures are clinical (ASD, ADHD, depression, disinhibited social engagement) rather than directly "ethical" in the philosophical sense INV-043 invokes. The broader ERA literature (and adjacent attachment-disorder, callous-unemotional-traits work) does extend to more ethics-relevant outcomes, but this paper does not centre them. Second, ERA-style deprivation is not just caregiving deprivation — it included severe nutritional, sensory, and stimulation deprivation. The caregiver-specific component cannot be cleanly isolated from this design.

The 20% problem-free subset is itself important for INV-043. It implies the failure is probabilistic, not deterministic. INV-043's "may resolve to survival, domination, or indifference instead" already commits to probability-not-certainty; ERA's heterogeneity is consistent with that commitment. The interesting follow-up question is what predicts which children fall into the problem-free 20% — adoptive-family quality, age-at-adoption interactions, genetic moderators (Kumsta and others have looked at all of these), and these moderators are themselves potential constraints on INV-043's mechanism.

## Caveats

The 22–25 follow-up is still relatively young; later moral-functioning outcomes are not yet fully measurable. The deprivation is multidimensional. Self-report versus clinician-administered measures show some divergence. The cohort is geographically and historically specific.

## Confidence reasoning

I land at 0.84. Source quality is near-ceiling — Lancet, large retained sample across 19+ years, natural-experiment design that no ethical experiment could replicate. Transfer risk is low (this is human longitudinal data of the right vintage). Mapping fidelity drops because outcomes are clinical rather than directly ethical, and the caregiver-specific component is not isolable. Even with these caveats, this is the strongest empirical anchor for INV-043's necessity claim that I am aware of.
