# Heusser, Ezzyat, Shiff & Davachi 2018 -- Perceptual boundaries cause mnemonic trade-offs

According to PubMed, this paper provides behavioural evidence that perceptual event boundaries produce a specific signature trade-off in episodic memory. [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1037/xlm0000503)

## What the paper does

Three behavioural experiments inserting context shifts ("event boundaries") between trains of stimuli, then testing item-context binding, temporal order memory, and free-recall structure. The authors asked: what is the specific shape of the boundary effect on memory?

## Findings relevant to MECH-288

Boundary items showed strengthened item-context binding -- the boundary anchored the item to its context. AND temporal order memory was reduced for items that flanked the boundary. AND event organisation imprinted structure on free recall. This is the testable signature of MECH-288 substrate behaviour: if MECH-288 boundaries are real and downstream-consumed, they should produce this specific trade-off pattern.

## Mapping to REE

This paper provides the falsifiability target. MECH-288 boundary detection has a specific behavioural signature in REE: items at boundaries should get strengthened anchor-binding (downstream MECH-269 anchor-set consequence) AND items across boundaries should have weakened temporal-order representation. A V3 experiment can run REE through a sequence with controlled boundaries (introduced by latent shifts) and check both: (a) does anchor-set show enhanced binding at boundary segments, and (b) does temporal-order representation degrade across boundaries?

## Limitations and caveats

Heusser used perceptual category shifts as boundary inducers. In REE the boundaries arise from latent change-points. Whether substrate latent shifts produce the same downstream trade-off is exactly what V3 experimentation needs to test -- but the directional prediction (yes, they should) is anchored by Heusser.
