# Lucey et al. 2018 -- Sleep deprivation increases CSF Abeta via elevated production

**Claims:** MECH-168, MECH-171
**Direction:** supports (0.80)
**Source:** Annals of Neurology, DOI: 10.1002/ana.25117, PMID: 29220873

## What the paper shows

Based on articles retrieved from PubMed: Lucey et al. used stable-isotope (13C-leucine) kinetic labelling with serial lumbar CSF sampling across three conditions: normal sleep, total sleep deprivation, and sodium oxybate (SWS enhancer). Sleep deprivation increased overnight CSF Abeta38, Abeta40, and Abeta42 by 25-30%. The mechanism was elevated Abeta production during the deprivation period -- not reduced clearance. Sodium oxybate suppressed the increase, consistent with SWS as the active phase for controlling Abeta production.

## REE relevance

MECH-168 predicts that chronically disrupted sleep accelerates the pathological substrate damage that eventually degrades the offline attribution pipeline. This paper establishes the direct mechanism: each night of sleep deprivation produces a significant acute amyloid production spike. Over years, this accumulation damages glymphatic and synaptic infrastructure that the pipeline depends on.

MECH-171 (vicious cycle) requires that AD pathology disrupts sleep, which in turn worsens pathology. This paper establishes the sleep-disruption-to-amyloid-accumulation arm of that cycle -- the most mechanistically detailed demonstration available.

## Limitations for REE

The paper measures amyloid production kinetics in healthy adults, not attribution pipeline function in AD patients. The connection to pipeline disruption requires MECH-169 (glymphatic substrate damage) as an intermediate. The attribution-specific symptom profile (episodic before semantic, procedural sparing) cannot be addressed by this design.

## Key finding for evidence direction

The ~25-30% overnight Abeta increase from a single night of total sleep deprivation, with the mechanism being elevated production during wakefulness rather than reduced clearance, is the strongest mechanistic evidence that disrupted sleep acutely worsens the amyloid component of substrate damage. The sodium oxybate reversal confirms SWS as the relevant phase.
