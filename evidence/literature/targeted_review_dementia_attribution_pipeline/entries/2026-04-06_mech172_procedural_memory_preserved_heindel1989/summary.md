# Heindel et al. 1989 -- Motor skill learning preserved in AD; double dissociation with HD

**Claims:** MECH-172
**Direction:** supports (0.83)
**Source:** Journal of Neuroscience, DOI: 10.1523/JNEUROSCI.09-02-00582.1989, PMID: 2521896

## What the paper shows

Based on articles retrieved from PubMed: Heindel et al. (San Diego VA, Butters lab) compared patients with dementia of the Alzheimer type (DAT), Huntington's disease (HD), and demented/nondemented Parkinson's disease (PD) on two implicit memory tasks:

1. **Pursuit-rotor motor learning** (procedural/motor skill)
2. **Lexical priming** (semantic/neocortical implicit)

Results:
- **DAT patients:** Normal pursuit-rotor learning AND normal weight-biasing task performance. Impaired on lexical priming and severely impaired on explicit memory.
- **HD patients:** Impaired on pursuit-rotor and weight-biasing. Relatively preserved on lexical priming.
- This is a **double dissociation**: AD damages declarative and some implicit systems but spares motor skill learning; HD does the reverse.

The authors conclude that motor skill learning is mediated by a corticostriatal system, while lexical priming depends on neocortical association areas -- and both are distinct from the hippocampal declarative memory system.

## REE relevance

**MECH-172** predicts that habit/procedural memory is architecturally spared in early AD because it depends on a model-free corticostriatal habit system that does not require offline consolidation phases. The offline NREM/REM consolidation pipeline is specifically required for contextual, episodic, and goal-directed memory -- not for motor skill learning that is encoded directly through repetition in corticostriatal circuits.

This paper is the foundational evidence for that claim. The double dissociation is essential: it rules out explanations like "AD patients are just less impaired generally" by showing that the specific tasks preserved in AD are precisely the tasks that HD patients (with striatal pathology) cannot perform. The cross-over establishes that distinct neural systems are involved.

## Limitations for REE

Does not directly test whether the preservation is because no offline consolidation is needed (the MECH-172 mechanistic claim) vs. anatomical sparing. The study predates modern AD biomarker confirmation and uses older diagnostic criteria. Sample sizes are modest by current standards. The paper shows lexical priming is also impaired in DAT -- so the sparing is specific to motor procedural tasks, not all implicit memory.

## Key finding for evidence direction

The double dissociation -- DAT spares pursuit-rotor and weight-biasing while HD impairs them; HD spares lexical priming while DAT impairs it -- is the cleanest available neuropsychological evidence for the architectural separation of the model-free habit system (corticostriatal, no offline consolidation required) from the hippocampal contextual attribution system (requires NREM/REM pipeline). This is exactly what MECH-172 predicts.
