# Xie et al. 2013 -- Sleep drives metabolite clearance from the adult brain

**Claims:** MECH-169
**Direction:** supports (0.82)
**Source:** Science, DOI: 10.1126/science.1241224, PMID: 24136970

## What the paper shows

Based on articles retrieved from PubMed: Xie et al. (Nedergaard lab) used real-time tetramethylammonium diffusion coefficient measurements and two-photon imaging in live mice to show that natural sleep or anaesthesia is associated with a 60% expansion of the brain's interstitial space. This expansion dramatically increases convective exchange between CSF and interstitial fluid via the perivascular glymphatic system. The rate of Abeta clearance during sleep was approximately double that during wakefulness. The glymphatic system is thus largely inactive during wakefulness and activated during sleep.

## REE relevance

MECH-169 states that the glymphatic clearance account and the attribution pipeline account are complementary explanations of AD, not competing ones. The glymphatic account explains the substrate damage component: chronic sleep insufficiency fails to adequately clear amyloid (and tau) metabolites, leading to their accumulation and eventual neurodegeneration. This is the pathological substrate on which attribution pipeline failure develops.

The attribution pipeline account then explains the symptom profile shape: why episodic memory fails before semantic memory, why procedural memory is spared, why the decline mirrors the reverse of the sleep phase dependency ordering (INV-047). Diffuse amyloid deposition alone cannot explain this specific staging -- but diffuse substrate damage + pipeline-stage-selective vulnerability can.

This paper is the canonical evidence for the glymphatic side of MECH-169.

## Limitations for REE

Mouse model -- the 60% interstitial space expansion has not been demonstrated at equivalent magnitude in humans, though human glymphatic tracer studies are consistent with the mechanism. Does not address which sleep phase drives clearance or the phase-specificity of pipeline damage.

## Key finding for evidence direction

The 60% interstitial space expansion during sleep with doubled Abeta clearance rate establishes that sleep is not merely correlated with reduced AD risk -- it is the active clearance period for neurotoxic metabolites. This makes the complementarity argument in MECH-169 concrete: loss of sleep = loss of clearance = substrate damage; but the symptom profile shape requires the attribution pipeline framing on top.
