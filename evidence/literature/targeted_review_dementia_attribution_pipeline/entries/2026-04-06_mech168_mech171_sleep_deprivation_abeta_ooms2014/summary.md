# Ooms et al. 2014 -- One night sleep deprivation prevents overnight CSF Abeta42 decrease

**Claims:** MECH-168, MECH-171
**Direction:** supports (0.78)
**Source:** JAMA Neurology, DOI: 10.1001/jamaneurol.2014.1173, PMID: 24887018

## What the paper shows

Based on articles retrieved from PubMed (AWAKE RCT): 26 cognitively normal middle-aged men were randomised to normal sleep (n=13) or total sleep deprivation (n=13). PSG was monitored continuously. Serial CSF was sampled via intrathecal catheter from 3pm to 10am. Normal sleep produced a 6% overnight decrease in CSF Abeta42 (25.3 pg/mL reduction). Sleep deprivation counteracted this, with a between-group difference of 75.8 pg/mL by morning. Abeta40, tau, and total protein were unchanged, suggesting specificity for Abeta42 processing.

## REE relevance

MECH-168 requires that sleep disruption accelerates amyloid accumulation, establishing the substrate damage that eventually degrades offline pipeline infrastructure. The physiological overnight Abeta42 decrease that occurs during normal sleep -- and is abolished by sleep deprivation -- is the most parsimonious demonstration of this mechanism in humans.

MECH-171 (vicious cycle) is supported by establishing the causal direction: sleep deprivation -> increased amyloid burden. This is the upstream arm of the cycle. The JAMA Neurology publication gives this the highest public and clinical profile of the sleep/Abeta series.

## Limitations for REE

Single night; all-male sample; Abeta42 only (not tau). Does not test attribution pipeline function. The 6% overnight decrease in normal sleepers is modest in absolute terms; the long-term accumulation hypothesis requires extrapolation.

## Key finding for evidence direction

The normal overnight Abeta42 decrease (6%) that sleep enables, and its abolition by total sleep deprivation, establishes that sleep-mediated clearance is a clinically measurable, pharmacologically addressable process -- making MECH-171's vicious cycle mechanism experimentally tractable.
