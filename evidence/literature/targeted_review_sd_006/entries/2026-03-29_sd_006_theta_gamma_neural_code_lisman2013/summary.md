# The Theta-Gamma Neural Code

**Lisman & Jensen (2013) -- Neuron**

## What the paper did

This review synthesises two decades of empirical and theoretical work on the theta-gamma neural code, beginning with the 1995 Lisman and Idiart model. The core proposal is that theta (~5-8 Hz, hippocampus) and gamma (~30-80 Hz) oscillations are not coincidental co-occurring rhythms but form a hierarchically nested coding scheme: within each theta cycle, roughly 5 to 9 gamma subcycles can occur, and separate informational items are represented in separate gamma subcycles. This nesting provides a mechanism for holding multiple items in working memory in an ordered temporal sequence (explaining Miller's 7 +/- 2 capacity limit) and for coordinating communication between brain regions that send and receive at characteristic rates. The review surveys electrophysiological evidence from rodent hippocampus, human intracranial recordings, and fMRI, showing that theta-gamma coupling correlates with working memory load, predicts memory performance, and is disrupted in conditions like schizophrenia. Evidence from phase precession (sequential place cell firing ordered within the theta cycle) demonstrates that theta-rate cycles organise the replay of sequential information, while gamma-rate cycles carry each individual item.

## Key findings relevant to SD-006

The paper establishes two claims that directly support SD-006. First, distinct characteristic rates are biologically real and functionally necessary -- the brain does not use a single uniform clock but instead runs different computational processes at different rates, and these rates are not arbitrary but matched to the timescale of the information being processed. Second, theta-rate (~4-8 Hz, 125-200 ms cycles) is specifically associated with deliberative planning, trajectory replay, and working memory operations, while gamma-rate (~30-80 Hz, 12-30 ms cycles) is associated with fast sensory-motor processing within each theta cycle. SD-006 maps E3 to the theta-rate and E2 to a gamma-analogous rate precisely because this matches their functional roles: E3 (hippocampal planning, trajectory evaluation, commitment gating) is the deliberative deliberative stage, E2 (fast transition model for immediate action-sensory loops) is the fast motor loop.

The paper also establishes that lockstep processing is not how the brain handles multi-rate computation -- different areas send and receive at their characteristic rates, and inter-area communication is organised by the oscillatory timing rather than by synchronous clocking. This directly supports SD-006's design decision to use async multi-rate execution rather than forcing E1, E2, and E3 to run at the same frequency.

## Mapping to REE

SD-006's rate assignments map onto the theta-gamma hierarchy as follows. E2 (fast transition model, ~10 Hz analogue in current implementation) corresponds to the gamma rate: it handles fast action-sensory cycles within the slower deliberative loop, exactly as gamma subcycles carry individual action-items within each theta cycle. E3 (~4-8 Hz in SD-006's design, corresponding to ~1/9 the step rate in EXQ-052b results) corresponds to the theta rate: it runs the slow deliberative cycle within which E2 completes multiple fast evaluations. E1 (LSTM, slow consolidation) runs at still-slower rates, analogous to the even-slower replay and consolidation processes that occur during sharp-wave ripples (slower than theta). This three-level hierarchy -- consolidation / deliberative-theta / fast-gamma -- matches REE's intended design and has direct precedent in the nested oscillation framework.

## Limitations and caveats

The theta-gamma code is primarily a theory of how information is organised within working memory (item capacity, sequential replay), not a theory of multi-module processing rate assignment. SD-006 borrows the rate hierarchy without necessarily implementing the full theta-gamma coding scheme (e.g., REE does not currently encode multiple gamma-subcycle items within each theta cycle in the sense Lisman and Jensen describe). The mapping from biological Hz to simulation timestep rates requires calibration that the paper does not provide. There is also a regional specificity concern: the paper focuses on hippocampus and medial temporal lobe, but SD-006's E3 is a planning circuit more homologous to prefrontal cortex, which may have different characteristic oscillatory properties. These are real caveats but they do not undermine the primary claim -- that characteristic rates rather than lockstep processing is the biologically appropriate design -- which is robustly supported.

## Confidence reasoning

Neuron review by the originators of the theta-gamma code, synthesising strong empirical evidence from multiple species and paradigms. The rate hierarchy claim is well-supported and directly relevant to SD-006's design rationale. Confidence is set at 0.80 -- high, reflecting the quality and relevance of the evidence, but not maximum, reflecting the conceptual gap between the paper's account of item storage within oscillatory cycles and SD-006's account of multi-module processing rate assignment.
