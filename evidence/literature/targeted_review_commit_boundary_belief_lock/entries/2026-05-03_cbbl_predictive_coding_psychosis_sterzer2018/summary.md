# Sterzer et al. 2018 - The Predictive Coding Account of Psychosis

[DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biopsych.2018.05.015) | PMID 30007575 | Biological Psychiatry, 2018 | According to PubMed.

## What the paper did

This is the canonical mid-decade review of predictive-coding accounts of psychosis. The author list is the field — Sterzer, Adams, Fletcher, Frith, Lawrie, Muckli, Petrovic, Uhlhaas, Voss, Corlett — covering the leading European and UK labs working on Bayesian models of delusions and hallucinations. The review summarises the canonical predictive-coding account (psychosis as decreased precision of prior beliefs relative to sensory data), then walks through the unresolved challenges it faces.

The challenges they raise are the relevant part for our purposes. Some studies implicate weakened priors in psychosis (the canonical account); others find stronger priors, especially for hallucinations. Hallucinations and delusions may relate to distinct alterations despite their co-occurrence. The pathophysiology may change over the course of illness. And — most directly relevant for the commit-boundary belief lock proposal — the canonical theories do not address active inference. The reviewers state this in plain terms: "canonical theories do not highlight active inference — the process through which the effects of our actions on our sensations are anticipated and minimized."

A second relevant challenge they raise is the emergence-vs-persistence asymmetry of delusions. The canonical account (weak priors) gives a story about how delusions emerge, but persistence requires the opposite — that the delusional belief have an excessive influence on the perception of new evidence. The reviewers cite ketamine experiments linking prediction-error signalling to memory reconsolidation as a candidate persistence mechanism, but acknowledge the canonical framework does not resolve the asymmetry on its own.

## Why this matters for the commit-boundary belief lock proposal

This is the orthogonality cross-check. The brief's pre-registration question for Stream 4 is whether the existing predictive-coding accounts already predict the action-history asymmetry the proposed mechanism targets — if they do, the proposed mechanism is just predictive-coding in different clothes and should be folded into existing claims rather than registered separately.

Sterzer et al. answer this question explicitly and against the canonical account. The action-loop is missing. The persistence mechanism for delusions is unsettled. Memory reconsolidation is mentioned as a candidate but not as a developed component of the framework. These are exactly the gaps the proposed commit-boundary belief lock mechanism targets. The orthogonality argument is supported.

What the review does NOT do is endorse the commit-boundary framing or anything resembling it. The authors propose three directions for filling the gaps they flag: (a) hierarchical predictive coding (the levels of the cortical hierarchy may have differently-mis-tuned precisions); (b) the social-prior account (perceptual content of psychosis is shaped by motivated social inference); (c) beliefs-as-utility (delusional beliefs persist because they provide identity, agency, advantage). These are the existing competition for filling the persistence gap.

So the proposed mechanism is genuinely orthogonal to the canonical account; the orthogonality argument passes. But it is not the only candidate for filling the gap, and any registration of the proposed mechanism needs to show explanatory work beyond what the alternatives can do — particularly the action-history asymmetry, which the alternatives do not specifically predict.

## Limits and caveats

This is a review, not original empirical data. Its strength is that it is the most authoritative survey of where the predictive-coding account stands, written by the people most invested in it. Its limit is that it tells us where the gaps are, not how to fill them.

The orthogonality argument it supports is asymmetric: the canonical predictive-coding account does not predict the action-history asymmetry, which means the commit-boundary mechanism is not a re-description of canonical predictive-coding. But the reverse asymmetry — does the commit-boundary mechanism explain anything that hierarchical predictive coding, social priors, or beliefs-as-utility cannot — is not addressed by this review and would need its own analytical work.

The single most pertinent quotation, which I will reproduce with care: "psychotic symptoms may provide a form of personal identity, and personal-level data may be assumed to be more reliable than those from others. Finally, beliefs have value in and of themselves. Psychotic symptoms may be seen as attempts to garner some advantage, perhaps by convincing others of their veracity. This renders them susceptible to the same biases and asymmetries in updating observed with nondelusional beliefs. These asymmetries can be explained with a Bayesian model, if we allow agents to derive utility from their beliefs."

The "asymmetries in updating observed with nondelusional beliefs" the authors gesture at here are the cognitive-dissonance / post-decisional consolidation effects that Stream 1 of this pull anchored on. So the same constellation of phenomena is being noted by both the predictive-coding and the cognitive-dissonance literatures, but they are not yet being unified at the architectural level. The commit-boundary mechanism is one candidate unification.
