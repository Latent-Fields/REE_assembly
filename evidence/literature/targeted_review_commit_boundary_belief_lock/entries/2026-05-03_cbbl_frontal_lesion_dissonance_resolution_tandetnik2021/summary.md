# Tandetnik et al. 2021 - Cognitive dissonance resolution depends on executive functions and frontal lobe integrity

[DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cortex.2021.02.018) | PMID 33799054 | Cortex, 2021 | According to PubMed.

## What the paper did

Naccache's group at the Pitié-Salpêtrière in Paris recruited patients with focal frontal-lobe lesions and classified them, on standard neuropsychological assessment, as either dysexecutive (DYS) or non-dysexecutive (NODYS). They then ran the classic free-choice paradigm — preference rating, choice between equally valued items, re-rating — on both lesion groups and on matched healthy controls. They added a memory probe to verify which choices each participant could subsequently recall.

The result is a clean dissociation. Non-dysexecutive lesion patients showed choice-induced preference change indistinguishable from controls. Dysexecutive lesion patients failed to show CIPC — even on the choices they could remember. The dissociation rules out memory failure as the explanation for the dysexecutive group's flat result. Whatever is missing in the DYS group is not the record of having committed; it is the machinery that detects and resolves the conflict between the just-made commit and the prior preference structure.

## Why this matters for the commit-boundary belief lock proposal

This pairs with Voigt 2018 to give the proposal its two-substrate skeleton. Voigt's study isolated the hippocampal commit-record substrate — preference shifts depend on remembering having made the choice. Tandetnik's study isolates the frontal/executive substrate — even with intact memory of the choice, preference shifts depend on functioning frontal-executive machinery.

The commit-boundary belief lock mechanism, as the brief sketches it, requires both. The agent must have the record of having committed (hippocampus, INV-021 territory) AND the executive machinery that converts the conflict between commit and prior belief into a durable representational rearrangement (frontal lobe / anterior cingulate, MECH-061 territory). Lose either substrate and the lock dissolves.

This is also where the dose-response framing of the proposed two-mechanism claim gets its empirical traction. The brief sketched an attribution-rigidity setpoint as the dial that distinguishes confabulation territory (too low) from delusion territory (too high). Tandetnik's dissociation suggests this dial is not a single parameter but at least two — commit-record strength and executive-conflict-resolution gain. Both can be set independently, and pathological lock requires both to be high. Pathological permeability (confabulation) requires commit-record weakness; pathological lock (delusion) requires both commit-record strength and executive gain.

This is more architectural detail than the brief originally proposed. If the claim is registered, the two-substrate distinction should be in the registration, with each substrate carrying its own pathological signature.

## Limits and caveats

Lesion neuropsychology is intrinsically correlational at the substrate level. We observe that dysexecutive patients fail; we do not directly manipulate the executive substrate in healthy controls. The patient sample is small (Tandetnik et al. report results on a modest number of patients per group, as is typical and unavoidable in this methodology). Statistical inference from small lesion samples requires care.

The transfer from low-stakes preference-change tasks to high-stakes delusional belief revision is the same one inferential step that limits Voigt 2018 and Izuma 2010. The architectural mapping is plausible — the same value-update / conflict-resolution circuitry plausibly handles propositional beliefs — but it is not directly tested.

The paper also does not engage with the clinical psychosis literature. It is a basic-science study of cognitive dissonance in a frontal-lesion population. The proposal that the same dissociation explains insight resistance in delusion is the brief's extrapolation, not Tandetnik's claim.

Confidence at 0.82. Source quality is good (Cortex; well-designed lesion-control comparison with neuropsychological characterisation; the Naccache lab is established in this methodology). Mapping fidelity is high because the dissociation directly maps onto the two-substrate architecture the brief proposes. Transfer risk is moderate because of the consumer-preference vs clinical-belief gap.
