# Jenkinson & Brown 2011 -- Beta Oscillations, Dopamine, and Action Threshold: MECH-106 Mapping

**Source:** Jenkinson, N., & Brown, P. (2011). New insights into the relationship between dopamine, beta oscillations and motor function. *Trends in Neurosciences*, 34(12), 611-618. DOI: [10.1016/j.tins.2011.09.003](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tins.2011.09.003)

---

## What the Paper Does

Jenkinson and Brown synthesise a growing literature on beta-frequency (15-30 Hz) oscillations in the motor cortex and basal ganglia, proposing a specific functional account that goes beyond the traditional view of beta as simply an antipodal marker of movement execution. Their hypothesis: beta amplitude provides a running estimate of how likely a new voluntary action will be needed. When action is unlikely or unwarranted, beta is high -- it acts as a brake. When action is imminent and appropriate, beta is suppressed, releasing the brake. The crucial additional claim is that this beta tone is actively modulated by net dopamine levels: higher striatal dopamine suppresses beta; lower dopamine permits or augments beta. The link to Parkinson's disease is direct -- DA depletion elevates resting beta, making it harder to suppress the motor brake and initiate movement.

The paper is an opinion piece rather than a primary empirical study, but it synthesises a coherent body of EEG, LFP, and pharmacological data to argue for the predictive, dopamine-modulated character of beta gating.

## Key Findings

The central proposal relevant to MECH-106 is that dopamine does not just modulate action learning (as in Frank 2004, 2005) but also gates ongoing action readiness through beta dynamics. This adds a second timescale to dopamine's influence on action commitment. The Frank papers address phasic DA effects on between-trial threshold changes (learning). Jenkinson and Brown address tonic/sustained DA effects on the within-episode threshold state (how easy it is to initiate action right now). For MECH-106's commitment hysteresis to work -- the threshold staying low for an extended period after a positive outcome, or high for an extended period after a negative one -- there needs to be a mechanism that translates the phasic DA event into a sustained threshold change. Beta dynamics regulated by net DA tone are a candidate mechanism for this persistence.

A key secondary point is that the modulation is described as predictive: beta changes prospectively, based on the current DA state as an index of context-appropriate action readiness. This fits MECH-106's account that commitment threshold is not just reactive (responding to outcomes as they occur) but has a forward-looking character -- it is modulated to make the appropriate commitment posture more likely given the recent history of outcomes.

## REE Mapping to MECH-106

MECH-106 needs more than just a learning asymmetry (Frank 2004) and a cellular mechanism (Frank 2005) -- it needs a physiological bridge explaining how phasic DA events translate into sustained threshold changes that persist across multiple steps within an episode. Jenkinson and Brown's beta account provides this bridge. The sequence would be: positive outcome -> phasic DA burst -> sustained net DA elevation -> beta suppression -> commit threshold lowered for subsequent steps. Negative outcome -> phasic DA dip -> sustained net DA reduction -> beta elevation -> commit threshold raised (and de-commitment threshold lowered). This is the hysteresis: not a single-step recalibration, but a sustained posture that makes commitment or de-commitment easier for some period after the outcome.

In REE terms, MECH-090 (beta oscillations gate E3->action propagation) and MECH-106 (asymmetric commit threshold) are mechanistically connected through exactly this logic. MECH-090 describes the gate; MECH-106 describes how outcome valence modulates that gate. Jenkinson and Brown's paper is the bridging source.

## Limitations and Confidence Reasoning

The two main limitations are significant. First, this is a theoretical review with no new data -- the mechanism is assembled from others' experiments, not tested directly in a unified paradigm. Second, Jenkinson and Brown's account of net DA modulating beta tone is about tonic DA effects measured in Parkinson's and pharmacological studies; they do not specifically address phasic DA bursts/dips following outcome feedback as the driver. The causal chain from outcome valence through phasic DA to net DA change to beta tone change is implied but not directly established. There is also a scale mismatch: beta dynamics as measured in the cited literature operate over seconds, and it is not clear how long the beta suppression following a positive outcome persists in a healthy system.

These are genuine limitations, not quibbles. The paper contributes an important mechanistic link in the MECH-106 chain, but that link is assembled rather than demonstrated. Confidence: 0.70.
