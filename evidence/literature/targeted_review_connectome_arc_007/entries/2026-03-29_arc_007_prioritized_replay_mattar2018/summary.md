# Summary: Mattar & Daw (2018) — Prioritized Memory Access Explains Planning and Hippocampal Replay

## What the paper did

Mattar and Daw start from a question that is deceptively practical: of all the memories an animal could replay during a rest period, which ones actually get replayed? The answer is not arbitrary, and the paper argues it should not be. They develop a normative theory of memory prioritisation grounded in the economics of information: a memory is worth replaying if and only if doing so would improve future decisions, weighted by the probability that those decisions will actually be faced. This translates into a simple prioritisation metric: utility = need x gain, where 'need' is the probability of visiting a state in the near future (forward-looking, trajectory-sensitive) and 'gain' is the expected improvement in decision quality from updating that state's value estimate (backward-looking, surprise-sensitive). States scoring high on both axes get replayed first. The authors simulate a spatial navigation agent using this algorithm, with replay implemented as sequential nonlocal reactivation of locations ordered by utility.

## Key findings

The theory accounts for a remarkable range of empirical replay phenomena that had previously seemed unrelated. Reverse replay (reactivating the recently traversed trajectory in reverse order upon reaching a reward) falls out naturally because recently traversed states have high 'need' (you came from there, you may return) and high 'gain' (you just received reward information that updates their value). Prospective replay of future trajectories before departure emerges from the same logic: upcoming states have high 'need' (you are about to traverse them) and moderate 'gain' (pre-planning updates their value estimates). The model also reproduces the empirical finding that replay tends to visit states on the optimal path to reward disproportionately often. A successor-representation variant of the model — where replay learns the SR rather than direct value estimates — provides an alternative implementation that connects back to Stachenfeld et al.'s framework. Crucially, the model predicts that replay content should reflect the value topology of the environment, not just recency or spatial proximity.

## REE translation

For ARC-007, the Mattar-Daw framework provides the most mechanistically specific support. If hippocampal replay is prioritised by utility — and utility is determined by the value topology of the planned trajectory — then paths through high-residue (high-harm) terrain would be candidates for prioritised replay precisely because they have high 'gain': the harm estimates of states along those paths are highly informative for decision quality. An agent planning a trajectory through a region of accumulated harm salience would replay that trajectory preferentially, building a more refined representation of the residue-field topology in that region. This is a concrete, testable prediction that ARC-007 can inherit from the Mattar-Daw framework: residue-field topology should predict replay priority, and replay priority should in turn predict the quality of harm-avoidant planning. The mechanism does not require any new neural circuitry — it is a natural extension of value-sensitive replay to a harm-valued landscape.

## Limitations and caveats

The central limitation for REE translation is the operationalisation of 'value.' In Mattar and Daw's model, value is expected reward — a scalar, future-discounted signal. REE's residue field is a harm footprint: it is aversive, distributed across a trajectory rather than localised at a terminal state, and partly derived from causal attribution rather than direct experience of harm. The 'gain' calculation — how much does replaying this state improve future decisions? — would need to be re-derived for a harm-landscape rather than a reward-landscape, and it is not obvious the mathematics carries over without modification. A specific concern: in a reward framework, 'need' and 'gain' both push toward replaying the neighbourhood of the reward locus. In a harm framework, the relevant region is dispersed along the trajectory and may not have a single high-gain locus. Whether the prioritisation algorithm remains tractable and behaviorally efficient in this case is an open modelling question. A second limitation is that the paper validates its predictions against rodent spatial navigation data; the connection to human moral decision-making (the domain where REE harm salience is most important) is not addressed.
