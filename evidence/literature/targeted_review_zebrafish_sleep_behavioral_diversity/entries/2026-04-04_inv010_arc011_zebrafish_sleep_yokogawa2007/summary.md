# Yokogawa et al. 2007 — Zebrafish Sleep: Seminal Characterisation

**Source:** Yokogawa T, Marin W, Faraco J, et al. "Characterization of sleep in zebrafish and insomnia in hypocretin receptor mutants." *PLoS Biology* 5(10):e277, 2007. DOI: [10.1371/journal.pbio.0050277](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pbio.0050277)

**Claims evidenced:** INV-010, ARC-011

---

## What the paper did

Yokogawa and colleagues provided the first systematic characterisation of sleep in adult zebrafish, establishing the species as a legitimate vertebrate sleep model. Using rest/activity monitoring, arousal threshold measurement, and pharmacological testing, they demonstrated that zebrafish satisfy all three canonical behavioural criteria for sleep: periods of sustained immobility that are (1) rapidly reversible, (2) associated with elevated arousal thresholds, and (3) show homeostatic rebound after deprivation. Fish also showed place preference during rest -- settling to the bottom of the tank, a posture-specific sleep state.

A secondary strand examined the hypocretin/orexin system. Zebrafish have a single hypocretin receptor (HCRTR), and null mutants show short, fragmented sleep -- strikingly different from the narcolepsy/cataplexy phenotype of orexin-deficient mammals. The hypocretin receptor in zebrafish colocalises not with monoaminergic wake-promoting cells but with GABAergic neurons, suggesting different circuit-level implementation of the same conserved neuropeptide system.

## Key findings relevant to REE

Sleep in zebrafish is not a passive quiescence but a regulated state with active homeostatic drive. The rebound after electrical-stimulation-based rest deprivation demonstrates that sleep pressure accumulates during waking and must be discharged -- a biological instantiation of the accumulated-integrative-burden concept underlying ARC-011. The conserved orexin system places zebrafish within the same molecular sleep-regulatory framework as mammals, validating cross-species comparisons.

The diurnal pattern is notable: zebrafish sleep primarily at night (as diurnal animals), consistent with a circadian gating of the offline consolidation phases. This supports the framing of MECH-030's offline mode as a periodic structural requirement, not an on-demand option.

## Translation to REE

INV-010 states that offline integration exists and is required. The Yokogawa data grounds this invariant at the minimal mind level: a vertebrate of zebrafish complexity -- the approximate calibration point for the REE minimal mind -- already requires sleep with homeostatic regulation. Sleep is not a mammalian innovation added on top of simpler circuitry; it is present and obligatory at the simplest vertebrate level of the vertebrate forebrain. This means the REE minimal mind (E1 + E2 + hippocampal core loop) is missing a fundamental operating requirement if it lacks a sleep substrate.

ARC-011 (offline integration required for stability) is directly supported: the sleep rebound demonstrates that the accumulation of waking experience creates an integrative load that can only be resolved offline. Without discharge, the animal shows increasing sleep pressure -- the behavioural analog of an unstable system whose representational capacity is degrading.

## Limitations and caveats

Zebrafish sleep lacks confirmed electrophysiological staging. There is no zebrafish EEG equivalent to SWS and REM -- the behavioural and pharmacological characterisation supports the presence of a sleep state, but whether functional analogs of slow-wave homeostasis (MECH-120) and REM recalibration (MECH-123) operate in zebrafish cannot be confirmed from behavioural data alone. The divergence from mammalian sleep (light suppresses rather than promotes sleep; no cataplexy in orexin mutants) indicates that the implementation of sleep regulation differs even while the function is conserved -- a caution against direct circuit-level analogies.

## Confidence reasoning

High confidence (0.88) for INV-010 and ARC-011. This is the seminal zebrafish sleep paper, widely replicated and extended over nearly two decades. The behavioural and molecular evidence is robust. The small dock reflects the absence of electrophysiological data and the architectural assumption that zebrafish complexity corresponds to the REE minimal mind -- an assumption that is well-motivated but not formally established.
