# Rodriguez et al. 2002 — Hippocampal Homolog for Spatial Memory Conserved in Ray-Finned Fish

**Source:** Rodriguez F, Lopez JC, Vargas JP, Gomez Y, Broglio C, Salas C. "Conservation of spatial memory function in the pallial forebrain of reptiles and ray-finned fishes." *Journal of Neuroscience* 22(7):2894-2903, 2002. DOI: [10.1523/JNEUROSCI.22-07-02894.2002](https://doi.org/10.1523/JNEUROSCI.22-07-02894.2002)

**Claims evidenced:** ARC-007

---

## What the paper did

Rodriguez and colleagues used a lesion-dissociation approach to test whether the pallial structures of non-mammalian vertebrates serve the hippocampal-equivalent spatial memory function. Two species were examined: freshwater turtles (for the medial cortex / reptilian hippocampal homolog) and goldfish (for the lateral pallium, LP, of ray-finned teleosts). Animals were trained on a place task (navigate to a submerged platform using allocentric spatial cues; probe trials with novel start positions) and a cue task (navigate to a visible cued platform regardless of spatial position). The place task requires hippocampal-like map-based navigation; the cue task does not.

In goldfish, three pallial regions were lesioned in separate groups: lateral pallium (LP), dorsal pallium (DP), and medial pallium (MP). Only LP lesions impaired place-task performance; DP and MP lesions had no effect on either task. The same pallium-specific dissociation was found in turtles.

## Key findings relevant to REE

LP lesions in goldfish produced a dramatic and selective impairment on place task probe trials -- animals could not locate the goal from novel start positions, indicating failure of allocentric map formation or retrieval. Cue learning was intact in all lesion groups. This is the canonical double dissociation for hippocampal function: lesions of the hippocampal-equivalent structure impair map-based but not stimulus-response navigation.

The cross-species convergence -- same dissociation in turtles (MC) and goldfish (LP) -- establishes that hippocampus-dependent spatial memory is a primitive vertebrate feature, not a mammalian novelty. The forebrain structures neuroanatomically equivalent to the mammalian hippocampus in both reptiles and teleosts appear to share its cognitive role.

## Translation to REE

ARC-007 posits that hippocampal systems store and replay paths through residue-field terrain -- a function that the REE architecture assigns to the HippocampalModule. The Rodriguez result provides the foundational justification for claiming that a system at zebrafish-level complexity already possesses a hippocampal-equivalent structure with map-based spatial memory function. Zebrafish are ray-finned teleosts, the same class as goldfish; the lateral pallium functional conservation demonstrated here almost certainly extends to zebrafish.

This matters for REE because it grounds the claim that the HippocampalModule is not an architectural assumption being projected downward from mammalian neuroscience but is a vertebrate-primitive structure present at the minimal mind level. The place-vs-cue dissociation directly mirrors the REE distinction between viability-map navigation (E3/Hippocampal terrain-based path selection) and habit-based action selection -- precisely the two strategies that the Hagewoud sleep deprivation study shows become dissociated when sleep is absent.

## Limitations and caveats

This paper demonstrates that the LP stores and retrieves spatial maps but does not demonstrate replay. The replay component of ARC-007 -- the claim that hippocampal systems *replay* paths during offline phases -- requires electrophysiological evidence that is not available from behavioural lesion studies. Whether the teleost LP generates SWR-equivalent events during rest is currently unknown; no zebrafish or goldfish electrophysiological replay study exists in the literature as of 2026. This is a significant architectural gap: the substrate is confirmed (hippocampal homolog present and functional for spatial memory), but the dynamic mechanism (replay during offline phases) remains inferred from mammalian data.

Additionally, goldfish and zebrafish share a class (Actinopterygii) but differ in pallial organization details. The mapping is reasonable but not identical.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence 0.83 for ARC-007. The substrate presence claim is strongly supported; the replay mechanism claim is inferred. The absence of teleost fish replay electrophysiology is the primary limitation. This paper motivates the hypothesis that such replay exists and is worth looking for experimentally.
