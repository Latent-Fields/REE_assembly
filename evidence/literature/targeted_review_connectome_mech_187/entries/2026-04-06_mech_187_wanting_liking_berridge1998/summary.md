# Berridge & Robinson (1998) -- Wanting vs. Liking: Dopamine and Incentive Salience

**Entry:** 2026-04-06_mech_187_wanting_liking_berridge1998
**Claim:** MECH-187 (Incentive Salience Gain Regulation by Serotonin)
**Source:** Berridge KC, Robinson TE. "What is the role of dopamine in reward: hedonic impact, reward learning, or incentive salience?" Brain Res Brain Res Rev. 1998;28(3):309-369. DOI: 10.1016/s0165-0173(98)00019-8. PMID: 9858756.

## What the paper did

Berridge and Robinson conducted a major synthetic review combined with new experimental data using 6-hydroxydopamine (6-OHDA) lesions achieving up to 99% depletion of dopamine in the nucleus accumbens and neostriatum in rats. Using the taste reactivity assay -- measuring orofacial hedonic and aversive reactions to sucrose versus quinine -- they systematically tested three candidate roles for dopamine: mediating hedonic pleasure ('liking'), mediating reward learning, and mediating incentive salience ('wanting'). The lesion manipulations were combined with conditioning paradigms (taste aversion) and pharmacological hedonic enhancement (benzodiazepine administration) to separately probe each candidate function.

## Key findings

The core result is a clean double dissociation. Near-total dopamine depletion abolished approach behavior and goal-directed motivation -- the rats would not work for food rewards, would not seek out sucrose -- yet left hedonic reaction patterns entirely intact. Depleted rats who had sucrose placed directly in their mouths showed normal positive taste reactivity (tongue protrusions, rhythmic mouth movements) and normal aversive reactions to quinine. They also showed normal learned hedonic modulation: taste aversion conditioning worked normally, and benzodiazepine-enhanced palatability was unaffected by DA depletion. The conclusion is that dopamine is necessary for attributing incentive salience to stimuli -- making them 'wanted', motivationally magnetic, approach-eliciting -- but is not required for the hedonic impact of those stimuli once encountered, nor for forming hedonic associations. DA systems mediate 'wanting' not 'liking'.

## REE mapping to MECH-187

MECH-187 is the claim that serotonin modulates the gain on the benefit_exposure -> z_goal transduction. This requires a clean architectural separation between wanting and liking in the first place, and Berridge & Robinson provide the empirical foundation for exactly that. In REE terms: the benefit_eval_head output is a liking predictor (responding to immediate hedonic value of benefit contact), while z_goal seeding is the wanting substrate (sustained approach motivation organised around a distal benefit target). MECH-187 formalizes serotonin as a gain multiplier on the wanting channel -- how strongly benefit terrain signals translate into z_goal elevation. This paper grounds that architectural choice: wanting and liking are dissociable, DA-dependent and DA-independent respectively, and therefore a gain modulator on the wanting channel can in principle be independent of hedonic processing. The mechanism of serotonergic gain modulation itself is not addressed here; this paper establishes the substrate being modulated.

## Quantitative constraints for EXP-0099

The paper does not provide quantitative estimates of 5-HT effect magnitude on incentive salience. It quantifies the wanting/liking dissociation in terms of dose-response to DA depletion (near-total depletion required to abolish wanting), but does not characterise the gain function. The proposed x0.2 reduction and x5 elevation in EXP-0099 are not calibrated by this paper. For EXP-0099 calibration, see Korte et al. (2016), which provides direct microdialysis measurements of 5-HT receptor manipulation effects on DA release in NAc.

## Limitations and caveats

The paper is the foundational theoretical and empirical anchor for incentive salience theory, but it predates any serious serotonergic modulation account. The rats are depleted of DA by lesion rather than by circuit-specific manipulation, so the locus of the effect within the mesolimbic system is not precisely resolved. The claim that serotonin specifically modulates the gain on the DA-wanting channel -- as opposed to simply co-modulating motivation generally -- requires the additional mechanistic literature (Dayan & Huys 2009; Korte et al. 2016). The mapping is therefore high-fidelity for the substrate architecture but does not constitute direct evidence for serotonergic gain control.
