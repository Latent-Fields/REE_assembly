# Coupland et al. 2019 -- Anticholinergic drug exposure and dementia (QResearch)

[DOI: 10.1001/jamainternmed.2019.0677](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamainternmed.2019.0677)

## What they did

A nested case-control study from the UK QResearch primary care database. 58,769 patients age 55+ with a dementia diagnosis were matched to 225,574 controls on age, sex, practice, and calendar time. Cumulative anticholinergic exposure was defined as TSDDs of 56 strong anticholinergic drugs prescribed 1--11 years before index. The authors also ran sensitivity analyses with longer lag windows (3--13 years; 5--20 years) to push the exposure further from prodromal influences.

## Key findings relevant to Q-031

The dose-response signal replicates Gray 2015 cleanly in a much larger sample. AOR rose from 1.06 in the lowest exposure tertile to 1.49 (95% CI 1.44--1.54) in the highest. Critically, the paper provides class-specific AORs at the highest exposure category: anticholinergic antidepressants 1.29, antiparkinson drugs 1.52, antipsychotics 1.70, bladder antimuscarinics 1.65, antiepileptics 1.39. The bladder antimuscarinic effect persisted in the long-lag analyses (5--20 years before diagnosis), and the population-attributable fraction for total anticholinergic exposure was 10.3 percent.

## How this maps to REE

This is the largest and most recent direct test of the cumulative-dose limb of Q-031, and it is the paper that most directly motivates the clinical part of the question -- the bladder antimuscarinic to mirabegron substitution. The 1.65 AOR for bladder antimuscarinics, sustained over a 5--20 year exposure window, is precisely the empirical foundation Q-031 builds on when it proposes that switching to a non-anticholinergic alternative (mirabegron, a beta-3 adrenergic agonist) should reduce dementia incidence.

What the paper still cannot do is partition the 1.65 into REE's two postulated mediators. A bladder antimuscarinic taken once daily may concentrate exposure during the sleep window (Pathway 1: REM suppression years), or distribute it through the waking day (Pathway 2: diurnal cholinergic deficit), or both. The class-level AOR is silent on this. The hint of a class hierarchy -- antipsychotics > bladder antimuscarinics > antiparkinson > antiepileptics > antidepressants -- correlates roughly with CNS penetration and total exposure load, but the paper does not report sleep-period vs daytime decomposition.

## Limitations and caveats

Class-specific AORs are averaged over many molecules per class; oxybutynin and trospium have different blood-brain barrier penetration, but both contribute to the bladder antimuscarinic AOR. The QResearch database has no PSG sleep data, so the REM-mediation question cannot be tested in this design. The case-control structure is robust against many confounders but is sensitive to indication confounding -- people prescribed antipsychotics may have neuropsychiatric symptoms that themselves track dementia risk; the authors address this with sensitivity analyses but cannot fully eliminate it.

## Confidence reasoning

I rate this 0.82, slightly above Gray 2015. Source quality is very high (huge sample, modern primary care records, JAMA IM, multiple long-lag sensitivity analyses). Mapping fidelity is higher than Gray's because class-specific decomposition partially probes the question by pharmacological category and explicitly evidences the bladder antimuscarinic risk Q-031 names. The paper still cannot test the REM mediation directly, which keeps it below 0.85. Transfer risk is low. The right reading: the empirical foundation for Q-031's clinical hypothesis is well-supported; what remains is the mechanistic dissociation, which needs sleep architecture data the QResearch design does not have.
