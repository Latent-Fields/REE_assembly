# Kim & Jeong 1999 -- Transdermal scopolamine and phasic REM activity

[DOI: 10.1093/sleep/22.4.515](https://doi.org/10.1093/sleep/22.4.515)

## What they did

Eight healthy young adult men took a 1.5 mg transdermal scopolamine patch and a placebo patch in a randomised double-blind crossover design, with PSG on each night. They scored both tonic REM measures (REM duration, REM latency) and phasic REM measures (total REM activity, total REM density). The motivation was that earlier injection studies of scopolamine had shown clear REM disruption; the question was whether the lower-dose, slower-release transdermal form -- the formulation actually used clinically for motion sickness -- did the same.

## Key findings relevant to Q-031

The phasic REM measures dropped under scopolamine: total REM activity and total REM density were both significantly reduced at p<0.05. The reduction was visible in the first two REM periods of the night and in the fourth period. The tonic REM measures -- duration and latency -- did not reach significance, in contrast to higher-dose injection studies that had shown both effects.

## How this maps to REE

Q-031 pathway 1 needs three things to be substrate-true: (i) anticholinergic drugs at clinical doses must alter REM neural signal in humans, (ii) the alteration must accumulate or have lasting consequences across nights, (iii) those consequences must mediate dementia risk. This paper supplies the first piece directly. A clinically used, commercial-dose muscarinic antagonist measurably alters phasic REM activity in healthy humans on the very first night of exposure. That is the substrate-level mechanism plausibility we need for the upstream half of the pathway.

The paper is silent on the second and third pieces, and its scope is short of what Q-031 really wants. The drug here is scopolamine; the drugs Q-031 cares about most are bladder antimuscarinics like oxybutynin (which has high CNS penetration) and tertiary tricyclics. The subjects are young healthy men; the population at risk is older polypharmacy patients. The exposure is one night; the relevant exposure is years.

## Limitations and caveats

The biggest limitation is sample size. With n=8 the study has limited power, and the fact that tonic REM measures did not reach significance while phasic measures did may be a power artefact rather than a real selectivity for phasic-over-tonic effects. The broader literature with injection scopolamine (e.g. Rao 1999, Sitaram 1976) shows tonic REM effects clearly; the more conservative reading here is "transdermal at 1.5 mg perturbs REM but more weakly than higher-dose injection."

A second limitation is the gap between scopolamine and the bladder antimuscarinic class. Scopolamine is a well-characterised CNS-penetrating muscarinic antagonist; oxybutynin is also CNS-penetrating but with a different receptor profile and chronic dosing. Inferences across this gap should be tentative.

## Confidence reasoning

I rate this 0.62. It is solid substrate-mechanism evidence -- a clinically used anticholinergic does measurably alter REM-related neural signature in humans -- but it is not directly about the chronic-elderly-bladder-antimuscarinic exposure that drives Q-031's clinical relevance. Source quality is moderate (good design, small n, older paper). Mapping fidelity is moderate (right family, wrong species). Transfer risk is high (acute young healthy adults vs chronic elderly polypharmacy). The right reading: this paper says "the upstream link of the mediation chain is substrate-plausible at clinical doses"; it does not say "chronic bladder antimuscarinic exposure suppresses REM enough to drive a 1.65 dementia AOR."
