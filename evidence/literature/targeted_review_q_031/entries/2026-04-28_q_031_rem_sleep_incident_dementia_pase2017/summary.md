# Pase et al. 2017 -- Sleep architecture and incident dementia (Framingham)

[DOI: 10.1212/WNL.0000000000004373](https://doi.org/10.1212/WNL.0000000000004373)

## What they did

A subset of 321 participants from the Framingham Heart Study Offspring cohort, age 60+, who underwent home polysomnography between 1995 and 1998 as part of the Sleep Heart Health Study. They were followed for up to 19 years (mean 12) for incident dementia. Sleep stages were scored from full PSG. Cox regression assessed dementia hazard as a function of each sleep stage, adjusted for age and sex, with richer adjustment models layered in (vascular risk, depression, medication use, MCI exclusion, early-converter exclusion).

## Key findings relevant to Q-031

Lower REM percentage and longer REM latency both predicted incident dementia. The headline estimate -- each 1 percentage-point reduction in REM corresponded to a 9 percent increase in dementia hazard -- survived all the sensitivity adjustments the authors threw at it. Notably, no NREM stage (N1, N2, or N3/SWS) predicted dementia after adjustment. The signal was REM-specific.

## How this maps to REE

Q-031 pathway 1 (the nocturnal mechanism, MECH-173) postulates that anticholinergic-induced REM suppression accumulating across years explains a meaningful fraction of the dose-response we see in Gray and Coupland. For this story to work, we need at minimum an independent demonstration that REM% itself tracks dementia risk -- otherwise the proposed mediator does not exist at the population level. Pase 2017 provides that demonstration. The fact that the signal is REM-specific (not SWS-driven) is also a point in REE's favour: MECH-173 specifically names REM, not total sleep, as the offline phase whose loss matters.

But the paper is silent on the upstream half of the mediation chain. It does not measure anticholinergic exposure, so it cannot tell us whether the people with low REM at baseline got there because of long-term anticholinergic prescriptions or because of endogenous REM circuit changes that are themselves prodromal markers of incipient neurodegeneration. The reverse-causation worry is partially addressed by excluding MCI cases at baseline and excluding early converters, but is not eliminated.

## Limitations and caveats

The 32 incident cases drive the precision floor. The 9% per-percentage-point estimate has wide confidence intervals at the per-percentage-point scale, even if the trend is robust. The PSG was a single night, in a sample of relatively healthy older adults (Framingham Offspring), so generalisability to the typical anticholinergic-burdened polypharmacy patient is a transfer step. Most importantly, the paper measures REM% at one timepoint, not cumulative REM-suppression-years across decades, so any inference about lifetime REM-debt is doing work the data does not directly support.

## Confidence reasoning

I rate this 0.78. Source quality is high (Framingham PSG, long follow-up, careful sensitivity layers). Mapping fidelity is high because REM% as predictor of dementia is exactly the population-level prerequisite Q-031 pathway 1 needs. Transfer risk is moderate -- the chain "anticholinergic-induced REM suppression behaves like endogenous REM reduction" is plausible but unverified, and one-night PSG underdetermines lifetime REM-years. Combined with the modest n=32 incident cases, I land at 0.78. The right reading is "Pase establishes that the proposed mediator is real; he does not establish that it carries the anticholinergic effect."
