# Gray et al. 2015 -- Cumulative anticholinergic use and incident dementia (ACT cohort)

[DOI: 10.1001/jamainternmed.2014.7663](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamainternmed.2014.7663)

## What they did

The Adult Changes in Thought (ACT) study followed 3,434 community-dwelling adults age 65+ in Seattle, dementia-free at entry, for a mean of 7.3 years. Cumulative anticholinergic exposure was extracted from Group Health's pharmacy dispensing records as total standardised daily doses (TSDDs) over the prior 10 years, with the most recent 12 months excluded to handle reverse causation from prodromal symptoms. Outcome was incident dementia and Alzheimer disease by standard diagnostic criteria. Analysis used Cox models adjusted for demographics, health behaviours, and comorbidities.

## Key findings relevant to Q-031

A clean monotonic dose-response emerged. Compared with no use, hazard ratios were 0.92 for 1 to 90 TSDDs, 1.19 for 91 to 365, 1.23 for 366 to 1095, and 1.54 (95% CI 1.21--1.96) for greater than 1095 TSDDs. The most heavily used anticholinergic classes were tricyclic antidepressants, first-generation antihistamines, and bladder antimuscarinics -- all three of which Q-031 names as candidate exposures of interest. The pattern was robust to sensitivity analyses and to lagged exposure windows.

## How this maps to REE

Q-031 asks whether dementia risk tracks (a) cumulative REM-suppression-years (a nocturnal mechanism, MECH-173 pathway 1) or (b) cumulative anticholinergic dose as a proxy for diurnal cholinergic deficit (MECH-175 pathway 2), or both. Gray's design measures (b) directly: it shows that dose-years predict dementia, and that the relationship is graded rather than threshold. What it cannot do is separate the two pathways, because both predict the same dose-response shape. Anyone who took 1095+ TSDDs of an anticholinergic also slept under that drug for years -- the exposure is non-orthogonal to the postulated mediator.

## Limitations and caveats

The biggest limitation for our question is the absence of polysomnography. ACT recorded prescription dispensing, not sleep architecture, so the paper has no purchase on REM% as a mediator. Second, TSDDs treat all strong anticholinergics as equivalent units, but CNS penetration and half-life differ substantially between agents -- a tertiary tricyclic taken at bedtime hits REM-generating circuits very differently from a peripheral bladder antimuscarinic dosed in the morning. Third, the ACT cohort is largely white and well-educated, which constrains generalisability but does not threaten internal validity.

## Confidence reasoning

I rate this 0.78. Source quality is high (large prospective cohort, modern dispensing data, careful lag-exposure handling, well-published in JAMA Internal Medicine). Mapping fidelity is moderate -- the paper covers the cumulative-dose arm of Q-031 directly but cannot speak to the REM-vs-diurnal-cholinergic mediation question that is the actual scientific content of Q-031. The right reading is "cumulative anticholinergic burden is a real and dose-graded dementia risk factor; Q-031's two-pathway question remains open even after this paper." Transfer risk is low because the population is exactly the population Q-031 cares about.
