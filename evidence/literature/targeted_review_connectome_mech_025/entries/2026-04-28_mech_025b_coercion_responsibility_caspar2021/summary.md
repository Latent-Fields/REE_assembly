# Caspar, Beyer, Cleeremans & Haggard (2021) — The obedient mind and the volitional brain: a neural basis for preserved sense of agency and responsibility under coercion

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0258884); PMID 34710149).

## What the paper did

A scanner-based variant of the Milgram obedience paradigm. Participants either freely chose, or were coerced by an experimenter, to administer mildly painful electric shocks to another participant (or to refrain). Intentional binding was measured as an implicit proxy for sense of agency, and explicit responsibility ratings were also collected. fMRI tracked the neural correlates of agency and responsibility across the free-choice / coerced manipulation.

## Key findings

Outside the scanner, the same group has previously reported reduced intentional binding under coercion. Inside the scanner, the average effect was attenuated (the scanner environment itself is socially salient), but greater medial frontal gyrus (mFG) activity was reliably associated with greater preserved sense of agency under coercion, and a similar association held for explicit responsibility ratings. Separately, participants who administered more shocks under free choice showed reduced activity in social-cognition regions during free-choice trials — a possible cognitive-distancing signature.

## Mapping to MECH-025b

MECH-025b is the bridge claim: the precision regime under which an action is committed determines the degree of ethical accountability assigned. This paper provides the most direct neural evidence I am aware of for the bridge in roughly the form REE wants. Coercion is a challenge that reduces the agent's effective control over action selection — and exactly there, when individuals nonetheless engage volitional/planning cortex (medial frontal gyrus), they preserve both agency and responsibility. The clean reading is: the volitional/precision machinery REE invokes is what carries responsibility, and reductions in that machinery's engagement reduce both the felt and the attributed responsibility together.

The translation to REE needs a caveat. Caspar's manipulation is coercion (a social-context variable) rather than action-selection precision in the active-inference sense. Coercion likely modulates several variables — error monitoring, deliberation depth, conflict signalling — that REE's precision construct lumps together. The medial-frontal-tracking-responsibility result is consistent with MECH-025b's bridge but does not isolate precision as such. The result is also between-individual (which participants engaged the mFG more, not within-participant precision manipulation), so the inference is cross-sectional.

## Caveats

The in-scanner sample did not reproduce the coercion-reduces-binding effect on average — only the individual-differences effect held. This is itself informative (the scanner environment is a strong social-salience context that competes with the coercion manipulation), but it weakens the headline. The sample is healthy young adults; clinical populations where the precision/responsibility coupling might break down (psychopathy, OCD, schizophrenia) are not tested.
