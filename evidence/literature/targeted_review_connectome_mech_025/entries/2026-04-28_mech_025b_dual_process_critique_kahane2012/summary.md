# Kahane (2012) — On the Wrong Track: Process and Content in Moral Psychology

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1111/mila.12001); PMID 23335831).

## What the paper did

A philosophical analysis of Joshua Greene's influential dual-process model of moral judgment. Greene's empirical claim is that automatic processing is associated with deontological judgments (don't push the man off the bridge) and controlled processing with utilitarian judgments (do push, to save five). The mapping from process to content underwrites a normative argument Greene makes elsewhere about which moral judgments to trust.

Kahane's analysis re-examines the empirical evidence behind this process-to-content mapping. He argues that the supposed signature of "utilitarian" controlled processing is actually a more general signature of moral deliberation — and that this deliberation is, ironically, in tension with consistent utilitarian commitment, not its expression. He cites neuroimaging showing similar neural correlates for intuitive and counterintuitive moral judgments regardless of whether the content is utilitarian or deontological.

## Mapping to MECH-025b

The relevance to MECH-025b is indirect but worth taking seriously. MECH-025b proposes a bridge from action precision to responsibility attribution: actions committed at higher precision carry higher accountability. This bridge does not directly commit to any process→content mapping at the content level (it doesn't say high-precision actions reach utilitarian conclusions, or any other content-level prediction). It commits only to a structural property: the *weight* of responsibility scales with precision.

Kahane's critique is therefore not a direct refutation. But it serves as a constraint on how MECH-025b can be elaborated. If a future extension tied high-precision action mode to particular moral verdicts (say, more utilitarian, or more deontological, or more cooperative), that extension would walk into exactly the empirical fragility Kahane diagnoses. The safer reading of MECH-025b is the one currently committed to: the bridge connects processing mode to responsibility *weight*, not to verdict.

There is a second-order point Kahane makes that is interesting for REE specifically. He argues that the apparent "controlled processing" signature in counterintuitive moral judgments may reflect general moral deliberation — the act of weighing reasons against intuitions — rather than any particular reasoning style. In REE terms, this maps onto the difference between "high precision because the agent is engaged in deliberate weighing" (the relevant variable for responsibility) and "high precision because the agent has reached a particular kind of moral conclusion" (not the relevant variable). MECH-025b should track the former, not the latter.

## Caveats

This is a philosophical paper, not new empirical evidence. The neuroimaging studies it re-interprets are cited rather than re-analysed in primary form. Mapping to MECH-025b is one inferential step removed (Kahane's target is Greene, not REE). The paper is over a decade old, but the conceptual point survives subsequent empirical waves.
