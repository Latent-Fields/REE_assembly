# Components of episodic memory: the contribution of recollection and familiarity

**Yonelinas (2001) -- Phil Trans R Soc B -- DOI: 10.1098/rstb.2001.0939 -- PMID: 11571028**

## What the paper did

Andrew Yonelinas presents the canonical dual-process signal-detection model of recognition memory. The central claim is that two computationally and neurally distinct processes contribute to recognition: recollection and familiarity. Recollection is modelled as a threshold process -- either you retrieve qualitative contextual information about the prior study event or you do not. It is episodic in character, all-or-nothing in functional form, and hippocampus-dependent. Familiarity is modelled as a graded signal-detection process -- an item produces a continuous strength signal, and recognition occurs when this signal exceeds a response criterion. It is perifamiliar in character, parametric in form, and perirhinal cortex-dependent. The paper marshals converging evidence from five methodological traditions: receiver-operating-characteristic analyses (familiarity produces a symmetric ROC; recollection produces an asymmetric 'hockey-stick' deviation), remember/know paradigms, process dissociation procedures, neuropsychological patient data, and early neuroimaging. The double dissociation is particularly striking in amnesia: patients with selective hippocampal damage show severely impaired recollection but near-normal familiarity, while patients with perirhinal damage show the inverse pattern.

## Key findings relevant to MECH-118

Three findings matter for MECH-118. First, familiarity operates as a graded strength signal -- it does not require retrieving any context, only assessing the match strength between a current stimulus and stored representations. This is directly analogous to Hopfield pattern familiarity: the stability measure (maximum attention weight across stored self-states) is a graded, threshold-free assessment of how closely the current z_self matches anything in memory. Second, the double dissociation is confirmed across multiple paradigms and patient populations. This is the existence proof for MECH-118's core claim: two distinct memory signals that can come apart in the same system. Third, the perirhinal cortex implements familiarity via what appears to be a match-strength mechanism, not a contextual retrieval mechanism -- again structurally analogous to Hopfield's associative completion rather than hippocampal pattern separation/completion for episodic context.

## REE translation

MECH-118 claims that Hopfield pattern familiarity of z_self states is a distinct self-maintenance signal from D_eff coherence. Yonelinas's dual-process framework is the biological existence proof for this kind of dissociation. The mapping runs as follows: D_eff is a population-level geometric measure of representational dimensionality -- how spread out z_self is across its dimensions. Hopfield familiarity is an item-level match-strength measure -- how closely the current z_self resembles any stored self-state. These are orthogonal by construction, just as Yonelinas shows that familiarity and recollection are doubly dissociable. The specific biological substrates differ (perirhinal cortex / hippocampus vs. z_self latent space), but the computational distinction -- between a graded match-strength signal and a population-level geometric measure -- is genuine and preserved in the REE implementation.

## Limitations and caveats

The transfer is functional rather than mechanistic. Yonelinas's framework concerns episodic memory for events in the world -- did I see this word on the study list? MECH-118 concerns self-state familiarity -- does this z_self pattern resemble stored self-states? The content is entirely different (world-objects vs. self-states), and the biological substrates are entirely different. The computational structure (graded match vs. dimensional measure) is the only element being carried across. A further caveat: the dual-process framework has been contested. Single-process accounts argue that recollection and familiarity differences reflect quantitative rather than qualitative distinctions, and the double dissociation evidence has been challenged on methodological grounds. Confidence in the dissociation is high but not unanimous in the field.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is set at 0.78. Source quality is very high -- the paper is the canonical statement of the most influential framework in the recognition memory literature, with thousands of citations and substantial convergent empirical support. Mapping fidelity is moderate because the functional analogy (graded match vs. geometric dimensionality) is genuine but the biological substrate distance is large. Transfer risk is moderate-high. The paper provides strong motivation for MECH-118's existence claim -- that two distinct self-monitoring signals are needed -- but the specific architecture of Hopfield familiarity in REE is not directly validated by this evidence.
