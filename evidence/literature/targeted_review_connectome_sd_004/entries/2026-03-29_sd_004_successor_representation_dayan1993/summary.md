# Summary: Dayan (1993) — Improving Generalization for Temporal Difference Learning: The Successor Representation

**Source:** Neural Computation, 5(4):613-624. DOI: 10.1162/neco.1993.5.4.613. No PubMed entry (predates PubMed biomedical indexing for CS/ML papers).

## What the paper did

In a short but foundational paper, Peter Dayan asked what kind of state representation is most appropriate for temporal difference (TD) learning. The answer he derived is what he called the successor representation (SR): a way of encoding each state not by its intrinsic features but by a vector of expected discounted future occupancies of all states reachable from it under the agent's current policy. The key insight is that effective generalisation in TD learning requires states with similar futures to be represented similarly -- which is exactly what the SR achieves. States that lead to the same successors get similar representations, regardless of their sensory features. Dayan illustrated this with a navigation task in which the SR adapts correctly to the presence of barriers, producing CMAC-like distributed representations that encode reachability structure rather than Euclidean distance. The paper is twelve pages and does not engage neural data at all; it is a pure computational argument about representation for learning.

## Key findings relevant to SD-004

The SR makes two claims that are directly load-bearing for SD-004. First, value computation becomes a simple linear operation on the SR: V(s) = M(s) * R, where M(s) is the SR vector for state s and R is the reward vector. This means that when the reward function changes, value can be recomputed without re-running trajectory simulation -- the SR already encodes the relevant multi-step structure. This efficiency argument is the computational rationale for SD-004's long-horizon planning claim: if action objects are SR units, then planning over action-object space replaces exhaustive multi-step z_world simulation with a single linear readout, at any horizon the SR covers. Second, the barrier-adaptation result shows that the SR encodes reachability structure: two states that are geometrically nearby but separated by a barrier get dissimilar SR vectors, because their futures diverge. This is the basis for the hippocampal flexible-rerouting behaviour that SD-004 must support -- the action-object map should encode which action-object sequences are reachable from a given state, not which ones are geometrically adjacent.

## REE translation: grounding SD-004

SD-004 can be understood as applying the SR framework at a higher level of temporal abstraction. Where Dayan applies SR over primitive states (each a single time-step), SD-004 proposes applying SR over action objects (each representing a multi-step consequence chunk). The efficiency argument transfers directly: value over action-object space = M_ao * R_ao, where M_ao is the SR matrix over action-object space and R_ao is the reward associated with each action-object. The planning horizon expands because each SR entry now covers multiple raw steps. The reachability-structure argument also transfers: the action-object map should represent which action-object sequences are reachable and lead to similar futures, not which action objects are locally similar in raw feature space. Dayan (1993) is thus the theoretical ancestor of Stachenfeld et al. (2017) for the spatial domain -- and of SD-004 for the action-consequence domain. The chain is: Dayan (SR efficiency) -> Stachenfeld (SR as hippocampal place cells) -> SD-004 (SR over action objects as HippocampalModule backbone).

## Limitations and caveats

Three caveats deserve attention. First, the SR is defined over primitive states; formalising SR over action objects requires the options or temporal-abstraction framework (e.g., Sutton et al. 1999 on options in MDPs). Dayan (1993) does not provide this formalisation -- it requires additional theoretical work to extend to SD-004's setting. Second, and importantly, the SR is policy-dependent: the SR matrix M is computed under a specific policy pi, and changes when pi changes. In REE, this means the action-object map backbone will shift as the agent's policy is updated through learning. Whether this is a feature (the map tracks current behavioural strategy) or a liability (long-horizon plans may be destabilised by mid-plan policy updates) is a genuine open design question for SD-004 that the original paper does not address. Third, the paper has no neural data and was published before the hippocampal-SR connection was established -- its relevance to SD-004 is entirely through the Stachenfeld et al. (2017) bridge.
