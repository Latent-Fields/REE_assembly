# Summary: Stachenfeld, Botvinick & Gershman (2017) — The hippocampus as a predictive map

**Source:** Nature Neuroscience, 20:1643-1653. DOI: 10.1038/nn.4650. PMID: 28967910.

## What the paper did

Stachenfeld, Botvinick, and Gershman asked a deceptively simple question: if you were designing a spatial representation to maximise future reward, what would it look like? Their answer is the successor representation (SR) -- a predictive map in which each state is encoded not by its geometric coordinates but by the expected discounted occupancy of all future states reachable from it under the agent's current policy. They then showed that this representation, applied to a 2D maze, produces firing fields that closely resemble hippocampal place cells, including reward-proximity distortions (cells expand near goals), barrier effects (cells shrink at environmental boundaries), and policy-dependence (firing fields shift when the rewarded path changes). They further proposed that entorhinal grid cells encode the eigenvectors of the SR matrix -- a low-dimensional basis set that allows the full predictive map to be reconstructed with far fewer stored quantities than a lookup table would require.

## Key findings relevant to SD-004

The central result is that place cells are not a geometric map of space -- they are a predictive map of future states. Two cells are represented similarly not because they occupy nearby positions in Euclidean space, but because they have similar futures under the current policy. This is an empirically testable and empirically supported claim: the model correctly predicts that place fields should be smaller and more precise near boundaries (because the boundary constrains future options), larger and more diffuse in open areas (because many futures are similar), and shifted toward goals (because goal proximity predicts future occupancy). The grid-cell eigendecomposition result is especially important for SD-004: it suggests the map backbone does not need to store every state-to-state predictive relationship explicitly. A compact basis set (the principal components of the SR matrix) is sufficient to reconstruct the full map. This is the biological precedent for using compressed action objects as map nodes rather than exhaustively enumerating raw state transitions.

## REE translation: grounding SD-004

SD-004 claims that the HippocampalModule navigates action-object space O rather than raw z_world. Stachenfeld et al. provide the most direct biological and computational grounding for this claim. The argument runs as follows: if the biological hippocampal map is already organised by predictive structure (what states follow from here, under this policy), then the natural extension for an agent operating over action consequences is to organise the map by action-object structure (what consequences follow from this action-chunk, under this policy). SD-004 is not proposing a departure from the SR framework -- it is proposing that the SR units are action objects rather than raw states. The compact basis-set result (grid cells as SR eigenvectors) further motivates the design: the action-object map backbone in REE can be a low-dimensional manifold of compressed action-consequence relationships, not a flat table of all possible z_world transitions. The efficiency gain is Dayan's original SR efficiency argument, now applied at a higher level of abstraction.

## Limitations and caveats

Two limitations are worth holding clearly. First, the SR as formulated operates at the level of individual states, not compressed action sequences. The extension to action objects requires treating each chunk as a 'super-state' and applying SR at that higher level -- which is conceptually clean but not what Stachenfeld et al. demonstrate. They show SR over spatial positions, not over action-consequence structures. Second, the SR is policy-dependent: when the agent's policy changes, the entire predictive map changes. In REE, this means the action-object backbone cannot be treated as a fixed, policy-invariant library. The map will shift as the agent learns -- which may be desirable (the map should track current policy) or problematic (if long-horizon plans depend on a stable backbone). This policy-dependence is the key design tension SD-004 inherits from the SR framework.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence 0.85. The source is excellent (Nature Neuroscience, widely replicated). The mapping fidelity is good but not perfect: the paper demonstrates SR over states, and SD-004 requires SR over action objects -- an extension that is theoretically motivated but not directly tested here. The transfer risk is low because the extension is additive (action objects as super-states) rather than transformative. The main uncertainty is whether policy-dependence of the action-object map creates instabilities that the raw spatial SR does not exhibit.
