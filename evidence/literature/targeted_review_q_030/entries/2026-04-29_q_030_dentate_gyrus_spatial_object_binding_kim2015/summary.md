# Kim et al. (2015): Effect of dentate gyrus disruption on remembering what happened where

## What the paper did

The team used Bax-knockout (Bax-KO) mice as a selective model of dentate gyrus (DG) circuit disruption -- in this strain, newborn granule cells accumulate without normal apoptotic pruning, which disrupts DG function specifically while leaving other hippocampal subfields relatively intact. They tested the mice on a delayed-non-match-to-place task that distinguishes online working memory (the animal stays on the maze across the delay) from episodic memory (the animal is removed from the maze, breaking the working-memory rehearsal pathway and forcing reliance on a stored episodic representation).

## Key findings

Bax-KO mice performed indistinguishably from wild-type when working memory could be maintained online (delays up to 5 min, no interruption). When the mice were removed from the maze during a 65-second delay -- forcing them to rely on a stored spatial-event memory rather than an active rehearsal -- Bax-KO mice were profoundly impaired. The interpretation is that DG is not required for online maintenance of working memory but is required for the episodic binding that links the internal spatial map (where I was) with external sensory information (what objects/events were present) in a form that survives interruption. Combined with the authors' prior findings on spatial-map-object association, this paper identifies DG specifically as the locus where internally-generated spatial information is bound with externally-derived nonspatial information to form an episodic memory.

## How the findings translate to REE

The Kim paper provides direct causal evidence (lesion-style, not just correlation) that the mammalian substrate uses fusion at a specific hippocampal subregion: the dentate gyrus binds spatial map (z_world-equivalent) with object/event (z_resource-equivalent) information. This supports MECH-162 (z_resource and z_world re-converge at hippocampal planning) and gives REE a more specific anatomical anchor than "somewhere in hippocampus": the convergence is DG-mediated, which has implications for the kind of computation REE's fusion locus should perform. DG is anatomically a sparse-coding pattern-separation network that receives spatial input from medial entorhinal cortex and object/contextual input from lateral entorhinal cortex. If REE follows this template, the z_resource/z_world fusion locus should be a sparse, pattern-separating layer that takes both streams as inputs and produces a discrete episodic representation. ## Limitations and caveats

The Bax-KO model disrupts DG by allowing newborn granule cells to accumulate without normal apoptotic turnover; this primarily affects pattern separation rather than DG function as a whole, so the inference "DG is required for spatial-object binding" is partly an inference "pattern separation is required for spatial-object binding." Other DG manipulations (e.g., Cre-driven granule cell silencing) might produce different patterns of impairment. The behavioural paradigm (delayed-non-match-to-place) is one operationalisation of episodic binding, and the impairment shows specifically in the interrupted-delay condition; how well this generalises to the kinds of object-in-context binding REE's z_resource/z_world fusion would need is an open question. Mouse-to-REE transfer also assumes the substrate's fusion locus has the same computational character as a sparse pattern-separating layer.
