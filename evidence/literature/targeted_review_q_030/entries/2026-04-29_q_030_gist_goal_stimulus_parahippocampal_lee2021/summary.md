# Lee et al. (2021): GIST model -- Goal-directed Interaction of Stimulus and Task-demand in the parahippocampal region

## What the paper did

Inah Lee's group reviews the dual-stream model of medial temporal lobe (MTL) function, identifies its empirical limits, and proposes a more general account: the GIST model (Goal-directed Interaction of Stimulus and Task-demand). The dual-stream model held that perirhinal cortex (PrC) handles object information, parahippocampal/postrhinal cortex handles spatial-scene information, and these converge at the hippocampus. The paper argues this is too rigid: it fails when sensory cues are ambiguous (a scene is also a collection of objects), and it ignores accumulating evidence that behavioural goal state modulates which stream is recruited for the same input.

## Key findings (synthesis)

The GIST model decomposes MTL processing into stages with different relationships to goal context. **Stage 1 (perceptual)**: PrC and postrhinal cortex both process input stimuli with relatively little dependence on behavioural task demand -- PrC is biased toward objects, postrhinal toward scenes, but both engage in parallel. **Stage 2 (routing)**: once perceptual ambiguity is resolved and stimuli identified, information routes through medial entorhinal cortex if the current goal is navigational, or through lateral entorhinal cortex if the goal is non-navigational (e.g., object-recognition memory). **Stage 3 (integration)**: the hippocampus integrates across goal contexts, allowing the same experience to support multiple downstream uses (navigation, episodic recall, planning). The paper marshals lesion and electrophysiology evidence from rodent and human studies to support the goal-conditioning of stage 2 specifically.

## How the findings translate to REE

Q-030 enumerates six permutation axes for z_resource (SD-015 object-identity encoding) and z_world (spatial-contextual encoding): encoding separation, E2 input, hippocampal fusion, E3 evaluation, feedback direction, and lateral binding. The GIST model gives REE a principled mammalian answer that resolves several of these axes: **encoding separation should be largely goal-independent and hard-wired** (mammalian PrC/postrhinal), so SD-015 and the parallel z_world encoder can be committed at the input stage. **The intermediate routing stage between encoder and hippocampus should be goal-conditioned**, which is a specific REE design instruction: there should be an EC-equivalent layer that takes z_resource and z_world as inputs and routes them differently based on the current goal context (z_goal, current task demand). **The hippocampus should be the convergence point** where flexible integration happens (consistent with MECH-162 and ARC-007). For the question of feedback direction, GIST is more agnostic, but the framework implies hippocampal output can update upstream representations through familiar entorhinal-EC return projections.

## Limitations and caveats

GIST is a theoretical model that synthesises a fragmented experimental literature; it is not a single experiment with a clean PASS/FAIL outcome. The specific claim that lateral EC handles non-navigational and medial EC handles navigational goals is one of several active accounts in the rodent MTL literature, and recent work has complicated the picture (lateral EC also encodes time and event boundaries; medial EC encodes more than spatial layout). REE should treat the goal-conditioned-routing principle as well-supported but the specific medial/lateral assignment as more provisional. The paper also leaves open the question Q-030 most needs resolved: *how* does the hippocampus integrate across goal contexts? The convergence locus is named but the binding mechanism (concatenation, multiplicative gating, cross-attention, factor-graph product) is not specified.
