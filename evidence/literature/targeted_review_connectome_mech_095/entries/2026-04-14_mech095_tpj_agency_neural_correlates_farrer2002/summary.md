# Farrer & Frith (2002) -- Neural correlates of agency: inferior parietal for other-caused, insula for self-caused

## What the paper did

Farrer and Frith presented subjects with a joystick task in which they drove a cursor along a T-shaped path on screen. The critical manipulation was purely instructional: in some conditions, subjects were told they were controlling the cursor themselves; in others, they were told the experimenter was driving it. Crucially, the motor task and visual stimuli were identical across conditions -- only the attributed cause changed. This tight control allows the fMRI signal to be attributed specifically to the experience of agency, not to the perceptual or motor demands of the task itself.

## Key findings relevant to MECH-095

The results produced a clean double dissociation. Experiencing oneself as the cause of the action was associated with activation in the anterior insula. Experiencing another person as the cause was associated with activation in the inferior parietal cortex, specifically on the right side. The authors interpreted the inferior parietal cortex as operating in an allocentric coding system -- one that can represent actions in a frame of reference applicable to oneself or to others, and which therefore becomes engaged specifically when an action needs to be attributed outward.

This is the pattern MECH-095 predicts. The comparator region does not activate when self-attribution succeeds (prediction matches outcome); it activates when attribution must be directed to an external cause. The inferior parietal / TPJ is not a passive agency-presence detector. It is a mismatch-driven switch.

## Translation to REE

MECH-095 frames the TPJ as the locus where efference-copy-predicted z_self change is compared against observed z_self change. When they match, the change is suppressed (self-caused, no residue). When they diverge, the event is flagged as world-caused (residue candidate). Farrer and Frith's finding maps cleanly onto this: the inferior parietal cortex is activated precisely when the self-attribution route is not taken, which in REE terms is exactly when the comparator fires a mismatch and routes the event to z_world.

One nuance worth holding: their paradigm manipulates high-level causal belief, not low-level sensory prediction error. A subject instructed to believe the experimenter is driving the cursor may engage a different (or additional) mechanism from one who is experiencing genuine sensory mismatch because the cursor moved unexpectedly. REE's AgencyComparator needs to handle both -- the automatic, millisecond-scale efference-copy comparison and the slower, reflective attribution process. Farrer and Frith's experiment speaks more directly to the latter.

## Limitations and caveats

The voluntary-instruction manipulation means this study cannot cleanly isolate the automatic forward-model comparator from top-down attribution processes. There is also no graded manipulation here -- attribution is binary (self or other), so the study cannot speak to whether the inferior parietal responds proportionally to the size of the prediction error. That question is addressed by Farrer et al. (2003). Additionally, the study used PET-era fMRI methods with relatively coarse spatial resolution; the precise subregion within the inferior parietal lobule (SMG vs. AG, dorsal vs. ventral TPJ) is not resolved. This matters for MECH-095 because the claim locates the comparator at the TPJ specifically, not the full inferior parietal complex.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is set at 0.82. The experimental logic is sound and the double dissociation is convincing as evidence that inferior parietal / TPJ territory is specifically engaged for external attribution. The mapping to MECH-095 is good but carries moderate transfer risk because the paradigm is belief-level, not automatic-comparator-level. The study supports the neural validity of the TPJ comparator mechanism without fully resolving whether it is the efference-copy-based forward-model comparator MECH-095 posits or a higher-level causal inference region.
