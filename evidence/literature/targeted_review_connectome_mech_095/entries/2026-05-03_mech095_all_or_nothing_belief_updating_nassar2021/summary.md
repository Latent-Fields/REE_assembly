# Nassar et al. (2021): All-or-Nothing Belief Updating in Schizophrenia

This 2021 *Brain* paper from the Frank computational-psychiatry group
is the load-bearing entry for the symmetric-over/under-attribution
diagnostic signature pull. The starting puzzle is a literature
paradox: schizophrenia patients have been described as both
over-sensitive and under-sensitive to feedback, in apparently
contradictory paradigms. Nassar and colleagues resolve this
mechanistically. Patients tend to *either* completely ignore new
information (perseverate, under-update) OR update completely (after
a single observation, over-update). What they don't do is gradually
integrate. A single computational pathology — all-or-nothing belief
updating — produces both apparent failure modes depending on which
behavioural paradigm probes the system. Paradigms that reward
gradual integration find under-sensitivity; paradigms that show
single-trial outliers find over-reactivity.

This is the empirical generalisation of Asai 2016's finding for
agency attribution. Where Asai showed that schizotypal participants
exhibit shallower agency-rating slopes producing both over- AND
under-attribution errors symmetrically across S/N regimes, Nassar et
al. show the analogous structure across belief-updating paradigms in
clinical schizophrenia. Both findings demonstrate the diagnostic
principle the lit-pull was commissioned to develop: a single
computational pathology can produce opposite-direction surface errors
depending on which test condition probes the system.

For MECH-095 and SD-047 the implication is twofold. First, the
substrate-ceiling diagnosis on MECH-095 is biologically licensed in
a much broader sense than I initially appreciated — the same shape
of computational pathology shows up across multiple comparators in
schizophrenia. Second, and more practically: SD-047 validation should
not assume that lifting substrate-ceiling will produce graded
behaviour automatically. If MECH-095's output is consumed downstream
by an all-or-nothing belief-update process, the system will show
flat performance with bimodal error patterns even after substrate
enrichment. The diagnostic move is to look for graded behaviour as a
function of noise level, not just binary PASS/FAIL on the C1-C3
discriminative pair.

For the ai-cognitive-failure-taxonomy more broadly, this paper
generalises the principle articulated in `agency_attribution_failure.md`:
asymmetric error patterns reveal which side of the comparator's
optimum the substrate sits on. The all-or-nothing signature is a
specific case of the broader phenomenon — comparator output being
consumed by downstream machinery that expects graded input but
receives bimodal input. Monitoring for bimodal error patterns is a
useful generic diagnostic that extends beyond agency attribution.
