# Wagner et al. 2004 -- Sleep inspires insight

## What the paper did

Wagner and colleagues trained participants on the number-reduction task -- a stimulus-response sequence task in which a long-winded explicit procedure can be radically shortened if the participant notices a hidden abstract rule (a logical invariant in the training data). After initial training, participants spent 8 hours either sleeping at night, awake at night, or awake during the day, and were then re-tested. Roughly twice as many participants in the post-sleep group spontaneously reported and used the hidden rule as in either wake group. The post-sleep advantage was not explained by simple speed-up of the explicit procedure -- it was specifically about the qualitative shift to the rule-based shortcut.

## Key findings relevant to MECH-275

Wagner et al. is the textbook demonstration that sleep does not just consolidate what was learned awake; it restructures the representation in ways that support new inferences. The same task data, after a night of sleep, becomes available to a different mode of analysis. This is the behavioural fingerprint of what MECH-275 calls schema revision: not just strengthening of existing structure but reorganisation of which inferences the structure supports.

For MECH-275 the value of this paper is less specific than Lewis & Durrant 2011 (which gives the mechanism) or Tse 2007 (which gives schema-acceleration of new learning) but it covers a part of MECH-275's scope those papers do not -- the qualitative-reorganisation arm. MECH-273's correction-of-spurious-self-attribution case requires precisely this kind of qualitative reorganisation, and Wagner et al. is the classic demonstration that sleep produces it.

## How the findings translate to REE

The translation is at the level of behavioural existence-proof: sleep produces qualitative cognitive reorganisation in humans on tasks where waking analysis alone does not. MECH-275 specifies the underlying mechanism (cross-episode Bayesian aggregation of counterfactual-backed attribution) and the consumers (E1 and SD-033a via the anchored channel). Wagner et al. constrains the kinds of reorganisation REE has to be able to produce; it does not constrain which mechanism produces them.

## Limitations and caveats

The mapping is loose. Wagner et al. do not isolate the sleep stage responsible for the insight effect (subsequent work has implicated REM specifically for some insight tasks, NREM for others; the picture is not settled). They do not show that the reorganisation depends on multi-episode aggregation as opposed to single-session re-analysis. And the hidden rule in the number-reduction task is a deterministic invariant of the training data, not a probabilistic structure that has to be inferred under uncertainty -- so the Bayesian framing of MECH-275 is a stronger commitment than this paper tests.

A subtler caveat: MECH-275 explicitly distinguishes counterfactual-backed input (which produces schema revision) from correlational input (which produces noise-fit). Wagner et al.'s task input is structured-but-correlational. So the paper's relevance to MECH-275 is to the consequence side -- yes, sleep can produce qualitative reorganisation on this kind of input -- not to the input-requirement side.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence 0.62 -- supports MECH-275 on the broad claim that sleep produces qualitative cognitive reorganisation, with the more specific aggregation-of-counterfactual-input mechanism not tested. Source quality high (Nature paper, widely replicated). Mapping fidelity moderate-low because the paper demonstrates the consequence shape but not the mechanism shape. Transfer risk moderate because the task is deterministic-rule discovery rather than the probabilistic schema revision MECH-275 generalises to.
