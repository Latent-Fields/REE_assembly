# Tse et al. 2007 -- Schemas and memory consolidation

## What the paper did

Tse and colleagues trained rats on a hippocampus-dependent paired-associate task in a fixed-context "event arena". After being given a cue flavour in a start box, the animal had to recall the spatial location associated with that flavour and run there to retrieve more of that flavour. Over weeks of training across multiple flavour-place pairs, a stable cortical "schema" of the task developed. They then asked: once that schema is in place, how does the brain consolidate a brand new flavour-place pair?

The answer is striking. New pairs added to the schema were consolidated within a single trial and became hippocampus-independent within roughly 48 hours -- a timescale that, in naive animals on equivalent tasks (contextual fear conditioning), takes weeks. Inactivating the hippocampus 48 hours after one-trial schema-consistent learning did not impair recall, while the same inactivation in schema-naive animals did. Inhibiting cortical protein synthesis around the time of one-trial encoding blocked the schema-accelerated consolidation, locating the mechanism in cortex.

## Key findings relevant to MECH-275

MECH-275 says sleep-phase Bayesian aggregation builds a schema, and that once the schema is built it should change how new compatible information is processed. Tse et al. give the behavioural fingerprint of exactly that change: schema-consistent new information is absorbed orders of magnitude faster than schema-naive new information, and the absorption depends on cortex rather than hippocampus. The acceleration is not a side-effect of practice -- it is a function of the schema being there.

For MECH-273 specifically, the mapping is one step removed: the self-model is the schema, and once it is built, new self-attributions consistent with it should be assimilated faster than they were earlier in development. Tse et al. do not test this -- their schema is flavour-place, not self-attribution -- but the structural prediction is the same.

## How the findings translate to REE

This is corroborating evidence for the consequence side of MECH-275: schemas, once formed by cumulative consolidation, change downstream processing in exactly the way MECH-275 predicts they should. The translation is at the level of "structures of this kind exist and behave as the claim says they should behave".

## Limitations and caveats

The translation has two genuine gaps. First, Tse et al. do not isolate sleep as the consolidation period -- the 48-hour timescale spans multiple sleep-wake cycles, and the role of sleep specifically has to be inferred from broader literature. Second, and more important, the schema in Tse et al. is built by repeated correlational pairing of cue and outcome, not by the counterfactual-backed attribution that MECH-275 names as the necessary input. So the paper supports the existence and behavioural function of cortical schemata, but it does not test the input requirement that MECH-275 makes load-bearing.

A subtler caveat: the paper demonstrates assimilation of consistent information, not revision of the schema in light of inconsistent information. MECH-273's spurious-self-attribution-correction case is the harder test, requiring schema revision rather than schema growth. Tse et al. do not address this.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence 0.74 -- supports MECH-275. High source quality (well-cited Science paper, replicated). Mapping fidelity moderate because the behavioural consequence is exactly what MECH-275 predicts but the construction mechanism in this task is correlational rather than counterfactual-backed. Transfer risk moderate: the leap from rat paired-associate learning to cross-domain Bayesian aggregation is real, and the paper's strongest contribution is to the consequence-side of MECH-275 rather than the mechanism-side.
