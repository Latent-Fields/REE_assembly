# Bjorness & Greene 2024 -- Orexin as motivated-arousal broadcast

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.peptides.2024.171280)), this recent review by Bjorness and Greene argues that the orexin/hypocretin system is best understood not as a primary reward signal nor a primary arousal signal but as a coupling signal that integrates the two under demanding internal-state conditions. Orexin neurons are sparse (a few thousand in mouse, ~50,000-80,000 in human, all in the LHA), but project broadly -- VTA, LC, raphe, BLA, PFC, PAG, basal forebrain -- and sense multiple homeostatic variables (sleep debt, glucose, CO2, stress hormones).

For REE, this is the clearest match in the lit-pull to the architectural pattern hypothesised by the spec. The override layer that V3 needs has these features: small substrate population, wide broadcast, gain modulation rather than primary drive, multi-variable integration of internal state. Orexin satisfies all four. The review's clinical case studies of orexin receptor antagonists (suvorexant for insomnia, addiction therapy applications) provide an independent validation: blocking the broadcast reduces both arousal and reward-seeking simultaneously, which is the signature of a multi-target gain modulator rather than a competing scalar.

For SD-012 specifically, the Bjorness/Greene review's emphasis that orexin is necessary for motivated arousal (the combination) but not for either component alone maps onto SD-012's contingent behaviour: drive_level should not produce z_goal seeding by itself, nor arousal modulation by itself, but should couple them when caloric need rises. The clinical knockout (narcolepsy NT1) supports this -- patients have intact reward computation and intact baseline arousal but specific failures in motivated arousal under demand (e.g. cataplexy triggered by emotion).

Caveat: the review focuses on reward and arousal, not on defensive behaviour. The orexin -> PAG and orexin -> BLA projections are mentioned but not characterised in terms of override-of-fear under metabolic challenge. Primary studies (e.g. Furlong et al on orexin/PAG in fear conditioning, or Sears et al on orexin/BLA) would be needed to sharpen the override claim. I include this review as the framework paper because no single primary study captures the broadcast architecture as cleanly.

Confidence 0.7: review-level evidence rather than primary, but the architectural fit is strong and the clinical translation independently validates the broadcast reading.
