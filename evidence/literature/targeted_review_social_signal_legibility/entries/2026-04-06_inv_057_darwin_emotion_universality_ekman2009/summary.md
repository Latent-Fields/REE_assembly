# Ekman (2009) -- Darwin's contributions to our understanding of emotional expressions

**Claims tested:** INV-057 -- Cross-species legibility evidences functional specificity; MECH-191 -- Causal externalization

## What the paper finds

Ekman (2009) reviews Darwin's five key contributions to the science of emotional expression:

1. Emotions are discrete modules (anger, fear, disgust, etc.) -- not a continuous affective space.
2. The face is the richest source of emotional information.
3. Facial expressions of emotion are universal across human cultures.
4. Emotions exist across species -- from bees to dogs to primates.
5. Emotional expressions evolved through "serviceable habits" -- movements that originally served practical functions (snarling to prepare to bite, frowning to shield eyes) that became ritualized signals.

Ekman notes that Darwin's universality claim has been "strongly supported by subsequent Western and Eastern research across literate and preliterate cultures."

## The serviceable habits principle and causal externalization

Darwin's "serviceable habits" principle is the historical precursor to MECH-191's causal externalization claim. The principle states that emotional expressions are not arbitrary signs -- they originated as movements that served a direct functional purpose. A snarl prepares the animal to bite; the expression is a byproduct of the motor preparation, not an arbitrary symbol. This is causal externalization: the signal is produced by the same neuromuscular systems that generate the internal state.

Over evolutionary time, these expressions became ritualized -- exaggerated and stereotyped as signals. But the ritualization does not sever the causal link; it strengthens the signal while preserving the underlying production mechanism. This is why expressions remain legible: they are still produced by (variants of) the same motor patterns that drove the original functional behavior.

## Universality as evidence for functional specificity

Ekman's cross-cultural universality evidence is the human-within-species version of INV-057's cross-species claim. If facial expressions are universal across human cultures despite vast differences in language, social convention, and lived experience, they cannot be purely conventional. They must be structurally tied to the internal states they signal. INV-057 extends this argument: if expressions are universal not just within humans but across mammalian species, this provides even stronger evidence for functional specificity.

## The constructionist challenge

An important caveat: the basic emotions framework (discrete emotion categories with universal expressions) has been challenged by constructionist theories (Barrett, 2017; Russell, 2003) which argue that emotional experience is constructed from domain-general affect (arousal + valence) through conceptual categorization. If the constructionist account is correct, MECH-191's discrete signal-state mappings (whine=wanting, yelp=nociception) may overstate the specificity of the link. The causal externalization mechanism would still operate for arousal and valence, but not for discrete functional states.

## What this means for novelty

Darwin's thesis that emotional expressions are biologically grounded, functionally originated, and cross-species continuous is well over 150 years old. Ekman's universality program has been the dominant framework for decades. MECH-191 and INV-057 are reframing this classical position in computational-architectural terms (causal externalization, functional specificity as prerequisite for z_beta leakage). The empirical base is classical; the REE interpretation is novel.
