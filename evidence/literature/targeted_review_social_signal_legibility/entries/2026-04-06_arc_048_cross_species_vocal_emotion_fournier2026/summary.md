# Fournier et al. (2026) -- What do we know about vocal communication of emotion between different species of terrestrial tetrapods?

**Claims tested:** ARC-048 -- Language as externalization of pre-existing functional states; MECH-192 -- Signal legibility as empathy prerequisite; INV-057 -- Cross-species legibility evidences functional specificity

## What the paper finds

Fournier et al. (2026) provide the most current systematic review of interspecies vocal emotion communication. Their central finding: many animals recognize a range of emotions in the vocalizations of other species in a manner qualitatively similar to their perception of conspecific emotions. The acoustic features mediating this recognition include pitch, harshness, call duration, and timing -- the same parameters Briefer (2012) identified as carrying emotional information within species.

Two important modulators: (1) familiarity with the vocalizing species enhances recognition accuracy, and (2) domestication is associated with improved interspecies emotion reading (domesticated species are better at reading human emotions than wild counterparts). However, baseline cross-species recognition exists even without familiarity or domestication.

## Support for three claims

**ARC-048:** Cross-species vocal emotion recognition demonstrates that the communication channel for emotional states operates across species boundaries without requiring language. If a dog can read the emotional content of a human vocalization (and vice versa), the functional states being communicated must exist independently of language. Language adds precision to this channel, but the channel itself is pre-linguistic and phylogenetically ancient.

**MECH-192:** The review confirms that signal legibility operates cross-species, providing the perceptual substrate for fast empathy coordination (MECH-183) to function between dogs and humans. If dogs can accurately categorize human emotional vocalizations by valence and arousal, the perceived signal maps onto corresponding internal states in the observer -- meeting MECH-192's prerequisite.

**INV-057:** The qualitative similarity between conspecific and heterospecific emotion recognition supports functional specificity. If the same acoustic features carry emotional information within and across species, this is because the same physiological production mechanisms (MECH-191) generate similar acoustic outputs in similar emotional states. The signals are structurally tied to their referent states, not arbitrary conventions.

## The familiarity complication

The finding that domestication and familiarity enhance cross-species recognition introduces a learning component that complicates INV-057's claim of automatic legibility. A pure automatic-legibility account would predict no familiarity effect. The data suggest a hybrid model: baseline legibility from shared acoustic-emotional structure (supporting INV-057) plus a learning-enhanced component from exposure (complicating the "automatic" claim).

## What this means for novelty

Cross-species vocal emotion communication is a well-studied phenomenon, with this 2026 review representing the current state of knowledge. The empirical base is thoroughly charted. The novel contributions of ARC-048, MECH-192, and INV-057 are in the functional-architectural interpretation: using these findings to ground the signal legibility prerequisite for fast empathy and the language-as-bandwidth-extension thesis.
