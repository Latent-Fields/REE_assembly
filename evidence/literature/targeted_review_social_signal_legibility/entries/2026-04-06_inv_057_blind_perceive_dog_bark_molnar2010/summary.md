# Molnar, Pongracz & Miklosi (2010) -- Blind humans' perception of dog barks tests structural rules in vocal communication

**Claims tested:** INV-057 -- Cross-species legibility evidences functional specificity; MECH-191 -- Causal externalization

## What the paper finds

Molnar et al. (2010) played prerecorded domestic dog barks from various contexts (stranger approach, play, isolation, walk anticipation, guarding food) to three groups: congenitally blind people with no canine visual experience, people who became blind later in life, and sighted controls. None were dog owners. All three groups accurately categorized the emotional content of the barks, and congenitally blind participants performed comparably to sighted controls.

The key finding: humans who have never seen a dog -- who have no visual experience of canine body language, facial expressions, or context-bark pairings -- can still read the emotional content of dog barks from acoustic structure alone.

## The strongest test of innate cross-species legibility

This study provides the most controlled test available of INV-057's core claim. If cross-species emotional reading were based on learned convention (seeing a fearful dog and hearing its bark, building an association), congenitally blind individuals should fail. They do not fail. This strongly constrains the space of explanations: the acoustic structure of the bark itself must carry the emotional information in a format that maps onto the listener's internal emotional categories without visual training.

For MECH-191, this confirms that the causal externalization mechanism works: the dog's internal state produces acoustic patterns (via the same source-filter mechanisms Briefer reviews) that are decodable by a listener from a different species who has never had visual exposure to the signaler. The acoustic structure is doing the work, not learned convention.

## The auditory exposure caveat

The important limitation is that congenitally blind people in urban environments still hear dogs bark. They may have built auditory associations between bark acoustic properties and emotional labels through other modalities (hearing other people's reactions to barking dogs, hearing bark contexts). This is a weaker confound than visual learning, but it cannot be dismissed entirely. A truly clean test would require participants with no auditory exposure to dogs, which is practically impossible.

## What this means for novelty

The finding that acoustic structure alone enables cross-species emotional reading is well-established. INV-057's contribution is in using this fact as evidence for a specific theoretical conclusion: that signal legibility entails functional specificity (the signal is causally tied to the state) rather than convention. The empirical fact is known; the theoretical use as evidence for functional architecture is the novel framing.
