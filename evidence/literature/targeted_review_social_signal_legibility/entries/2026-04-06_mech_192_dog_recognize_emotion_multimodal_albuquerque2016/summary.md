# Albuquerque et al. (2016) -- Dogs recognize dog and human emotions

**Claims tested:** MECH-192 -- Signal legibility as prerequisite for fast empathy; INV-057 -- Cross-species legibility evidences functional specificity

## What the paper finds

Albuquerque et al. (2016) presented 17 domestic dogs with pairs of facial photographs (one positive, one negative expression) alongside a vocalization of matching or mismatching emotional valence. Critically, they tested this with both conspecific stimuli (dog faces + dog vocalizations) and heterospecific stimuli (human faces + human vocalizations). Dogs looked significantly longer at the face whose expression was congruent with the valence of the vocalization, for both dogs and humans.

This is the first demonstration that a non-human animal can integrate emotional information across two sensory modalities (visual + auditory) and across two species. Previously, this ability was documented only in humans. The authors interpret this as evidence that dogs form abstract mental representations of positive and negative emotional states.

## Cross-species emotional legibility (MECH-192, INV-057)

This result directly supports both MECH-192 and INV-057. Dogs can read human emotional valence from face and voice and integrate the two -- without sharing language, without explicit training, and across species boundaries. This is precisely the "signal legibility" that MECH-192 identifies as the prerequisite for fast empathy coordination. If dogs can categorize human emotional expressions by valence, the perceptual substrate for state-matching is present.

For INV-057, the cross-species reading is particularly telling. Dogs and humans diverged approximately 96 million years ago (as mammals), yet dogs can read human emotional valence. If emotional expressions were arbitrary conventions, this cross-species reading would require learning each species' conventions separately for each modality. The fact that it works cross-modally and cross-species is evidence that the signals are structurally (causally) tied to their referent states.

## The domestication confound

One important caveat: dogs have been domesticated for approximately 15,000-40,000 years and have had extensive exposure to human expressions. It is possible that the cross-species reading of human emotions is a product of domestication-driven selection or extensive individual experience, rather than innate cross-architectural legibility. The Molnar et al. (2010) study with blind humans reading dog barks provides a complementary test -- humans without visual experience of dogs can still read emotional content from barks, which is harder to attribute to domestication.

## What this means for novelty

Cross-species emotion recognition in dogs is well-established. MECH-192's specific contribution is framing signal legibility as the prerequisite that enables fast empathy (z_beta leakage) to produce coordination. The empirical phenomenon is known; the functional-architectural interpretation is novel.
