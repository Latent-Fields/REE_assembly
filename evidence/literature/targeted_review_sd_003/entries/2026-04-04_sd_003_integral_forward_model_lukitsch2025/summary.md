# Lukitsch 2025 -- Integral Forward Model: Extending the Comparator to Thought

**Source:** Lukitsch, O. (2025). "An integral forward model of agency experience in thought and action." *Frontiers in Psychology*, 16:1524904. DOI: [10.3389/fpsyg.2025.1524904](https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2025.1524904). PubMed PMID 39917733.

---

The prior literature pull for SD-003 included Frith, Blakemore, and Wolpert's 2000 canonical account of the forward model comparator. Lukitsch 2025 is best read as a 25-year update to that account, asking whether the same comparator machinery can cover both bodily action and *thought* -- and answering yes, on condition that we adopt a predictive processing framing. For the comparator to unify thought and action, Lukitsch argues, it must be understood not as a single moment of prediction-versus-outcome comparison but as a temporally extended process of prediction error cancellation: the sense of agency is the experience of successfully suppressing prediction errors across a sequence of predictions. When the forward model predicts well -- when prediction errors are small -- experience is agentive. When prediction errors persist -- when the forward model was wrong about what the action would cause -- the experience attributes the outcome elsewhere.

What this adds to the prior SD-003 literature record is less empirical and more architectural. The key move is that the same forward model can operate on both executed and simulated (counterfactual) actions because predictive processing does not distinguish qualitatively between real and imagined forward evaluations. This directly addresses one of the gap concerns in the SD-003 evidence_quality_note: the single-pass comparator (E2(a_actual) - observed) is well-evidenced, but the two-pass counterfactual difference (E2(a_actual) - E2(a_cf)) is an inferential extension. Lukitsch's account provides a theoretical rationale for that extension: if the forward model machinery is general enough to operate on thought as well as action, then evaluating E2 on a counterfactual a_cf is a form of mental simulation that recruits the same forward model. The counterfactual is not a qualitatively different computation -- it is the same computation on a different input.

The precision-weighting component of the account is worth sitting with. Lukitsch follows Friston in treating the sense of agency as precision-weighted prediction error cancellation. Predictions about actions we are highly confident in carry high precision; deviations from them are weighted heavily. For SD-003 this has a practical implication: E2_harm_s will naturally have higher precision for actions the agent has taken many times (high training signal) and lower precision for counterfactual actions that were rare or never executed. Causal_sig will therefore be noisier for uncommon counterfactuals than for common ones. Whether this gradient of precision degrades attribution quality at the margins is an open empirical question -- but it is now a clearly articulated risk.

The honest accounting: this is a theoretical-philosophical review, not an experiment. The empirical validation of the unified account remains, in Lukitsch's own words, an open question. What the paper provides is a coherent update to the Frith 2000 framework that extends it to counterfactual simulation and precision weighting -- two features directly relevant to SD-003 that were not foregrounded in the 2000 review. Given that SD-003 is currently validated in one condition (EXQ-030b on z_world) but awaiting retest on the z_harm_s pipeline, the theoretical grounding this paper offers is useful orientation rather than evidential weight.
