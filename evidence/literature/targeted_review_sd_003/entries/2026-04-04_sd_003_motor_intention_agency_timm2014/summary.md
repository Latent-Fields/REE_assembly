# Timm et al. 2014 -- Motor Intention and the Forward Model Input

**Source:** Timm, J., SanMiguel, I., Keil, J., Schroger, E., and Schonwiesner, M. (2014). "Motor intention determines sensory attenuation of brain responses to self-initiated sounds." *Journal of Cognitive Neuroscience*, 26(7):1481-9. DOI: [10.1162/jocn_a_00552](https://doi.org/10.1162/jocn_a_00552). PubMed PMID 24392902.

---

What would it mean for an agent to be the causal author of an outcome? One answer, now standard in sensorimotor neuroscience, is that self-caused outcomes are those whose sensory consequences were *predicted* by a forward model running from the agent's own motor intention. Timm and colleagues put this answer to a clean test. They had participants both press a button voluntarily and receive TMS-triggered involuntary finger movements -- in each case producing an identical sound. The auditory brain response (N1-P2 complex, measured by EEG) was attenuated only for voluntary presses, not for TMS-triggered movements. The sensory outcome was the same; what differed was whether the brain had a motor plan from which to generate a forward prediction.

The implication for SD-003 is direct, if not immediately obvious. SD-003 uses E2 as a forward model computing E2(z_t, a_actual) and E2(z_t, a_cf), where a_actual and a_cf are the agent's actual and counterfactual intended actions. For causal_sig = E2(z_t, a_actual) - E2(z_t, a_cf) to mean anything, the action input to E2 must represent the agent's *causal intent*, not merely the resulting state change. Timm et al. confirm that the brain's analogous computation makes precisely this distinction -- and makes it *before* motor cortex activation, on the basis of the motor plan alone. If E2's action input were simply a description of what happened in the environment (a state-diff rather than an action-intent), it would lose the property that makes the counterfactual subtraction informative.

There is a temporal asymmetry worth acknowledging honestly. The brain's forward model cancels predicted sensory consequences *prospectively*, before the action completes. SD-003 computes causal_sig *retrospectively*, after observing z_harm_s_next. The mechanisms are related but not identical: both require an action-intent input to the forward model, but the prospective cancellation mechanism the paper studies does not directly validate the retrospective counterfactual subtraction SD-003 performs. Whether a forward model trained to predict harm-stream transitions under actual actions will also produce accurate predictions for counterfactual actions it never took is a separate empirical question -- one the current experimental programme is attempting to answer.

A further caveat: this is sensorimotor-auditory neuroscience, and SD-003 operates on a harm latent stream in a grid-world navigation task. The principle -- action-intent as the critical forward model input -- is shared, but the domain differences are not trivial. The paper cannot tell us whether E2_harm_s learns a representation of a_actual that is selective for harm-causal structure rather than general positional change. That selectivity is precisely what EXQ-166b is designed to test. What Timm et al. do establish is that the biological precedent for the SD-003 architecture is real: the forward model in the brain takes motor intention as its input, and the resulting prediction is what grounds self-attribution.
