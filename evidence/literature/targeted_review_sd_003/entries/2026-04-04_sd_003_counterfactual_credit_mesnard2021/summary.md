# Mesnard et al. 2021 -- Counterfactual Credit Assignment in RL

**Source:** Mesnard, T., Weber, T., Viola, F., Thakoor, S., Saade, A., Harutyunyan, A., Dabney, W., Stepleton, T.S., Heess, N., Guez, A., Moulines, E., Hutter, M., Buesing, L., and Munos, R. (2021). "Counterfactual Credit Assignment in Model-Free Reinforcement Learning." *Proceedings of the 38th International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML 2021)*, PMLR 139:7654-7664. arXiv 2011.09464. DOI: [10.48550/arXiv.2011.09464](https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2011.09464).

---

The credit assignment problem in reinforcement learning is, at bottom, a causal question: which of the agent's past actions was responsible for a reward that arrives later? Standard temporal-difference learning aggregates credit imprecisely, weighting action contributions by temporal proximity rather than causal efficacy. Mesnard and colleagues formalise a sharper answer. They define credit as the action's counterfactual contribution to future rewards -- the difference between the reward trajectory the agent obtained and the reward trajectory it would have obtained under a baseline that marginalises out the agent's action while preserving everything else. This counterfactual-conditioned advantage, computed by conditioning value functions on hindsight information about what happened later while excluding information about the agent's own action from the conditioning set, is provably lower variance than standard advantage estimates. The key constraint is anti-leakage: the baseline must not contain information about the action taken, or the advantage estimate becomes biased.

This is the model-free analogue of what SD-003 does with a forward model. SD-003 computes causal_sig = E2(z_t, a_actual) - E2(z_t, a_cf): the difference between the harm-stream trajectory under the actual action and the harm-stream trajectory under a counterfactual action, both evaluated by the forward model E2. Where Mesnard et al. construct the counterfactual baseline from learned value functions conditioned on hindsight, SD-003 constructs it from a forward model's predictions. Both computations isolate the agent's action-specific contribution to outcomes by replacing the agent's action with a counterfactual while holding the state z_t fixed.

The variance-reduction result provides indirect support for SD-003's two-pass architecture. If counterfactual baselines reduce variance in the model-free setting by removing outcome uncertainty that is shared between actual and counterfactual trajectories, the same logic applies to SD-003's E2-based difference: subtracting E2(z_t, a_cf) from E2(z_t, a_actual) cancels the component of the predicted harm-stream trajectory that is state-driven rather than action-driven, leaving a signal that is more precisely attributable to the agent's choice. The stronger the state-action dependence in the harm stream, the larger this variance reduction will be.

The anti-leakage constraint is the harder part. Mesnard et al. prove their estimator is unbiased only if the hindsight information does not carry information about the agent's action. In SD-003, the analogous condition is that E2's evaluation on a_cf must not be systematically distorted by the training distribution over actions -- if E2 has seen many trajectories of a_actual but few of a_cf, its predictions for a_cf will be biased in ways that have nothing to do with what would causally have happened under a_cf, but merely reflect the model's unfamiliarity with that action. This is precisely the out-of-distribution concern flagged in the ARC-033 design, and the interventional training requirement identified in the Scholkopf 2021 entry of the prior pull. Mesnard et al. do not solve this problem for the model-based case -- but they do name it clearly in the model-free case, which at least confirms that the concern is architecturally principled rather than idiosyncratic to SD-003.

One honest note of domain difference: this paper operates on scalar reward signals in standard RL environments, while SD-003 operates on z_harm_s, a continuous latent vector of potentially high dimension. Credit assignment in latent space is less well-studied, and the variance-reduction guarantees do not automatically transfer to this setting. The paper also builds on COMA (Foerster et al. 2018, already in the prior SD-003 pull) and can be read as extending the COMA credit assignment principle from multi-agent discrete action spaces to general model-free settings with theoretical backing. Together, the two papers bracket the computational rationale for counterfactual credit attribution from both the multi-agent and single-agent perspectives.
