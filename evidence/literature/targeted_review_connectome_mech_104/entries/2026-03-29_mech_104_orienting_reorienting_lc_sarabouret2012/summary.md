# Summary: Sara & Bouret (2012) — LC Mediates Cognition Through Orienting and Reorienting

**Entry:** 2026-03-29_mech_104_orienting_reorienting_lc_sarabouret2012
**Claim tested:** MECH-104 (unexpected harm events spike commitment uncertainty via LC-NE volatility interrupt, enabling de-commitment)

---

## What the paper did

Sara and Bouret published a review in *Neuron* synthesising a programme of work on LC function in behaving animals, focused on the specific cognitive role of phasic LC discharge in mediating transitions between behavioral states. Unlike Aston-Jones and Cohen's more computational framing, Sara and Bouret emphasise the phenomenological and ethological dimensions: the LC is a reset button for ongoing cognition, firing to any event that is simultaneously unexpected and behaviorally relevant, and producing the noradrenergic milieu in prefrontal and cingulate cortex that allows the animal to disengage from the current context. The paper reviews Sara's own extensive single-unit recording work in freely behaving and task-performing rats and monkeys, combined with pharmacological studies in humans and animal models. It includes detailed treatment of the anatomical projection patterns of LC to frontal cortex, ACC, and basal ganglia -- directly relevant to the circuitry that REE assigns to commitment gating.

## Key findings

Several findings are particularly germane to MECH-104. First, LC neurons in behaving animals respond reliably to unexpected salient events -- including noxious or aversive events -- with short-latency phasic bursts (50-100ms), well before the animal's behavioral response. This confirms that the LC is in a position to generate the interrupt signal before behavioral de-commitment would need to occur. Second, pharmacological disruption of NE in prefrontal cortex specifically impairs the ability to shift behavioral set in response to unexpected contingency changes -- while leaving performance under stable contingencies intact. This is a double dissociation that supports the view that LC-NE is not just an arousal system but specifically a flexibility/reorienting system. Third, Sara and Bouret document that LC axons project densely to ACC and dorsal PFC -- regions central to REE's commitment-gating architecture.

A particularly important section reviews the LC response to aversive outcomes specifically: LC fires to unexpected shocks and pain stimuli, and this activation precedes and presumably causes the behavioral disengagement from the current task that follows. This is the most direct evidence available that the MECH-104 Route-2 (harm_surprise-gated interrupt) is neurobiologically realistic.

## REE translation

MECH-104 requires two things: that unexpected harm triggers LC-NE discharge, and that this discharge enables de-commitment from the current plan. Sara and Bouret provide evidence for both. For the first: they document LC responses to aversive stimuli across multiple paradigms. For the second: they document that NE in PFC and ACC is specifically required for the cognitive flexibility response -- disengaging from the current set. In the REE architecture, E3 commitment gating involves ACC and PFC circuits; the LC projections documented by Sara and Bouret are precisely the projections one would expect to modulate E3's commitment gate. Their framing of LC as a 'reset mechanism' is essentially the functional description of what MECH-104 calls the volatility interrupt.

The orienting/reorienting distinction Sara and Bouret draw is worth noting for REE. Orienting is the initial attentional capture by a novel/salient event; reorienting is the subsequent redirection of ongoing behavior. MECH-104's de-commitment maps more closely onto reorienting -- it is not just noticing that something went wrong (orienting) but actively disengaging from the plan (reorienting). Sara and Bouret explicitly argue that both functions are LC-mediated, with phasic NE release serving as the proximate cause of reorienting.

## Limitations and caveats

The primary limitation is the same one that applies to all the entries in this cluster: harm is not distinguished from other aversive or surprising stimuli. Sara and Bouret treat aversive events as a subset of "unexpected, behaviorally significant events" without asking whether harm-specific features (violation of safety model, moral valence, consequence for others) produce distinct LC responses. For MECH-104 to be a genuine architectural claim about harm-specifically, rather than just surprising-events-generally, this gap needs closing. A second limitation is that the reorienting studied in Sara's animal paradigms is set-shifting in spatial and reward learning tasks, not plan de-commitment in a hierarchical action sequence -- a meaningful difference from the REE context. Finally, some of the cited evidence for NE-mediated flexibility comes from pharmacological studies with NE uptake blockers or alpha-2 receptor drugs that affect both central and peripheral NE, limiting mechanistic precision.

## Confidence reasoning

Sara and Bouret provide stronger empirical grounding than the other entries because they draw on direct LC unit recording in behaving animals, with aversive stimuli, showing short-latency LC responses preceding behavioral reorienting, combined with pharmacological evidence that this NE release in frontal areas is specifically required for disengagement. The anatomical projection data to ACC and PFC is directly relevant to REE's commitment-gating architecture. The main docking of confidence comes from the harm-specificity gap and the set-shifting vs. plan-de-commitment distinction. Confidence: 0.80.
