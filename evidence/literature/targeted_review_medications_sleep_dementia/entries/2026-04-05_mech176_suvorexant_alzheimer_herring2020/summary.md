# Herring et al. 2020 -- Suvorexant in Alzheimer's disease and insomnia

**Entry:** 2026-04-05_mech176_suvorexant_alzheimer_herring2020
**Claims:** MECH-176, MECH-177 | **Direction:** supports | **Confidence:** 0.79
**Source:** Alzheimer's & Dementia (2020) 16(3):541-551 | DOI: 10.1002/alz.12035 | PMID: 31944580

---

Herring and colleagues conducted a 4-week randomised placebo-controlled trial of suvorexant (10mg for adults aged >=65, 20mg for those <65) in 285 patients with mild-to-moderate probable Alzheimer's disease and comorbid insomnia. Eligible participants had objective sleep impairment confirmed by screening polysomnography. The primary endpoint was PSG-derived total sleep time (TST) change at week 4.

The trial found a 28-minute TST improvement with suvorexant vs placebo (95% CI 11-45, p<0.01), with concurrent WASO reduction. Treatment was well-tolerated. This is the first and, at time of writing, only published RCT of an orexin antagonist in an established AD+insomnia population using objective PSG measurement.

For MECH-176, this trial establishes a critical proof-of-concept: the orexin wake-promoting mechanism remains a viable pharmacological target even in the context of AD-related sleep disruption. The neurodegeneration in AD progressively damages the brainstem sleep circuitry -- but the orexin-blocking mechanism still achieves meaningful sleep improvement. This is consistent with MECH-176's prediction that architecture-preserving sleep promotion (via orexin antagonism rather than global GABAergic suppression) should be effective in cognitively impaired populations.

For MECH-177, this trial provides the feasibility component: sleep improvement with an architecture-preserving agent is achievable in AD. However, the trial cannot test the disease-modification hypothesis for three reasons: (1) it studied established AD (MMSE 12-26, mild-to-moderate dementia), while MECH-177 predicts the largest effects in early MCI before schema scaffold destabilisation; (2) 4 weeks is far too short to assess cognitive trajectory effects -- MECH-177's pipeline protection mechanism requires months to years of sustained sleep improvement to show cognitive benefit; (3) the trial was designed and powered as a sleep efficacy study, not a disease-modification trial, with no cognitive primary endpoints.

The critical gap in the published literature that MECH-177 would close is a trial with: (a) early MCI population (not established AD), (b) 12-24 month follow-up, (c) sleep architecture monitoring (PSG confirming SWS and REM preservation), and (d) cognitive endpoints (episodic > semantic decline rate as primary outcome, per MECH-170 prediction). This trial demonstrates that suvorexant is a practical candidate for such a study.

**According to PubMed**, this article (PMID 31944580) is the only Phase 3 RCT of suvorexant in Alzheimer's disease with insomnia and is frequently cited as the key clinical study supporting orexin antagonist use in dementia-related sleep disorders.
