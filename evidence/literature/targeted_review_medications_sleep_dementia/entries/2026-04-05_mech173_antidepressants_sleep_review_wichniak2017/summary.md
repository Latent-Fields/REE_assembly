# Wichniak et al. 2017 -- Effects of Antidepressants on Sleep

**Entry:** 2026-04-05_mech173_antidepressants_sleep_review_wichniak2017
**Claims:** MECH-173 | **Direction:** supports | **Confidence:** 0.74
**Source:** Current Psychiatry Reports (2017) 19(9):63 | DOI: 10.1007/s11920-017-0816-4 | PMID: 28791566

---

Wichniak and colleagues provide a clinical pharmacology review of how different antidepressant classes affect sleep architecture. The review synthesises polysomnographic data across drug classes, providing the drug-class-specific profiles that MECH-173 requires to stratify medications by Phase 4 disruption risk.

The key finding for MECH-173 is class-differential REM suppression. TCAs (amitriptyline, clomipramine, imipramine) are the most potent REM suppressors, acting via combined anticholinergic blockade of PGO-wave generating neurons + serotonergic/noradrenergic REM-off enhancement; at therapeutic doses, REM% can be reduced by 50% or more. MAOIs (phenelzine, tranylcypromine) produce near-complete REM suppression at therapeutic doses via monoamine accumulation and cholinergic-noradrenergic interaction. SSRIs produce moderate REM suppression via 5-HT2A-mediated REM-off tone enhancement; fluoxetine and paroxetine are more suppressive than citalopram/escitalopram (the paroxetine exception is due to its potent muscarinic blockade, unusual for an SSRI). SNRIs (venlafaxine > duloxetine) suppress REM moderately, similar to SSRIs.

The important exceptions are directly relevant to MECH-176 and MECH-177: mirtazapine (5-HT2 antagonist + alpha-2 antagonist + H1 antagonist) is relatively REM-sparing because 5-HT2A antagonism blocks the primary REM-off serotonergic signal; it also increases SWS via H1 antagonism. Trazodone (5-HT2A antagonist at low dose) similarly preserves or slightly increases REM while enhancing SWS. These two medications represent a low-Phase-4-risk profile in the antidepressant class and are potential disease-modifying candidates in the MECH-177 framework for older adults with comorbid depression and MCI risk.

Depression itself alters sleep architecture (shortened REM latency, prolonged first REM period, reduced SWS). The review notes that the goal of antidepressant therapy is to normalise these changes -- but the normalisation achieved by REM suppression is pharmacological, not architectural restoration. The REE distinction is: restoring normal awake-attribution by suppressing pathologically elevated REM is different from preserving the healthy REM machinery that MECH-123 requires for precision recalibration.

The review does not discuss chronic use in cognitively at-risk or elderly populations, and does not address dementia risk. The transfer from this source to MECH-173's MCI-prodrome-acceleration prediction requires extrapolation.

**According to PubMed**, this article (PMID 28791566) is a widely cited clinical review from the Polish Institute of Psychiatry and Neurology, a major sleep medicine centre.
