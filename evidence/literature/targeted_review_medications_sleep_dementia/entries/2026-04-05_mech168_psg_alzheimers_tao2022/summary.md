# Tao et al. 2022 — PSG in Alzheimer's disease vs. non-demented elderly with insomnia

**Entry:** 2026-04-05_mech168_psg_alzheimers_tao2022
**Claims:** MECH-168, MECH-174 | **Direction:** supports | **Confidence:** 0.72
**Source:** Sleep Medicine (2022) | DOI: 10.1016/j.sleep.2022.11.027

---

Tao and colleagues compared baseline polysomnography data between 239 people with mild-to-moderate Alzheimer's disease and insomnia (ADI) and 837 elderly controls with insomnia but no cognitive impairment (EI), using propensity score matching on age group and total sleep time. The elegant feature of this design is the insomnia matching: by selecting controls who also have insomnia, the authors effectively subtract out the effects of sleep problems per se, leaving the differential PSG signature of Alzheimer's disease on top of an insomnia baseline.

The findings are worth sitting with. Compared to elderly-only insomnia, AD with insomnia showed: lower SWS%, higher N1% (lighter sleep), fewer spindles per minute in N2, and lower absolute EEG power in low-frequency bands. The REM% trend was in the predicted direction (lower in AD) but did not reach statistical significance.

For MECH-168 (consolidation pipeline staged failure mechanism), this paper documents precisely the infrastructure damage that the pipeline account predicts. SWS reduction impairs Phase 1 (MECH-120 synaptic homeostasis) and Phase 2 (MECH-121 schema replay). Spindle reduction impairs Phase 3 (MECH-122 spindle coordination for schema packaging). The low-frequency EEG power reduction extends the finding: the slow oscillation band that drives hippocampal-cortical dialogue during SWS is attenuated in AD. This is not just a quantitative reduction in stage time -- it is a qualitative reduction in the machinery that makes consolidation possible.

The REM non-finding is worth noting carefully. MECH-168 predicts that Phase 4 (REM, precision recalibration) fails first in early neurodegeneration (INV-047). But this paper is examining mild-to-moderate AD -- not early prodromal or MCI stage. At mild-moderate AD, Phases 1-3 failures may be dominant and measurable, while Phase 4 failure, though present, may be proportionally less marked against the background of severe SWS and spindle disruption. The cross-sectional design cannot test the temporal ordering that INV-047 predicts; it can only show the pattern at one disease stage.

For MECH-174 (SWS suppression as Phase 1/2 disruption), this paper is relevant as a validation anchor: it shows that the SWS/spindle disruption pattern that GABAergic medications are predicted to produce pharmacologically is precisely the same pattern observed in established AD. This convergence supports the claim that medications producing this PSG signature are doing damage to the same pipeline infrastructure that is damaged in AD. It does not establish causation for the medications, but it confirms that the target mechanism (SWS/spindle integrity) is genuinely implicated in AD pathology.

The inherent limitation of cross-sectional PSG in established AD is that causal direction cannot be inferred. Does SWS disruption cause AD, or does AD cause SWS disruption, or is the relationship bidirectional (the vicious cycle of MECH-171)? This paper cannot answer that. For the pharmacological claims, the relevant point is narrower: medications that suppress SWS produce a PSG signature matching what is seen in AD, which is itself a reasonable proxy for pipeline damage.

**According to PubMed**, this article (DOI: [10.1016/j.sleep.2022.11.027](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.sleep.2022.11.027)) provides the most comprehensive PSG characterisation of the AD-insomnia combination using propensity-matched controls, and is directly relevant to suvorexant's clinical development program in AD.
