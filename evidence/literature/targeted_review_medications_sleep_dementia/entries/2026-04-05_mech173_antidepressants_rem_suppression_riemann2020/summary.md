# Riemann et al. 2020 -- Sleep, insomnia, and depression

**Entry:** 2026-04-05_mech173_antidepressants_rem_suppression_riemann2020
**Claims:** MECH-173, INV-048 | **Direction:** supports | **Confidence:** 0.80
**Source:** Neuropsychopharmacology (2020) 45:74-89 | DOI: 10.1038/s41386-019-0411-y | PMID: 31071719

---

Riemann and colleagues (Freiburg sleep group) provide a comprehensive review of sleep in depression, tracing PSG research from early observations through modern pharmacology. The review covers: the characteristic PSG signature of depression (shortened REM latency, prolonged first REM period, reduced SWS, fragmented sleep continuity); the effects of antidepressant drug classes on sleep architecture; and the mechanistic interpretation of REM changes.

The most directly relevant statement for MECH-173 and INV-048 is unambiguous: "almost all antidepressant agents suppress REM sleep." The review further documents "a time-and-dose-response relationship between total REM sleep suppression and therapeutic response to treatment" -- a claim that has driven decades of REM suppression research and positions REM suppression as potentially mechanistic to antidepressant action, not merely a side effect.

Cross-class convergence is the key finding for INV-048. Agents acting via entirely different primary molecular targets all converge on REM suppression: MAOIs (via serotonin/norepinephrine accumulation and pre-synaptic inhibition of REM-on cholinergic neurons); TCAs (via combined serotonergic, noradrenergic, and anticholinergic mechanisms -- the most potent REM suppressors); SSRIs (via 5-HT2A-mediated REM-off neuron activation); SNRIs (similar to SSRIs with additional NE contribution); and even some atypicals. Mirtazapine is an important exception -- 5-HT2 and H1 antagonism with relatively preserved REM.

For MECH-173, the critical implication is that the pipeline-disruption consequence of REM suppression is mechanism-agnostic at the molecular level: any agent that reduces REM% or fragments REM continuity, regardless of its primary pharmacological target, degrades Phase 4 (precision recalibration, MECH-123). This is exactly the equivalence principle stated in INV-048.

There is an interpretive tension the REE account must acknowledge. The dose-response between REM suppression and antidepressant response could suggest that REM suppression has therapeutic value. The REE resolution is that acute REM suppression in a young adult with intact pipeline phases may be tolerable or even briefly adaptive (disrupting pathological REM hypnosis of depressive rumination), while chronic suppression in an aging brain with already-compromised MECH-168 staging produces cumulative Phase 4 deficit. The paper does not test this distinction and does not study cognitively at-risk populations; the chronic-accumulation-in-MCI prediction of MECH-173 is an REE extrapolation not supported or refuted by this source.
