# Richardson et al. 2018 -- Anticholinergic drugs and risk of dementia

**Entry:** 2026-04-05_mech175_anticholinergic_dementia_richardson2018
**Claims:** MECH-175, INV-048 | **Direction:** supports | **Confidence:** 0.76
**Source:** BMJ (2018) | DOI: 10.1136/bmj.k1315 | PMID: 29695481

---

Richardson and colleagues analysed 40,770 dementia cases and 283,933 matched controls from UK general practices (Clinical Practice Research Datalink), examining anticholinergic drug exposure 4-20 years before dementia diagnosis. This is the largest UK case-control study of the anticholinergic-dementia association and one of the three major epidemiological papers cited in the MECH-175 claims notes.

The overall finding is an adjusted odds ratio of 1.11 (95% CI 1.08-1.14) for any anticholinergic drug with definite anticholinergic activity. The key result for MECH-175 is the class specificity: antidepressants, urological drugs (bladder anticholinergics: oxybutynin, tolterodine), and antiparkinson drugs (trihexyphenidyl, procyclidine) showed increased dementia risk with greater exposure. Gastrointestinal anticholinergics (hyoscine, dicycloverine) did not.

Two aspects are directly relevant to MECH-175's dual-pathway account. First, the long latency finding: exposure 15-20 years before diagnosis still confers elevated risk. This argues strongly for a causal mechanism rather than reverse causation (dementia prodrome causing greater medication prescription). The 15-20 year window aligns with the cumulative pipeline disruption model: gradual Phase 4 degradation via chronic REM suppression (Pathway 1) or basal forebrain cholinergic deficit (Pathway 2) accumulating over decades. Second, the class specificity: the positive associations cluster in CNS-penetrating anticholinergics (antidepressants with muscarinic blockade, bladder drugs that cross the blood-brain barrier) rather than peripherally-restricted agents. This is consistent with MECH-175 Pathway 1 (nocturnal REM suppression requires CNS access to the PGO wave generating brainstem circuits) and Pathway 2 (diurnal NBM-analog deficit requires CNS access to basal forebrain cholinergic neurons).

MECH-175's Pathway 2 is more directly supported than Pathway 1 by this paper. The daytime cholinergic deficit mimicking an NBM lesion is a well-established prior mechanism in the anticholinergic-dementia literature (Pathway 2 is the standard explanation). Pathway 1 (nocturnal REM suppression as the novel REE-derived contribution) is consistent with these findings but requires the additional step of attributing the risk to the specific nocturnal sleep phase rather than to daytime cholinergic deficit alone. Richardson et al. cannot distinguish the two pathways because they lack polysomnographic data.

The indication bias concern is genuine but does not easily explain the long latency or the class specificity pattern. Depression is a dementia risk factor, which could inflate the antidepressant-class association, but bladder anticholinergics are not prescribed for depression and still show elevated risk -- suggesting a genuine anticholinergic mechanism beyond indication confounding.

**According to PubMed**, this BMJ paper (PMID 29695481) is one of the most widely cited articles on anticholinergic burden and dementia risk and has been influential in UK prescribing guidance for older adults.
