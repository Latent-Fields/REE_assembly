# McCleery & Sharpley 2020 — Pharmacotherapies for sleep disturbances in dementia (Cochrane)

**Entry:** 2026-04-05_mech177_pharmacotherapy_dementia_mccleery2020
**Claims:** MECH-177 | **Direction:** mixed | **Confidence:** 0.72
**Source:** Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews (2020) | DOI: 10.1002/14651858.CD009178.pub4

---

McCleery and Sharpley conducted a Cochrane systematic review of all randomised controlled trials comparing drug treatment to placebo for sleep disorders in people with dementia. Nine RCTs met inclusion criteria (n=649 total), covering melatonin (5 studies), trazodone (1 study, n=30), ramelteon (1 study, n=74, unpublished), and orexin antagonists suvorexant and lemborexant (2 studies, n=323). The review found no RCTs for benzodiazepines or Z-drugs in this population -- clinicians evidently avoid them, though without formal RCT evidence of harm, making this an important evidence gap.

The key findings for MECH-177's disease-modifying candidates:

**Orexin antagonists** (moderate certainty): probably increase total nocturnal sleep time by 28 minutes (95% CI 11-45 minutes), decrease time awake after sleep onset by 16 minutes (95% CI 3-28 minutes) in mild-to-moderate AD over 4 weeks, with no evidence of increased adverse events. Sleep efficiency probably increases by ~4%.

**Trazodone 50mg** (low certainty, small trial): improves total sleep time by 42 minutes (95% CI 0.9-84 minutes) and sleep efficiency by 8.5% (95% CI 1.9-15.1%) in moderate-to-severe AD over 2 weeks. No serious adverse effects.

**Melatonin up to 10mg** (low certainty): little or no effect on any major sleep outcome. The synthesis of two trials (n=184) shows TNST MD of just 10.7 minutes (95% CI -16 to +38).

**Ramelteon 8mg** (low certainty): no important effect from the single small unpublished trial.

What does this mean for MECH-177? The claim predicts that architecture-preserving agents -- specifically orexin antagonists and trazodone as the two candidates with most pipeline-protective mechanisms -- should be beneficial in AD sleep. Both show measurable sleep improvement in the Cochrane review, and both are the only agents showing positive evidence. Melatonin fails despite circadian-restoration rationale. This is partly consistent with MECH-177, though the melatonin null is puzzling if circadian pathway intervention is the mechanism: the review notes melatonin-ER and ramelteon differ pharmacologically from standard melatonin, and all melatonin trials used relatively moderate doses (up to 10mg) with variable formulations.

The critical limitation for MECH-177 is the outcome measure. The Cochrane review assesses sleep quality metrics: total sleep time, WASO, efficiency, awakenings. MECH-177's disease-modification claim requires cognitive decline outcomes measured over months to years. None of these trials are designed or powered for that. The question is whether improving sleep in established AD changes the trajectory of cognitive decline -- which requires trials like the COSMOS Sleep study or future orexin-antagonist MCI prevention trials. The present evidence shows the sleep improvement is real and well-tolerated in AD; the DPM claim remains to be tested.

The remarkable absence of benzodiazepine RCTs in dementia is itself informative. Clinicians prescribing practice has apparently diverged substantially from evidence in this population -- widespread avoidance of GABAergic hypnotics in dementia without RCT confirmation of harm, and a gap that MECH-174's predictions make more pressing to fill. The absence of negative RCT data for benzodiazepines in dementia is not evidence of safety.

**According to PubMed**, this article (DOI: [10.1002/14651858.CD009178.pub4](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD009178.pub4)) is the most comprehensive systematic review of sleep pharmacotherapy in dementia and is the primary evidence base for clinical guideline recommendations on this topic.
