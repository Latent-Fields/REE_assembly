# Coupland et al. 2019 — Anticholinergic drug exposure and dementia risk

**Entry:** 2026-04-05_mech175_anticholinergic_dementia_coupland2019
**Claims:** MECH-175 | **Direction:** supports | **Confidence:** 0.80
**Source:** JAMA Internal Medicine (2019) | DOI: 10.1001/jamainternmed.2019.0677

---

Coupland and colleagues used the QResearch primary care database for England in a nested case-control design to examine anticholinergic drug exposure and dementia risk at a scale that dwarfs most prior studies: 58,769 patients with dementia diagnoses matched against 225,574 controls (55 years and older), drawing on computerised prescribing records for 56 drugs with strong anticholinergic properties. The result is a study with sufficient power to detect class-specific effects and test dose-response relationships across a 1-20 year exposure window.

The headline finding confirms Gray et al. 2015 and extends it: adjusted OR for dementia at the highest TSDD tier (>1095 TSDDs, roughly 3 years of daily use over the prior 1-11 years) was 1.49 (95% CI 1.44-1.54). More usefully for MECH-175, the class-specific breakdown reveals differential risk: bladder antimuscarinic drugs carry the highest OR (1.65), followed by antipsychotics (1.70), anticholinergic antidepressants (1.29), and antiepileptics (1.39). The population-attributable fraction associated with any anticholinergic drug exposure was 10.3% -- making this one of the most significant modifiable dementia risk factors identified to date.

The class-specific pattern is where MECH-175's dual-pathway framework becomes interesting and generates testable predictions. Bladder antimuscarinics (e.g., oxybutynin, solifenacin) act primarily peripherally but have significant central penetrance; oxybutynin in particular crosses the blood-brain barrier. Anticholinergic antidepressants (amitriptyline, clomipramine) carry high REM suppression burden via both muscarinic blockade and serotonergic mechanisms. Antipsychotics vary enormously in anticholinergic burden (clozapine/olanzapine vs. risperidone/aripiprazole). MECH-175 predicts that within each class, drugs with higher REM suppression potency should carry disproportionate risk -- a prediction the TSDD metric aggregates away. That is the most important gap this paper leaves for future work.

The robustness of the association across three different exposure windows (1-11 years, 3-13 years, 5-20 years) -- all producing similar ORs in the 1.44-1.49 range -- is important. It argues against the association being entirely driven by short-window confounding, supporting a long-duration accumulation mechanism consistent with both MECH-175 pathways (nightly REM suppression compounding; progressive cholinergic system load). Associations were somewhat stronger for cases diagnosed before age 80, which is consistent with early-onset or more aggressive disease having a higher pharmacological-mechanism contribution.

What this paper cannot do is separate the nocturnal from diurnal pathway (see Q-031). Both pathways accumulate with years of use; both would produce dose-response patterns in the TSDD metric. A within-class comparison -- oxybutynin (high central ACh, high REM suppression) versus mirabegron (no ACh burden, no REM effect) for bladder indications -- would provide the cleanest natural experiment for isolating whether the nocturnal pathway adds risk independently of the bladder indication.

**According to PubMed**, this article (DOI: [10.1001/jamainternmed.2019.0677](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamainternmed.2019.0677)) is the largest study to date of anticholinergic drug exposure and dementia risk in a general population, and directly informs NICE and NHS England prescribing guidance on anticholinergic deprescribing.
