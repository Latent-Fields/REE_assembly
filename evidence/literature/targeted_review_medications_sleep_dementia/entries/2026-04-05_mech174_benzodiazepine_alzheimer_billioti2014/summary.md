# Billioti de Gage et al. 2014 — Benzodiazepine use and Alzheimer's disease risk

**Entry:** 2026-04-05_mech174_benzodiazepine_alzheimer_billioti2014
**Claims:** MECH-174, INV-048 | **Direction:** supports | **Confidence:** 0.70
**Source:** BMJ (2014) | DOI: 10.1136/bmj.g5205

---

Billioti de Gage and colleagues used the Quebec provincial health insurance database (RAMQ) to conduct a case-control study examining benzodiazepine use and Alzheimer's disease risk. The design imposed a 5-year exposure lag (exposure counted only from at least 5 years before diagnosis) to address the obvious confound of prescribing anxiety, depression, and insomnia in prodromal dementia. With 1,796 cases and 7,184 matched controls, the study had reasonable power for dose-response analysis.

The main result: benzodiazepine ever-use was associated with increased Alzheimer's disease risk (adjusted OR 1.51, 95% CI 1.36-1.69). Crucially, this association persisted after further adjustment for anxiety, depression, and insomnia as potential prodromal markers (OR 1.43, 1.28-1.60). The dose-response relationship was pronounced: no association for <91 prescribed daily doses (OR 1.04), but clear association for 91-180 (OR 1.32) and strong for >180 (OR 1.84). And the detail that matters most for MECH-174: long-acting benzodiazepines carried a higher OR (1.70) than short-acting ones (1.43).

The long-acting differential is important. Long-acting benzodiazepines (diazepam, chlordiazepoxide, nitrazepam) maintain plasma and brain concentrations throughout the night, continuously suppressing SWS homeostatic gradients and REM throughout all sleep cycles. Short-acting benzodiazepines (temazepam, triazolam) are metabolised before the early morning REM-rich cycles. MECH-174 predicts that SWS homeostasis impairment scales with nocturnal drug concentration duration; the long-acting/short-acting differential is consistent with this prediction. It is not discriminative -- this pattern is equally consistent with longer half-life producing more daytime residual sedation and encoding impairment -- but it is at least consistent with the nocturnal mechanism.

The fundamental limitation of this paper is the one that applies to all observational pharmacoepidemiology: the indication for benzodiazepines (anxiety, insomnia) may itself be associated with dementia risk, independent of the drug. The authors adjusted for these comorbidities and the OR barely moved (1.51 to 1.43), which is encouraging -- but the adjustment variable is a diagnostic code, not a severity measure, and prodromal dementia-associated anxiety/insomnia may be severe yet uncoded. This is the honest uncertainty that any mechanistic claim (MECH-174) built on this association must carry.

For INV-048 (pharmacological sleep disruption equivalence), this paper provides the clearest available epidemiological anchor: benzodiazepines -- the archetype of sleep phase-disrupting medications -- are associated with Alzheimer's risk at ORs in the range produced by moderate-severe behavioural sleep deprivation studies. The equivalence is not tested mechanistically, but the epidemiological consistency is what the claim requires as a starting point for mechanistic investigation.

**According to PubMed**, this article (DOI: [10.1136/bmj.g5205](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.g5205)) generated substantial clinical discussion about benzodiazepine prescribing in older adults and has been cited extensively in deprescribing guidelines and pharmacovigilance literature.
