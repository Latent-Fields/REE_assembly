# Gray et al. 2015 — Cumulative anticholinergic use and incident dementia

**Entry:** 2026-04-05_mech175_anticholinergic_dementia_gray2015
**Claims:** MECH-175, INV-048 | **Direction:** supports | **Confidence:** 0.75
**Source:** JAMA Internal Medicine (2015) | DOI: 10.1001/jamainternmed.2014.7663

---

Gray and colleagues followed 3,434 community-dwelling adults aged 65 and over from the Group Health cohort for a mean of 7.3 years, using computerised pharmacy dispensing records to compute cumulative anticholinergic exposure in total standardised daily doses (TSDDs) over 10 years. Dementia and Alzheimer's disease were diagnosed via standard clinical criteria. The question was simple: does cumulative anticholinergic burden, measured objectively from prescribing records rather than self-report, predict dementia incidence in a dose-dependent way?

The answer was clear. Against no anticholinergic use, the adjusted hazard ratio for dementia at the highest exposure tier (>1095 TSDDs -- roughly 3 years of daily use) was 1.54 (95% CI 1.21-1.96). The dose-response relationship was statistically significant (test for trend p<0.001), and similar results were found specifically for Alzheimer's disease. The most commonly prescribed anticholinergic classes were tricyclic antidepressants, first-generation antihistamines, and bladder antimuscarinics -- exactly the classes predicted by MECH-175 to carry highest dual-pathway risk.

What should we make of this for MECH-175? The claim posits two independent pathways: nocturnal REM suppression (ACh drives PGO wave generation; blockade suppresses REM nightly) and diurnal cholinergic deficit mimicry (creating an iatrogenic analog of the basal forebrain cholinergic loss that characterises Alzheimer's). This paper can speak to neither pathway mechanistically -- it is a pharmacoepidemiology study, not a mechanistic one. What it provides is robust epidemiological confirmation that the association exists, is dose-dependent, and persists across multiple adjustment models. The plausibility of both pathways is supported; neither is specifically tested.

The honest caveat here is reverse causation. The authors addressed this carefully: they excluded the most recent 12 months of use (removing the most probable prodromal confounding), and they show the association persists for exposures 1-11 years before diagnosis. Anxiety, depression, and insomnia -- all potential prodromal dementia features that might drive anticholinergic prescribing -- were included as adjustment variables without substantially altering the result. The residual concern is that years 2-11 of prescribing still partially capture prodromal clinical trajectories not yet captured in coding.

For INV-048 (pharmacological sleep disruption equivalence), this paper is supporting but indirect: it documents that a class of medications with strong REM-suppressing and cholinergic effects produces the same downstream outcome (dementia) as behavioral sleep deprivation does epidemiologically. The pipeline equivalence claim requires mechanistic evidence that is not present here. What Gray et al. contribute is that the epidemiology is consistent with INV-048 -- the outcome pattern is what we would predict if pharmacological anticholinergic exposure damages the same pipeline as sleep deprivation.

**According to PubMed**, this article (DOI: [10.1001/jamainternmed.2014.7663](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamainternmed.2014.7663)) is a foundational prospective cohort study on anticholinergic burden and dementia risk, widely cited in deprescribing guidelines for older adults.
