# Snyder et al. 2016 — Suvorexant sleep architecture and power spectral profile

**Entry:** 2026-04-05_mech176_suvorexant_psg_snyder2016
**Claims:** MECH-176 | **Direction:** supports | **Confidence:** 0.78
**Source:** Sleep Medicine (2016) | DOI: 10.1016/j.sleep.2015.10.007

---

Snyder and colleagues (Merck) pooled PSG data from two Phase 3 suvorexant RCTs (n=1,482 insomnia patients) to characterise the sleep architecture effects of orexin receptor antagonism over 3 months. The analysis examined changes in sleep stage percentages, REM latency, and EEG spectral power -- the metrics most directly relevant to MECH-176's prediction that orexin antagonists preserve sleep architecture by removing wake-promoting drive rather than globally suppressing the nervous system.

The PSG findings are informative in detail. When expressed as percentage of total sleep time (the correct metric for assessing architectural effects, not absolute minutes which conflate architecture with total sleep time increases), suvorexant produces: small decreases in N1 (≤1%), N2 (≤2.2%), and N3/SWS (≤0.8%), and an average increase in REM% of up to 3.9%, largest at Night-1 and diminishing over time. REM latency was reduced by approximately 40-50 epochs at Night-1. In EEG power spectral terms, delta power (0.5-3 Hz, the slow oscillation signature of SWS homeostasis) increased 4-8% in non-REM sleep.

How should we read this for MECH-176? The strong form of the architecture-preservation claim would predict zero SWS change. The actual finding shows a small SWS% reduction (≤0.8%) alongside a meaningful REM% increase. The honest reading is that suvorexant does not fully preserve SWS in absolute stage terms, but the SWS delta power increase suggests that the quality -- the homeostatic depth -- of the SWS that does occur is maintained or enhanced. This is architecturally significant: what MECH-120 requires is adequate slow-oscillation power for synaptic normalisation, not necessarily the highest possible SWS stage time. The delta power increase is directly consistent with this mechanistic requirement.

The REM% increase of 3.9% at Night-1 is the clearest support for MECH-176. REM generation requires cholinergic rebound from REM-off monoaminergic suppression; orexin antagonism does not interfere with this machinery (unlike anticholinergics which suppress PGO waves directly, or benzodiazepines which compete with homeostatic REM generation). The reduced REM latency confirms that the REM-generating system is being uninhibited rather than suppressed.

The critical comparison this paper cannot provide -- suvorexant head-to-head against zolpidem or temazepam on the same PSG metrics -- is what would most directly test the architecture-preservation claim. The comparison to placebo establishes the profile; the claim about superiority to GABAergic agents requires a different study. Other published head-to-head or indirect comparisons (Herring 2020, Kishi meta-analysis 2020) support the architecture advantage, but this paper alone cannot establish it. Industry sponsorship by Merck is a genuine bias concern, though objective PSG measurement limits the scope for outcome manipulation.

**According to PubMed**, this article (DOI: [10.1016/j.sleep.2015.10.007](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.sleep.2015.10.007)) is a key Phase 3 characterisation study for suvorexant's sleep architecture profile and is frequently cited in comparative pharmacology reviews of hypnotics.
