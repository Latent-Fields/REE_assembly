# Summary: Salamone et al. 2016 -- Activational and Effort-Related Aspects of Motivation

**Claim tested:** ARC-030 (symmetric Go/NoGo sub-channels required; pure NoGo produces behavioral flatness)

**Source:** Salamone JD, Yohn SE, Lopez-Cruz L, San Miguel N, Correa M. "Activational and effort-related aspects of motivation: neural mechanisms and implications for psychopathology." Brain 2016;139(Pt 5):1325-47. DOI: 10.1093/brain/aww050

## What they did

Major translational review integrating animal model findings (rat T-maze effort tasks, barrier-climbing, progressive-ratio schedules with accumbens DA manipulations) with human clinical evidence from Parkinson's disease, depression, and schizophrenia effort-based decision-making studies. Focuses specifically on the "activational" dimension of motivation (speed and vigor of initiation and persistence) as distinct from directional motivation (what the goal is).

## Key findings

- Behavioral activation (approach vigor, effort exertion) is selectively impaired when accumbens dopamine signaling is disrupted, while avoidance sensitivity remains relatively preserved.
- Clinical syndromes of anergia, fatigue, lassitude, and psychomotor retardation across depression, schizophrenia, and Parkinson's disease all share this pattern: approach activation is lost while aversive/avoidance responding persists.
- In Parkinson's disease, dopamine restoration (L-DOPA) preferentially restores approach/effort behavior, consistent with D1/Go channel restoration.
- Animal models of effort-based decision-making are highly translatable to human clinical syndromes.

## REE relevance

This paper provides the translational bridge between the circuit-level optogenetic evidence (Kravitz 2010/2012) and the clinical/behavioral manifestation of pure-NoGo states. ARC-030 predicts that an architecture without Go sub-channels will produce behavioral flatness -- agents that can detect harm but cannot initiate approach. This paper documents exactly this syndrome in humans (anergia, apathy, psychomotor retardation) as the consequence of selective loss of the Go/approach signal.

For the REE paper gate, this establishes that the architectural requirement for symmetric Go/NoGo channels has clinical face validity: human disorders of motivational flatness are specifically characterized by approach-channel dysfunction with relatively preserved avoidance, not loss of both channels simultaneously.

## Limitations

Clinical syndromes involve many brain systems beyond striatum; DA depletion is global rather than D1-specific in most clinical populations. The paper does not directly test the Go/NoGo symmetry claim at the circuit level. The mapping to ARC-030 is at the functional syndrome level rather than mechanistic circuit level.
