# Summary: Kravitz et al. 2010 -- Optogenetic Control of Basal Ganglia Circuitry

**Claim tested:** ARC-030 (symmetric Go/NoGo sub-channels required; pure NoGo produces behavioral flatness)

**Source:** Kravitz AV, Freeze BS, Parker PR, Kay K, Thwin MT, Deisseroth K, Kreitzer AC. "Regulation of parkinsonian motor behaviours by optogenetic control of basal ganglia circuitry." Nature 2010;466(7306):622-6. DOI: 10.1038/nature09159

## What they did

Using Cre-dependent ChR2 viral expression in D1-Cre and D2-Cre transgenic mice, Kravitz et al. delivered bilateral optogenetic stimulation selectively to either direct-pathway (D1) or indirect-pathway (D2) medium spiny neurons (MSNs) in the striatum. They measured locomotion, freezing, and locomotor initiations before, during, and after stimulation.

## Key findings

- Bilateral D2 MSN (indirect/NoGo pathway) activation: increased freezing, bradykinesia, reduced locomotor initiations -- a parkinsonian phenotype. The animal became behaviorally flat (quiescent, unresponsive to approach incentives).
- Bilateral D1 MSN (direct/Go pathway) activation: reduced freezing, increased locomotion and approach.
- In a 6-OHDA Parkinson model, D1 pathway activation completely rescued approach deficits.
- The two pathways are not symmetric: D2 activation does not substitute for D1 -- it replaces approach behavior with suppression.

## REE relevance

This is the landmark bidirectional optogenetic demonstration of the Go/NoGo asymmetry. The pure-NoGo state (bilateral D2 activation) produces exactly what ARC-030 predicts: behavioral flatness (quiescence). The D1/Go channel is not merely a positive modifier of an avoidance signal -- it is a qualitatively distinct sub-channel required for behavioral initiation.

In the REE three-loop BG model, each loop requires both Go and NoGo sub-channels. This paper shows that replacing Go with additional NoGo does not produce normal behavior -- it produces Parkinson-like suppression. An architecture with only avoidance channels will fail to generate approach, regardless of incentive value.

## Limitations

The outcome measured is locomotor behavior, not reward-seeking in a cost-benefit task. Behavioral suppression could reflect motor deficits rather than motivational flatness per se. However, decreased locomotor initiations maps directly onto a failure to approach, and subsequent work (Kravitz 2012) confirmed the reinforcement-learning dissociation.
