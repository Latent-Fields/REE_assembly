# Botvinick et al. 2019 -- Reinforcement learning, fast and slow

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tics.2019.02.006), PMID 31003893), Botvinick and the DeepMind team review the question that has been animating deep RL since the field went mainstream: deep RL works, but it is too slow. Standard deep-RL agents need millions of episodes where animals need tens. The review surveys two research directions that close the gap. The episodic-memory direction (fast retrieval) augments the agent with explicit memory mechanisms -- recall a previously-seen state and bypass slow weight-learning. The meta-learning direction (slow algorithmic shaping) trains the weights of a recurrent network across a task family so that the network's hidden-state dynamics implement an efficient inner-loop learning algorithm. The two directions are complementary, and the strongest deep-RL systems combine them: episodic memory for rapid recognition of recurring situations, meta-RL for adaptive generalisation within and across task instances.

For MECH-318 this is the architectural-framework anchor. The Wang 2018 paper (also in this pull) is the primary meta-RL anchor; the Duan 2016 paper is the foundational RL² architecture anchor. Botvinick et al. 2019 supplies the synthesis that places meta-RL within a broader architecture that includes episodic memory. The implication for MECH-318 is that it should NOT be designed as a standalone meta-RL module. The right architectural framing is that MECH-318 (rule-state abstraction via recurrent hidden state) composes with REE's existing episodic-memory substrates -- MECH-269 anchor sets, MECH-292 ranked ghost-goal bank, MECH-293 awake ghost-goal probes -- which together carry the fast-retrieval role.

The architectural reading: V3 already has substantial fast-retrieval substrate (the MECH-269 family). What V3 lacks is the slow-meta-RL substrate, which is what MECH-318 names. The right substrate-design move is therefore not to build MECH-318 from scratch in isolation but to design it to integrate with the existing episodic substrates. There are at least three integration patterns:

- **Trigger-layer integration**: episodic retrieval signals when the meta-RL substrate should be active. When the agent recognises a familiar anchor-cluster, the episodic substrate handles the response; when it doesn't, the meta-RL substrate engages.
- **Readout-layer integration**: both substrates feed into action selection in parallel. The episodic retrieval supplies a fast direct-policy bias; the meta-RL recurrent-state supplies a slower adaptive-policy bias. They sum at the dacc_score_bias level (where SD-033a and ARC-062 already compose).
- **Training-layer integration**: episodic substrates supply training data for the meta-RL substrate. The trajectory record stored in anchor sets and ghost-goal banks becomes the multi-task training distribution that shapes the meta-RL substrate's weights -- a partial answer to the W2 gap the absorption check identified.

The third integration pattern is the most architecturally interesting because it addresses the W2 multi-task-training gap that the 2026-05-10 absorption check flagged as not absorbed by any current V3 substrate. If the agent's accumulated trajectory record across many anchor-clusters constitutes the 'task family' over which the meta-RL substrate's weights should be shaped, then the W2 gap may be closeable within V3 without commissioning a separate multi-task training environment. The trajectory record is the multi-task training distribution. This is a substrate-design hypothesis that the V3-EXQ-543c-successor (or a substrate-design pass following this lit-pull) should test.

The fast-and-slow framing also reframes the V4 question. The W5 cross-episode-continuity gap (which Duan 2016 RL² anchors as the defining feature) does not need to be closed by MECH-318 alone. The episodic substrates already provide cross-episode persistence (anchor sets, ghost-goal banks are accumulated across episodes; they reset within-episode but not across). If MECH-318 reads its 'task identity' input from the episodic substrates, the cross-episode continuity is provided by the episodic substrates rather than by the meta-RL substrate's own hidden-state continuity. This is a softer form of the W5 property than RL² strict-reading but may be empirically sufficient.

The mapping caveat I would not want to bury: Botvinick et al. 2019 is a review at the framework level, not a substrate-prescriptive paper. The fast-and-slow framework is normative -- it says the best agents combine episodic memory with meta-RL -- but does not specify how the coupling should work at the substrate level. For REE the implication is that MECH-318 should be designed to compose with existing episodic substrates rather than as a standalone module. Whether the composition is at the trigger layer, the readout layer, or the training layer is a substrate-design call that this review does not by itself resolve. The substrate-design pass that follows this lit-pull should treat the fast-and-slow framing as the architectural constraint and choose among the three integration patterns based on empirical tractability.
