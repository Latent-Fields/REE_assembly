# Constantinescu, O'Reilly & Behrens (2016): Organizing conceptual knowledge in humans with a gridlike code

**Source:** Science 352(6292):1464-1468. DOI: 10.1126/science.aaf0941
**Claim tested:** MECH-155 (spatial navigation as general associative indexing)

## What the paper did

Constantinescu and colleagues tested whether the hexagonal grid-cell code -- thought to implement a metric for spatial navigation -- would emerge when humans navigate a non-spatial two-dimensional conceptual space. Participants viewed and learned images of birds that varied continuously along two independent morphological dimensions: neck length and leg length. After training, participants underwent fMRI while performing a task that required them to navigate this conceptual space -- to mentally transform one bird into another by changing one or both dimensions. The key analysis looked for the hexagonally symmetric BOLD modulation in entorhinal cortex that is the canonical fMRI signature of grid cell activity in spatial tasks.

## Key findings relevant to MECH-155

The study found a clear hexagonal signal in entorhinal cortex during conceptual navigation: BOLD signal was modulated at 60-degree intervals of the conceptual trajectory direction, matching the rotational symmetry of a hexagonal grid. The signal was present in entorhinal cortex and ventromedial prefrontal cortex -- regions previously implicated in spatial grid coding and value-based cognition respectively. Critically, the hexagonal modulation was specific to the conceptual navigation condition rather than to passive viewing of bird stimuli, indicating that the grid-like code was recruited during relational traversal rather than mere sensory processing. The pattern of activation was strikingly similar to what is observed during spatial navigation, supporting the interpretation that the entorhinal grid system is a domain-general relational indexing mechanism.

## REE translation

MECH-155 requires that the hippocampal-parietal spatial navigation system represents a general-purpose associative indexing mechanism -- one that has been co-opted for abstract cognitive domains beyond physical space. Constantinescu et al. 2016 is the strongest primary empirical evidence for this claim. The finding that entorhinal cortex produces grid-like geometry for an abstract, non-spatial conceptual space (bird morphology) is a direct demonstration that the spatial indexing system is recruited for a non-spatial relational dimension. For the REE architecture, this matters because E1's indexed manifold geometry -- the feature enabling addressing and traversal -- should not be confined to spatial stimuli. If the underlying structure is relational (items can be ordered along meaningful dimensions with a metric), the entorhinal-hippocampal system will impose grid-like geometry on it. E1, as a learned forward model of the agent's world, will acquire representations across many structured dimensions, and this paper provides the existence proof that grid-like indexing emerges in at least one non-spatial case.

## Limitations

The abstract dimension tested is perceptually continuous and parametric -- it is not symbolic or fully compositional in the way conceptual knowledge often is. Bird-neck and bird-leg length form a clean two-dimensional metric space; real abstract knowledge is higher-dimensional and less metrically regular. The fMRI-to-mechanism gap is non-trivial: the hexagonal BOLD modulation is a population-level signal inferred from BOLD, not direct evidence of single-cell grid firing as in rodent electrophysiology. The grid-cell fMRI literature has encountered replication difficulties in some paradigms, and the signal at the whole-brain level is relatively small. The ventromedial prefrontal co-activation suggests value and relationship computations are entangled, which raises questions about whether the hexagonal signal reflects pure indexing or mixed relational-evaluative coding.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence of 0.82 reflects a genuinely strong primary study in humans providing direct evidence for the core claim of MECH-155, moderated by the limitations of fMRI methodology and the specific (perceptually grounded, 2D) nature of the abstract space tested. The paper has been widely cited as foundational for the cognitive-map-as-general-indexer view, and subsequent replication attempts in related domains (social hierarchy, value spaces) have extended the finding, though not without controversy. The confidence is not higher because the mechanistic gap between BOLD modulation and circuit-level grid-cell firing is irreducible with current human neuroscience methods.
