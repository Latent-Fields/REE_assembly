# Warren et al. 2017 — Atomoxetine selectively modulates random exploration

**Citation.** Warren CM, Wilson RC, van der Wee NJ, Giltay EJ, van Noorden MS, Cohen JD, Nieuwenhuis S. (2017). The effect of atomoxetine on random and directed exploration in humans. *PLoS One* 12(4):e0176034. [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0176034). PMID 28445519.

## What the paper did

This is the pharmacological mirror image of Zajkowski 2017. Warren and colleagues asked which neuromodulatory system supports random exploration. The Aston-Jones & Cohen 2005 adaptive-gain theory of locus coeruleus function predicts that elevating tonic norepinephrine should disengage the brain from the current task and promote exploratory behaviour. Atomoxetine, a selective norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor, raises extracellular NE and is approved for ADHD. In a within-subject double-blind crossover, 22 healthy adults played the Horizon task under 40mg atomoxetine and under placebo.

The result was selective in the substrate sense but unexpected in direction. Atomoxetine had no significant effect on directed exploration but did significantly affect random exploration — and in the opposite direction from the adaptive-gain prediction. Random exploration DECREASED under atomoxetine. The authors offer three explanations: the tonic-NE/performance curve may be non-linear (inverted-U with the placebo state already near the peak), atomoxetine may interact with other neuromodulators in ways that obscure the LC-specific effect, or atomoxetine may preferentially affect phasic rather than tonic NE.

## Relevance to Q-043

The selectivity finding is the load-bearing piece. Combined with Zajkowski 2017's frontopolar TMS dissociation, Warren 2017 gives a (partial) double dissociation: a cortical lesion-like manipulation selectively impairs directed exploration; a pharmacological NE manipulation selectively affects random exploration. This is much stronger evidence for the architectural premise of Q-043 than either paper alone — it makes the two-mechanism hypothesis difficult to dismiss as a measurement artifact.

For REE, this anchors MECH-313 in the LC-NE substrate. Together with Aston-Jones & Cohen 2005, the ARC-066/067 family doc's commitment to MECH-313 as an LC-NE tonic analog has its strongest empirical support here.

## Caveats — the direction problem

The reason I classified this as "mixed" rather than "supports" is the directional mismatch. If higher tonic NE produces more random exploration (the adaptive-gain prediction, encoded in REE as "higher MECH-313 weight produces more diversity"), then atomoxetine should INCREASE random exploration, not decrease it. The decrease is real and replicated by parameter recovery in the Warren paper. The three explanations the authors offer are all plausible but mutually inconsistent, and the paper cannot adjudicate among them.

For REE's V3 parametric sweep, this should not undermine MECH-313's existence as a substrate, but it should make us cautious about assuming the relationship between MECH-313 weight and behavioural diversity is monotone. The sweep design in Q-043 should be prepared to find a non-monotone Pareto frontier — analogous to a Yerkes-Dodson inverted-U — where there is an optimum at intermediate weight and behaviour worsens (in either direction) at the extremes. The REE design implicitly assumes monotonicity by parameterising the regulator with a single scalar weight; the empirical literature suggests this is too simple.

I am also conscious that atomoxetine is not a clean LC-tonic probe — it affects cortical NE broadly via reuptake blockade. The MECH-313 ≈ LC-NE mapping is convenient but loose; what Warren 2017 directly establishes is that some neuromodulatory system distinct from the frontopolar directed-exploration substrate modulates random exploration. Calling it "LC-NE tonic" is an architectural simplification.
