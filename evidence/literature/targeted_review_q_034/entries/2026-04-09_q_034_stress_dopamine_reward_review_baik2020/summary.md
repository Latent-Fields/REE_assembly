# Stress and the Dopaminergic Reward System
**Baik (2020) — Experimental and Molecular Medicine**

Baik's review provides the mechanistic bridge that the other Q-034 entries lack. Where Maier & Seligman (2016) establish that resource access (controllability) is the decisive threshold parameter, and Lehmann & Herkenham (2011) demonstrate the protective effect of resource-rich environments against fixed hazard, Baik explains the circuit by which this interaction is implemented at the level of mesolimbic dopamine. Chronic stress suppresses dopaminergic reward transmission: VTA firing patterns become dysregulated, NAc dopamine release to rewarding stimuli is blunted, and D1/D2 receptor sensitivity is altered. The consequence is anhedonia -- reduced capacity to derive benefit from reward-delivering events in the environment. Crucially, the review frames this not merely as collateral damage but as a functional failure: the dopaminergic reward system is required for selecting and sustaining adaptive coping responses under stress. When reward sensitivity fails, the agent's ability to use available resources is impaired even if those resources remain physically present.

This has a direct implication for Q-034. The claim is that the hazard/resource ratio -- not absolute hazard -- determines the onset threshold for the depression attractor (INV-053). Baik's review establishes two things relevant to this threshold. First, chronic stress progressively degrades the reward system's sensitivity, meaning that a fixed level of benefit_exposure becomes functionally equivalent to lower and lower resource density as the system deteriorates. The threshold is dynamic, not static: the same resource density that is protective at baseline may be insufficient after sustained harm exposure because the agent's capacity to benefit from resources has eroded. Second, the NAc integrates both reward and aversive signals -- it is not a pure reward monitor but a value-tracking system that reflects the ratio of positive and negative inputs. This positions the NAc as the biological candidate for the integrator that Q-034's threshold mechanism requires.

In the REE framework, SD-012 (homeostatic drive) proposes that drive_level must scale benefit_exposure to seed z_goal. Baik's review grounds this computationally: mesolimbic dopamine is the biological drive_level signal, and chronic stress suppresses it. If z_goal seeding requires a dopamine signal above threshold, and chronic harm exposure erodes that signal, then there exists a ratio of harm to benefit below which z_goal seeding fails -- precisely the attractor entry condition Q-034 describes. The review also supports the clinical prediction: interventions targeting benefit availability (not just stress reduction) should be therapeutic, because restoring reward-system sensitivity breaks the suppression cycle independently of hazard reduction.

The limitation of this entry is that it is a narrative review offering mechanistic grounding rather than direct experimental evidence for the ratio or threshold. No study is cited that holds hazard constant while varying reward density parametrically across the threshold. Confidence is 0.52 -- useful mechanistic support but insufficient as standalone evidence. It functions best in combination with the Pizzagalli (2014), Maier/Seligman (2016), and Lehmann/Herkenham (2011) entries, which together establish the principle, the behavioral evidence, and the mechanism respectively.
