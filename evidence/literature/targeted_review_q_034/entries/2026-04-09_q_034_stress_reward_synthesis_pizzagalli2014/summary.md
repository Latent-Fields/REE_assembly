# Summary: Pizzagalli (2014) -- Depression, Stress, and Anhedonia: Toward a Synthesis and Integrated Model

## What the study did

Pizzagalli (2014) is a comprehensive review synthesising evidence on the relationship between stress and reward system dysfunction in depression, with particular attention to anhedonia as a transdiagnostic symptom. The paper draws on animal models, human neuroimaging, and clinical data to construct an integrated model in which depression arises not from stress or reward dysfunction in isolation, but from their dysfunctional interaction. The central argument is that stress sensitises the reward system, and that reward engagement under stress provides protection against the neurobiological consequences of chronic harm exposure.

## Key findings

The critical evidence for Q-034 is the observation that animals and humans who successfully acquire and maintain appetitive behaviour under stress show protection against stress-induced dopaminergic changes. In animal models of chronic stress, those with prior reward learning show preserved dopamine transporter function in striatal reward circuits compared to stress-only controls. In human data, reward processing engagement under mild stress conditions predicted resilience against subsequent depression symptoms in a longitudinal design. The paper explicitly frames controllability -- the availability of effective responses -- as a more powerful moderator of depression risk than stress magnitude itself. A fixed level of stress with active reward engagement (resources, viable responses) does not produce the same neurobiological signature as the same stress level in a reward-depleted context.

## Limitations and caveats

This is a review paper; while the synthesis is persuasive, the original studies vary widely in methodology, stress type, reward measure, and population. No single study provides a clean parametric test of the ratio (fixed harm, varying reward density). The human longitudinal data are correlational. The animal data are from forced-swim and social defeat models -- established but contested analogues for human depression. The paper does not use attractor state language and does not formalise a threshold function; the ratio logic must be inferred from the interaction framing.
