# Environmental Enrichment Confers Stress Resiliency to Social Defeat through an Infralimbic Cortex-Dependent Neuroanatomical Pathway
**Lehmann & Herkenham (2011) — Journal of Neuroscience**

Lehmann and Herkenham ask a simple question with far-reaching implications: does the quality of an animal's resource environment before stress exposure determine whether that stress produces lasting depression-like pathology? The answer is yes. Mice housed in an enriched environment for 21 days prior to 14 days of social defeat stress showed significantly less depressive-like behaviour than mice in standard or impoverished housing. The protective effect was not minor: enriched mice maintained near-normal scores on forced swim, tail suspension, and social interaction tests, while their standard-housed counterparts showed the characteristic defeat-induced passivity, anhedonia, and social avoidance. Critically, the infralimbic cortex lesion experiment establishes circuit-level mediation — destroy this prefrontal region before enrichment and the protective effect disappears, implying that the benefit of a richer resource environment is computed and maintained in frontal circuits, not simply expressed as a general fitness advantage.

The relevance to Q-034 is direct: this is one of the few experiments that explicitly holds the hazard fixed (social defeat is standardised across groups) while varying the resource environment, then measures the binary outcome of whether the depression attractor is entered. The prediction of Q-034 is exactly this: sufficient resource density, at fixed hazard, prevents the attractor transition. The Lehmann/Herkenham result confirms the qualitative prediction. The enriched environment elevated baseline FosB/DeltaFosB expression in nucleus accumbens and prefrontal cortex during stress — precisely the reward-circuit regions whose sustained activity is associated with resilience in the social defeat literature (cf. Krishnan et al. 2007). The infralimbic cortex (rodent homologue of human vmPFC subgenual region) acts as the interface between resource environment history and stress response — a function analogous to E3's role in REE as the module that evaluates whether current resource density is sufficient to achieve harm-reduction goals.

The primary limitation is that environmental enrichment is a composite manipulation. It increases novelty exposure, physical exercise opportunities, social interaction, and access to rewarding objects simultaneously. The experiment cannot isolate which component drives protection; reward density per se is never varied independently. Q-034 requires that benefit_exposure (reward opportunity density) is the active ingredient, not exercise-induced neuroplasticity or novelty-driven arousal. A second limitation is the absence of parametric threshold data: the study compares enriched vs standard vs impoverished at fixed time points, not a graded resource-level experiment that would reveal the ratio value at which protection fails. The claim requires a threshold; this experiment establishes that the threshold exists but does not locate it.

Confidence is 0.66: strong structural support for the qualitative prediction (resource environment determines stress outcome at fixed hazard), moderate mapping fidelity due to compound nature of enrichment, and moderate transfer risk from social defeat / mouse model to CausalGridWorldV2 / REE agent. The infralimbic lesion result is a valuable mechanistic detail that links the finding to E3-mediated goal maintenance rather than peripheral or non-specific mechanisms.
