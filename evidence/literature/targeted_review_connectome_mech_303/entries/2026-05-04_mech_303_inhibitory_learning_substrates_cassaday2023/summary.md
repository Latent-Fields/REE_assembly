# Neural Substrates of Inhibitory Learning: Appetitive vs. Aversive (Cassaday et al. 2023)

**Source:** Cassaday HJ, Muir C, Stevenson CW, Bonardi C, Hock R, Waite L. "From safety to frustration: The neural substrates of inhibitory learning in aversive and appetitive conditioning procedures." *Neurobiology of Learning and Memory* 202:107757, 2023. DOI: [10.1016/j.nlm.2023.107757](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.nlm.2023.107757). From PubMed (PMID 37044368).

**What the study did.** A review examining whether the neural substrates of conditioned inhibition are shared or distinct across aversive procedures (shock-based, generating safety signals) and appetitive procedures (food-based, generating frustration signals when reward is unexpectedly omitted). The question is architecturally fundamental: does conditioned inhibition have a unitary substrate, or does substrate depend on the motivational valence of the omitted outcome?

**Key findings.** Three substrates are consistently implicated across both valences: (1) mesocorticolimbic dopamine pathways; (2) retrosplenial cortex; (3) hippocampus. Some additional substrates appear modality- or task-specific -- particular to the sensory modality of the conditioned inhibitor or to the type of procedural variant used. The translational implications extend to obesity, addiction, and anxiety disorders, reflecting the broad scope of conditioned inhibition as a mechanism.

**REE translation.** MECH-303 claims the safety-cue prediction store is parallel to but distinct from the 'things liked' (reward/approach) goal-cue store, routing through prefrontal-hippocampal rather than ventral-striatal substrate. This review complicates the 'rather than ventral-striatal' framing in two ways.

First, the mesocorticolimbic dopamine pathway -- which in most architectures overlaps substantially with the ventral-striatal reward circuit -- is shared between aversive inhibitory learning (safety signals) and appetitive inhibitory learning (frustration signals). If dopamine projections participate in safety signal encoding, the clean reward-vs-safety substrate dissociation MECH-303 assumes may be oversimplified.

Second, the hippocampus being consistently implicated across both valences supports MECH-303's hippocampal component but suggests this is not a unique signature of the safety store -- the hippocampus is involved in inhibitory learning generally.

The fact that some substrates are task- and modality-specific is indirect support for partial separability, but the review does not resolve whether aversive inhibitory learning (safety) and appetitive excitatory learning ('things liked') have dissociable substrates -- which is the dissociation MECH-303 specifically requires.

**Limitations and caveats.** Being a review, findings aggregate across heterogeneous paradigms, species, and labs. The appetitive-vs-aversive comparison used in the review (frustration vs. safety) is not the same as the reward-predictor-vs-safety-predictor comparison that MECH-303 requires. The shared dopamine finding's challenge to MECH-303 is genuine but not decisive -- the ventral vs. dorsal striatal projection may differ, and the dopamine teaching signal is not the same as the encoding store.

**Confidence: 0.72.** Useful scope-setting review in a matched venue. Mapping fidelity is lower because the comparative axis (appetitive inhibition vs. aversive inhibition) differs from the architectural axis MECH-303 needs tested (safety-prediction store vs. reward-prediction store).
