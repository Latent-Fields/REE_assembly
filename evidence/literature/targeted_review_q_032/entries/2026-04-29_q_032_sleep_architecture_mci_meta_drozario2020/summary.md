# D'Rozario et al. 2020 -- Sleep architecture in MCI: systematic review and meta-analysis

[DOI: 10.1016/j.smrv.2020.101308](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.smrv.2020.101308)

## What they did

Systematic review of objective sleep measurement (PSG) in mild cognitive impairment, searching PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science, Embase, and Psycinfo. After application of inclusion criteria, 10 case-control studies were retained. The combined sample was 430 participants -- 209 with MCI and 221 cognitively healthy controls. All 10 reported macro-architecture (sleep stages); 4 reported microarchitecture (spindles, EEG spectral analysis). The analytic plan was meta-analytic pooling for macro metrics where homogeneity permitted, and qualitative synthesis for microarchitecture.

## Key findings relevant to Q-032

The MCI architectural signature, compared with healthy controls, was multi-faceted: greater wake after sleep onset, reduced total sleep time, lower sleep efficiency, longer sleep onset latency, longer REM latency, reduced REM sleep, greater N1 sleep, and worse hypoxemia severity. Microarchitecture pooling was limited by the small number of qualifying studies, but reduced spindles in NREM and greater EEG slowing during REM were both reported. This is the cleanest cross-sectional architectural-fingerprint of MCI available in the literature, and it is the kind of multi-metric signature any Q-032 PD biomarker would have to capture.

## How this maps to REE

The REE pipeline-phase architecture decomposes sleep into four functional phases: Phase 1 (SHY homeostasis, MECH-120), Phase 2 (schema replay, MECH-121), Phase 3 (spindle coordination, MECH-122), and Phase 4 (REM precision recalibration, MECH-123). Q-032's PD-biomarker proposal asks whether PSG can detect drug-induced disruption of these phases, beyond what total-sleep-time captures. D'Rozario 2020 establishes that MCI shows simultaneous disruption across multiple phases, with the strongest signals in Phase 4 (reduced REM, longer REM latency, EEG slowing in REM) and supporting signals in Phase 3 (reduced spindles) and Phases 1/2 (greater N1, worse efficiency). This multi-phase signature is what a properly architecture-aware Q-032 biomarker would target -- not a single stage percentage, but a fingerprint across all four phases.

The microarchitecture findings are particularly interesting from an REE pharmacological standpoint. Spindle reduction in MCI maps onto Phase 3 disruption (MECH-122). EEG slowing in REM maps onto Phase 4 disruption (MECH-123). These are both pharmacologically actionable -- benzodiazepines suppress spindles, anticholinergics suppress REM -- which means a drug-class differential PD biomarker is in principle constructible from these features.

## Limitations and caveats

The most important caveat is design. Case-control studies cannot establish predictive direction. The architectural signature observed in MCI could be a downstream consequence of the same neurodegeneration that produces MCI, rather than an upstream cause or a pharmacodynamically modifiable mediator. To say "drug X preserves Phase 3 spindles, therefore drug X protects against MCI" requires not just that MCI shows reduced spindles cross-sectionally, but that pharmacologically preserving spindles in at-risk individuals reduces conversion to MCI. The latter is the trial-level evidence Q-032 ultimately needs, and D'Rozario 2020 does not provide it.

The 10 included studies were also heterogeneous in PSG protocol (lab vs home), montage (full vs limited), and MCI definition (some studies used Petersen criteria, others used clinical adjudication). The pooled estimates therefore have wider real-world uncertainty than the formal CIs imply. Microarchitecture pooling was particularly limited because only 4 of 10 studies reported microarchitecture metrics -- precisely the features most relevant to a pharmacologically actionable PD biomarker.
