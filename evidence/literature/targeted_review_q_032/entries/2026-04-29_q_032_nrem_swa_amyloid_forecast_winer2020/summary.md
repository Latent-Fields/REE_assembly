# Winer, Mander, Walker et al. 2020 -- Sleep disturbance forecasts beta-amyloid accumulation

[DOI: 10.1016/j.cub.2020.08.017](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cub.2020.08.017)

## What they did

A cohort of clinically normal older adults underwent overnight polysomnography at baseline and longitudinal [11C]PiB positron emission tomography imaging to track cortical beta-amyloid deposition over subsequent years. The analytic question was whether PSG-derived sleep metrics could prospectively forecast the rate (slope) of amyloid accumulation, controlling for age, sex, and sleep apnea. Crucially, the design tested architecture-specific specificity: each macro-stage (N1, N2, N3, REM) and each microarchitecture metric (slow-wave activity in different frequency bands, spindle density, sleep efficiency) was examined separately for forecasting sensitivity.

## Key findings relevant to Q-032

Two PSG measures emerged as prospective forecasters of amyloid slope: the proportion of NREM slow-wave activity (SWA) below 1 Hz, and sleep efficiency. These associations remained robust after adjustment for age, sex, and sleep apnea. The specificity test is the methodological centerpiece -- no other macro- or microarchitecture metric demonstrated this forecasting sensitivity. Total SWS percentage at the macro-stage level was not the signal; rather, it was the sub-1 Hz frequency component of slow-wave activity within NREM. This is exactly the kind of architecture-specific signature Q-032's pharmacodynamic biomarker proposal requires.

## How this maps to REE

Q-032 sits at the junction of three sub-questions: (a) does PSG-measured sleep architecture predict dementia, (b) do drugs differentially preserving that architecture produce differential cognitive outcomes, and (c) is the resulting metric a validated PD biomarker for trial design. Winer et al. address (a) via the biological-surrogate route and provide the strongest methodological template for (c). The forecasting design -- prospective, with longitudinal tracking of a biological intermediate, with explicit cross-metric specificity testing -- is what any Q-032 trial would need to demonstrate before SWS% (or SWA <1 Hz) could be granted regulatory acceptance as a PD biomarker.

For the REE pipeline-phase model, the localisation to SWA <1 Hz is interesting. The REE framework decomposes sleep into Phase 1 (SHY homeostasis, MECH-120) and Phase 2 (schema replay, MECH-121), and predicts these are most directly linked to glymphatic amyloid clearance. The <1 Hz signal corresponds to slow oscillations classically interpreted as Phase 1/2 substrate, which is at least directionally consistent. Phase 3 (spindle coordination, MECH-122) and Phase 4 (REM precision recalibration, MECH-123) did not carry the forecasting signal here, which the REE framework would predict for amyloid specifically (vs. e.g. tau or memory-consolidation outcomes).

## Limitations and caveats

Two transfer steps separate this finding from Q-032's intended endpoint. First, beta-amyloid accumulation is a biological surrogate, not clinical dementia. The path from "drug X preserves SWA <1 Hz; this slows amyloid accumulation" to "drug X reduces dementia incidence" requires the amyloid-dementia link to hold over the trial timescale, which it does on average but with substantial inter-individual variability. Second, the forecasting signal localised to NREM SWA below 1 Hz, which is a research-grade microarchitecture metric requiring spectral analysis rather than routine clinical PSG stage scoring. Treating clinically-reported SWS% as equivalent to SWA <1 Hz is a methodological simplification that any Q-032 trial design would have to confront -- either by upgrading PSG analysis to research grade, or by demonstrating that macroscopic SWS% tracks SWA <1 Hz tightly enough for trial-level inference.

The cohort was also clinically normal at baseline. The paper does not test whether the architecture-amyloid relationship holds in mild cognitive impairment, nor in samples where SWS suppression is pharmacologically induced. The latter is exactly the comparison Q-032's second sub-question requires.
