# Himali et al. 2023 -- Slow-wave sleep loss and incident dementia (Framingham)

[DOI: 10.1001/jamaneurol.2023.3889](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamaneurol.2023.3889)

## What they did

Framingham Heart Study participants age 60+ underwent two overnight polysomnography (PSG) nights, separated by a mean of 5.2 years (1995-1998 and 2001-2003). Cohort of 346 with no dementia at the second PSG. Over 17 years of subsequent follow-up, 52 incident dementia cases were adjudicated. The exposure was the within-person change in slow-wave sleep percentage between the two PSGs. Cox regression modelled dementia hazard as a function of the per-year SWS slope, adjusting for age, sex, cohort, APOE epsilon-4 carrier status, smoking, sleep medication use, antidepressant use, and anxiolytic use.

## Key findings relevant to Q-032

Each percentage-point reduction in SWS per year was associated with a 27 percent increase in dementia hazard (HR 1.27, 95% CI 1.06-1.54, p=0.01). APOE epsilon-4 carriers showed accelerated SWS decline, implicating Alzheimer-typical genetic risk in the rate of SWS loss. Hippocampal volumes measured near the first PSG were not associated with SWS decline, which weakens but does not eliminate the reverse-causation worry. Importantly, the result survived adjustment for sleep medication and antidepressant use -- the most plausible non-disease drivers of SWS suppression.

## How this maps to REE

Q-032 asks whether PSG-derived sleep architecture metrics, particularly SWS%, can serve as pharmacodynamic biomarkers for disease-modifying trials in dementia. The premise of that proposal has two parts: (1) SWS% must independently predict dementia at the individual level, and (2) drug-induced SWS suppression must produce a similar outcome signature to endogenous SWS loss. Himali 2023 establishes the first half. The within-person change design is the strongest possible epidemiological evidence for SWS% as a real biomarker -- not a ratio to total sleep time, not a one-night snapshot, but the trajectory of SWS across years. INV-048 in the REE pipeline framework needs exactly this: a phase-specific signal that decouples from total sleep time. The fact that APOE4 accelerates SWS decline is a plausibility argument for the SHY homeostasis / schema replay (MECH-120/121) pathway being upstream of dementia outcome, not just downstream.

## Limitations and caveats

The 52 incident cases drive precision. The HR confidence interval is wide enough that a much smaller effect cannot be ruled out, even if the trend direction is robust. APOE4 acceleration is a double-edged finding: it suggests SWS decline is partly driven by neurodegeneration itself, not just by upstream sleep-disrupting exposures. Two PSGs five years apart is a sparse sample of a multi-decade trajectory; it cannot distinguish a person whose SWS dropped suddenly at age 65 from one who declined gradually. The paper cannot tell us whether drug-induced SWS suppression carries the same dementia signature as endogenous decline -- that is the second half of the Q-032 proposal and remains untested. Total SWS% is also coarse: it cannot distinguish Phase 1/2 (homeostatic SWS depth) from Phase 3 (spindle-mediated coordination) signatures, which the REE pipeline architecture treats as separable.

## Confidence reasoning

I rate this 0.82. Source quality is high (Framingham, JAMA Neurology, two-PSG within-person design, 17y follow-up, comprehensive covariate adjustment including all major sleep-affecting medication classes). Mapping fidelity is high because the paper directly measures SWS-trajectory as predictor of dementia, which is the population-level prerequisite for Q-032's PD-biomarker proposal. Transfer risk is moderate -- the chain "drug-induced SWS suppression behaves like endogenous SWS decline" is plausible but unverified, and APOE4 acceleration suggests some reverse-causation. The right reading is "Himali establishes the first half of Q-032 cleanly; the second half (drug-class differential outcomes) is still empirically open."
