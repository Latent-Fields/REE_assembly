# Posture-Specific Harm Geography -- Burnay et al. (2020)

## What the paper does

Burnay et al. examined how specific locomotor experiences shape infants' avoidance behavior at drop-offs, using the classic visual cliff paradigm with both real and water cliffs. N=102 infants, split between crawlers (mean 11.6 months, crawling experience ranging 0-7.4 months) and walkers (mean 14.8 months, walking experience ranging 0.1-5.2 months). The key manipulation was measuring the association between duration of crawling vs. walking experience and drop-off avoidance behavior in each group.

## Key findings relevant to REE

Crawling experience -- not walking experience -- predicted drop-off avoidance in walkers. Longer crawling experience facilitated faster recalibration to the walking posture, suggesting cumulative exploratory benefit. However, the recalibration is incomplete: walkers who become novice walkers must re-learn the drop-off affordance even if they are experienced crawlers. Avoidance of the water cliff and real cliff did not differ, but infants explored the water cliff more -- suggesting differential enticement to examine bodies of water, which is a valence-relevant finding (water is intrinsically interesting/attractive despite drop-off danger).

## How this maps to REE

INV-055 specifies that the infant stage must accumulate harm/benefit geography through novelty-driven exploration. Burnay et al. provide direct evidence that harm geography is encoded in a posture-specific, action-mode-specific manner. For REE's design: (1) the harm residue field (ARC-007 / harm geography) accumulated during the exploration epoch must be populated using the action repertoire the agent will use in subsequent stages, not some proxy modality; (2) the cumulative benefit of experience suggests longer exploration epochs produce better-calibrated geography; (3) the water cliff enticement finding is intriguing -- novel/interesting stimuli attract approach even when dangerous, which is a candidate mechanism for how benefit contacts are discovered alongside harm contacts.

The posture-specificity finding strengthens INV-073: the exploration epoch must not only be extensive (quantity) but must cover the action modalities the agent will actually use in goal-directed behavior (quality).

## Limitations and caveats

The paper addresses harm avoidance only -- the benefit/reward side of the valence geography is not addressed. The mapping to REE's residue field requires that the harm geography operates analogously to the posture-specific perceptual calibration described here. Cross-sectional design limits causal inference.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence 0.70 reflects good source quality (peer-reviewed, N=102, Developmental Science) with moderate mapping fidelity (harm geography component addressed, benefit component not). The posture-specificity finding provides specific substrate design guidance that the other papers do not.
