# Ramirez, Moscarello, LeDoux & Sears 2015 — Active avoidance requires a serial basal amygdala to nucleus accumbens shell circuit

## What the paper did

The LeDoux lab took rats trained on two-way signaled active avoidance — the animal hears a tone (CS), and if it shuttles to the other side of the chamber it avoids the footshock that would otherwise follow — and asked which circuit is necessary for the avoidance behaviour itself. They started with c-Fos mapping after a shock-free retention test, found increased activity in the nucleus accumbens shell (NAcSh) but not the adjacent NAc core. They then silenced each region pharmacologically, finding that NAcSh inactivation disrupted avoidance and NAc core inactivation did not. Finally, they used a disconnection design — silencing the basal amygdala (BA) on one side and the NAcSh contralaterally — to test whether the BA-to-NAcSh projection is the relevant pathway. It was.

## Key finding

Active avoidance — behaviour that terminates an impending aversive episode — depends on a serial circuit running from the basal amygdala to the nucleus accumbens shell. This sits in clear contrast to passive defensive responses (freezing, escape jumps, etc.), which depend on the central amygdala and periaqueductal gray. The avoidance circuit is not simply a defensive-system circuit; it routes through reward-system territory at its action-selection node.

## How it translates to REE

The architectural implication is the one Andreatta 2012 already gestured at in fMRI and Navratilova 2012 showed at the dopamine level, but extended now to the *behavioural-control* side rather than just the reinforcement-signal side. Behaviour that successfully reduces suffering is gated by a circuit that overlaps with the goal-directed-behaviour circuit. For REE, this is consistent with Model 1: the avoidance-policy machinery (selecting and executing actions that reduce suffering) shares substrate with the approach-policy machinery (selecting and executing actions that achieve goals). Both route through the BA-to-NAcSh node, with the polarity of the reinforcement set by which input drives the BA.

This complements the Andreatta / Navratilova entries by adding a third locus to the convergence: the *teaching signal* sits in the ventral striatum (Andreatta), the *dopaminergic source* of that signal is the value-coding VTA population (Navratilova), and the *behavioural action-selection node* that uses the signal is also in the NAc shell (Ramirez). Three independent paradigms, three converging results, all pointing to the same overlap with reward-system substrate.

## Limitations and caveats

The paradigm is two-way signaled active avoidance, where the animal terminates an *anticipated* shock by a discrete shuttle response. This is one specific instance of suffering-reduction behaviour and not the dynamic, long-horizon suffering-state-change that REE's broader architecture would have to model. The transfer is licensed by the convergence with the Andreatta and Navratilova results, but the paper itself does not directly record the relief-completion event at termination — it identifies the circuit, not the timing of the signal.

The paper also does not test whether the BA-to-NAcSh circuit is sufficient, only that it is necessary. A REE substrate that wants to model both Pavlovian freezing (CeA-PAG) and active avoidance termination (BA-NAcSh) would need to instantiate both pathways and gate between them, which is the kind of architectural detail this paper does not resolve.
