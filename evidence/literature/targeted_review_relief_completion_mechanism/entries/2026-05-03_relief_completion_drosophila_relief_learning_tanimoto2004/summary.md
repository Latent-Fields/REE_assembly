# Tanimoto, Heisenberg & Gerber 2004 — Event timing turns punishment to reward

## What the paper did

Drosophila were trained in olfactory conditioning where an odour was paired with electric shock. The critical manipulation was the temporal order: in one group the odour preceded the shock (forward pairing), and in the other the odour followed the shock (backward pairing). After training, flies were given a choice between the conditioned odour and a control. The question was whether the shock acts as a unitary aversive reinforcer or whether its valence flips when the cue lands on the offset rather than the onset.

## Key finding

Forward pairing produced robust avoidance of the conditioned odour, as expected. Backward pairing — where the odour fell during the relief that followed shock cessation — produced approach. The same shock acted as either an aversive or an appetitive reinforcer depending purely on the timing of the cue. This is a clean behavioural demonstration of the opponent-process structure originally proposed by Solomon, anchored in an organism with an unusually tractable nervous system.

## How it translates to REE

The architectural question we are trying to answer is whether suffering-reduction should fire the same release/completion machinery as goal-achievement (Model 1) or a distinct one (Model 2). Tanimoto's result does not resolve the circuit-level question, but it does establish the more basic claim that relief is *reinforcing in the same valence sense as reward is*, not just a return to baseline. If pain offset can act as an appetitive reinforcer behaviourally, then the REE architecture cannot leave it unrepresented as a learning signal — the avoidance system needs a structurally analogous "this just worked, tag it" event, the way goal achievement has.

The cross-species reach matters. The same opponent-timing structure has since been replicated in rodents, humans, and other invertebrates. That convergence makes it more likely that relief uses an old, conserved mechanism rather than a recent mammalian elaboration — which marginally favours reuse of an existing reinforcement substrate over a parallel one.

## Limitations and caveats

This is a behavioural-only paper. It does not tell us whether the fly's relief-circuit is a redirection of the aversive pathway, an opponent activation of an appetitive pathway, or a third dedicated channel. Subsequent Drosophila work (the Yarali/Tanimoto cluster) has begun to address this, and points toward dopaminergic involvement, but the 2004 result on its own is only a structural anchor. The transfer from olfactory conditioning in flies to suffering-reduction in a long-horizon goal-pursuit substrate is non-trivial; the symmetry-of-timing-rule is what generalises, not necessarily the circuit.

## Confidence

Source quality is high (Nature, replicated paradigm). Mapping fidelity is moderate — the paper licenses the principle but leaves the mechanism open. Transfer risk is real because the relevant REE substrate is much more elaborate than fly olfaction. Net confidence 0.78, supports.
