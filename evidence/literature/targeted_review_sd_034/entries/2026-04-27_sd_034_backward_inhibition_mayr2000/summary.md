# Mayr & Keele 2000 — Changing internal constraints on action: the role of backward inhibition

According to PubMed, Mayr & Keele (2000, [J Exp Psychol Gen 129:4-26](https://doi.org/10.1037/0096-3445.129.1.4)) ran six experiments in healthy adult humans to test a single hypothesis: that intentional task-set switching carries a measurable cost on the just-abandoned task set, and that this cost — backward inhibition — is a primitive of cognitive control rather than a peripheral artifact. The headline finding is the lag-2 task-repetition effect: when the sequence of task sets goes A-B-A (returning to a task that was the *immediately-previous-but-one* set), response times on the final A trial are slower than when the sequence is C-B-A (returning to a task that has been latent for longer). The just-abandoned task set carries residual inhibition that needs to be lifted before re-entry.

The six experiments build a tight case. Experiment 1 establishes the basic effect across abstractly-defined perceptual task sets (number/letter classification, etc.). Experiment 2 controls for negative priming — the inhibition is on the *task set*, not on individual stimulus-response mappings. Experiment 3 ties the effect to top-down sequential control by manipulating the predictability of the next task. Experiment 4 shows that backward inhibition accounts at least partially for residual shift costs that earlier task-switching literature had attributed to other mechanisms. Experiment 5 demonstrates the effect persists even when the task sequence is preplanned by the participant, ruling out reactive-only accounts. The conclusion: backward inhibition on a recently-abandoned task set is a real, measurable, behaviourally-defined refractory period in healthy human cognition.

For SD-034's closure-operator specification — a "done" token that emits (a) release of the commitment latch and (b) a temporary No-Go on the just-completed rule_state — Mayr & Keele 2000 is the most direct empirical analogue available for component (b). The mapping is tight at the conceptual level: SD-034's "rule_state" maps onto Mayr & Keele's "abstractly defined task set" (both are the operative cognitive control variable, abstracted from the specific stimulus content), and SD-034's "temporary No-Go" maps onto Mayr & Keele's "backward inhibition / residual inhibition." The mechanism specified in SD-034 (a temporary No-Go bias on the just-completed rule_state, lasting some refractory window) is the natural computational reading of what Mayr & Keele observed behaviourally.

I want to flag two distinctions that matter for the transfer to SD-034. The first is that Mayr & Keele induce task switching by external cue — the participant is forced to abandon a task set because the experimenter says so. SD-034's closure operator is specified differently: it fires when the agent detects that a committed rule_state has been satisfied by its own behaviour. The neural and computational mechanism that produces refractoriness on intentional/forced abandonment may or may not generalise to refractoriness on satisfaction-driven release. They are conceptually adjacent (both involve task-set disengagement), but they are not the same operation, and the biology may handle them differently. This is a transfer-risk flag, not a refutation: it suggests that SD-034's "closure operator" should be implemented in a way that accommodates both intentional abandonment and satisfaction-driven release, with the No-Go bias firing on either path.

The second distinction matters for SD-034's implementation. The empirical signature in Mayr & Keele is a graded RT cost — typically tens to a few hundred milliseconds — not a hard binary No-Go gate. An SD-034 implementation that uses a strict admit/reject refractory gate on the just-completed rule_state would over-specify what the biology supports; the closer empirical match is a temporary additive negative `score_bias` on the just-completed rule_state, decaying over a refractory window. This is consistent with SD-033a's existing per-candidate `score_bias` projection scaffold (the closure operator could simply post a transient negative bias to the just-completed rule's score channel), and it would be cleaner to falsify than a hard gate (the No-Go RT-cost analogue is the strength and decay rate of the score_bias post-completion).

A third caveat is that Mayr & Keele do not localise the inhibitory mechanism to any particular brain region. They establish the phenomenon as cognitive-control-level. If subsequent work showed that the substrate of backward inhibition is basal ganglia or supplementary motor cortex rather than OFC/ACC, SD-034's architectural commitment to OFC/ACC-located closure machinery (motivated by Schuck 2016 and Rich & Shapiro 2009 in the existing entries) would still need separate empirical support for the localisation claim. The localisation question is one ARC removed from what Mayr & Keele 2000 measured.

Confidence components: source quality very high at 0.92 (canonical paper for backward inhibition; six replicating experiments; J Exp Psychol Gen 2000; foundational for the task-switching and cognitive-control literature). Mapping fidelity good at 0.78 — the No-Go-on-just-completed-rule_state specification is the natural reading of the phenomenon. Transfer risk moderate at 0.30 because of the forced-abandonment vs satisfaction-driven-release distinction and the graded-RT-cost vs hard-gate distinction. Net direction is **supports** SD-034's closure operator specification, with an implementation-level recommendation: implement the No-Go as a graded `score_bias` decay rather than a hard refractory gate.
