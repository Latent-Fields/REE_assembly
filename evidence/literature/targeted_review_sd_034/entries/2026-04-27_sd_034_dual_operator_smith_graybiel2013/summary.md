# Smith & Graybiel 2013 — A dual operator view of habitual behavior reflecting cortical and striatal dynamics

According to PubMed, Smith & Graybiel (2013, [Neuron 79:361-74](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2013.05.038)) recorded single-unit activity from both sensorimotor striatum and infralimbic neocortex in rats undergoing overtraining on a T-maze task, covering the transition from purposeful to habitual behaviour. They additionally used selective optogenetic disruption of infralimbic activity to test whether IL activity is causally necessary for habit formation. The paper makes two interlocking claims. First, the striatum rapidly acquires an action-bracketing activity pattern — increased firing at routine onset and at routine completion, with relatively quiet middle — that becomes insensitive to reward devaluation but tracks running automaticity. Second, a similar bracketing pattern develops in the upper layers of infralimbic cortex but only late during overtraining, where it closely tracks habit state; selectively disrupting IL activity during the overtraining window prevents habit crystallization. The two structures function as dual operators: each is necessary, and they develop on different timescales, but together they crystallise the habit.

For SD-034's closure-operator specification, the most directly relevant component is the **stop-spike at routine completion**. SD-034 specifies a closure operator that detects when a committed rule_state has been satisfied and emits a release token (with a temporary No-Go on the just-completed rule). The stop-spike is the closest direct neural analogue of a "done" token available in the rodent literature: a phasic activity boundary marker, locked to routine completion, present in both the striatal and infralimbic recordings. This complements the existing Barnes 2011 entry in the SD-034 review (advance-cued action-boundary patterns in sensorimotor striatum), showing that the same bracketing structure spans the purposeful-to-habitual transition rather than being a habit-specific signature.

The dual-operator finding has a second implication that deserves a flag in the SD-034 architectural commitment. SD-034 currently names OFC/ACC sequence-completion cells as the substrate for the closure operator (motivated by Schuck 2016 and Rich & Shapiro 2009 in the existing entries). Smith & Graybiel 2013 does not record OFC or ACC, and the regions where it *does* find bracketing — sensorimotor striatum and infralimbic cortex — are different substrates. The implication is that closure-detection in biology may be anatomically distributed across multiple regions, and SD-034's V3 commitment to OFC/ACC alone may be too narrow. This is not a refutation; OFC/ACC may carry the rule_state-level "done" token while striatum and IL carry the routine-level action-bracketing. But it suggests that an empirically grounded V4 elaboration of SD-034 may need to elevate from a single-substrate operator to a multi-region operator with each region carrying a different grain of completion signal.

I want to flag two distinctions that constrain the transfer to SD-034. The first is that Smith & Graybiel 2013 records during habitual maze-running, not during rule-conditional decision-making with explicit rule_state representations. The bracketing pattern is most clearly demonstrated for crystallised habit routines (devaluation-insensitive, automaticity-tracking). SD-034's closure operator is meant to fire on rule_state satisfaction in *flexible / planned* operating modes too, not just in habit mode. Whether the same bracketing mechanism applies to flexible-mode closure or whether closure is implemented differently across operating modes is empirically open. This is a transfer-risk flag with direct implications for the test battery: if the V3 implementation of SD-034 closure works for habit-mode but not for planned-mode behaviour, the dual-operator paper would have warned us.

The second distinction is that the optogenetic causal manipulation tests habit *formation*, not habit *release*. Smith & Graybiel show that disrupting IL activity during acquisition prevents habits from crystallising; they do not test what happens to a crystallised habit when its rule_state is satisfied. The closure-as-release function — the specific operation SD-034 cares about — is one ARC removed from what was empirically tested. The bracketing-at-completion pattern is consistent with closure-as-release, but the causal claim "IL/striatal closure machinery emits the release token" is not directly demonstrated by this paper.

Confidence components: source quality high at 0.88 (Neuron 2013, Graybiel lab, dual-region single-unit + causal optogenetic manipulation, well-cited as the canonical dual-operator paper for habit). Mapping fidelity is moderate-good at 0.65 — action-bracketing maps onto closure-detection but only for the habit-mode case; the multi-region distribution of the closure mechanism suggests SD-034 may need to elevate from single-substrate to operator-distributed-across-regions. Transfer risk is moderate at 0.35 because of the habit-mode-only support and the non-canonical OFC/ACC localisation. Net direction is **supports** the closure-detection component of SD-034, with two architectural flags: closure may be multi-region, and habit-mode closure may not be the same mechanism as planned-mode closure.

## SD-034 synthesis with the new entries

The targeted review now spans six entries covering the four components SD-034 specifies. (1) **Closure detection** — Schuck 2016 (OFC state-map) and Rich & Shapiro 2009 (rat PFC strategy-switch neurons) cover the OFC/ACC-side substrate; Barnes 2011 (advance-cued action-boundary patterns) and Smith & Graybiel 2013 (dual-operator action-bracketing) cover the striatal/infralimbic side. The two substrate clusters likely carry different grains of completion signal — rule-level vs routine-level — and SD-034's V4 elaboration may need to make this anatomical distribution explicit. (2) **Release of commitment latch** — supported indirectly through the bracketing-at-completion patterns, but the strict causal claim that closure emits a release token is not directly tested in any of the entries. (3) **Temporary No-Go on the just-completed rule_state** — Mayr & Keele 2000 provides the strongest direct evidence (backward inhibition as a behavioural primitive). The implementation-level recommendation that follows is graded `score_bias` decay rather than a hard refractory gate. (4) **Residue discharge within the relevant domain** — not directly addressed by any of the entries; remains an open lit-pull dimension for V4 reconsideration.

Combined synthesis: SD-034's three primary components (closure detection, release, No-Go) are biologically grounded; the closure mechanism is plausibly multi-region rather than OFC/ACC-only, and the No-Go signature is graded rather than a hard gate. The implementation-level recommendations for V3 are: (a) a modest negative `score_bias` on the just-completed rule_state with exponential decay over a refractory window; (b) closure-detection as a bracketing-style boundary signal that can fire from any of multiple substrates rather than being canonically located in OFC/ACC. SD-034 is an acceptable V3 commitment; the architectural elaboration for V4 should be flagged.
