# Schmitz, Bier, Joubert, Lejeune, Salmon, Rouleau & Meulemans (2014) — Errorless learning benefits SRT performance in Alzheimer's

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.3233/JAD-130887); PMID 24157724).

## What the paper did

The authors gave AD patients and healthy older controls a six-element perceptual-motor sequence to acquire under two preliminary conditions: errorless (the experimenter prevents incorrect responses during early acquisition) versus errorful (standard SRT, errors permitted). Then they measured serial-reaction-time differentiation between the trained sequence and a novel sequence at two time points in the learning curve.

## Key findings

Healthy controls showed normal sequence-specific RT differentiation under both conditions. AD patients showed sequence-specific differentiation only under errorless conditions, and the errorless advantage was larger in the AD group than in controls. The interpretation the authors offer is that errorless learning protects new procedural acquisition in AD by removing the load on episodic/error monitoring, allowing the procedural channel to operate without interference.

## Mapping to MECH-172

This paper supplies the AD half of MECH-172's prediction, but with a twist that is worth saying out loud. MECH-172 in its current statement asserts that habit and procedural memory are *preserved* in early AD because the model-free channel does not require the offline consolidation pipeline that AD breaks. Schmitz's data is consistent with that — the procedural channel does in fact still acquire — but the preservation is conditional on bypassing the part of the pipeline that AD has degraded (error monitoring, episodic comparison). Standard errorful SRT *fails* in AD; only errorless succeeds.

That is, I think, a refinement rather than a refutation. The architectural-separation claim is right that the procedural acquisition machinery is not directly damaged in early AD. But the channel is not purely independent in practice — it is normally regulated by feedback signals that AD's hippocampal/cortical pathology degrades. Once that contamination is removed, acquisition proceeds normally. MECH-172 is on solid ground, but the "preservation" should be read as "preservation of the channel given appropriate compensation for the failed monitoring component."

## Caveats

Modest sample, specialty journal. The paper does not address Huntington at all, so the dissociation argument has to come from Holl 2012 / Vaca-Palomares 2019 (other entries in this review). The errorless manipulation is a methodological intervention; in the wild, AD patients in everyday life are constantly subjected to errorful learning conditions, which is why the clinical observation of preserved procedural memory often refers to *previously acquired* skills (riding a bicycle, daily routines) rather than newly acquired ones. MECH-172's claim is mostly about the previously-acquired case, which Schmitz does not directly test.

## Confidence reasoning

I land at 0.68. The result is real and supports MECH-172, but the conditional preservation refines the simple statement. Source quality is moderate (specialty journal, n in the tens). Mapping is good but the test is narrower than MECH-172's full claim. Transfer risk is low — patients are clinical AD, not a model.
