# Holl, Wilkinson, Tabrizi, Painold & Jahanshahi (2012) — Probabilistic classification with feedback selectively impaired in early Huntington's

According to PubMed ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuropsychologia.2012.05.021); PMID 22659110).

## What the paper did

18 early Huntington's disease patients and 18 matched controls completed two versions of the weather-prediction probabilistic classification task: a feedback-based version (the patient receives corrective feedback after each response) and a paired-associate version (cues and outcomes are simply presented, no feedback). The two versions allow the authors to isolate which component of "implicit" learning is striatum-dependent.

## Key findings

HD patients were selectively impaired on the feedback version and not on the paired-associate version. The authors interpret this as evidence that the striatum is specifically required for learning with corrective feedback, not for "implicit" learning per se. Earlier work had already shown the same dissociation in Parkinson; this paper completes the case for HD, where the BG dysfunction is intrinsic (not dopamine-medication-mediated).

## Mapping to MECH-172

This paper supplies the HD half of the double dissociation MECH-172 invokes. REE asserts that pure habit-system disorders should impair model-free RL while leaving the hippocampal/declarative system intact, and that this is what produces the inverse pattern to early AD. Holl's feedback-vs-PA dissociation is the cleanest available test of that prediction. The PA version preserves the declarative/associative-learning route; HD patients can use it. The feedback version requires the striatal RPE machinery; HD patients cannot.

The mapping needs a caveat. Holl is testing feedback-based reinforcement learning specifically, not procedural-motor memory in general. MECH-172 talks about "habit and procedural memory" in a broader way that includes motor-sequence learning, riding a bicycle, and so on. The architectural inference is: if feedback-based RL is the model-free component the striatum specifically subserves, and motor-sequence acquisition has the same model-free shape, then the same selective impairment should appear there too — which is what the older HD motor-learning literature does in fact show.

## Caveats

The authors themselves flag an executive/working-memory confound: HD patients may have additional load when monitoring feedback, so the selective deficit might partly reflect a general executive cost. They argue against this by pointing to the intact PA performance, but the issue is not fully closed. The 50% caudate MSN loss in early HD is the structural anchor for the striatal-causation reading; that part is robust.

## Confidence reasoning

I land at 0.78. The design is the right design, the result is the result MECH-172 predicts, and the patient group is the relevant one. I drop from a higher number because (a) the test is feedback-RL specifically rather than procedural memory broadly, and (b) the early-HD population has 50% MSN loss but also some early cortical involvement; the inference still holds but is not free of confounders.
