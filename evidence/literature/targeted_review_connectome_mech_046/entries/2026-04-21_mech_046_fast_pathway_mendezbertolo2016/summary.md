# Méndez-Bértolo et al. 2016 — A fast pathway for fear in human amygdala

This is the paper that finally nailed the latency question for the "low road" in humans. Prior support for a fast subcortical thalamo-amygdala route was largely fMRI and behavioural — suggestive, but fMRI cannot resolve tens of milliseconds, and critics (notably Pessoa & Adolphs 2010) argued the data were equally consistent with purely cortical routes. Méndez-Bértolo and colleagues recorded directly from the amygdala and from visual cortex in 11 epilepsy patients with depth electrodes. They could see the waveform arrive.

The headline number is stark: amygdala local field potentials discriminated fearful from neutral or happy faces beginning at **74 ms** post-stimulus onset. Over in visual cortex, the same fear-specific discrimination did not appear at the P100 or N170 — it emerged only around **400 ms**, and only for high-spatial-frequency stimuli. So there is a window of roughly 300 ms during which the amygdala "knows" something that cortex has not yet decided. That window is the anatomical and temporal niche for a fast-prime signal. Critically, the early amygdala response was limited to low spatial frequency components of the face, exactly as one would predict from a magnocellular thalamo-amygdala route — and absent for arousing non-social scenes, so it is not a generic arousal response but a selective feature gate.

For REE this is the best available quantitative anchor for MECH-046 and MECH-074c. It gives us two numbers we can actually use: a fast-route latency of ~75 ms (about 1-2 simulation steps at typical REE control-plane tick rates) and a cortical-route latency for emotional discrimination of ~400 ms (roughly an order of magnitude slower). CeAAnalog should emit mode_prior and fast_prime on the first or second tick after z_harm_a_coarse crosses threshold, while AIC/dACC analogues continue integrating. The coarse/low-frequency selectivity tells us that CeA's input should not be the fully decoded z_world or even the full z_harm_a — it should be a down-sampled or magnitude-only projection that trades specificity for speed.

The paper also suggests a natural confound test: if the fast CeA response fires to every arousing stimulus (scenes as well as threat faces), we have built a generic salience detector, not an amygdala analogue. The data are unambiguous that the fast response is selective, not generic. Our CeA should reproduce that selectivity — it gates on affective-harm valence, not on arousal magnitude.

## Quantitative defaults for CeAAnalog config

- **Fast-route latency**: emit `fast_prime` within 1-2 sim steps (mapped to ~75 ms biological) of `z_harm_a_coarse` crossing threshold. Cortical analogues (AIC/dACC) should take ~5x longer (roughly 5-10 sim steps, mapped to ~400 ms) to produce a comparable discrimination signal.
- **Cortical vs fast route ratio**: ~5:1 (biological 400/75). CeA should "commit" well before cortical integration completes.
- **Activation threshold on z_harm_a**: gate on the magnitude of a coarse / low-frequency projection of `z_harm_a`, not on the full vector. Threshold tuned so the false-positive rate is deliberately high (~25-30%) — a low-threshold first-pass filter that is later corrected by cortical override. Use `||LowFreq(z_harm_a)||_1 > theta_cea_fast` where `theta_cea_fast` is set empirically so the fast gate fires on ~70-80% of true harm events and ~25% of arousing-but-neutral events.
- **Selectivity constraint**: CeA must fire selectively to harm-affective valence, not generic arousal.
- **Gating placement**: `fast_prime` biases SalienceCoordinator **before** the softmax (additive log-odds adjustment), not post-softmax multiplicative.
