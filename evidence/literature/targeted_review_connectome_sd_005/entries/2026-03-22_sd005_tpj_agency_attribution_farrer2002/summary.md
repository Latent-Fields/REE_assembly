# Literature Summary: 2026-03-22_sd005_tpj_agency_attribution_farrer2002

## Claims Tested

- `SD-005`

## Source

- Farrer C, Frith CD (2002). *Experiencing oneself vs another person as being the cause of an action: The neural correlates of the experience of agency*. NeuroImage, 15(3): 596-603.
- DOI: `10.1006/nimg.2001.0994`
- URL: `https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1053811901909940`

## Source Wording

Participants performed a joystick task in which they either directly controlled an on-screen avatar or observed the avatar being moved by the experimenter. fMRI contrasts revealed a double dissociation between brain regions engaged during self-agency vs other-agency attribution. The anterior insula showed significantly greater activation when participants caused the action themselves -- tracking the experience of being the agent. The right inferior parietal cortex (corresponding to the temporo-parietal junction, TPJ) showed significantly greater activation when an external agent caused the movement -- tracking the attribution of causation to an external source. The two regions were anti-correlated in their profile across conditions. The authors interpret this as evidence that the sense of agency is subserved by a dedicated neural network separable from the system that tracks external causation.

## REE Translation

**SD-005 (z_self/z_world split)**: The anterior insula/self-attribution circuit and the right TPJ/external-causation circuit constitute the empirical neural double dissociation that grounds the z_self/z_world split. z_self (E2 domain) tracks the agent's own causal footprint -- the neural correlate is the insula self-attribution circuit. z_world (E3 domain) tracks causation attributed to external sources -- the neural correlate is the TPJ/inferior parietal circuit. These are not two regions computing the same thing at different scales; they are computationally dissociable networks with distinct update conditions.

The TPJ finding is particularly important for E3's harm/goal role: the TPJ is also the canonical locus of harm attribution, moral judgement, and theory of mind in subsequent literature (Saxe & Kanwisher 2003; Young et al. 2010; Decety & Lamm 2007). The convergence of agency attribution, harm attribution, and social causation processing in TPJ/right parietal regions is consistent with z_world serving as the domain for residue-field harm/goal processing in REE's E3 complex.

**MECH-069 (incommensurable error signals)**: The anti-correlation between insula and TPJ activity supports the claim that self-attribution error and external-attribution error are structurally distinct signals that cannot be collapsed. Updating z_self on world-state error, or z_world on self-attribution error, would require routing signals across this dissociation -- architecturally equivalent to an insula-to-TPJ credit transfer that has no straightforward biological mechanism.

## Caveat

The paradigm contrasts self-agency with other-person agency, not with impersonal world-state monitoring. z_world in SD-005 encompasses all non-self causation (environmental changes, external events), not only other-agent actions. The generalisation from other-person attribution (TPJ) to all external-cause monitoring is an inferential step supported by the broader TPJ/STS literature but not directly evidenced by this paper. Sample size is 12 participants -- adequate for a foundational fMRI study but modest. The basic dissociation has been replicated extensively; anatomical specifics vary across studies.
