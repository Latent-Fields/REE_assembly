# McGovern & Barto 2001 -- Automatic Discovery of Subgoals in Reinforcement Learning using Diverse Density

**Citation:** McGovern A, Barto AG. Automatic Discovery of Subgoals in Reinforcement Learning using Diverse Density. *Proceedings of the Eighteenth International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML)*. 2001. DOI: 10.5555/645530.655681. UMass Scholarworks: https://scholarworks.umass.edu/cs_faculty_pubs/8/.

## What the paper does

McGovern and Barto propose an algorithm for an RL agent to automatically discover useful subgoals -- intermediate states worth treating as targets in their own right, around which options can be constructed for hierarchical decomposition of the policy. The criterion they use is statistical: a candidate subgoal is a state that appears with high frequency on successful paths but not on unsuccessful paths, indicating that traversing it is necessary or near-necessary for reaching the goal. They formalise this as a multiple-instance learning problem and use the diverse density framework to identify such states. The empirical examples are gridworld navigation tasks where the algorithm discovers "doorway" states that link strongly-connected regions; once a doorway is identified, an option is built for reaching it, and the agent's policy effectively decomposes into "reach the doorway, then reach the goal."

The paper is a foundational reference for hierarchical reinforcement learning with auto-discovered subgoals. It established the bottleneck-state framing of decomposition that has been refined in many subsequent papers (graph-theoretic methods, betweenness centrality, eigenoptions, etc.). The mechanism is purely statistical -- it does not involve prediction error in any direct sense.

## Why this matters for ARC-070

ARC-070's R5 verdict asks whether bottleneck-state decomposition (McGovern-Barto framing) and error-driven decomposition (Zacks-event-segmentation framing) are the same mechanism in different vocabularies, or genuinely distinct mechanisms. The clean answer this paper supplies: they are DISTINCT MECHANISMS. McGovern-Barto's bottleneck states are identified by statistical frequency on paths; Zacks's event boundaries are identified by transient prediction-error pulses on the predictive engine. The two mechanisms could in principle co-occur on the same task -- bottleneck states are often also states where prediction is uncertain because they are decision-critical junctures -- but they are distinct in their triggering signal and in the conditions under which they fire.

The R5 verdict therefore: the two are distinct mechanisms with overlapping outputs on a subset of tasks. ARC-070's child-MECH should be PE-driven primary (per the biological anchors: Zacks, Schacter, Schapiro, Pfeiffer-Foster) with the bottleneck framing as a complementary signal -- when the rollout proposal stream encounters a bottleneck state, the predictive engine's V_s on the surrounding region is often also low, so the PE-driven trigger and the bottleneck signal will frequently co-fire. But the architectural commitment is to PE-driven primary; bottleneck-aware secondary is a fallback design choice, not the primary mechanism.

There is an additional architectural lesson. McGovern-Barto's algorithm produces decomposition by graph-statistical analysis without any predictive engine running -- it relies only on the agent's accumulated success/failure paths. This is a viable mechanism for STORED chunked primitives (a chunked primitive could be examined for bottleneck structure offline, decomposed at bottleneck states, and the decomposition cached for future rollouts). The synthesis treats this as a possible OFFLINE / CONSOLIDATION-PHASE decomposition operation, complementary to the ONLINE / PREDICTION-ERROR-DRIVEN decomposition that ARC-070 names. Two phases, two mechanisms, both contributing to the final shape of the proposal stream.

## Caveats

The biggest caveat is that the framework is purely computational and lacks direct biological grounding. There is no claim in the paper that the brain implements diverse-density subgoal discovery; the algorithm is a mathematically-motivated mechanism for an artificial agent. ARC-070's primary commitment is to the biologically-supported PE-driven mechanism (Zacks, Schacter); the McGovern-Barto framing is the computational foil that disambiguates the R5 verdict, not a direct biological anchor.

A second caveat is that the diverse-density mechanism specifically requires multiple paths to a goal -- it is a frequency analysis. This restricts its applicability to repeatedly-traversed task structures. ARC-070 in REE needs to support one-shot / first-pass decomposition (the agent encounters a chunk for the first time and finds it cannot ground); the McGovern-Barto mechanism cannot do this. The synthesis flags this as a structural limitation: bottleneck-state framing is useful for offline consolidation but not for the online imagination-side trigger.

A third caveat: subsequent work in the hierarchical-RL literature has refined and partially superseded the diverse-density framework. Betweenness centrality, eigenoptions, successor representations, and more recent option-discovery methods have produced cleaner algorithms. The 2001 paper remains the canonical first-principles reference but the field has moved on. The synthesis treats the paper as the canonical R5 anchor for the bottleneck-state framing, while acknowledging the algorithm-specific details have been refined elsewhere.
