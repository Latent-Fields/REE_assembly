# Schapiro, Turk-Browne, Botvinick & Norman 2017 -- Complementary learning systems within the hippocampus

**Citation:** Schapiro AC, Turk-Browne NB, Botvinick MM, Norman KA. Complementary learning systems within the hippocampus: a neural network modelling approach to reconciling episodic memory with statistical learning. *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B*. 2017;372(1711):20160049. PMID: 27872368. DOI: 10.1098/rstb.2016.0049.

## What the paper does

Schapiro and colleagues build a neural-network model of the hippocampus that resolves a long-standing tension in the memory literature: how can the same structure rapidly memorise individual episodes (episodic memory) and also extract statistical regularities across episodes (statistical learning)? The CLS (Complementary Learning Systems) framework had previously placed these on opposite sides of a hippocampus-vs-cortex division of labour, with the hippocampus doing fast episodic learning and the cortex doing slow statistical extraction. Schapiro et al. show that BOTH operations can run inside the hippocampus itself, on different anatomical pathways.

The trisynaptic pathway (entorhinal cortex -> dentate gyrus -> CA3 -> CA1) implements pattern separation: incoming inputs are mapped to highly differentiated representations, supporting fast memorisation of individual episodes that remain discriminable from neighbours. The monosynaptic pathway (entorhinal cortex -> CA1) does NOT pattern-separate; it produces overlapping representations that aggregate naturally across episodes, supporting statistical learning of regularities. The two pathways operate in parallel from the same input. The model demonstrates this dual machinery is computationally viable and reproduces empirical patterns from human and animal hippocampal experiments.

## Why this matters for ARC-070

ARC-070 needs a substrate that can support multi-grain trajectory decomposition. Schapiro 2017 provides direct evidence that the hippocampus natively has at least two parallel representational pathways with different grain properties. The trisynaptic pathway's pattern-separated representations are well-suited to representing chunked primitives as discriminable units (each chunk is a separable representation that the rollout can manipulate atomically); the monosynaptic pathway's overlapping representations are well-suited to representing grain-invariant statistical regularities across primitives (the same rule signature emerges across multiple chunks). When ARC-070 decomposes a chunk, the output sequence is the trisynaptic-pathway content of the chunk's stored representation; when ARC-062 apprehends a rule across the decomposed primitives, the rule may live in the monosynaptic-pathway representation that aggregates across decomposed instances.

This grounds R3 (multi-level hierarchy) and gives partial support for R1 (trigger primary). The trisynaptic pathway's pattern-separation IS a region-conditioned discriminability operation -- it produces highly differentiated representations precisely where the input warrants disambiguation. The V_s scalar (region-conditioned predictability) maps naturally onto a read-out of this discriminability: regions where pattern separation is strong have high V_s (the substrate represents distinct possibilities clearly, predictions are reliable), regions where pattern separation is weak have low V_s (the substrate cannot discriminate, predictions are unreliable). ARC-070's V_s-drop trigger therefore has a candidate biological basis in pattern-separation strength.

The cross-link with the broader R2 verdict matters: Schapiro 2017 shows the hippocampus has the substrate to support both grain levels and both episodic and statistical operations. Combined with Pfeiffer-Foster 2013's evidence that the hippocampus generates forward-trajectory rollouts AND with Schacter 2008's evidence that the hippocampus is the central node of the constructive-simulation core network, the architectural picture is consistent: the hippocampus is the locus where rollout trajectories are generated, where they can be stored at variable grain (chunked or decomposed), and where the predictability signal that triggers re-segmentation can be read from pattern-separation strength. The four papers (Zacks 2007, Schacter 2008, Pfeiffer-Foster 2013, Schapiro 2017) converge on a single substrate story for ARC-070.

## Caveats

The biggest caveat is that Schapiro et al. address episodic memory and statistical learning, NOT rollout-side trajectory decomposition. The mapping to ARC-070 is multi-step: the paper's pathway distinction MAY map onto the chunk / sub-element grain distinction in REE's rollout, but this is an architectural inference that the paper itself does not make. The bridge to ARC-070 runs through the constructive-episodic-simulation literature -- if imagination-side rollout shares substrate with episodic memory (Schacter 2008's claim), then the dual pathways available for memory are also available for rollout, and the multi-grain capability transfers. This is a defensible inference but it is one architectural step further than the paper directly demonstrates.

A second caveat is that the model is computational rather than directly empirical at the pathway-attribution level. The functional split between trisynaptic and monosynaptic pathways is supported by broader anatomy and lesion data, but the specific division of labour Schapiro et al. propose -- pattern-separation for episodes vs overlap-based statistical learning -- is a MODEL-LEVEL claim that has been refined and challenged in subsequent work. The synthesis treats this as substrate-grounding evidence, not as definitive substrate-attribution.

A third caveat: the paper does not address the trigger question (what signal drives a switch between pathway use, or a re-segmentation operation). The substrate is documented; the dynamics are not. ARC-070's trigger commitment must be sourced from elsewhere (Zacks 2007's PE framework primarily).
