# Zacks, Speer, Swallow, Braver & Reynolds 2007 -- Event perception: a mind-brain perspective

**Citation:** Zacks JM, Speer NK, Swallow KM, Braver TS, Reynolds JR. Event perception: a mind-brain perspective. *Psychological Bulletin*. 2007;133(2):273-93. PMID: 17338600. DOI: 10.1037/0033-2909.133.2.273.

## What the paper does

This is the foundational theoretical statement of Event Segmentation Theory. The authors propose that the perceptual system maintains a working event model -- a representation of what is currently happening -- and that this model continuously generates predictions about what will happen next. When the predictive output diverges from incoming input, a transient prediction-error pulse is registered, and the perceptual system marks that point as an event boundary, updates the event model, and begins a new event. Importantly, the framework explicitly accommodates a hierarchy of boundaries: coarse-grain boundaries between high-level activity units and fine-grain boundaries between sub-actions, both arising from the same prediction-error mechanism applied at different levels of representation.

The paper integrates fMRI evidence showing transient activity increases at event boundaries in posterior temporal/parietal cortex and lateral frontal regions, behavioural evidence showing memory and attention effects locked to boundaries, and computational arguments about why a system whose goal is accurate prediction would profit from segmenting its input into stable predictable chunks. The framework's reach extends across stimulus types (film, narrative, naturalistic action) and grains (sub-second to multi-minute).

## Why this matters for ARC-070

ARC-070 is, in effect, the imagination-side application of Zacks's framework. Three of the five verdicts the synthesis must produce lean on this paper directly. For R1 (trigger primary), Zacks 2007 names prediction error as the canonical boundary signal -- this licenses ARC-070's commitment to V_s drop on the chunk's region as the primary trigger, since V_s is exactly a region-conditioned predictability scalar and a low V_s value IS a forward-model unreliability signal that satisfies the framework's "prediction error" role. For R5 (bottleneck-state vs error-driven), the framework says event boundaries are PE-driven; the McGovern-Barto bottleneck framing turns out to be a special case where bottleneck states happen to also be high-PE states (the agent's predictions are most uncertain when the policy graph is most constrained), so the two are mostly the same mechanism in different vocabularies. For R3 (hierarchy depth), Zacks's coarse/fine boundary nesting predicts multi-level decomposition -- a chunk can decompose into sub-chunks, each of which can decompose further, with the recursion bounded by the depth of the predictive model, not by an arbitrary single-level cap.

The framework also constrains R2. Zacks 2007 says event segmentation is the operation a *predictive system* performs on its inputs; in principle, that includes any input the predictive system processes, observed or imagined. The 2007 paper itself does not directly test imagination-side segmentation, but the framework is substrate-agnostic about the input stream's source, and that creates the *theoretical license* for ARC-070 to share substrate with MECH-288 (the observation-side event_segmenter). The empirical confirmation -- that imagined event sequences ARE segmented at boundaries via the same machinery -- comes from the constructive-episodic-simulation literature (Schacter Addis Buckner 2008) in the companion entry.

## Caveats

The biggest caveat is what Zacks 2007 does NOT contain: a direct empirical demonstration that imagination-side event sequences are segmented by the same mechanism as observation-side ones. The 2007 paper relies on observed activity streams; the imagination-side claim is a theoretical extension licensed by the framework's structure but requiring separate empirical anchoring. The R2 verdict therefore cannot rest on this paper alone -- the synthesis must triangulate Zacks 2007 (theoretical framework) with Schacter 2008 (shared neural substrate for past + future) to produce the SHARED-SUBSTRATE recommendation.

A second caveat is the timescale gap. Zacks's behavioural data come predominantly from observation of multi-second to multi-minute activity (films, naturalistic actions). REE's rollout-time decomposition operates on the much faster timescale of a single rollout pass. The mathematical structure transfers cleanly (a PE-locked re-segmentation pulse at any grain), but the empirical anchor data are NOT at REE's rollout timescale, so the transfer to ARC-070's specific runtime is theoretical rather than empirical. This is a moderate transfer risk; the synthesis treats it as an open child-MECH-design question rather than a falsifier.

## Confidence reasoning

Source quality is very high (foundational Psych Bull review, more than 2000 citations, principled integration of perception / cognitive-control / fMRI literatures). Mapping fidelity is strong on R1 and R5 (PE-as-trigger and error-driven framing land naturally), moderate on R2 (theoretical framework supports shared-substrate, empirical evidence requires the Schacter triangulation), strong on R3 (multi-level grain explicitly supported). Transfer risk is moderate: framework transfers cleanly to REE's E3 + V_s scoring chain, but the empirical anchor is at a different timescale and modality than REE's rollout machinery. Aggregate 0.84 -- second-highest of the seven-paper ARC-070 cohort, reflecting Zacks 2007's load-bearing role across R1, R2 (theoretical half), R3, and R5.
