# Cox et al. (2015) -- Striatal D1 and D2 Signaling and Approach/Avoidance Learning

## What the paper did

Cox, Frank, Larcher and colleagues used positron emission tomography with two separate radioligands to measure striatal D1 and D2 dopamine receptor binding in healthy human adults, then correlated individual differences in binding with performance on a probabilistic reinforcement learning task. The task required participants to learn from positive and negative outcomes across multiple stimulus pairs with different reward/punishment probabilities. The study was designed to test a specific prediction from Michael Frank's Go/NoGo computational model: that D1 receptor stimulation in the direct pathway mediates approach learning, while D2 receptor signaling in the indirect pathway mediates avoidance learning, and that these are dissociable individual differences rather than a single 'sensitivity' parameter. A separate group received acute dopamine precursor depletion (APTD) before the learning task, allowing a quasi-causal manipulation of dopamine availability.

## Key findings relevant to ARC-030

The results confirmed the core prediction with striking specificity. Striatal D1 binding selectively predicted learning from positive outcomes -- individuals with higher D1 binding learned more efficiently from rewards. Striatal D2 binding predicted avoidance learning in an inverted-U fashion -- both too little and too much D2 signaling impaired learning from negative outcomes. Acute dopamine depletion, which preferentially reduces DA at D2 receptors due to their higher sensitivity, improved avoidance learning in the depletion group, consistent with D2-mediated avoidance. The paper concludes that striatal dopamine exerts 'a bidirectional modulatory role' via segregated D1 and D2 corticostriatal pathways, with approach and avoidance being functionally independent learning dimensions that must both be present for calibrated behavior.

## How this translates to REE (ARC-030)

ARC-030 claims that each BG-like loop in REE requires symmetric Go (approach/execute) and NoGo (suppress/avoid) sub-channels. The Cox et al. findings provide direct empirical support for this in humans. The D1/direct/Go channel controls approach learning; the D2/indirect/NoGo channel controls avoidance learning; and critically, these are not redundant -- you cannot learn proper approach behavior from the avoidance channel, nor vice versa. An agent that only has the D2/NoGo channel available (harm avoidance without goal approach) would show exactly the behavioral profile that ARC-030 predicts: it accumulates no harm signal while doing nothing, but it also accumulates no goal signal, and the planner has no terrain to navigate toward. The Cox et al. data show this is not merely a theoretical concern -- it is a real functional dissociation in human striatal physiology.

## Limitations and caveats

The study measures receptor binding density as a proxy for pathway function. Binding density reflects the baseline number of available receptors, not the dynamic activation of the pathway during learning. The causal argument is indirect: 'people with more D1 receptors learn better from rewards' does not directly show that the D1/Go pathway is the mechanism -- it could be that high-D1 individuals differ on other dimensions. The APTD manipulation provides more causal traction but is a blunt instrument that depletes dopamine globally, not just at D2. The probabilistic RL task is also a simple two-alternative paradigm where approach and avoidance are operationalised as 'choose the more rewarded option' and 'avoid the more punished option' -- a much thinner learning problem than REE's harm_eval with trajectory proposals. Whether the D1/D2 segregation principle operates at the richer planning level required by E3 is not directly tested.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is set at 0.80 -- the highest in this claim's review cluster. The paper provides the most direct human evidence for D1/D2 channel segregation in approach/avoidance learning, the specific prediction that ARC-030 relies on. The mapping fidelity is good: the D1/approach vs D2/avoidance dissociation directly instantiates the Go/NoGo sub-channel claim. Transfer risk is relatively low because the principle is substrate-independent at the functional level. The limitations are real but do not undercut the core finding: segregated D1/D2 pathways are necessary for behavioral balance, and blocking either produces a specific learning deficit.
