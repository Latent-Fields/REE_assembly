# Bariselli et al. (2019) -- A Competitive Model for Striatal Action Selection

## What the paper did

Bariselli, Fobbs, Creed and Bhatt review the literature on striatal dMSN/iMSN function and argue for a 'competitive model' of action selection that differs importantly from both the classic Go/NoGo model (where Go facilitates the current action and NoGo suppresses competing actions) and the complementary model (where Go and NoGo learn different classes of responses). In the competitive model, dMSNs (D1/direct pathway) and iMSNs (D2/indirect pathway) respond to the same actions and compete via their relative activation levels to determine whether an animal approaches or avoids. When dMSN activity exceeds iMSN activity for a given action, the thalamus is disinhibited and the animal approaches. When iMSN activity dominates, thalamic inhibition continues and avoidance is the default. Dopamine -- acting as a reward prediction error signal -- drives experience-dependent synaptic plasticity in both populations, shifting the competition balance toward whichever outcome (approach or avoid) was rewarded or punished.

## Key findings relevant to ARC-030

The paper's central contribution to ARC-030 is the architecture it describes: the Go and NoGo channels evaluate the same action candidates simultaneously. This is not a division of labor over different action inventories (Go handles reward-approach actions, NoGo handles aversive-avoidance actions) -- both channels evaluate every proposed action, and the competition balance determines execution. The paper also emphasises that this architecture links action selection to reinforcement through a natural mechanism: when dMSNs win and the animal approaches, iMSNs for that action are co-activated but undergo plasticity based on the outcome, priming them to shift the balance on future encounters. Symmetric competition creates a self-tuning balance that can adapt to changing reward/punishment contingencies without requiring a separate 'conflict resolution' module.

## How this translates to REE (ARC-030)

ARC-030's implementation notes explicitly state: the architecture is NOT two parallel pipelines selecting different trajectories. It is dual evaluation of the same hippocampal trajectory proposals, with the harm channel (SD-010/NoGo) and goal channel (MECH-112/Go) evaluating each proposal. The Bariselli competitive model is the biological precedent for precisely this architecture. In REE, the hippocampal module proposes a set of trajectory candidates. Both E3's harm_eval (NoGo/D2-analogue) and E3's goal approach channel (Go/D1-analogue) score each candidate. The commit threshold (MECH-106, ARC-016) is the competition balance point: a trajectory is executed when the weighted Go signal exceeds the weighted NoGo signal sufficiently. Bariselli's competitive model tells us why this architecture is biologically motivated -- it is not a design choice but a functional consequence of how striatal circuits actually operate.

## Limitations and caveats

This is a theoretical review paper, not a direct experimental test of the competitive model. The paper acknowledges that the competitive model is one of several models and that the empirical evidence is not yet decisive between them. The classic Go/NoGo model (Go facilitates current action, NoGo suppresses competing actions) is not definitively ruled out. The competitive model as described also operates at the motor action selection level -- relatively short timescale decisions about which of several available motor programs to execute. REE's trajectory evaluation operates over many steps (rollout_horizon=30), which is qualitatively different. Whether dMSN/iMSN competition can operate over multi-step trajectory representations in the way ARC-030 requires -- or whether it is restricted to single-step action selection -- is not addressed by this paper. The extension to ethical trajectory evaluation is an architectural inference.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is set at 0.74. The mapping fidelity is the highest in this cluster because the competitive model describes almost exactly the architecture ARC-030 specifies: same trajectory proposals, dual evaluation by Go/NoGo channels, competition at commit threshold. This is unusual alignment between a biological model and an architectural claim. However, the confidence ceiling is limited by the paper being a theoretical review rather than an experimental test, and by the timescale mismatch between short-horizon motor action selection and long-horizon trajectory planning. The two causal papers (Cox et al. 2015 and Hikida et al. 2013) provide stronger experimental evidence for the underlying D1/D2 channel segregation; this paper provides the best available architectural precedent for how those channels interact on shared action candidates.
