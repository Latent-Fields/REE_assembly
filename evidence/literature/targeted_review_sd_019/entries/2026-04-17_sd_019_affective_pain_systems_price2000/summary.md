# Psychological and neural mechanisms of the affective dimension of pain
**Price, D. D. (2000). *Science*, 288(5472), 1769-1772.**
DOI: [10.1126/science.288.5472.1769](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.288.5472.1769)

## What the study did

This is a synthetic review by Donald Price, one of the principal researchers in the pain-affect dissociation field, published in Science in 2000. Price integrates experimental and clinical evidence to characterise the multi-dimensional architecture of pain affect, tracing its neural implementation from spinal cord to cortex. The review distinguishes two sources of affective input to limbic structures: a direct route via the paleospinothalamic tract to medial thalamic nuclei and thence to the ACC and anterior insula, and an indirect cortico-limbic route in which nociceptive signals first reach SI and SII (the sensory-discriminative system) and are then routed through association cortices to limbic structures. Both routes converge on ACC and related regions that Price argues establish emotional valence and response priorities. The review also introduces the concept of "secondary affect" -- the emotions evoked by anticipated future implications of ongoing pain -- as a further downstream dimension distinct from immediate unpleasantness.

## Key findings

Price's framework makes explicit what the Rainville PET experiments implied: there are two anatomically distinct pathways from spinal cord to pain affect. The neospinothalamic/lateral system (carrying primarily A-delta fast pain, projecting to the ventroposterior thalamus and then to SI/SII) handles sensory discrimination -- where and how intense. The paleospinothalamic/medial system (carrying primarily C-fiber slow pain, projecting to medial thalamic nuclei and then directly to the ACC and limbic cortex) handles affective-motivational processing -- how unpleasant and how urgent. The existence of a direct paleospinothalamic input to the limbic system means that the affective signal has an independent source that does not require processing through the sensory-discriminative pathway. Damage to ACC (or its disconnection from limbic inputs) can abolish pain unpleasantness while leaving pain intensity largely intact -- the clinical phenomenon of pain asymbolia.

## Relevance to SD-019

Price 2000 provides the architectural grounding for what SD-019 requires at the substrate level. The dual spinal pathway architecture -- neospinothalamic for z_harm_s, paleospinothalamic for z_harm_a -- is the biological implementation of the claim that the affective stream has a source that is structurally independent of the sensory-discriminative stream. A valid z_harm_a cannot simply be a transformation of z_harm_s because it receives input the z_harm_s pathway does not: the C-fiber-driven paleospinothalamic route with its characteristic temporal integration (C-fibers are slow-conducting, slowly adapting, and their signal accumulates over repeated stimulation via wind-up and sensitisation). The "secondary affect" construct further maps onto z_harm_a's role in modulating commitment urgency and future-oriented planning in REE -- the system is not merely encoding current unpleasantness but evaluating the motivational weight of the accumulated threat load for downstream decision circuits.

## Limitations and caveats

The review's anatomical claims, while well supported, involve a biological system of considerable complexity. The two pathways are not cleanly separated in practice: C-fibers also project via the neospinothalamic route, A-delta fibers have some paleospinothalamic connections, and extensive cross-talk occurs at the thalamic and cortical levels. The clean architectural nonredundancy that SD-019 demands as an engineering constraint goes beyond what the biology strictly requires -- it is a deliberate design choice intended to prevent z_harm_a from collapsing onto z_harm_s during training. Price 2000 supports the biological motivation for this choice but does not rule out that a learning system could achieve the same behavioural outcomes with a shared substrate. There is also an unresolved tension in the literature between structural dissociation (independent pathways) and dynamic dissociation (independently modulable states in a shared network) -- both are consistent with Price's data, and which matters more for REE is an open question.
