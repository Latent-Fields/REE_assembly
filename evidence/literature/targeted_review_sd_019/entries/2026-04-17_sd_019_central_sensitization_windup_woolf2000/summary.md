# Neuronal plasticity: increasing the gain in pain
**Woolf, C. J., & Salter, M. W. (2000). *Science*, 288(5472), 1765-1769.**
DOI: [10.1126/science.288.5472.1765](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.288.5472.1765)

## What the study did

This is a review by Clifford Woolf and Michael Salter, published in the same Science issue as Price 2000, summarising the mechanisms of activity-dependent amplification in nociceptive spinal circuits. The central topic is central sensitization: the observation that nociceptive processing in the dorsal horn of the spinal cord is not fixed but can be dramatically and persistently upregulated by prior nociceptive input. The authors focus on two closely related phenomena: wind-up (the progressive amplitude increase in dorsal horn responses to repeated C-fiber stimulation at suprathreshold frequencies) and the longer-lasting central sensitization that follows sustained or repeated nociceptive drive. Both phenomena depend critically on NMDA receptor activation and downstream intracellular signaling cascades in dorsal horn neurons.

## Key findings

The key mechanistic insight is that C-fibers (slow, unmyelinated nociceptors) and A-delta fibers (fast, thinly myelinated nociceptors) differ not only in conduction velocity but in the temporal dynamics of their central effects. Repeated A-delta stimulation at frequencies up to a few Hz does not produce progressive response amplification -- each stimulus produces roughly the same dorsal horn activation, reflecting the near-instantaneous intensity of the peripheral input. C-fiber stimulation at the same inter-stimulus intervals is qualitatively different: each successive C-fiber volley adds to an accumulating depolarization of dorsal horn neurons, and the response amplitude grows progressively. This is wind-up. It requires that prior C-fiber input has partially relieved the Mg2+ block on NMDA receptors, allowing subsequent inputs to produce larger calcium influx and stronger depolarisation. The consequence is that the C-fiber pathway carries a signal to higher centres that has already been temporally integrated at the spinal level: the "second pain" reaching the brain from a series of C-fiber inputs is not proportional to the instantaneous peripheral input but to the accumulated history of recent C-fiber activity.

Central sensitization extends this further: sustained nociceptive input triggers phosphorylation events (PKC, PKA, Src kinase) and transcription-factor-mediated gene expression changes that keep dorsal horn neurons in a state of heightened excitability long after the original stimulus has ended. This means the affective pain system (receiving C-fiber input via the paleospinothalamic pathway) can remain in an amplified state even when current peripheral nociceptive input has returned to baseline.

## Relevance to SD-019

SD-019 (affective_harm_nonredundancy_constraint) requires that z_harm_a encodes temporally integrated threat load, not a monotone or delayed transformation of z_harm_s. The wind-up and central sensitization findings ground this requirement at the biological input level. The sensory-discriminative pathway (A-delta, neospinothalamic, z_harm_s) encodes intensity in a near-instantaneous, approximately linear way -- each moment's hazard proximity maps onto a proportional z_harm_s value. The affective-motivational pathway (C-fiber, paleospinothalamic, z_harm_a) receives input that has already been temporally summed and amplified through NMDA-dependent spinal plasticity before it reaches the brain. This means z_harm_a at any moment contains information about the recent history of harm exposure that is simply not present in the current z_harm_s value. The harm-history FIFO buffer in REE's AffectiveHarmEncoder is the computational implementation of this biologically grounded temporal integration: it provides z_harm_a with access to the trajectory of recent harm_obs_s values, enabling a representation that reflects accumulated burden rather than instantaneous intensity.

The wind-up mechanism also directly predicts a key REE design property: brief, isolated harm spikes should not saturate z_harm_a (they do not produce sustained wind-up at low rates), while repeated or sustained harm should produce disproportionately elevated z_harm_a values (wind-up accumulates nonlinearly at >0.3 Hz C-fiber rates). This matches MECH-219's asymmetric rise/decay constraint.

## Limitations and caveats

Wind-up is a spinal phenomenon; REE implements the analogous temporal integration at the encoder level via a harm-history buffer rather than NMDA dynamics. The biological mechanism and the computational implementation share the property of temporal accumulation but differ in mechanism, substrate, and timescale. The nonrecoverability constraint in SD-019 -- that a valid z_harm_a cannot be reconstructed from any single-timestep z_harm_s value -- goes beyond what spinal wind-up alone requires, because a sufficiently powerful learned transformation might still approximate z_harm_a from a long enough time series of z_harm_s values. The REE cosine-similarity penalty (compute_harm_nonredundancy_loss) enforces representational divergence rather than input nonrecoverability, which is the operationally measurable version of the constraint. Additionally, central sensitization in humans is not exclusively C-fiber dependent -- A-beta fibers can drive central sensitization in the context of prior tissue injury, which complicates the clean A-delta/C-fiber binary assumed by the REE dual-stream model.

## Confidence reasoning

High source quality (Woolf is the principal investigator of central sensitization; Science 2000; among the most cited pain papers). Strong mapping fidelity for the temporal-integration angle -- this is the primary gap not covered by Price 2000 (which addresses pathway architecture but not the temporal dynamics) or Rainville 1997 (which addresses cortical dissociation under top-down modulation, not the input-stream temporal properties). Slight attenuation for the implementation-level gap between spinal NMDA dynamics and REE's harm-history buffer. No transfer risk from a different pain modality: wind-up and central sensitization are canonical properties of repetitive nociceptive input across modalities.
