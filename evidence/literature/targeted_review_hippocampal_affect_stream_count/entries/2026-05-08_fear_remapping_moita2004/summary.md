# Moita, Rosis, Zhou, LeDoux & Blair 2004 — Putting Fear in Its Place

**Source:** Journal of Neuroscience, [DOI 10.1523/JNEUROSCI.5492-03.2004](https://doi.org/10.1523/JNEUROSCI.5492-03.2004) (PubMed PMID 15295037). According to PubMed.

## What the paper did

Sprague-Dawley rats were exposed to two spatial environments: a training environment in which they underwent fear conditioning, and a neutral control environment they entered at matched intervals but without aversive training. The authors recorded dorsal CA1 place cells across the conditioning and asked whether the affective experience of being shocked in environment A would alter the spatial map *of* environment A even though the physical environment did not change.

## Key findings relevant to the SD-011 generalization

Two clean results:

1. *Aversive reinforcement remaps place cells.* In the training environment, many CA1 place cells altered their preferred firing locations after fear conditioning. The control environment showed stability. So the remapping cannot be ascribed to passage of time, electrode drift, or mere repeated exposure — the affective experience is the difference.
2. *Contextual fear remaps more than auditory fear.* When the conditioned stimulus is the environment itself, place cells remap more than when the conditioned stimulus is a discrete tone in the same environment. This is critical: the remapping is keyed to *learned fear of the environment*, not to a generic fear-state-induced firing modulation.

This is the canonical existence proof for fear/threat as a second hippocampal-map-tagged affect stream alongside reward.

## REE translation

For the SD-011 generalization, this lifts the count from 1 (reward, Gauthier & Tank) to 2 (reward + fear/threat). The remapping is at the level of place-field *location*, which is the strict criterion — not just firing-rate modulation. So fear satisfies the same architectural test that reward does.

What this *doesn't* do is dissect SD-011's specific sensory/affective split. Fear conditioning in this paradigm bundles the aversive sensory input (shock) with the affective reaction (fear-state). For the dual-nociceptive split, we'd want to see whether sensory-discriminative pain signals (Adelta-pathway analogues) and affective-motivational signals (C-fibre/paleospinothalamic) drive distinct hippocampal remappings. That experiment, to my knowledge, has not been done.

## Limitations and caveats

This is 2004 work, dorsal CA1 only, single-unit recording with limited yield by modern standards. The aversive stimulus is electric shock, which is the most-studied but not the only fear-relevant input — predator-odour and innate-threat paradigms might recruit different circuits. The remapping is "many place cells" rather than a quantified percentage in the abstract, but follow-up work (Wang et al. 2012) gives the proportions.

## Confidence

0.86 — foundational paper, widely replicated, clean control design. Confidence in the existence claim (fear is a second map-tagged affect stream) is very high; confidence in any specific architectural inference about *how many* sub-streams of fear exist within the hippocampus is lower, because the paradigm doesn't address that.
