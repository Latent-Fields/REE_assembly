# Walker & van der Helm 2009 -- Overnight Therapy? The Role of Sleep in Emotional Brain Processing

**Evidence direction: mixed**

## What the paper claims

REM sleep provides a neurochemical environment uniquely suited to emotional memory processing. During REM, the brain reactivates emotional memories in a state of low noradrenaline (reduced stress-hormone tone) and elevated acetylcholine, allowing the informational content of the memory to be consolidated while its affective charge -- the autonomic arousal, the emotional 'sting' -- is simultaneously depotentiated. The SFSR (Sleep to Forget and Sleep to Remember) hypothesis formalises this: REM retains the episodic content but reduces the emotional reactivity. REM theta oscillations synchronise limbic and cortical activity during this process. The late-night prevalence of REM sleep (relative to the early-night NREM dominance) is noted but not theorised as a functional dependency.

## Does it make the full derivation argument?

No. The paper treats REM as performing affective recalibration but does not argue that this step must follow NREM consolidation as a logical dependency. The observation that NREM dominates early in the night and REM dominates late is noted as a biological pattern, but Walker and van der Helm do not ask: "why must affect recalibration wait until after hippocampal-cortical transfer?" The SFSR mechanism itself implies a dependency -- you cannot depotentiate the affect of a memory unless that memory is already consolidated enough to be stably represented -- but this implication is never made explicit.

## Relevance to INV-045

Direct support for Phase 4 mechanism: the SFSR process is the biological implementation of INV-045's precision recalibration. In REE terms, REM updates z_beta (affective/harm weighting) while preserving hippocampal schema content, which is exactly what Phase 4 specifies. The paper also provides a failure-mode argument by implication: patients with REM disruption (PTSD, sleep apnoea) show persistent heightened emotional reactivity, suggesting that the recalibration function fails when REM is disrupted. This is consistent with the INV-045 claim that Phase 4's failure mode is imprecise harm weighting. However, the ordering necessity -- why affect recalibration must be Phase 4 rather than Phase 1 -- is not argued.

## Key gap for INV-045

The SFSR hypothesis implicitly requires that consolidated memories exist before affect recalibration can operate (you cannot recalibrate something that hasn't been formed), but Walker and van der Helm do not make this dependency explicit. The paper establishes Phase 4's mechanism and its failure mode, without establishing its position in the sequence.
