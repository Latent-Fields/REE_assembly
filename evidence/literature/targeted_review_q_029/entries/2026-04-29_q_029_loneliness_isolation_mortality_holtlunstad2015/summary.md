# Holt-Lunstad et al. (2015): Loneliness and social isolation as risk factors for mortality

## What the paper did

This 2015 meta-analysis specifically tackled a question the 2010 paper had left implicit: are subjective and objective deprivation different? The team extracted prospective studies that separately measured loneliness (the felt experience of unshared suffering / unmet need for connection), social isolation (the objective absence of relations), and living alone (a structural housing variable). They computed pooled effect sizes for each, then tested whether the type of measurement moderated the mortality association.

## Key findings

All three deprivation channels independently predicted mortality with comparable effect sizes: loneliness OR 1.26, social isolation OR 1.29, living alone OR 1.32 -- all statistically significant and clinically meaningful. The headline finding is what they did *not* find: no significant difference between objective and subjective measures of social isolation. They also reported that effects were stronger in samples younger than 65, which is informative -- the protective effect of relations is not just a frailty story about old age. The geographical and temporal stability suggested the deprivation-mortality coupling is a robust feature of the human substrate, not an artefact of any one cohort.

## How the findings translate to REE

Q-029 asks whether loneliness -- unshared suffering -- is an ethical harm category in its own right. The paper supplies what Q-029's wording requires: an empirical separation of loneliness from mere structural isolation, with both showing harm. That at least licenses the architectural framing, because it means loneliness-as-felt-state is doing work that is not reducible to "the agent has fewer connections." If REE wants to claim, per INV-029 (love as long-horizon care-investment) and INV-028 (shared-world modelling), that the agent's substrate is harmed when its other-modelling channel runs unshared, this paper says: yes, subjectively felt unshared-ness independently predicts substrate failure. The clinical-philosophical position implied -- treat enforced isolation as a wrong, not just a misfortune -- is consistent with the paper's framing in its discussion section, though the authors are appropriately cautious about translating effect sizes into policy.

## Limitations and caveats

The crucial caveat is the *non-difference* between objective and subjective measures. Statistically, this is consistent with two stories: either loneliness and isolation are independent harm channels of similar magnitude, or they measure the same underlying deprivation through different lenses and cannot be empirically dissociated. Q-029's architectural claim ideally wants the first story, because that is what would make loneliness a distinct ethical harm. The paper cannot tell us which story is correct. Additionally, the included studies' loneliness and isolation measures are typically highly correlated within the same cohort, which limits within-study dissociation. As with the 2010 paper, this is human all-cause mortality data, not architectural harm in any computational substrate -- the transfer remains the inferential step Q-029 itself is asking about.
