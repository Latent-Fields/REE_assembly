# Wang et al. (2023): Systematic review and meta-analysis of 90 cohort studies on social isolation, loneliness and mortality

## What the paper did

This is the largest and most methodologically rigorous of the meta-analyses on this question. Wang and colleagues registered a PROSPERO protocol (CRD42022299959), screened the prospective cohort literature, and pooled effect sizes from 90 cohorts covering 2,205,199 adults. Crucially, they stratified outcomes by cause of death (all-cause, cardiovascular disease, cancer) and tested the deprivation-mortality relationship within disease-specific subpopulations -- those with existing CVD and those with breast cancer -- so the analysis is not just a generic frailty story.

## Key findings

In the general population, social isolation independently predicted all-cause mortality (HR 1.32, 95% CI 1.26-1.39), CVD mortality (HR 1.34), and cancer mortality (HR 1.24). Loneliness predicted all-cause mortality (HR 1.14) and cancer mortality (HR 1.09). The pattern replicated within disease subpopulations: socially isolated individuals with existing CVD had HR 1.28 for all-cause mortality, those with breast cancer had HR 1.51, and breast-cancer-specific mortality was elevated by isolation as well (HR 1.33). The replication within disease subgroups is the methodologically interesting result: it argues that deprivation operates over and above pre-existing morbidity, weakening the reverse-causation reading that "sick people become isolated."

## How the findings translate to REE

The Wang paper updates and refines what the 2010 and 2015 Holt-Lunstad meta-analyses established: at the human substrate, severance of relational coupling carries measurable mortality cost. The cause-stratification adds something the earlier work did not: harm tracks with specific physiological pathways (cardiovascular regulation, immune-mediated cancer outcomes), which is the kind of mechanistic anchor REE-style architectural arguments can build on. Q-029 frames loneliness as an architectural harm category derivable from INV-029 (love as long-horizon care-investment). The Wang results are consistent with that framing: an agent that loses its shared-world coupling sustains structural harm at the regulatory and immune-defence levels of its substrate, not merely subjective distress. The paper does not make this architectural claim itself, but it tightens the empirical premise on which the claim rests.

## Limitations and caveats

The most important caveat for Q-029 is that the loneliness-only effect (HR 1.14) is notably smaller than the isolation effect (HR 1.32) and is also smaller than the 2015 Holt-Lunstad pooled estimate (OR 1.26) for loneliness. This is captured in the failure_signatures field. Two readings: the broader literature has dragged the loneliness effect down (publication bias correction, more heterogeneous measures, larger denominator), or the genuine independent contribution of subjective unshared suffering above objective isolation is modest. If the second reading is correct, Q-029's specific claim that loneliness is a *distinct* harm category becomes harder to defend, though the more general claim that relational severance harms agents remains strongly supported. The paper still cannot rule in any specific physiological mechanism that translates loneliness into mortality, so the architectural reading remains an inference.
