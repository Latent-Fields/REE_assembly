# Holt-Lunstad, Smith, Layton (2010): Social relationships and mortality risk

## What the paper did

Holt-Lunstad and colleagues conducted a meta-analysis of 148 prospective studies covering 308,849 adults to test how the quality and quantity of social relationships predict subsequent mortality. They extracted effect sizes across cause of death, initial health status, age, sex, and follow-up period, then compared several types of social-relationship measure -- from binary indicators of living alone, through structural measures of network size, to complex indices of social integration that capture both quantity and felt support.

## Key findings

The random-effects pooled odds ratio for survival was 1.50 (95% CI 1.42-1.59) -- a 50% increase in odds of survival for those with stronger social relationships. The effect was stable across age, sex, follow-up duration, cause of death, and initial health. Importantly, the effect *varied by what was measured*: complex measures of social integration produced the largest effect (OR 1.91, CI 1.63-2.23), while binary residential indicators produced the smallest (OR 1.19, CI 0.99-1.44, crossing the null). The authors' framing was that the magnitude is comparable to canonical mortality risk factors -- smoking, obesity, hypertension -- which moved the social-relationship literature out of the soft-correlate category.

## How the findings translate to REE

Q-029 asks whether loneliness -- unshared suffering -- is an ethical harm category in its own right, derivable from INV-029 (love as long-horizon care-investment is a structurally coherent agent disposition). The paper does not address that ethical claim directly, but it supplies the empirical floor on which the architectural claim can stand. If a biological agent loses its relational coupling and its substrate measurably fails, then the loss of shared-world coupling is not merely an unpleasant psychological state but a structural perturbation with material cost. REE asserts that ethics is a structural constraint on viable agents (INV-042 derived ethical objectives, INV-028 shared-world modelling). This paper says: in the only substrate we can empirically check -- humans -- the structural cost of relational-coupling failure is real, of a magnitude comparable to physical risk factors, and not explained away by initial health.

## Limitations and caveats

The paper reports on human cohorts and all-cause mortality. It does not test architectural harm in any computational substrate, does not separate loneliness (subjective unshared suffering) from social isolation (objective absence of relations) -- a distinction Q-029 cares about specifically -- and the moderator analysis suggests that any substrate that operationalises isolation as a binary flag will under-detect the effect. The transfer to a computational REE substrate (do z_world or z_resource manifolds degrade when the agent's other-modelling channel is severed?) is a strong inference, not a tested one. The paper is also pre-registration-era and observational -- causal direction is plausible but not nailed down.
