# Summary: Khosla et al. (2020) -- Supervised Contrastive Learning

**Entry:** 2026-04-04_mech_153_supervised_contrastive_label_necessity_khosla2020
**Claim tested:** MECH-153 -- E1 ContextMemory requires supervised context-labeling objective for differentiated representations
**Evidence direction:** supports | **Confidence:** 0.72

---

The cleanest way to understand what Khosla and colleagues demonstrated in 2020 is to frame it as a separation theorem: given a representation learning objective, the representations that emerge are shaped by what information the objective *attends to*, and unsupervised contrastive objectives attend to augmentation invariances, not to semantic category boundaries. SimCLR learns that an image and its rotated or cropped version should map to nearby points in embedding space. This works well when the categories of interest are correlated with the invariances encoded in the augmentation -- which, for natural image classification, they often are. But when the relevant category boundary is orthogonal to all augmentation-defined invariances, the unsupervised objective produces representations that look coherent by its own metric while failing entirely on the downstream task. The supervised contrastive loss (SupCon) solves this by providing the label: same-class instances are pulled together across *all* augmentations, and different-class instances are pushed apart, so the representation is forced to track the semantic partition the task requires rather than the augmentation invariances alone.

This is not an abstruse ML result. It is a direct instantiation of MECH-153 in gradient space. E1 ContextMemory trained on world-model prediction error is a SimCLR-analog: the training signal attends to state-transition accuracy. The LSTM encodes what makes states predictable given their predecessors. Hazard-proximate and hazard-distal contexts, in the specific CausalGridWorld environment of EXQ-181b, have overlapping z_world statistics -- both are traversable, both contain navigable paths, both generate similar prediction errors during training. The world-model objective does not attend to hazard proximity because it is not rewarded for attending to hazard proximity. The cosine_sim=0.9999 result is therefore not a failure of the architecture; it is the expected output of a correctly-functioning LSTM that has been given the wrong training objective. The SupCon paper makes this structural point explicit: the representation you get is the representation your objective shapes toward, and an objective that cannot see the category cannot shape the representation to encode it.

The supervised context-labeling objective that MECH-153 requires is the SupCon label for E1: it defines which contexts are "same" (hazard-proximate) and which are "different" (hazard-distal), and it provides this definition as a gradient signal that the LSTM can actually use. The EXQ-211 result -- cosim_supervised=0.997, marginally reduced from the unsupervised 0.9999 -- is also consistent with the SupCon framework: the reduction in cosine similarity when the label is present confirms the label is providing information the unsupervised objective cannot, but the small magnitude (delta=0.003) suggests the supervised signal strength is insufficient, not that the mechanism is wrong. SupCon with weak labels on high-dimensional problems also shows partial improvement before full discrimination; more training, stronger supervision, or a more discriminative label derivation would be the first places to look.

Two caveats deserve precision. SupCon is a batch-level contrastive loss applied to ResNets; MECH-153 involves a sequential LSTM trained with MSE prediction error plus an auxiliary classification head. The training dynamics are different, and the predicted failure geometry under unsupervised training is not identical: SupCon's SimCLR baseline still achieves partial discrimination in most image tasks, whereas EXQ-181b shows near-total collapse. This suggests the CausalGridWorld creates an unusually severe version of the problem -- the hazard proximity distinction is more orthogonal to the world-model signal than typical image categories are to augmentation invariances. The structural lesson still holds; the magnitude of predicted failure is REE-environment specific.

Confidence 0.72: the SupCon paper provides the clearest ML-level mechanistic account of why the supervised-label gradient is necessary for category-discriminated representations and why unsupervised experience with the same data is insufficient. The architectural gap (ResNet vs LSTM, image classification vs spatial context memory) moderates confidence, but the gradient-level argument is architecture-agnostic enough to transfer.
