# Summary: Heald, Wolpert & Lengyel (2023) -- The Computational and Neural Bases of Context-Dependent Learning

**Entry:** 2026-04-04_mech_153_context_dependent_learning_neural_heald2023
**Claim tested:** MECH-153 -- E1 ContextMemory requires supervised context-labeling objective for differentiated representations
**Evidence direction:** supports | **Confidence:** 0.70

---

The question Heald, Wolpert and Lengyel pose in this Annual Review paper is one that sits uncomfortably close to the MECH-153 diagnostic: what does a learning system actually need to correctly identify which context it is in? Their answer, arrived at through a careful synthesis of motor adaptation studies, reinforcement learning formalisms, and hippocampal remapping data, is that context discrimination is not a consequence of sensory experience per se but of sensory experience plus outcome feedback. The Bayesian framework they develop treats context as a latent variable inferred jointly from current sensory cues, past sensory history, and crucially, the outcome feedback stream -- the signal that carries information about whether the agent is succeeding under the current context's rules. Unsupervised sensory statistics alone produce a prior over contexts that is, in their formalism, necessarily diffuse: without outcomes to sharpen the inference, the posterior over contexts cannot partition the state space into distinct representations.

This maps onto the EXQ-181b result with the kind of directness that makes one want to verify the derivation twice. In that experiment, E1 ContextMemory was trained exclusively on world-model prediction error -- the LSTM receives z_world trajectories and minimises next-state prediction error. This is precisely Heald's sensory experience without outcome stream: the LSTM learns the statistics of states encountered near and far from hazards, but the world-model objective does not carry signal about whether the current policy is appropriate for a hazard-proximate versus hazard-distal context. The result -- cosine_sim(mean_prior_A, mean_prior_B) = 0.9999 across all three seeds -- is the predicted failure mode of a system that has the sensory component but is missing the outcome-labeled channel. The contexts look similar in z_world space because the LSTM has no reason to represent the hazard-proximity distinction in a form that is retrievable.

Heald's treatment of hippocampal remapping is particularly relevant to the MECH-153 architecture. Remapping -- the wholesale reorganisation of place cell representations across contexts -- occurs, they argue, when the behavioral policy required is different, not merely when the sensory environment changes. This implies that the hippocampus is tracking something like outcome-conditioned context identity, not pure sensory dissimilarity. If pattern separation in the dentate gyrus were driven solely by sensory input statistics, we would not expect the policy-contingency of remapping that the literature shows. The supervised context-labeling objective in MECH-153 -- a gradient derived from hazard_gradient_view.max() threshold -- is providing exactly this outcome-conditioned signal: it tells the memory system that this context requires a different policy, not merely that the sensory statistics differ.

Two caveats complicate the transfer to the REE architecture. First, Heald's framework is designed around motor adaptation contexts where outcome is movement success or failure -- a clean, binary, externally-verifiable signal. MECH-153's context label is derived from hazard proximity, which is spatially computed and internally generated. Whether an internally-derived label carries the same gradient quality as an external performance outcome is not directly addressed. Second, the Bayesian framework allows partial context discrimination from sensory statistics alone when the contexts have sufficiently different outcome distributions; the near-complete failure observed in EXQ-181b requires specific conditions (contexts with similar z_world statistics but different hazard proximity) that may not always hold. The EXQ-181b result confirms this is the regime we are operating in, but the theoretical framework does not guarantee complete failure in all environments.

Confidence 0.70: the computational framework provides a principled, multiply-grounded account of why outcome-labeled feedback is necessary for context discrimination -- and why prediction error alone fails. The moderate confidence reflects the real gap between the motor adaptation and spatial navigation evidence base and the LSTM-based ContextMemory architecture.
