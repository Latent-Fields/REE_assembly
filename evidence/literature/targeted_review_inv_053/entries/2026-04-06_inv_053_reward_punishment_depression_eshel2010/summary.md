# Eshel & Roiser (2010) -- Reward and Punishment Processing in Depression

## What the Paper Did

Eshel and Roiser synthesised the behavioural, neuroimaging, and computational evidence on
reward and punishment processing in major depressive disorder. Their central thesis is that
depression involves dysfunction in a frontostriatal network -- OFC, anterior cingulate, and
striatum -- that is modulated by monoamine systems (primarily serotonin and dopamine) and
that this dysfunction distorts the guidance of behaviour by affective information. The failure
is specifically in reward and punishment learning: depressed patients learn more slowly from
rewarding outcomes (blunted positive prediction errors) and show heightened sensitivity to
punishment (enhanced negative prediction error signalling). This is not, they argue, simply
about felt affect -- it is about the computational representation of reward and punishment
information and its use in directing behaviour. The review draws on probabilistic reversal
learning, monetary reward tasks, and monoamine depletion/manipulation paradigms.

## Key Findings

The consistent empirical picture is a double asymmetry: reduced reward sensitivity and enhanced
punishment sensitivity, producing a systematic bias toward negative outcomes in the information
that guides behaviour. This is not explained by simply feeling worse -- it is a learning and
representation failure. Patients who are equally aware that a stimulus was rewarded still
update less toward it. Neuroimaging shows blunted striatal activation to reward prediction
errors in depression, and computational modelling identifies this as a reduced reward learning
rate or blunted prediction error signal. The monoamine link is established through depletion
studies: tryptophan depletion (lowering serotonin) in recovered depressed patients reinstates
mood and learning biases; catecholamine depletion produces similar but attenuated effects.

## The Key Underdetermination

Eshel and Roiser interpret their findings at the value-learning layer: the failure is in how
the frontostriatal system computes and updates reward predictions. INV-053 adds the hypothesis
that there is an upstream substrate failure -- terrain collapse -- that would produce the
pattern they observe as a downstream consequence. These accounts are not mutually exclusive,
but they are not identical. The distinguishing test is whether the reward processing
abnormalities persist in low-harm environments where benefit_exposure is abundant. The INV-053
account predicts they should be environment-sensitive (terrain recoverable with sufficient
benefit exposure); a pure Q-value account predicts more persistent learning abnormalities
regardless of current environmental conditions. This is a testable prediction that neither
the Eshel/Roiser review nor existing clinical studies have specifically targeted.

## Limitations and Caveats

The paper necessarily operates at the correlation and average-difference level -- it is a
review of group differences in reward/punishment learning in depressed vs. control groups.
The heterogeneity of depression is not addressed, and the wanting/liking distinction (central
to MECH-117 and INV-053's architecture) is not explicitly discussed. The paper predates much
of the effort-based decision-making literature that Treadway and Zald developed, so the
motivational anhedonia component is underemphasised relative to the hedonic and learning
components.
