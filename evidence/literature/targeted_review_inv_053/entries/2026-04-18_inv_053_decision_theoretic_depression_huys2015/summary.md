# Huys, Daw & Dayan (2015) -- Depression: A decision-theoretic analysis

*Annual Review of Neuroscience* 38: 1-23. DOI: [10.1146/annurev-neuro-071714-033928](https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-neuro-071714-033928). PMID: 25705929. (According to PubMed.)

## What the paper does

Huys, Daw and Dayan offer a selective review that reframes depression as a dynamical failure in Bayesian decision-making machinery. They start from the clinical observation that the symptoms of depression are, individually, common and often adaptive features of healthy life -- sadness, withdrawal, loss of appetite, ruminative attention -- and ask what distinguishes the transient instance from the pathological one. Their answer, worked out through the decision-theoretic formalism, is that the malignant transformation happens when these symptoms enter a self-propelling spiral: the agent's evaluation of decision variables becomes systematically pessimistic, action selection follows from that evaluation, and the consequent action outcomes reinforce the pessimistic model. Depression, in their account, is miscalibration that perpetuates itself.

They locate the failure more specifically at three overlapping sites. First, the computation of decision variables -- estimated rewards, costs, transition probabilities -- can acquire pessimistic priors, perhaps through aberrant learning in the face of uncontrollable aversive experience. Second, the state-estimation machinery can drift such that the agent misrepresents the situation it is currently in (this is where they connect to learned helplessness and to the Beck-style negative cognitive schemas). Third, and most distinctively, they argue that model-based reasoning is peculiarly vulnerable: the Bellman-style tree search over future consequences admits an inhibitory pruning mechanism that, when it fails, leads to ruminative dwelling on aversive branches. The upshot is an account of depression situated entirely within the machinery of evaluation and control over a pre-existing motivational structure.

## How this maps to INV-053

INV-053 asserts something the HDD account does not: that the motivational substrate itself can fail. In the REE architecture, VALENCE_WANTING terrain and z_goal seeding are what generate goal-directed options in the first place; once benefit_exposure drops below the SD-012 seeding threshold and the terrain collapses, the agent does not simply have bad evaluations of its goals -- it has no goals to evaluate. The HDD framework, by contrast, presupposes that goals exist and studies how their evaluation goes wrong. Their pessimistic-evaluation story predicts a ruminative, cognitively active depressive phenotype; INV-053 predicts the opposite signature, a flattening in which HABIT and PLANNED modes become behaviourally indistinguishable because there is no goal-representation separating them.

These two accounts are not in competition. It is more accurate to read HDD as the contrast class against which INV-053's architectural contribution must be stated: the computational psychiatry literature has successfully located depression at the decision-variable layer; REE proposes that there is a further layer, the motivational substrate, that can fail independently. The clinical analogue is roughly the melancholic-versus-ruminative split: HDD captures the cognitively active, pessimistic, ruminative presentation; INV-053 captures the flat, psychomotorically retarded, motivationally extinguished presentation. A complete account likely needs both.

## Limitations and caveats

The HDD paper is a review, not a primary empirical study. Its claims are well-supported across the literature it draws on, but the specific mapping to INV-053 requires care. The Bayesian decision-theoretic mathematics do not directly import REE's terrain/seeding ontology; translating between the two frameworks is non-trivial. I have flagged the mapping_caveat accordingly: treating HDD as evidence against INV-053 would be a category error, and treating it as evidence for INV-053 would be an overreach. What it establishes is that the contrast class is real, coherent and well-developed, which is what the REE paper needs it for.

A second caveat concerns the self-perpetuation argument. HDD's claim that depression is self-sustaining overlaps structurally with INV-054 (the depressive maintenance loop as attractor), but the mechanics are different: HDD's self-perpetuation runs through action selection confirming a pessimistic prior; INV-054's runs through reduced benefit-oriented exploration keeping benefit_exposure below the seeding threshold. A careful treatment in the paper should note this convergence on structure from different mechanics rather than conflating the two.

## Confidence reasoning

I assigned confidence 0.60 with mapping_fidelity weighted most heavily. Source quality is very high -- Annual Review, three leading authors, well-cited. The mapping is genuinely modest: this is the canonical decision-theoretic account, but it operates at a different computational layer from INV-053. For a framing/architecture claim like INV-053, the fidelity of that mapping is what matters, and the honest answer is that the paper establishes the contrast class rather than providing direct support. That is itself valuable evidence -- it documents the layer above the one INV-053 operates at and confirms that the field has not yet addressed the substrate layer -- but it is not the kind of corroborating evidence that would warrant higher confidence.
