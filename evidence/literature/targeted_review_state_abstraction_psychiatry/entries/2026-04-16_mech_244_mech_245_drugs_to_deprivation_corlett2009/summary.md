# From Drugs to Deprivation: A Bayesian Framework for Understanding Models of Psychosis

**Corlett PR, Frith CD, Fletcher PC (2009). Psychopharmacology, 206:515-30. DOI: 10.1007/s00213-009-1561-0. PMID: 19475401.**

## What the paper did

Corlett and colleagues address a puzzle that faces any mechanistic account of psychosis: different pharmacological manipulations -- dopamine agonists, NMDA receptor antagonists, serotonergic hallucinogens -- and non-pharmacological manipulations like sensory deprivation all produce psychosis-like experiences, yet they operate through apparently different neurobiological mechanisms. The paper argues that a Bayesian/predictive coding framework can account for both the overlap and the differences. The framework maps onto frontostriatal prediction error circuits: the ventral striatum and midbrain compute prediction errors (the discrepancy between expected and received outcomes), and these signals update the cortical generative model. Manipulations that disrupt this system at different nodes produce different symptom profiles that are nonetheless interpretable within the same framework.

## Key findings for MECH-244

For psychosis specifically, the DA agonist and NMDA antagonist models produce aberrant prediction error signaling: the signal that should update the world model only when genuine prediction errors occur is either amplified without cause (DA agonists producing aberrant salience) or corrupted (NMDA antagonists disrupting the precision-weighted update). The result is a generative model that forms strong associations on the basis of spurious signals -- beliefs that are held with high confidence because the model has been repeatedly signaled that they matter, even when they don't. This is the self-sealing quality: the model's prior beliefs generate predictions that are confirmed by the aberrant prediction error system, preventing normal updating by contradictory sensory evidence. The REE framing of MECH-244 maps precisely here: prior beliefs persistently dominate because the error signal that should correct them is compromised.

## Key findings for MECH-245

The sensory deprivation section of the paper describes a different entry point: when bottom-up sensory input is removed (extreme isolation, total darkness, deafferentation), top-down predictions propagate forward without the constraining correction from sensory reality. The generative model runs in "open loop" -- it generates predictions about what should be perceived, and without sensory input to generate a prediction error, these predictions are experienced as percepts. This is the deafferentation hallucination mechanism: not a failure of precision weighting per se, but a failure of bottom-up grounding. The paper uses Charles Bonnet syndrome and sensory deprivation hallucinations as examples. This maps onto MECH-245: generative-model dominance producing percepts without bottom-up sensory grounding.

## REE mapping and the dissociation

This paper is valuable precisely because it begins -- without fully resolving -- the distinction between the MECH-244 and MECH-245 mechanisms. Psychosis (MECH-244) arises when the precision-weighting system that governs belief updating is compromised: the world model cannot be revised by experience. Hallucination (MECH-245) arises when sensory input is absent or suppressed, allowing top-down predictions to be experienced as percepts. These can co-occur (as in psychosis with hallucinations) but they are not the same mechanism. The paper's contribution to the REE taxonomy is to show that a single Bayesian framework can accommodate both, while implying that they correspond to different node failures within that framework.

## Limitations and caveats

The paper does not argue explicitly for a mechanistic dissociation between psychosis and hallucination -- that is a reading the REE framework imposes on it. The paper's primary goal is to show that a single framework can account for diverse models of psychosis, not to dissect them. Additionally, the Bayesian framework is presented at a relatively high level of abstraction; the mapping onto specific neural circuits (which prediction error signals, which precision regulators) remains schematic rather than anatomically precise. The sensory deprivation account of hallucination is better developed in other literature (Ffytche 2005 for CBS, Adams 2013 for the full active inference account). This paper is most useful as a conceptual bridge between the pharmacological and perceptual literatures.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence 0.74 for both MECH-244 and MECH-245. The paper supports both claims but does not cleanly resolve the dissociation between them. It should be cited as foundational framework alongside the more specific papers (Adams 2013 for MECH-244; Ffytche 2005 for MECH-245) rather than as primary evidence for the dissociation itself.
