# The Computational Anatomy of Psychosis

**Adams RA, Stephan KE, Brown HR, Frith CD, Friston KJ (2013). Frontiers in Psychiatry, 4:47. DOI: 10.3389/fpsyt.2013.00047. PMID: 23750138.**

## What the paper did

Adams and colleagues present a unified active inference account of psychotic symptoms, arguing that hallucinations, delusions, sensory attenuation deficits, catatonia, and abnormal eye movements are all expressions of a single core pathology: aberrant encoding of precision. In predictive coding terms, precision is the inverse variance (confidence) assigned to beliefs at each level of the cortical hierarchy, encoded by the post-synaptic gain of prediction error units. The paper proposes that dysregulation of NMDA receptors -- which modulate the gain of these units -- and downstream dopaminergic signalling produce aberrant precision allocation. The consequence is that the balance between top-down predictions (priors) and bottom-up sensory evidence is disrupted: when prior precision is aberrantly elevated, top-down predictions overwhelm sensory inputs, producing percepts and beliefs that are not updated by current evidence.

## Key findings relevant to MECH-244

The central claim for MECH-244 is the self-sealing generative model: when prior beliefs carry inappropriately high precision, sensory prediction errors are effectively discounted -- they carry insufficient weight to update the model. The generative model becomes circular: it predicts what it predicts because it never encounters evidence sufficient to revise itself. Clinically this manifests as delusions that are maintained despite contrary evidence (the precision of the delusional belief outweighs the precision assigned to contradicting sensory information). In the active inference framework, this is quantitatively specified: when prior precision is much greater than likelihood precision, Bayes-optimal inference converges on the prior regardless of the observation. Dopamine is proposed to regulate this balance by modulating the gain of prediction error signals in the striatum and cortex.

## REE mapping

MECH-244 claims that psychosis is a precision-weighting failure where prior beliefs persistently dominate sensory evidence, making the generative model self-sealing. Adams et al. 2013 is the canonical formal statement of this claim. In the REE architecture, the bottom-up prediction error signal that drives E1 (the sensory world model) is discounted relative to the top-down generative predictions. E1 encodes a world model that is no longer being updated by incoming sensory evidence at appropriate weight -- the model predicts, and its predictions are accepted as evidence rather than tested against it. This is mechanistically distinct from confabulation (MECH-094): confabulation involves write-gate failure allowing inappropriate content into phi(z), whereas psychosis involves the world model itself becoming unfalsifiable. An agent with MECH-244 failure still generates percepts and updates phi(z), but the inputs to that update are dominated by prior predictions rather than by sensory reality. The terrain the agent navigates is increasingly a projection of its priors rather than a veridical map of the environment.

## Limitations and caveats

The paper treats hallucination and delusion as co-expressions of the same underlying precision pathology. This creates a tension with the REE framework's requirement to treat MECH-244 (psychosis) and MECH-245 (hallucination) as mechanistically distinct. Adams et al. do not claim the distinction is impossible -- they offer a framework -- but their unified account provides less support for the MECH-244/245 dissociation than would be ideal. A clinician reviewing this paper should note that the active inference account of hallucination specifically (top-down generative dominance producing false percepts) is covered more directly by MECH-245 entries; what MECH-244 takes from this paper is the broader claim about self-sealing beliefs and suppressed sensory updating, which corresponds to the delusion and thought disorder components of psychosis more than to hallucination per se. The NMDA receptor hypothesis, while coherent, remains contested; ketamine (NMDA antagonist) produces psychosis-like states but the mechanism may involve disinhibition rather than direct precision modulation.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence 0.80. This is the best available formal account of MECH-244, by the leading group in the field. The mapping fidelity is high for the delusion/belief-updating component of MECH-244; it is less tight for the hallucination component, which belongs more specifically to MECH-245. The paper should be cited as supporting MECH-244 specifically, with the caveat that it partially overlaps with MECH-245 in its treatment of hallucinosis.
