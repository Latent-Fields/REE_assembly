# Understanding source monitoring subtypes and their relation to psychosis: a systematic review and meta-analysis

**Damiani, Donadeo, Bassetti, Salazar-de-Pablo, Guiot, Politi & Fusar-Poli (2022) — Psychiatry and Clinical Neurosciences**
DOI: [10.1111/pcn.13338](https://doi.org/10.1111/pcn.13338)
*Based on articles retrieved from PubMed*

## What the paper did

Damiani and colleagues conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis of 44 studies examining source monitoring in psychosis. Source monitoring is the metacognitive process of determining where an experience came from: did I imagine it or perceive it? Did I say it or hear someone else say it? Reality monitoring specifically asks whether an event was internally generated or externally derived. The review classified source monitoring tasks by subtype (internal, external, reality monitoring) and stimulus modality (visual, auditory, imagined, performed) and performed random-effects meta-analyses on each combination to identify which deficits are specific to psychosis versus general cognitive impairment.

## Key findings

Psychosis patients showed a general source monitoring deficit relative to controls (SMD=0.458) but the deficit was not uniform. Internal source monitoring -- discriminating between things the agent itself imagined versus things it actually did -- was most severely impaired (accuracy SMD=0.733, errors SMD=0.513). Imagined stimuli showed the largest deficit of any modality (accuracy SMD=0.978). Crucially, patients with hallucinations showed an elevated externalising bias (SMD=0.410): they specifically over-attributed internally generated events to external sources. This is the quantitative signature of a threshold failure: the boundary between 'this came from inside' and 'this came from outside' is displaced, making internal events read as external authority signals.

## REE translation

For Q-018, the externalising bias finding is the most important. The RC threshold controls where REE draws the line between 'this conflict signal is generated by my own world model' and 'this conflict signal represents genuine external authority input that should update my model'. A threshold miscalibrated in the externalising direction causes the agent to treat its own internally generated prediction errors as if they were external authority overrides -- the system spoofs itself. The meta-analytic effect size (SMD ~0.4 for externalising errors in hallucinating patients) gives a quantitative anchor: this is not a small or negligible failure; it is a medium-to-large effect that meaningfully impairs function. The subtype specificity is also informative: external source monitoring (distinguishing two external sources) was less impaired than internal monitoring. In REE, this suggests that the most vulnerable part of the threshold is the internal/external boundary, not the between-external-sources discrimination. The calibration focus for Q-018 should prioritise the internal-generation tracking function over general conflict detection.

## Limitations

The meta-analysis aggregates studies using diverse tasks, populations, and methodologies; the heterogeneity in effect sizes is substantial across studies (not reported uniformly). The behavioural source monitoring tasks used in these studies are retrospective memory tests, not real-time conflict detection paradigms. The externalising bias in psychosis may reflect multiple mechanisms (altered self-prediction, attentional bias, pharmacological effects of antipsychotics in treated samples) rather than a single threshold parameter. The mapping from a population-level statistical effect to a specific threshold calibration principle requires considerable inference. REE's RC mechanism is designed for online inference, which may behave differently from retrospective source attribution.
