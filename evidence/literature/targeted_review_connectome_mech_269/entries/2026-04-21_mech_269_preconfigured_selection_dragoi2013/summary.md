# Dragoi & Tonegawa (2013) — "Selection of preconfigured cell assemblies for representation of novel spatial experiences"

**Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B** 369:20120522. [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1098/rstb.2012.0522). PMID 24366134.

*(According to PubMed.)*

## What the paper does

This is a review paper in which Dragoi and Tonegawa take their preplay findings and draw out the architectural implication. The core claim is reframed: the hippocampus does not construct cell-assembly sequences de novo for each novel experience. Instead, it holds a repertoire of preconfigured temporal sequences, and external experience *selects* from that repertoire rather than instructing its formation.

They extend this framework clinically. Dysfunctions of the selection mechanism, they argue, would manifest as characteristic neurological and psychiatric symptoms: anterograde amnesia (inability to engage the selection mechanism for new experience), impaired imagination of new experiences (loss of productive selection from the repertoire), and hallucinations (selection producing content that is mis-tagged as perceptual).

The review has no new data beyond the 2011 Nature paper. Its value is architectural and interpretive.

## Findings relevant to MECH-269

The selection-from-repertoire frame is the closest theoretical statement in the literature to MECH-269's two-channel architecture. MECH-269 holds that the hippocampal proposer has a repertoire (the cognitive map and its associated trajectory grammar) and selects from it under two distinct regimes: anchored (current-V_s-gated, toward committable goals) and probe (inverted gate, curiosity-driven, hypothesis-tagged). Dragoi and Tonegawa's argument that the hippocampus is a selector-from-repertoire rather than a de-novo constructor is exactly the architectural commitment MECH-269 makes.

The clinical framing also lines up sharply with MECH-094. Dragoi and Tonegawa attribute impaired imagination to selection failure and hallucinations to selection producing content that is mis-tagged. REE's MECH-094 tag loss produces the same structural failure modes: loss of probe-tag integrity means probe content gets written as anchored (which MECH-094 identifies as the confabulation / psychosis mechanism). The two framings are independently derived but converge on the same failure-signature picture.

## How it translates to REE

This paper provides architectural reinforcement rather than new empirical evidence. It tells us that the field's strongest existing theoretical framing of hippocampal function — selection from preconfigured repertoire, with dysfunctions producing characteristic clinical syndromes — is consistent with the two-channel architecture MECH-269 proposes. The REE translation is that the hippocampal proposer can be implemented as a selector over a learned repertoire, with the anchor/probe distinction operating on the gating function rather than on the repertoire itself.

The clinical mappings are worth preserving for REE's failure-mode discussion. If REE's V3 substrate is later lesioned or perturbed to show the tag-loss / selection-failure picture, the Dragoi-Tonegawa clinical correspondence provides external validation.

## Limitations and caveats

This is a review, not primary evidence. Its empirical weight depends entirely on Dragoi & Tonegawa 2011 and related work, already pulled separately. The confidence assigned should reflect that — this paper is architecturally valuable but does not independently strengthen MECH-269's empirical grounding.

The clinical correspondences (amnesia, imagination-loss, hallucinations) are suggestive but not mechanistically demonstrated. The paper argues for them as consequences of the selection architecture; the actual clinical literature on hippocampal dysfunction is consistent with but does not uniquely identify the selection-failure mechanism. MECH-094 and MECH-269 inherit the same caveat — structural agreement, not causal identification.

Rodent spatial framework throughout. No per-stream generalisation tested.
