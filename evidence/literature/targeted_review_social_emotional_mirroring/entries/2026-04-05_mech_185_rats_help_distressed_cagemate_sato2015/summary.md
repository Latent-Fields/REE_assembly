# Sato et al. (2015) -- Rats demonstrate helping behavior toward a soaked conspecific

**Claims tested:** MECH-185 (surprise-doubled approach gradient toward distressed other), MECH-184 (other-directed harm avoidance)

## What the paper finds

Sato et al. (2015) show that rats rapidly learn to open a door to rescue a cagemate soaking in water (distressed state). Key results: (1) rats opened the door much faster when the cagemate was soaked (distressed) than when undistressed or absent; (2) rats with prior soaking experience learned faster than rats with no prior experience of soaking; (3) in a choice paradigm, rats preferred to help the distressed cagemate over obtaining a food reward, suggesting the motivational value of helping exceeds that of food; (4) the rapid door-opening is described as empathy-motivated.

## The approach-not-avoidance finding

MECH-185's core claim is that empathic responses to a distressed loved one produce *approach* toward them, not just avoidance of harm from a distance. Sato et al. provide direct evidence: the rat actively opens a door to approach and help the distressed cagemate. This is instrumental approach behavior directed at the other's state, not avoidance of the other's distress. The helping behavior is prioritized over food reward, showing the strength of the approach motivation.

## The self-experience acceleration finding

The finding that rats with prior soaking experience helped faster is directly relevant to MECH-182 and MECH-183: the rat's own prior experience of the aversive state accelerates recognition and response to the same state in another. This is the self-experience-grounding mechanism: prior first-person experience of harm makes the harm-signal from the other more legible/salient, driving faster approach. This convergent finding supports the broader REE hypothesis that empathic coupling is grounded in self-experience.

## What the paper cannot show

The paper describes the behavioral output (approach, door-opening) without characterizing the neural mechanism. MECH-185 specifically claims a *dual gradient* -- prediction error from unexpected acoustic cues PLUS z_beta leakage both converging on approach. This two-gradient structure cannot be distinguished from a single-gradient account using behavioral data alone. The paper is consistent with MECH-185 but does not provide evidence for the specific mechanistic structure.
