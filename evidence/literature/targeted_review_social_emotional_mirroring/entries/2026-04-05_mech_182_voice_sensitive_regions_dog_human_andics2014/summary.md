# Andics et al. (2014) -- Voice-sensitive regions in the dog and human brain are revealed by comparative fMRI

**Claim tested:** MECH-182 -- vocalization timbre as learned cross-modal harm-approach signal

## What the paper finds

Andics et al. (2014) conducted the first comparative neuroimaging study between a non-primate mammal (dog) and humans. Both species listened to the same set of vocal and non-vocal auditory stimuli while undergoing fMRI. The key findings are: (1) dogs have voice-sensitive cortical regions in the auditory cortex that are topographically analogous to the human anterior temporal voice area; (2) in both species, emotional valence in vocalizations -- encoded in basic acoustic parameters including fundamental frequency -- modulates activity in these non-primary auditory regions similarly. The similarity is interpreted as evidence for an evolutionarily ancient voice-processing system that predates the ~90-100 million year split between primate and carnivore lineages.

## Why this matters for MECH-182

MECH-182 claims that harm-approach signals are learned from self-experience with physiological arousal (which alters one's own vocal timbre) and then generalize cross-agent when the same acoustic features are heard from another agent. For this mechanism to operate, there must be a shared acoustic vocabulary of arousal that is legible across agents in the same species and, in the domestic dog case, cross-species. Andics et al. provide exactly this substrate: emotional valence in vocal timbre is processed by conserved auditory cortical regions in both dogs and humans, using the same basic acoustic parameters. This means the acoustic signal that carries harm/arousal information is not species-specific -- it can in principle be read cross-agent and even cross-species.

## What the paper does not address

The paper cannot speak to whether the dog's sensitivity to emotional valence in vocalizations was *learned* from self-experience or is innate. MECH-182 specifically requires that the cross-modal association forms through the agent's own physiological arousal episodes -- connecting internal states to acoustic output -- and that this learned association then activates harm-approach when the same acoustic features are heard from another agent. The passive listening paradigm used here cannot test the acquisition or transfer of this learned association. The paper shows the endpoint (shared processing of vocal emotional valence) without testing the developmental or learning mechanism.

## Novelty implications

The paper does not describe, even implicitly, the specific mechanistic claim in MECH-182: that the harm-approach signal is learned from self-vocalization experience and generalizes cross-agent via acoustic timbre matching. The shared-substrate finding is necessary for MECH-182 to be possible but does not uniquely support it over alternative accounts (e.g., innate acoustic template matching for emotional valence, with no role for self-experience in establishing the template).
