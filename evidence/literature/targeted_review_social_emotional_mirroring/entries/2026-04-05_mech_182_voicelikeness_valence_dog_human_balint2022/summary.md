# Balint et al. (2022) -- Dog and human neural sensitivity to voicelikeness: A comparative fMRI study

**Claim tested:** MECH-182 -- vocalization timbre as learned cross-modal harm-approach signal

## What the paper finds

Balint et al. (2022) extend the Andics (2014) comparative fMRI findings by testing whether the dog and human auditory cortical preference for vocalizations reflects tuning to conspecific calls specifically, or a more general preference for voice-like sounds. Using artificial stimuli parametrically varying voicelikeness (voice-like vs. sine-wave control sounds) and two acoustic parameters (fundamental frequency f0, and call length), they show: (1) both dogs and humans have auditory cortical regions responding preferentially to voice-like sounds over sine-wave tones in bilateral primary and non-primary auditory regions; (2) in bilateral primary and emotional-valence-sensitive auditory regions, f0 modulates responses to voice-like and sine-wave sounds in opposite directions in both species. The key conclusion is that the mammalian auditory system has a general tuning to voicelikeness -- not just to conspecific vocalizations -- that modulates how acoustic emotional cues are processed.

## Why this matters for MECH-182

MECH-182 requires that the acoustic features learned from self-experience with physiological arousal (changes in f0, timbre, amplitude under fear/surprise) are legible as emotional signals when heard from another agent. Balint et al. show that the auditory system's emotional valence processing is organized around voicelikeness as a general acoustic category, not species-specific templates. This means a dog hearing a human's fear-tinged voice would process the f0 and voicelikeness cues through the same emotional valence system that processes the dog's own vocalizations -- supporting the cross-agent generalization step in MECH-182.

## The innate vs. learned question

The paper does not address whether the voicelikeness preference is innate or acquired through experience. MECH-182 specifically requires a *learned* association formed through the agent's own physiological arousal episodes. If the emotional valence processing of voice-like sounds is largely pre-wired (as the evolutionary framing in the paper implies), then the learning-from-self-experience mechanism posited by MECH-182 may be supplementary rather than constitutive -- the generalization across agents could occur via innate acoustic templates, with self-experience learning playing a secondary role. This is not a fatal objection to MECH-182 (innate templates could be refined by self-experience), but it means the paper supports the output of the claim while leaving the specific mechanism underspecified.
