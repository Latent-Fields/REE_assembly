# Ben-Ami Bartal et al. (2016) -- Anxiolytic Treatment Impairs Helping Behavior in Rats

**Claims tested:** MECH-185 (surprise-doubled approach gradient), MECH-183 (z_beta leakage)

## What the paper finds

Ben-Ami Bartal et al. (2016) investigate what motivates rat helping behavior (door-opening to release a trapped cagemate). Key findings: (1) midazolam (benzodiazepine anxiolytic) reduced helping behavior without reducing chocolate-seeking, showing the effect is socially specific, not a general motivational impairment; (2) the peripherally restricted beta-blocker nadolol did NOT impair helping, ruling out a simple sympatholytic mechanism; (3) crucially, rats with the *lowest* corticosterone responses to a trapped cagemate showed the *most consistent* helping behavior at the shortest latency -- rats with high corticosterone (high stress) were the least reliable helpers; (4) door-opening appeared reinforcing in itself, with motivational value exceeding that of food.

## The corticosterone dissociation and MECH-185

The corticosterone finding is the most important result for MECH-185. MECH-185 claims that approach toward a distressed other is driven by EMPATHIC CURIOSITY (prediction error + affective resonance), not by the observer's own harm-avoidance system being strongly activated. If the observer's harm system were the driver, you would expect high-cortisol animals (more aroused, more reactive) to help most. The opposite is true: low-cortisol animals help most. This means the motivational structure of helping is not "I'm stressed by the other's distress, therefore I act to reduce my own stress." Instead, it is consistent with MECH-185's account: a moderate, curiosity-tinged affective resonance (not high-stress overwhelm) drives the convergent approach. The helper who is too stressed is overwhelmed and withdraws; the helper with moderate affective resonance is curious and approaches.

## The anxiolytic finding and MECH-183

Midazolam suppresses helping specifically while preserving food-seeking. This pharmacologically dissociates affective resonance (needed for helping) from general reward motivation. In MECH-183 terms: reducing GABAergic activity required for the affective coupling system reduces z_beta leakage, removing the approach motivation that derives from the other's distress state. This is strong evidence that the approach toward the distressed other depends on an affective resonance mechanism (z_beta leakage) rather than purely cognitive planning.

## Novelty implications

The paper establishes that affective resonance (not high-stress arousal) is the approach-motivating state for prosocial helping, and that moderate affective coupling produces more consistent approach than high arousal. MECH-185's specific claim -- that TWO approach gradients simultaneously converge (prediction error/curiosity AND z_beta leakage) -- is not directly tested. But the corticosterone finding is consistent with this account and inconsistent with pure harm-avoidance or pure high-arousal accounts.
