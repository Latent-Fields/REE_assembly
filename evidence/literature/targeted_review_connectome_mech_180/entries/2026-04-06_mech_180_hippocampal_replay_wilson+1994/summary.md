# Wilson & McNaughton 1994 -- Reactivation of hippocampal ensemble memories during sleep

**Claim tested:** MECH-180 -- Novel environments and high-MEL learning episodes adaptively upregulate the learning drive component of sleep, producing measurable increases in hippocampal replay rate proportional to novelty and prediction error load.

## What the paper did

Wilson and McNaughton implanted multi-tetrode arrays in rat hippocampus and recorded from large ensembles of place cells during three phases: slow-wave sleep before behavior, a novel spatial exploration task, and slow-wave sleep immediately afterward. They then examined whether cell pairs that co-fired during the task (because their place fields overlapped in the environment) showed enhanced co-firing in post-behavior sleep compared to pre-behavior sleep. Cells that were inactive during the task, or active but with non-overlapping fields, served as controls.

## Core finding

Place cell pairs with overlapping fields during the novel exploration showed a significant and progressive increase in co-firing during subsequent slow-wave sleep. This effect was absent in pre-task sleep and absent in non-overlapping control pairs. The enhanced co-firing was concentrated during hippocampal sharp waves and declined gradually over the sleep session. The paper interprets this as the re-expression of waking experience in hippocampal circuits -- the first direct demonstration of what is now called hippocampal replay or memory reactivation during sleep.

## Why this matters for MECH-180

MECH-180 claims that novel environments specifically trigger upregulation of the learning drive component of sleep (INV-050 third drive), manifesting as increased hippocampal replay rate among other endpoints. This paper provides the foundational empirical demonstration that novel spatial experience does exactly that. The novel environment is the causal manipulation; the increased replay is the measured outcome. The finding is consistent with the directional claim that novelty drives replay upregulation.

## What the paper does not establish

The study does not test proportionality -- whether the magnitude of replay increase scales with the degree of novelty or prediction error encountered. It also does not measure slow-wave activity power or sleep spindle density alongside replay, so the multi-endpoint prediction of MECH-180 (SWA power, spindle density, and replay all increase together) is only partially covered here. The design uses a single novel environment per animal, so there is no within-subject dose-response manipulation. It is also worth noting that the co-firing metric is a proxy for replay; it does not recover the temporal sequence of place cell firing as later studies (e.g., Louie & Wilson 2001) would do.

## Mapping caveat

The proportionality sub-claim in MECH-180 -- that replay rate is proportional to novelty and PE load -- requires parametric experiments (e.g., varying maze novelty, task difficulty, or prediction error magnitude across sessions). This paper establishes the phenomenon but not the dose-response relationship. The SWA power and spindle density endpoints require separate EEG-concurrent recording studies (e.g., Huber et al. 2004).
