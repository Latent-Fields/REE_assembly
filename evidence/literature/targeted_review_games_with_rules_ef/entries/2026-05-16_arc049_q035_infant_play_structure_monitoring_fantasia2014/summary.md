# Changing the Game: Exploring Infants' Participation in Early Play Routines

**Fantasia, Fasulo, Costall, & Lopez (2014) -- Frontiers in Psychology, 5, 522**
DOI: 10.3389/fpsyg.2014.00522

## What the study did

Twenty 3-month-old infants participated in structured mother-infant play routines in three conditions: normal (full multimodal structure with both sound and gesture), no-gesture, and no-sound. Infant engagement was measured via limb movements, gaze direction, and positive affect displays. The question: do infants track the full multimodal structure of familiar play routines, or just respond to salient cues at routine boundaries?

## Key findings

Infant limb movements were significantly higher in the normal condition compared to both altered conditions (large effect sizes). Infants gazed away more and showed decreased positive affect when game structure was disrupted, despite mothers maintaining full affective engagement throughout. Stunned expressions -- particularly in no-sound conditions -- suggested surprise at structural disruption rather than distress at partner withdrawal. The response pattern was temporally precise: infants showed immediate adjustment to structural changes rather than delayed recovery, suggesting moment-by-moment tracking of multimodal game structure.

## REE relevance

This is the earliest human data point on continuous play-frame monitoring, directly relevant to Q-035. If 3-month-old infants track the internal structure of play routines continuously rather than merely detecting open/close boundaries, this suggests that ongoing frame monitoring is a phylogenetically primitive capacity -- not something that emerges specifically with games-with-rules. What the games-with-rules stage then adds may be the normative complexity of what is monitored (shared rules, strategic rationality, fairness), not the monitoring architecture itself.

For the REE Q-035 question, this provides indirect evidence that continuous monitoring is the default: even pre-normative infants track game structure moment-by-moment. The ARC-049 heartbeat (if it exists) may therefore not be a novel architectural requirement of the games-with-rules stage but a primitive capacity that is later recruited for increasingly complex shared-state content.

## Limitations and caveats

There is a large developmental gap between 3-month sensorimotor games and peer games-with-rules at age 5-6. The monitoring mechanism at 3 months is dyadic, adult-driven, and pre-normative -- there is no enforcement, protest, or rule representation. Transfer to peer-generated rule games requires substantial interpretive extrapolation. The game structure alteration was adult-imposed (experimenter-controlled), not a partner violation, so bilateral enforcement cannot be assessed. Effects are on engagement metrics, not normative response.

## Confidence

Confidence: 0.72. Good empirical design, interesting developmental anchor for Q-035. Mapping fidelity is reduced by the large developmental gap and the pre-normative nature of the monitoring observed. Transfer risk is moderate.
