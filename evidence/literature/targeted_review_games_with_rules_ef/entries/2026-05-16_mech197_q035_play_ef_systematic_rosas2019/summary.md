# Executive Functions Can Be Improved in Preschoolers Through Systematic Playing in Educational Settings

**Rosas, Espinoza, Porflitt, & Ceric (2019) -- Frontiers in Psychology, 10, 2024**
DOI: 10.3389/fpsyg.2019.02024

## What the study did

A longitudinal study assessed whether systematically playing 32 purpose-designed games targeting specific EF components could improve preschool children's EF. Children (ages 4-6) in experimental classrooms received 60 consecutive daily sessions of 45 minutes each, while control classrooms followed standard curricula. EF was assessed at three timepoints: before intervention (T1), immediately after (T2), and 8 months later (T3).

## Key findings

Significant differences between experimental and control groups emerged at T1 vs. T3 (8-month follow-up), but not at T1 vs. T2 (immediately post-intervention). The effect was not apparent immediately but consolidated over the subsequent months. Games were categorized into playground games, expression games, and classroom games, each targeting different EF dimensions (inhibitory control, working memory, cognitive flexibility).

## REE relevance

Two points matter for REE. First, this is an existence proof for DEV-NEED-013: rule-based play can be designed to train EF components in 4-6 year olds. The specific EF components trained (inhibitory control, working memory, cognitive flexibility) are precisely the capacities that MECH-197 predicts games-with-rules should train -- constraint adherence requires inhibitory control, tracking shared game state requires working memory, turn-taking and role-switching require cognitive flexibility.

Second, the delayed emergence of effects (T1-T3 but not T1-T2) is theoretically interesting. If play training produces EF gains that consolidate over months rather than appearing immediately, this is consistent with sleep-mediated consolidation processes. REE's sleep architecture (MECH-272/273) predicts that learning during synthetic play episodes is consolidated offline; the Rosas et al. pattern is at least consistent with this, though it does not test it directly.

## Limitations and caveats

Games were adult-designed and researcher-administered -- the social coordination, bilateral frame-maintenance, and partner-monitoring dimensions of peer games-with-rules are absent. This limits relevance to ARC-049, INV-059, and Q-035. The delayed effect (no T1-T2 significance) could also reflect general habituation and deferred transfer rather than consolidation specifically. No active control matched for adult attention and engagement, so general engagement effects cannot be excluded.

## Confidence

Confidence: 0.70. Good longitudinal design with three timepoints. Frontiers in Psychology is a solid but not top-tier venue. Mapping fidelity moderate -- the study confirms that structured rule-based play trains EF but does not test the social/bilateral frame dimensions most relevant to Q-035 and ARC-049.
