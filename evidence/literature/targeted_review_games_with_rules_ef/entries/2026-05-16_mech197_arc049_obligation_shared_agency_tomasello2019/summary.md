# The Moral Psychology of Obligation

**Tomasello, M. (2019) -- Behavioral and Brain Sciences, 43, e56**
DOI: 10.1017/S0140525X19001742

## What the paper does

This is a BBS target article -- Tomasello's synthetic account of how humans develop a sense of obligation. The central argument is that obligation arises developmentally within collaborative activities structured by joint agency: when two agents interdependently link their fates (each relying on the other), the resulting 'we' agent creates normative pressure on each participant that is qualitatively different from individual goal pursuit. The formula is 'we > me' self-regulation: the agent must suppress individual preferences to preserve the cooperative identity they have entered.

## Key theoretical claims

Obligation first appears inside collaborative activities, not outside them. Children's earliest normative responses are within, not outside, joint agency contexts -- in dyadic partner collaboration initially, then in in-group membership. The peremptory quality of obligation (it feels coercive) arises from the joint agent 'we' placing demands on the individual 'me' who has voluntarily entered the shared practice. Breaches of obligation trigger protest, apology, guilt -- all asymmetric social responses that are predicted by the frame of shared agency being violated.

## REE relevance

This directly grounds MECH-197's developmental logic. Games-with-rules are the first context in which the 'we' agent is explicitly constituted by agent-neutral rules, not just shared attention (which infants have) or shared counterfactual premises (which pretend play adds). The obligation to follow the game's rules -- not cheat, not play irrationally -- is the earliest manifestation of the 'we > me' mechanism in a rule-governed context.

For ARC-049, this provides a deeper account of why the play frame requires bilateral co-maintenance: the joint-agency structure constitutively creates the 'we' that holds both participants to the frame's constraints. A unilateral frame assertion is not just strategically problematic (INV-059) but is structurally incoherent -- you cannot have 'we play by these rules' if only one agent holds the frame. This is Tomasello's point: obligation requires a genuine 'we', not a unilateral declaration.

For the ARC-049 frame structure: the 'we' in games-with-rules carries specific content (the rules, the rational engagement requirement). This matches MECH-197's claim that by games-with-rules, the ARC-049 frame becomes structured -- not just 'this is play' but 'this is play with these specific shared constraints that we jointly hold.'

## Limitations and caveats

This is a theoretical review, not new empirical data. The developmental claim relies on cited empirical work (including Rakoczy et al. and related studies). The paper addresses collaborative activities generally; games-with-rules are one specific case. Whether the 'we > me' mechanism maps specifically to games-with-rules versus earlier cooperative contexts (shared pretense at age 3) requires the inference that rule-governed frames are the first genuinely agent-neutral 'we'. Some commentaries dispute aspects of the theoretical framework.
