# Preschoolers Understand the Normativity of Cooperatively Structured Competition

**Schmidt, Hardecker, & Tomasello (2015) -- Journal of Experimental Child Psychology, 143, 34-47**
DOI: 10.1016/j.jecp.2015.10.014

## What the study did

Thirty-six 3- and 5-year-old children played a competitive game (playing for a prize) against a puppet opponent who varied on two dimensions: (1) rule compliance vs. rule violation and (2) rational play (trying to win) vs. irrational play (helping the child win). The key question: do children protest only rule violations, or do they also protest an opponent who follows the rules but plays irrationally -- and what does this reveal about the normative structure children attribute to games?

## Key findings

Children at both ages protested when the opponent helped them win (irrational play) even if no rule was broken -- demonstrating that they expected the opponent to rationally compete within the shared frame. But there was a developmental gap: 3-year-olds protested irrational play only when the opponent was also breaking rules (irrational + rule-violating combined). Five-year-olds protested irrational behavior even when rules were followed. The interpretation: 5-year-olds fully grasp the dual-level normative structure of competitive games -- compliance at the rule level AND rational engagement at the strategic level are both normatively required.

## REE relevance

This is among the strongest empirical evidence for the claim that the ARC-049 frame in games-with-rules carries structured content beyond a simple "play is authorized" tag. Children monitor not just whether the game rules are being followed but whether the opponent is genuinely trying to play -- that is, whether they are properly engaged in the shared practice. This dual-level monitoring requires tracking game state continuously, not just marking open/close boundaries: you cannot assess whether an opponent is trying to win without monitoring their moves across the episode.

For MECH-197, the developmental gap (3-year-olds not yet grasping dual-level normativity, 5-year-olds succeeding) provides a fine-grained timeline for when games-with-rules competence consolidates. The transition from single-level (rule compliance only) to dual-level (rule + strategic engagement) monitoring is a measurable developmental signature of the games-with-rules stage.

For INV-059, the finding that children protest a cheating or non-engaged opponent -- even at personal cost (they had to give up something to protest) -- demonstrates that bilateral frame-maintenance is genuinely obligatory from the child's perspective, not optional. A unilateral play assertion without partner engagement is experienced as a frame violation.

## Limitations and caveats

The paradigm uses discrete events (opponent moves) and discrete response measures (protest/no-protest). Whether children monitor game state between moves -- or compute normativity reactively when an opponent acts -- cannot be distinguished from this design. Q-035 (continuous heartbeat vs. reactive sampling) is therefore not resolved, only made more plausible for continuous monitoring. The puppet paradigm may not capture full naturalistic peer game dynamics.

## Confidence

Confidence: 0.80. High source quality (Max Planck group, JECP), strong and nuanced design, high mapping fidelity for ARC-049 structured frame and MECH-197 developmental timeline. Q-035 continuous vs. reactive distinction cannot be directly tested.
