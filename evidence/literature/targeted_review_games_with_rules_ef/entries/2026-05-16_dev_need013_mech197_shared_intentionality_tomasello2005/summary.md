# Understanding and Sharing Intentions: The Origins of Cultural Cognition

**Tomasello, Carpenter, Call, Behne, & Moll (2005) -- Behavioral and Brain Sciences, 28(5), 675-735**
DOI: 10.1017/S0140525X05000129

## What the paper does

This landmark BBS target article proposes that shared intentionality -- the uniquely human capacity to form joint goals and joint attention with full mutual understanding of each participant's perspective and role -- is the foundation of all human cultural cognition. The argument is built by contrasting human infants' trajectory with that of great apes (who share basic intentional action understanding but not shared intentionality), and autism spectrum profiles (which confirm that shared intentionality is a separable capacity). Two intertwined developmental pathways are identified: (1) understanding others as animate, goal-directed agents (shared with great apes); (2) a species-unique motivation to share emotions, experience, and activities with other persons (emerging around 9-14 months in humans).

## Key theoretical claims

Shared intentionality creates what the authors call 'dialogic cognitive representations' -- the agent simultaneously represents their own and the other's perspective as part of a joint perspective. This is what makes genuinely collaborative activity possible (as opposed to coordination through behavioral cues alone). The developmental outcome -- beginning around 9-14 months but elaborating through childhood -- is children's ability to participate in cultural practices including language, games, social norms, and institutions.

## REE relevance

This paper provides the foundational cognitive-science grounding for ARC-049 and MECH-197. The developmental progression Tomasello et al. document -- from joint attention to joint goals to shared normative practices -- directly maps to MECH-197's play type progression. What each play type trains is progressively richer shared intentionality: sensorimotor play trains joint attention, constructive play trains shared project-level goals, pretend play trains shared counterfactual premises, games-with-rules train shared agent-neutral normative constraints, and cooperative play trains the full bilateral shared-intentionality architecture that ARC-049 requires.

The contrast between great apes (who share basic behavioral coordination) and humans (who share genuine joint goals with mutual role-awareness) is directly relevant to what makes ARC-049 specifically human: ape play is behavioral coordination without genuine shared intentionality about what the play frame is. Human games-with-rules are possible because children can form joint intentions about a frame that both participants hold as genuinely mutual.

For DEV-NEED-014 (cooperative play as training objective), the paper provides the theoretical case: full bilateral shared intentionality -- the endpoint of the developmental progression -- is what cooperative play exercises, and it is both late-developing and species-specific.

## Limitations and caveats

The paper grounds shared intentionality as a general cognitive capacity rather than specifying the architecture of play frame maintenance. REE's specific claims about ARC-049 (bilateral frame tag, external legibility, exploitation prevention) require translating from Tomasello et al.'s abstract shared intentionality framework to a concrete computational mechanism. The paper also predates the more specific empirical work on normativity in games (Rakoczy et al. 2008, Schmidt et al. 2015), so it does not address the rule-violation detection architecture directly.
