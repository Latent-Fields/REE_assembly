# The Effect of Child-Appropriate Play with Various Types on Preschoolers' Executive Function

**Zhao et al. (2025) -- Acta Psychologica, 262, 106022**
DOI: 10.1016/j.actpsy.2025.106022

## What the study did

A 10-week randomized controlled trial (N=62, 4-5 year olds) with a 3-month follow-up compared three adult-guided play interventions -- movement play, puzzle play, and language play -- against a control group on executive function outcomes (inhibitory control, working memory, cognitive flexibility).

## Key findings

All three play types produced significant, sustained improvements in inhibitory control and cognitive flexibility relative to control, with effects maintained at 3 months. Working memory effects were more variable: movement play gains did not persist at follow-up, puzzle play showed improvement only at follow-up, and language play showed consistent enhancement across all three EF components. No significant differences among the three play groups on individual EF components were found.

The authors conclude that the critical shared mechanism is "the systematic integration of EF demands into adult-guided, rule-based structures, in synergy with the implicit practice of emotional regulation and contextual engagement in role-play scenarios." The play type matters less than the presence of a rule-based structure that requires children to monitor their behavior against constraints and regulate impulse under social observation.

## REE relevance

This evidence directly supports MECH-197's claim that games-with-rules train constraint satisfaction competence. The finding that rule-based structure -- not play content -- is the operative mechanism aligns with the REE thesis that games-with-rules constitute a distinct play type because they install a structured normative frame (not just a social context). The specific EF gains (inhibitory control for rule-following, cognitive flexibility for constraint switching) map to the competencies MECH-197 predicts should emerge at the games-with-rules stage.

For DEV-NEED-013 (games-with-rules learning substrate), this provides empirical backing that structured rule-based play is trainable in 4-5 year olds and produces EF gains that transfer beyond the play context itself.

## Limitations and caveats

The play interventions are adult-directed, not peer-generated games, so the bilateral frame-maintenance dimension (ARC-049, INV-059) and social coordination learning are not being tested. Play categories (movement, puzzle, language) do not map directly onto the Piaget/Smilansky taxonomy (sensorimotor/constructive/games-with-rules/cooperative), limiting direct MECH-197 mapping. The active ingredient -- rule-based structure -- is inferred from comparison across play types but was not systematically varied within a single type. Facilitator effects cannot be excluded without a matched adult-engagement control.

## Confidence

Confidence: 0.74. Good RCT design with follow-up (strong source quality), but non-standard play taxonomy reduces mapping fidelity. Finding aligns with MECH-197 mechanism but doesn't test the social/bilateral dimensions most relevant to Q-035 and ARC-049.
