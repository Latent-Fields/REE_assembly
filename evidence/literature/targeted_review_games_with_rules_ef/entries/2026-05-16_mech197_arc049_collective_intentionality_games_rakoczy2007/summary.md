# Play, Games, and the Development of Collective Intentionality

**Rakoczy, H. (2007) -- New Directions for Child and Adolescent Development, 115, 53-67**
DOI: 10.1002/cd.182

## What the paper does

Rakoczy provides a theoretical synthesis of the developmental literature on play and games, arguing that games are the primary arena in which young children first enter into genuinely collective, conventional practices. The central thesis is that play and games form a developmental progression from individual pretense (the child alone pretending) through shared pretense (jointly agreed counterfactual premises) to rule games (constitutive, agent-neutral normative structures). Each stage requires and trains increasingly sophisticated collective intentionality infrastructure.

## Key theoretical claims

The progression is not arbitrary: shared pretense requires the child to grasp that both participants co-hold a counterfactual premise (the banana is a phone) and that violating it is a normative breach. Games with rules require understanding that rules are constitutive -- they create the game, not merely regulate a pre-existing activity -- and apply to any participant regardless of their desires. This is the key transition: from "we pretend X" (agreement-dependent) to "in this game, one must do X" (agent-neutral normativity). Children first develop this understanding in the context of games around ages 3-5.

The paper draws on Wittgenstein's concept of rule-following and Searle's framework of constitutive rules, grounding developmental psychology in a principled analysis of what games-with-rules actually require cognitively.

## REE relevance

This paper provides the strongest direct theoretical grounding for MECH-197's play type progression claim. The sensorimotor/constructive/pretend/games-with-rules/cooperative sequence in MECH-197 matches Rakoczy's developmental logic: each stage is not just more complex but requires the previous stage's achievements as prerequisites. The specific claim that games-with-rules introduce constitutive, agent-neutral normativity directly explains why this play type requires structured shared-state monitoring (MECH-197's claim) and why the ARC-049 frame becomes structured at this stage rather than merely a bilateral authorization tag.

The paper also partially addresses Q-035: if game rules apply to any participant and children enforce them on third parties, this implies the frame is not purely dyadic -- it has a publicly legible structure that survives beyond the individual pair. This is consistent with ARC-049 requiring external legibility.

## Limitations and caveats

This is a theoretical review, not primary empirical data. The developmental timing claims depend on the empirical literature Rakoczy cites, and that literature has varied in quality. The paper does not distinguish continuous monitoring (heartbeat) from reactive computation, so the Q-035 question is not directly resolved. The REE mapping from collective intentionality stages to neural subsystems (E1/E2/E3) is interpretive -- the paper describes psychological-level progression, not mechanistic substrate.
