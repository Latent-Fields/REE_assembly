# Borbely (1982) -- The Two-Process Model: Foundation and Limitation

**Claim tested:** INV-050 -- sleep phase architecture is regulated by three drives, the third (MEL) being proportional to daily prediction error accumulation and determining update sufficiency.

## What Borbely Got Right

In 1982, Alexander Borbely published what would become the dominant quantitative framework for sleep regulation. The two-process model is elegant: sleep propensity at any moment reflects the interaction of two processes. Process S is homeostatic -- it rises exponentially during waking and dissipates exponentially during sleep, with its magnitude tracked by slow-wave EEG activity. Process C is circadian -- it oscillates on a roughly 24-hour period, independent of the sleep-wake history, driven by the endogenous clock. The timing of sleep onset, its duration, and the intensity of slow-wave activity are all reasonably well accounted for by the S+C interaction. The empirical evidence is solid: sleep deprivation extends and deepens slow-wave sleep in predictable ways; recovery sleep timing follows the model's predictions.

## The Gap That INV-050 Targets

What Borbely's model cannot distinguish is the following scenario: two individuals are awake for identical durations. Their Process S values are therefore identical. One has spent the day in repetitive, low-novelty activity; the other has been intensively acquiring new skills and building updated world models under high prediction error. On Borbely's model, they should have identical sleep architecture, because they have identical S and C values. INV-050 asserts that this is wrong -- that the second person requires a longer or more intense update phase to adequately consolidate the higher error burden generated during waking. Process S, operationalised as a function of time-awake rather than of learning demand, cannot represent this difference.

## INV-050's Claim Precisely

INV-050 proposes a third drive -- Model Error Load (MEL) -- defined as proportional to daily prediction error accumulation. This drive determines not when sleep occurs (that is largely S+C) but whether the overnight update phase is sufficient given the error burden. An agent with high MEL after a heavy cognitive day needs more update time than the two-process model predicts. Borbely's model is not falsified by this -- it was designed to model sleep timing and SWA kinetics, not update sufficiency -- but it is incomplete for the purpose INV-050 addresses.

## Why 'Mixed' Rather Than 'Weakens'

The evidence direction here is mixed rather than weakening because Borbely 1982 is part of the INV-050 framework, not an opposing view. INV-050 accepts drives 1 (circadian) and 2 (homeostatic pressure) as real and empirically established. The Borbely paper provides the empirical grounding for both. What it does not provide is any evidence for or against MEL as a distinct third drive. The gap is one of scope, not contradiction.

## Honest Caveats

The two-process model has been extended and refined repeatedly since 1982 -- Daan, Beersma, and Borbely's 1984 elaboration, subsequent two-process models incorporating ultradian dynamics, and more recent neuromechanistic accounts. The core S+C architecture remains the consensus framework. The question of whether Process S is sensitive to learning content (rather than just waking duration) has been explored in subsequent literature, which is where the evidence for INV-050's MEL drive becomes relevant.
