# Walker & Stickgold (2004) -- Sleep-Dependent Learning: The Learning History Shapes the Offline Phase

**Claim tested:** INV-050 -- three drives regulate sleep, with the third (MEL) determining whether the overnight update is sufficient for the error burden generated during waking.

## The Core Finding

Walker and Stickgold's 2004 Neuron review assembled the evidence that had been accumulating through the late 1990s and early 2000s: sleep is not passive rest but an active process that enhances and consolidates what was learned during the preceding waking period. The finding that performance on a motor sequence task improves overnight -- and that this improvement is abolished if sleep is disrupted -- established that sleep's consolidation function is not incidental. What happened during the day determines what sleep does at night. This seems obvious in retrospect, but it was a significant shift from the default assumption that sleep's memory benefit was simply a matter of avoiding interference from waking activity.

## Learning Demand Shapes Sleep Architecture

The more specific finding is that it is not merely the presence of sleep that matters, but its architecture. Motor sequence learning gains correlate with NREM stage 2 sleep spindles; visual discrimination gains correlate with early-night SWS and late-night REM sleep. This means the learning history is not just triggering more sleep -- it is shaping the distribution of sleep stages and physiological features. An intensive motor learning day drives spindle-rich NREM; intensive perceptual learning drives a different profile. The offline phase is being calibrated to the prior day's demand.

## Relevance to INV-050's Third Drive

INV-050 proposes Model Error Load (MEL) as the third regulatory drive, distinct from circadian timing and generic homeostatic pressure. MEL is defined as proportional to prediction error accumulation during waking -- the total burden on the world model's update queue. Walker and Stickgold's data is the most direct human empirical evidence that something like MEL exists: learning demand during waking drives specific sleep processes at night. The two-process model (Borbely 1982) predicts that two identical waking periods of equal duration should produce identical sleep. Walker and Stickgold show this is false -- a period of intensive motor learning produces more spindle-rich NREM than an equivalent rest period. The extra spindle activity is MEL's signature in the sleep architecture.

## What the Paper Does Not Establish

Walker and Stickgold do not frame their findings in terms of prediction error. They operationalise learning demand as practice on specific tasks and measure consolidation as overnight behavioural improvement. The mapping to INV-050's MEL drive requires accepting that 'prediction error during task performance' is the relevant variable driving the learning-dependent sleep changes -- a plausible but not tested interpretation. There is also a specificity question: the sleep architecture effects are task-specific (spindles for motor, SWS for visual), which might argue for multiple learning-specific signals rather than a single MEL aggregate. INV-050 needs MEL to be a summary variable that integrates across all sources of prediction error; whether that is how the brain actually operates is an open question.

## Summary

This paper provides strong support for the existence of INV-050's third drive without naming it as such. The offline phase is not a fixed nightly process -- it is shaped by the prior day's learning demand. The Borbely model cannot accommodate this finding without extension. MEL is a coherent theoretical framework for the missing drive.
