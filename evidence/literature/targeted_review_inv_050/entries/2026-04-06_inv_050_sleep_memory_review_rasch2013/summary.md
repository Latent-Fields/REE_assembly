# Rasch & Born (2013) -- Sleep's Role in Memory: Local Use-Dependence and the MEL Argument

**Claim tested:** INV-050 -- the third regulatory drive (MEL) is proportional to prediction error accumulation and determines whether the overnight update is sufficient.

## The Scope of This Review

Rasch and Born's 2013 Physiological Reviews article is the most thorough synthesis of sleep-memory research available. It covers molecular synaptic mechanisms, cellular electrophysiology, systems-level hippocampal-neocortical dynamics, and behavioural evidence across species. For INV-050 purposes, three findings from this review are particularly load-bearing.

## Finding 1: Offline Reprocessing Is Active and Selective

Sleep does not apply a uniform consolidation process to everything encountered during the prior day. It selects. Hippocampal sharp-wave ripples during SWS reactivate specific memory traces from the preceding waking period -- traces that were encoded during periods of high engagement and novelty. This reactivation is temporally coupled to cortical slow oscillations and NREM sleep spindles in a precise sequence that drives hippocampal-to-neocortical transfer. The selectivity is critical for INV-050: if consolidation were uniform and generic, MEL would be irrelevant. The fact that replay is selective and experience-dependent implies that the contents of the prior day's learning shape the offline phase.

## Finding 2: Local Use-Dependent SWA Is the Spatial Signature of MEL

The most direct evidence for INV-050's third drive is the local use-dependence of slow-wave activity. Cortical regions that were heavily activated during prior waking learning show enhanced local SWA in subsequent sleep. The magnitude of local SWA correlates with the degree of consolidation in tasks relying on those regions. This is a spatial mapping of error load to update intensity: where prediction errors occurred during waking, that is where the offline phase allocates more consolidation resources. For REE, this would mean that if E1 (world prediction) was heavily stressed during a navigational day, the overnight quiescent phase allocates more replay events and parameter updates to E1's representations than to less-stressed components. MEL is not a scalar sum -- it has a modular profile, and the offline phase distributes resources accordingly.

## Finding 3: The Overnight Update Can Be Insufficient

Rasch and Born document that sleep deprivation or disruption causes consolidation failure -- but so can an inadequate sleep architecture even without full deprivation. If the balance of SWS and REM is disrupted, specific memory types suffer. This implies that there is a threshold effect: not just any sleep is sufficient, but a specific configuration calibrated to the prior day's learning demands. This is exactly INV-050's claim about MEL: the third drive determines sufficiency. An agent with high MEL after an intensive day needs not just more sleep, but sleep of the right configuration. When that configuration is not achieved, the world model is incompletely reorganised, and the agent carries excess unprocessed error into the next waking cycle.

## Honest Caveats

The local use-dependence of SWA is an empirical finding, but the translation to 'MEL-proportional update allocation in REE' requires accepting an architectural analogy: 'cortical region' maps to 'model component' (E1, E2, hippocampal module), and 'SWA amplitude' maps to 'replay density and parameter update intensity.' This analogy is principled but not demonstrated. The Rasch-Born review is also primarily a synthesis of what we know about biological sleep -- it does not claim to characterise sleep as a computational process in the way INV-050 does. The computational interpretation of their findings is an inference, not a direct statement from the paper.
