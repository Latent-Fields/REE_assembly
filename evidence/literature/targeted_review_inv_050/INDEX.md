# Targeted Review: INV-050 -- Three-Drive Sleep Regulation (Circadian + Homeostatic + MEL)

## Research Question

Is sleep regulation governed by three distinct drives -- circadian timing, homeostatic synaptic pressure, and a learning/model-update demand drive proportional to prediction error accumulation (MEL) -- with only the third determining whether the overnight update phase is sufficient?

## Key Questions Addressed

1. Are circadian and homeostatic drives empirically established as real, distinct regulatory processes? -- YES (Borbely 1982: two-process model)
2. Does prior waking learning independently shape the subsequent sleep phase, beyond time-awake effects? -- YES (Walker 2004: sleep-dependent learning and memory consolidation)
3. Is there evidence that learning demand calibrates the distribution and intensity of offline consolidation resources? -- YES (Rasch 2013: local use-dependent SWA)

## Summary Assessment

The evidence package collectively supports INV-050's three-drive architecture:
- Borbely 1982 establishes drives 1 (circadian) and 2 (homeostatic) as real and empirically robust; is 'mixed' because it does not address drive 3 (MEL) and cannot distinguish adequate from inadequate update phases
- Walker 2004 shows that prior waking learning shapes the subsequent sleep phase -- the empirical signature of MEL operating as a drive
- Rasch 2013 provides the mechanistic detail: local use-dependent SWA shows learning demand calibrates the spatial distribution of consolidation resources during sleep

**Main gap remaining:** No study directly operationalises MEL as prediction error accumulation and tests whether MEL magnitude predicts the sufficiency of the subsequent overnight update. The evidence package supports the existence of a learning-demand drive and its effect on sleep architecture; the specific operationalisation as 'prediction error' rather than 'practice time' or 'novelty' is an inference, not a direct finding. MEL as a precisely defined computational variable remains a theoretical proposal with indirect empirical support.
