# Sleep-Dependent Protein Synthesis is Required for Cortical Plasticity Consolidation

## Source
Seibt J, Dumoulin MC, Aton SJ, Coleman T, Watson A, Naidoo N, Frank MG (2012). Protein synthesis during sleep consolidates cortical plasticity in vivo. *Current Biology*, 22(8), 676-82. DOI: 10.1016/j.cub.2012.02.016

---

## What the paper did

In cats at the visual critical period (postnatal days 28-41), the investigators induced ODP by 6 hours of monocular deprivation during waking, then pharmacologically inhibited mTOR-dependent protein synthesis in visual cortex during either the subsequent 8-hour sleep period or during the waking deprivation period itself. A parallel set of experiments assessed translational (but not transcriptional) regulation of ARC and BDNF during sleep.

## Key findings

Blocking mTOR during post-MD sleep abolished ODP consolidation. Critically, blocking mTOR during the waking MD period had no effect on plasticity induction. Sleep promoted phosphorylation of 4E-BP1 and eEF2 (translation regulators) and increased ARC and BDNF protein without increasing mRNA -- sleep activates a translational program, not a transcriptional one.

## Translation to REE

This paper directly addresses whether wakefulness alone is sufficient for plasticity. The answer: sleep executes a specific translational program (mTOR -> ARC, BDNF) gated to the sleep state. Blocking this program during sleep while leaving waking intact abolishes consolidation. For MECH-120, this establishes the mechanistic ground floor: the offline phase is executing a consolidation program that cannot be replicated during waking. The ARC and BDNF findings indicate the offline phase is selectively translating mRNAs already upregulated during waking, converting them into protein during a window when the changes can be written without disrupting ongoing navigation.

## Limitations

Rapamycin has broad effects on cellular protein synthesis. The study uses a single acute plasticity paradigm and may not generalise to longer-timescale competition dynamics. The study addresses consolidation of a normal waking experience, not correction of pre-established competitive dominance -- a distinct question.

## Confidence

Confidence 0.83. Strong causal evidence with Frank lab replication. Mapping fidelity reduced slightly because the paper speaks to normal consolidation after a single plasticity event rather than to the rescue of pre-established WTA dynamics.
