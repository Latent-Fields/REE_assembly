# REM Sleep Closes the Critical Period via BDNF-Dependent Inhibitory Maturation

## Source
Shaffery JP, Lopez J, Roffwarg HP (2012). Brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) reverses the effects of rapid eye movement sleep deprivation (REMSD) on developmentally regulated, long-term potentiation (LTP) in visual cortex slices. *Neuroscience Letters*, 513(1), 84-8. DOI: 10.1016/j.neulet.2012.02.012

---

## What the paper did

In late-adolescent rats (beyond the visual critical period, approximately postnatal day 50-60), the investigators used platform-over-water REM sleep deprivation for 4 days. This reopened LTPWM-III -- a form of LTP normally restricted to the visual critical period. Unilateral BDNF minipump infusion into visual cortex of REM-deprived animals prevented LTPWM-III induction and restored age-appropriate inhibitory tone, while the contralateral non-BDNF hemisphere showed the reopened LTPWM-III.

## Key finding

REM sleep does not merely help consolidation within an open critical period -- it is required to CLOSE the critical period. REM deprivation re-opens a pathologically juvenile plasticity state (high LTPWM-III, reduced inhibitory tone). BDNF is the molecular bridge: REM sleep drives BDNF expression, which matures cortical inhibitory circuits (parvalbumin interneurons), which applies the brakes on runaway plasticity and closes the critical period.

This directly answers the question: sleep closes critical periods. It does not extend them, nor operate independently.

## Translation to REE

For INV-049 and the monostrategy question: sleep deprivation traps the substrate in a perpetually juvenile, high-plasticity state where waking WTA dynamics continue unchecked. Normal sleep progressively closes the critical period by maturing inhibitory circuits that constrain waking plasticity.

In REE computational terms: the offline phases are not just consolidating individual episodes but are progressively building the inhibitory scaffolding that limits how much any single waking experience can dominate the weight landscape. Without adequate offline phases, this scaffolding never matures, and each waking episode can continue to competitively capture synaptic resources without limit.

The practical implication: sleep deprivation produces a double deficit -- the acute WTA amplification (Oksenberg 1996) AND the failure to mature the inhibitory scaffolding that would limit future WTA dynamics. Rescuing the substrate requires both the acute normalisation mechanism (MECH-120/SHY) and the long-term maturation mechanism (BDNF/inhibitory circuit closure) to function. A substrate with pre-existing severe WTA lock-in cannot be rescued by sleep alone if the maturation mechanism was absent during the lock-in period.

## Limitations

Platform-over-water REMSD is significantly more stressful than instrumental avoidance; elevated corticosteroids could reduce BDNF expression independently of sleep loss. The ex vivo LTP protocol may not linearly reflect in vivo plasticity dynamics. The causal direction (REM drives BDNF vs REMSD stress reduces BDNF) is not fully resolved.

## Confidence

Confidence 0.78. Moderate source quality (Neuroscience Letters, ex vivo, stress confound); conceptually important for the critical period closure question but requiring multiple inferential steps to apply to REE computational architecture.
