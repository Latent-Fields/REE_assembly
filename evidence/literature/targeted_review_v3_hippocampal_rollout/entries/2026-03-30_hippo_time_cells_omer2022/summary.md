# Summary: Contextual and Pure Time Coding for Self and Other in the Hippocampus

**Source (PubMed):** Omer, Las, & Ulanovsky (2022), *Nature Neuroscience*, [DOI: 10.1038/s41593-022-01226-y](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41593-022-01226-y)

**Claims tested:** ARC-018, ARC-039

---

The problem of temporal credit assignment -- attributing a distant outcome to an earlier action -- is one of the hardest in reinforcement learning and one of the most underspecified in REE's current architecture. Omer et al. address a prerequisite question: does the hippocampus simply encode where and what happened, or does it also encode when, independently of context? The answer, based on single-unit recordings in freely flying Egyptian fruit bats, is that CA1 contains two genuinely distinct populations. The first, contextual time cells, fire in sequential temporal patterns that shift when the bat hangs at different locations -- they encode time, but time that is context-stamped. The second, pure time cells, maintain similar preferred firing times across different spatial contexts -- they encode elapsed time per se, independent of where the bat is. Both populations anchor to the bat's own landing event. In a social imitation task, a subset of time cells also activate relative to another bat's landing, encoding the temporal context of an observed action.

For ARC-018, this matters because hippocampal rollout proposals are not just spatial paths -- they are temporal sequences where the order and timing of action-consequence events determines the utility of the trajectory. A trajectory that produces harm at step 3 is very different from one that produces the same harm at step 15, even if the z_world states visited are similar. Pure time cells provide the architecture for tagging each rollout step with elapsed time from a reference event (goal contact, harm event, episode start), enabling E3 to correctly weight outcomes by their temporal proximity. Without a temporal scaffold of this kind, E3's trajectory evaluation is necessarily myopic: it can assess what happens but not when.

For ARC-039, the social imitation finding is unexpectedly relevant. If time cells can track the temporal context of another agent's actions -- firing relative to another bat's landing -- then the hippocampal module may naturally encode temporal information about multi-agent scenarios. In REE's V4 multi-agent work, the ability to attribute temporal credit across both self and other actions (MECH-127's counterfactual other-cost reasoning) likely depends on exactly this kind of other-referenced time coding. This is speculative at V3 scale but worth noting as a motivation for ARC-039's consolidation architecture.

This evidence motivates new candidate claim MECH-148: hippocampal pure time cells provide a temporal scaffolding signal for E3 credit assignment across delayed outcomes. The claim would assert that the hippocampal rollout module must include a time-coding layer that produces pure elapsed-time signals independent of z_world state, enabling E3 to correctly discount or upweight outcomes based on their temporal distance from the reference event in the trajectory. Empirically, this predicts that ablating the time-coding component of the hippocampal module should specifically impair delayed credit assignment (long-horizon trajectories) without degrading immediate trajectory evaluation (short-horizon rollouts).

The main caveat is that bat CA1 time cells fire relative to real elapsed time, while REE's rollout steps are simulated and discretized. The assumption that time cells fire at fixed intervals during E3's internal simulation is mechanistically plausible -- there is evidence that hippocampal replay sequences are temporally compressed versions of experienced sequences -- but it has not been directly tested in a planning-as-simulation paradigm. The contextual-time-cell population also introduces complexity: step-level temporal tags in REE will naturally be confounded with z_world state unless the architecture specifically separates the two signals. Confidence 0.78.
