# Summary: A Neural Signature of Pattern Separation in the Monkey Hippocampus

**Source (PubMed):** Sakon & Suzuki (2019), *PNAS*, [DOI: 10.1073/pnas.1900804116](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1900804116)

**Claims tested:** ARC-018, MECH-033

---

The central problem Sakon & Suzuki address is one that should resonate immediately with REE's trajectory proposal problem: how does the brain generate distinct memory traces from inputs that are objectively similar? They trained macaques on a visual recognition task in which animals had to discriminate 'lure' images -- closely matched variants of previously seen images -- from repeats and genuinely novel items. Single-unit recordings from CA3 and dentate gyrus (DG) neurons captured the neural signature of this disambiguation. The key finding is crisp: when monkeys correctly classified lure images as distinct from repeats (the behavioural signature of pattern separation), CA3/DG neurons responded to lures more like novel images than like repeats. A subpopulation of cells -- not the majority, but a functionally coherent subset -- was specifically responsible for the lure/repeat discrimination, classifying all three image types from firing rates alone. Pattern separation, in other words, has a sparse and selective neural implementation at the population level, concentrated in CA3/DG rather than being a diffuse property of hippocampal activity.

The REE translation runs directly through ARC-018 and MECH-033. ARC-018 claims that the hippocampal rollout module constructs counterfactual trajectory proposals and maps their viability -- but this function is only useful if the proposals are genuinely distinct from each other and from previously traversed paths. If two proposed trajectories produce near-identical z_world sequences, their viability assessments are redundant and the planning process wastes E3 evaluation cycles. Sakon & Suzuki tell us that CA3/DG is the circuit that prevents this redundancy in biological systems: before a sequence gets promoted to downstream evaluation, DG applies a sparse, high-dimensional expansion that accentuates differences between similar inputs. For MECH-033, the implication is that E2 forward-prediction kernels seeding hippocampal rollouts need to pass through a DG-equivalent separation stage before being committed to trajectory proposals. Without this stage, the kernel-chaining interface will systematically generate similar trajectories from similar z_world states -- exactly the failure mode MECH-033 is designed to prevent.

This evidence directly motivates a new candidate claim, MECH-147: DG-mediated pattern separation gates trajectory disambiguation in hippocampal rollout. The claim would assert that a DG-equivalent sparse encoding stage must precede trajectory proposal generation, ensuring that proposed paths through z_world terrain are non-redundant even when the originating z_world states are similar. Empirically, this predicts that an ablation of the DG-equivalent layer in V4 should produce a specific failure mode: not reduced planning accuracy overall, but collapsed trajectory diversity (multiple proposals converging on the same path through similar z_world regions).

Two caveats belong at the front. First, the mapping is from item-level disambiguation (visual images) to trajectory-level disambiguation (action sequences). These are not the same computational problem -- image lures are single-timestep inputs while trajectory lures are sequential structures unfolding over many E2 forward steps. The temporal dimension adds complexity that the Sakon & Suzuki paradigm does not address. Second, pattern separation in DG is probabilistic and sparse: most neurons do not participate, and the discrimination is never perfect. REE should not assume a DG-equivalent layer produces binary separation; it should model graded disambiguation that improves as trajectory divergence increases. Confidence 0.81.
