# Kurzban, Duckworth, Kable & Myers 2013 -- Opportunity-cost model of subjective effort

**Citation:** Kurzban R, Duckworth A, Kable JW, Myers J. An opportunity cost model of subjective effort and task performance. *Behav Brain Sci*. 2013;36(6):661-79. PMID: 24304775. [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1017/S0140525X12003196).

## What the paper does

Kurzban et al. propose a unifying account of cognitive effort in which the felt aversiveness of mental labour, the deterioration of performance with time-on-task, and the disengagement-when-better-alternatives-arise pattern are all manifestations of a single cost-benefit computation. Their central move is to reject resource-depletion explanations and instead identify subjective effort with the OUTPUT of an opportunity-cost computation: deploying executive computational mechanisms on the current task forecloses their use on alternatives, and the opportunity cost of that foreclosure rises monotonically with the value of the alternatives. The felt-effort signal, they argue, IS the introspective readout of this opportunity cost. Performance reductions over time-on-task are motivated disengagement, not resource exhaustion. The paper is a Behavioral and Brain Sciences target article and was published with peer commentary (notably Westbrook & Braver in the same issue, PMID 24304803, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1017/S0140525X13001179), explicitly endorsing the opportunity-cost framing while raising measurement concerns).

## Why this matters for ARC-068

The user-observed phenomenology that triggered the ARC-068 registration -- "the felt pull of I am wasting time when capacity is high and nothing specific is being pursued" -- is most cleanly anchored to this paper. Niv 2007 supplies the substrate-and-form anchor (avg-reward-rate scalar, additive form on time-consumed); Kurzban 2013 supplies the felt-output anchor (the introspective signal of "I should be doing something else" IS the opportunity cost expressed as conscious experience). For the child MECH design, this licenses a routing decision: the ARC-068 score-side cost can also be exposed to a valence-side signal, plausibly via the z_harm_a affective channel that ARC-067 boredom also routes through.

The paper is the load-bearing anchor for the synthesis R4 verdict (effort-cost vs opportunity-cost separation). Kurzban's UNIFIED account argues against separating effort cost (cost of expensive action; Salamone 2003 territory) and opportunity cost (cost of inaction; Niv 2007 territory) at the FELT-OUTPUT layer. They are the same felt signal, even if the substrate-input terms differ. For REE this implies that ARC-068 should NOT be implemented as a wholly novel substrate parallel to MECH-258 (cingulate.precision_weighted_pain_PE) and the existing effort-cost machinery; rather, the trajectory-score-side cost should compose into a unified cost output, with the avg-reward-rate scalar adding to the existing cost ledger.

The paper is also relevant to R1 (ARC-068 vs SD-032b boundary) -- but more weakly. Kurzban does not engage the dACC-foraging-value vs trajectory-score-cost question directly; the framework is at a coarser level that is consistent with either. R1 must lean on Kolling 2016 and Shenhav 2016 instead.

## Caveats

The paper is THEORETICAL. It is a unified-account proposal, not an empirical demonstration of ARC-068's specific score-side instantiation. The peer-commentary structure of BBS target articles is itself the relevant epistemic context: a theoretical proposal that has been pressure-tested by published responses but has not been proven mechanistically. Treat the paper as the felt-phenomenology anchor and the unified-account argument; do not lean on it for substrate identification.

A second caveat is regime mismatch. Kurzban's examples are subjects engaged in explicit cognitive tasks. ARC-068 most cares about the regime where NO specific task is in scope -- the well-fed-safe-familiar agent with no z_goal active. Kurzban's framework licenses extension into that regime (the no-task regime is just a degenerate case where the next-best alternative is generic action) but does not directly demonstrate it. The transfer-risk score is moderate-to-high for that reason.

A third caveat is the temporal-kernel silence. Kurzban does not commit to whether opportunity cost is computed against a long-window historical alternative-value (Niv) or a short-window current-context alternative-value (Kolling). R2's reward-rate kernel verdict cannot lean on this paper alone.
