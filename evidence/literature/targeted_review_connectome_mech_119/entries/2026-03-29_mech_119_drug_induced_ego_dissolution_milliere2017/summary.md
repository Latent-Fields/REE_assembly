# Looking for the Self: Phenomenology, Neurophysiology and Philosophical Significance of Drug-induced Ego Dissolution

**Milliere (2017) -- Frontiers in Human Neuroscience -- DOI: 10.3389/fnhum.2017.00245 -- PMID: 28588463**

## What the paper did

Raphael Milliere reviews drug-induced ego dissolution (DIED) -- the disruption of the minimal or embodied sense of self produced by hallucinogenic compounds -- with a focus on phenomenology, neurophysiology, and philosophical implications. The paper draws on self-report questionnaire data from the psychedelic research literature and organises the findings around three pharmacologically distinct drug classes: classical psychedelics (psilocybin, LSD, DMT -- acting on serotonin 5-HT2A receptors), dissociative anesthetics (ketamine, PCP -- acting on NMDA glutamate receptors), and kappa-opioid receptor agonists (salvinorin A). These three classes produce ego dissolution by different molecular mechanisms and, Milliere argues, produce phenomenologically distinct forms of self-dissolution. The paper situates these findings within Bayesian models of phenomenal selfhood and asks what ego dissolution reveals about the minimal conditions for self-consciousness.

## Key findings relevant to MECH-119

The three-drug-class framework is the key contribution for MECH-119. Classical psychedelics produce what questionnaires label 'oceanic boundlessness': a positive-valence expansion of self-boundaries, dissolution of the felt distinction between self and world, often experienced as blissful. Cognitively, this corresponds to high entropy, high novelty, expansive and unfamiliar self-states -- the D_eff should be high (dispersed) and familiarity low. This is Regime 2 of MECH-119 with an affective dimension (positive valence) not fully captured by the regime taxonomy alone.

Dissociatives produce a different pattern: cognitive clarity is preserved, self-location may be intact, but there is a profound sense of detachment -- 'I am here but I am not really here.' This phenomenology overlaps substantially with clinical depersonalisation described by Sierra and David 2011. The self-model appears coherent (low D_eff) but the familiar felt-quality of selfhood is absent (low familiarity). This is Regime 3.

Kappa-opioid agonists produce a third pattern: dysphoric unfamiliarity -- the world and body feel alien, unfamiliar, wrong -- but with retained sense of self-location. This is a variant of Regime 3 (coherent but unfamiliar) with an aversive affective quality distinct from the neutral detachment of dissociatives.

Crucially, these three patterns are pharmacologically specific -- they are produced by drugs acting on different receptor systems. This specificity suggests they correspond to distinct computational configurations, not just quantitative variation on a single axis. The pharmacological dissociation is the most suggestive evidence that the MECH-119 regime space is real and not a conceptual convenience.

## REE translation

The three drug classes provide existence proofs for three distinct points in the (D_eff, familiarity) space:
- Psychedelics: high D_eff, low familiarity (Regime 2 -- dispersed and unfamiliar)
- Dissociatives: low D_eff, low familiarity (Regime 3 -- coherent but unfamiliar)
- Kappa-opioids: low D_eff, low familiarity with dysphoric quality (Regime 3 variant -- coherent, unfamiliar, aversive)

Normal: low D_eff, high familiarity (Regime 1).

The pharmacological independence of the three patterns -- different receptors, different mechanisms -- supports the view that D_eff and familiarity are separately instantiated computational processes, not different readouts of the same underlying state. This is the most direct available support for Q-022's central dissociation question. For MECH-119, it confirms that the coherent-but-unfamiliar regime is not a theoretical construct but a pharmacologically accessible state that biological systems actually enter.

## Limitations and caveats

The evidence base is phenomenological self-report. No study in this literature has measured D_eff or Hopfield stability computationally under any of these drug conditions. The translation from questionnaire dimensions (oceanic boundlessness, dread of ego dissolution, visionary restructuralisation) to computational regimes (D_eff, familiarity) is interpretive, not derived from direct measurement. The three-class taxonomy is also an idealisation: drug effects are dose-dependent, set-and-setting modulated, and individually variable. Substantial overlap exists between drug classes at certain doses. The philosophical framing of the paper -- whether consciousness requires self-awareness -- is somewhat removed from the architectural question MECH-119 addresses.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is set at 0.68. The pharmacological specificity of the three patterns is genuinely suggestive, but the evidence is phenomenological rather than mechanistic, and the translation to computational regimes is interpretive. This is the weakest entry in the MECH-119 cluster in terms of direct evidentiary strength, but it adds a valuable independent dimension: the three drug classes provide pharmacological dissociation evidence that complements the clinical (Sierra/David) and connectivity (Paul) evidence. Together the three entries make a stronger case than any one individually.
