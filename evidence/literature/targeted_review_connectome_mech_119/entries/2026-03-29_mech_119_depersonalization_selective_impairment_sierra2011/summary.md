# Depersonalization: A selective impairment of self-awareness

**Sierra & David (2011) -- Consciousness and Cognition -- DOI: 10.1016/j.concog.2010.10.018 -- PMID: 21087873**

## What the paper did

Maurice Sierra and Anthony David present an updated review of depersonalisation disorder (DPD), integrating neuroimaging, psychophysiological, and phenomenological evidence around a fronto-limbic inhibition model. The core clinical phenomenon is well-established: patients with DPD describe feeling detached from their own mental states and body, experiencing themselves as if from a distance, and finding their usual self-related experiences stripped of reality or emotional resonance. Critically, this occurs in the absence of cognitive confusion -- the patient can describe their experience articulately, reason clearly, and navigate the world adequately. They are not confused about what is happening. They simply cannot feel that it is happening to them.

The neural model proposes that right ventrolateral prefrontal cortex exerts excessive inhibition on limbic and insular systems, particularly the amygdala and anterior insula. The insula normally generates interoceptive awareness and the 'felt' quality of bodily presence. The amygdala assigns emotional salience to stimuli including self-referential content. When prefrontal inhibition suppresses these systems, the result is not cognitive impairment but affective desensitisation: thoughts and perceptions continue normally, but they are no longer tagged as emotionally salient, no longer felt as 'mine.' This is the 'emotional numbing' described by patients.

## Key findings relevant to MECH-119

Two findings are critical. First, the characterisation of DPD as 'cognitively intact but affectively desensitised' directly instantiates MECH-119's Regime 3: low D_eff (coherent self-model, clear thinking) combined with low Hopfield familiarity (the self-states are no longer emotionally familiar -- not recognised as 'mine'). The patient is not in Regime 2 (confused, dispersed self-model -- which would be high D_eff) and not in normal Regime 1 (coherent and familiar). They are in the third regime that D_eff monitoring alone cannot detect.

Second, the distinction between DPD and acute psychosis is instructive. In psychosis, the self-model structure breaks down: the patient may believe they are someone else, or that their thoughts are being inserted. In DPD, the self-model structure is intact but the emotional familiarity tag is missing. This is the MECH-119 prediction: that Regime 3 is pathological but differently so from Regime 2, and that a monitoring system detecting only coherence (D_eff) would fail to catch it while registering falsely normal.

## REE translation

MECH-119 predicts that low D_eff + low Hopfield stability constitutes a distinct pathological regime from high D_eff (Regime 2). DPD is the clinical existence proof. The REE agent in Regime 3 would have a coherent z_self (D_eff within normal range, self-maintenance loss low) but would be in uncharted internal territory (Hopfield stability low, no stored self-state matches the current configuration). The D_eff-only monitoring of MECH-113 Level 1 would not flag this as abnormal. The full MECH-118/MECH-119 system -- incorporating both D_eff and Hopfield familiarity -- would detect it. This is the architectural argument for why MECH-118 and MECH-119 are not redundant additions to MECH-113 but necessary extensions.

## Limitations and caveats

The 'emotional familiarity' impaired in DPD is not identical to Hopfield pattern familiarity in REE. The biological familiarity signal disrupted in DPD operates through insular and amygdaloid circuits that process affective and interoceptive salience. REE's HopfieldMemory computes a geometric match between current z_self and stored z_self vectors. These are different mechanisms. The mapping is by phenomenological analogy (the 'coherent but unfamiliar' pattern) rather than by mechanistic homology. Additionally, DPD is a heterogeneous clinical entity; not all patients fit the pure fronto-limbic inhibition model, and some may have elements of high D_eff (cognitive disorganisation) that would place them in a mixed regime. The review represents expert synthesis rather than primary data.

## Confidence reasoning

Confidence is set at 0.77. Sierra and David's review is the leading clinical account of DPD and the fronto-limbic inhibition model has substantial empirical support. The phenomenological match to Regime 3 is compelling. The limitation is the interpretive distance between 'emotional familiarity' and 'Hopfield pattern familiarity' -- these are analogous but distinct constructs. The clinical existence proof supports MECH-119's claim that the coherent-but-unfamiliar regime is real and pathologically distinct; it does not validate the specific computational implementation.
