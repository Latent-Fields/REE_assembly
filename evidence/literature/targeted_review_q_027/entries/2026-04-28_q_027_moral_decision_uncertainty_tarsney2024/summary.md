# Tarsney, Thomas & MacAskill, "Moral Decision-Making Under Uncertainty" (SEP 2024) -- relevance to Q-027

## What the entry covers

The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry by Christian Tarsney, Teruji
Thomas, and William MacAskill is a synoptic review of how rational agents
should choose when they are uncertain not only about empirical consequences
but about which moral theory is correct. Its central question is whether the
classical decision-theoretic apparatus (expected utility, risk-aversion,
lexicographic priority) can be lifted from individual prudence into ethics.

The entry surveys three main approaches:

- **My Favorite Theory (MFT).** The agent acts according to whichever moral
  theory it has greatest credence in. Simple, but criticised for ignoring
  substantial credences in rival theories.
- **Expected Moral Value (MEC).** The agent maximises expected moral
  choiceworthiness across rival theories. Requires intertheoretic comparison,
  which the entry treats as an unsolved foundational problem.
- **Lexical Priority.** The agent treats certain constraint-violations as
  absolutely prior to anything else. The entry is dismissive: such
  approaches are "implausibly fanatical" because vanishingly small
  probabilities of catastrophic constraint-violation can dominate every
  decision.

The entry also names sources of deep uncertainty that classical decision
theory cannot accommodate: imprecise probabilities, unawareness (the agent
does not even have the right state-space), and cluelessness (the agent
cannot estimate long-term consequences with any precision).

## Why this matters for Q-027

Q-027 has two pieces of uncertainty inside it. The first is empirical: will
this harm, in fact, be irreversible? The second is normative: is this class
of harm, in fact, ethically distinctive enough to warrant categorical
treatment? The SEP entry's framework speaks to both, in slightly different
ways.

If REE adopts strict lexical priority for any harm tagged irreversible,
it inherits the fanaticism objection: a vanishingly small subjective
probability of irreversible harm can paralyse the agent. This is
philosophically substantial: Q-027 is *not* answered by saying "treat
irreversibility as lexically prior". A bare lexicographic rule is the
objection, not the solution.

If REE goes the other way and computes expected moral value across "this
harm is irreversible" and "this harm is recoverable" with some weighting,
then it inherits the intertheoretic-comparison problem: the unit by which
irreversible-harm-cost is compared with reversible-harm-cost has to come
from somewhere, and the SEP entry treats that "somewhere" as foundationally
unresolved.

The entry's most useful pointer for Q-027 is the cluelessness diagnosis: at
long horizons, REE may be structurally unable to estimate whether a
consequence is irreversible. This is *not* curable by gathering more data;
it is a feature of the agent's epistemic position. Any Q-027 answer that
pretends irreversibility forecasts are reliable at long horizons is
overclaiming.

## Possible REE re-framing

Combining this with Sunstein 2006 (separately filed in this directory):
REE's principled answer to Q-027 should probably *not* be lexical-priority
of irreversible harms; it should be a graded, probability-weighted treatment
in which irreversibility-related categorical-classes (touching INV-026
self-existence and INV-029 love-bearing capacities) carry extra weight in
the harm signal but never absolute priority. The graded version dodges
fanaticism. The categorical-class scaffolding gives the structural
protection Q-027 is reaching for.

That hybrid is structurally consistent with the SEP entry's analysis but
not endorsed by it -- the entry surveys it as a possibility space rather
than recommending a specific point in that space.

## Caveats

The SEP entry's core problem is uncertainty across moral *theories*.
Q-027's core problem is uncertainty about a *property* (irreversibility) of
a single harm under a more-or-less fixed moral theory (REE's eight axioms).
The two problems have similar formal structure but different content. The
translation is interpretive work that the entry does not do, and applying
the entry's apparatus to REE requires filling in steps the entry leaves
open. I am marking this *mixed* (the entry both supports and constrains
candidate answers) with confidence 0.42.
