# IEP, "Precautionary Principles" -- relevance to Q-027

## What the entry covers

The Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry on precautionary principles
surveys the conceptual landscape behind a family of policy rules that share
a common form: when there is a threat of serious or irreversible harm, lack
of full scientific certainty should not be used as a reason to delay
preventive action. The Rio Declaration (1992) provides the canonical
formulation. The article distinguishes weak versions (lack of certainty
does not preclude action) from strong versions (lack of certainty *requires*
preventive action), and surveys decision-theoretic operationalisations:
maximin (act as if the worst case is true), minimax regret (minimise the
maximum regret across possible worlds), and risk-versus-uncertainty
distinctions in the spirit of Knight and Hansson.

It also names the canonical objections: strong formulations risk paralysis
(if irreversible harms are on every side, the rule prohibits both action
and inaction), and weak formulations risk triviality (they reduce to
ordinary consequentialist prudence).

## Why this matters for Q-027

Q-027 asks whether REE can have a principled definition of irreversible
harm that does not require certainty about irreversibility. The IEP entry
answers the form of this question affirmatively at the conceptual level:
the precautionary literature has explicitly converged on the position that
the trigger condition is a *plausible threat* of irreversibility, not a
verified fact of irreversibility. So the philosophical scaffolding for
Q-027 does exist, and it has been worked through in detail in the
environmental-ethics tradition.

The decision-theoretic operationalisations are also useful pointers. REE
already operates over forward models that produce something like a
possible-worlds distribution. Maximin (treat the worst forecasted outcome
as the active comparator) and minimax regret (minimise the worst-case gap
between the action taken and the action that would have been best in
hindsight) are recipes the agent could in principle run on those forecasts.

The entry's most important caution for Q-027 is the under/over-precaution
trade-off. A REE rule that always escalates any harm tagged "possibly
irreversible" inherits the paralysis failure mode -- which has a familiar
echo in psychiatric phenomenology, where excess preoccupation with
irreversibility is itself a clinical sign. Conversely, a rule that
under-weights irreversibility is no more protective than the existing harm
signal, and so does not do the architectural work Q-027 was asking for.

## Possible REE answer shape

Reading this entry alongside Sunstein 2006 and the SEP entry on Moral
Decision-Making Under Uncertainty (both filed in this directory), a
defensible Q-027 answer for REE has the following shape:

1. Treat irreversibility as a graded property of forecasts, not a binary
   fact. The trigger condition is a plausible threat (computed from forward
   models with imprecise probabilities), not a certainty.
2. Carry a small categorical-class scaffold tied to INV-026 (self existence)
   and INV-029 (love-bearing capacities). Harms that touch these classes
   carry extra weight, but never absolute lexical priority -- the
   fanaticism objection from the SEP entry rules absolute priority out.
3. Choose a decision rule that tolerates imprecise probabilities. Minimax
   regret is a defensible default. Maximin is too paralysing in the limit.

This is not an answer the entry gives directly; it is the synthesis the
entry supports.

## Caveats

The IEP entry is unsigned and undated in the visible metadata, which limits
how strongly any specific phrasing should be cited. The entry is also
firmly at the policy level -- it does not address single-agent
decision-making under uncertainty, which is REE's situation. Source quality
is therefore moderate, not high.
